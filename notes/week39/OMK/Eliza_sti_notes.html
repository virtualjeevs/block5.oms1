LO 2 Identify pathogen virulence factors & describe mechanisms of host defense evasion

🦠 HSV — Virulence & Immune Evasion

Virulence FactorsMechanism of Host Defense Evasion
Envelope aids attachment; cell fusion yields syncytia with inclusion bodiesRemains latent in neurons to avoid immune surveillance
High viral load in vesicles ("super infectious")Lytic phase triggers inflammation, but latency helps the virus persist
Hides in neuronal cells; undergoes lytic phase when activeOnly LAT transcripts produced during latency — no viral proteins → invisible to immune system
Key Concept: HSV has four structural layers: dsDNA core → protein capsid → tegument → lipid envelope. Glycoprotein G1 = HSV-1, Glycoprotein G2 = HSV-2. Virus enters via receptor-mediated endocytosis or endosome formation.

🦠 HPV — Virulence & Immune Evasion

Virulence FactorsMechanism of Host Defense Evasion
Oncogenes E6 and E7E6 binds to p53 → mediates degradation → uncontrolled proliferation & resistance to apoptosis
Capsid proteins L1/L2E7 binds to pRb → frees E2F → unrestricted entry into S-phase
E6 stimulates expression of TERT (telomerase)Downregulation of type I interferon responses prevents macrophage detection
Early genes (E1-E7) for replication; Late genes (L1/L2) structuralNo lytic phase → no cell death → no inflammation → invisible to immune system
Oncogenic Mechanism: E6 degrades p53 (normally promotes cell cycle arrest/apoptosis) → uncontrolled proliferation. E7 binds Rb (normally blocks E2F) → unrestricted S-phase entry. Both together = malignant transformation. Viral DNA integrates into host DNA in cancer.

🦠 HIV — Virulence & Immune Evasion

Virulence FactorsRole
gp120 / gp41 envelope proteinsViral attachment to host CD4 receptor
Reverse transcriptaseSynthesizes DNA from genome RNA
IntegraseIntegration of proviral DNA into host genome → gets integrated into host DNA
p24 nucleocapsid antigenImportant serologic marker for diagnosis
Transmission: Blood, semen, vaginal fluid contact with mucous membrane ± micro-abrasions. Enters via direct infection, transcytosis, or transmigration. The presence of other STIs increases probability of HIV transmission. Disrupts cell-mediated immunity.

🦠 Chlamydia trachomatis — Virulence & Immune Evasion

Virulence FactorsMechanism of Host Defense Evasion
Elementary Body (EB) — for host cell entryEB survives outside cells and initiates infection
Reticulate Body (RB) — for replicationRB multiplies within host cells, triggering immune response only after intracellular replication
Type III Secretion System (T3SS) effectorsInjected into cytoplasm; modifies inclusion membrane to prevent lysosomal degradation
Inclusion formation ensures intracellular survivalLysis releases EBs to continue infection cycle → host cell death
Life Cycle: EB attaches → enters vacuole (= inclusion) → differentiates into RB → RB proliferates by binary fission → RB → EB → lysis releases new EBs. The inclusion expands, hijacks nutrients from Golgi/ER, fills the cytoplasm, then bursts.

🦠 Neisseria gonorrhoeae — Virulence & Immune Evasion

Virulence FactorMechanism
Pili (with antigenic variation)Mediate attachment to host cells; antigenic variation helps evade initial immune detection
Opacity (Opa) proteinsPromote uptake into cells, sheltering the pathogen; evade immune response
Porin proteinsPrevent phagolysosome formation and aid in nutrition
Reduction modifiable proteins (Rmp)Block host bactericidal antibodies, diminishing the immune response
Pathogenesis: Colonizes → invades and undergoes transcytosis → releases peptidoglycan, LOS, and OMVs → activates TLR/NOD signaling → cytokine/chemokine release → massive neutrophil influx → purulent exudate that facilitates transmission.

🦠 Treponema pallidum — Virulence & Immune Evasion

Virulence FactorMechanism
Adhesion molecules (TP0453/Tpp17)Adhere to epithelial cells and extracellular matrix
Flagellar apparatusFlagellar rotation assists tissue penetration — "hides and goes where it likes"
TP0751 proteasePenetrates extracellular matrix via proteolytic activity
TprK (Tpr proteins)Antigenic variation → immune evasion of spirochetes
Note from Steve: "don't need to memorize names" of surface proteins. Key concept: produces no important toxins or enzymes. Pathology is from proliferative endarteritis → vascular lesions → ischemia. Antigenic variation helps evade immune response. Systemic infection persists despite immune response.

🦠 Trichomonas vaginalis — Virulence & Immune Evasion

Virulence FactorMechanism
LPG (lipophosphoglycan)Binds host receptor galectin-1 → secures attachment and colonization
Membrane proteinsAdditional adhesion factors for epithelial attachment
ProteasesReleased by parasite; associated with pathogenesis and immune evasion
Key Feature: Exists only as a trophozoite — no cyst form. Pear-shaped with 4 anterior flagella and an undulating membrane. Transmitted sexually so doesn't need environmental durability.
LO 3 Describe pathogenesis, clinical presentation, and possible complications

🔬 HSV — Clinical Presentation

Pathogenesis

  • Transmitted via direct contact with infected mucosa/body fluids
  • Enveloped, dsDNA virus
  • Replicates at site of entry → travels via sensory neurons → latent infection
  • HSV-1: oral/facial lesions
  • HSV-2: anogenital lesions

Clinical Features

  • Fever, malaise, tender inguinal lymph nodes (3–7 days post-exposure)
  • Lesions: red papules → vesicles → painful ulcers
  • Vesicles = high viral load ("super infectious")
  • Cervical/vaginal lesions → whitish mucopurulent discharge
  • Lesions heal spontaneously in 1–3 weeks
Complications: Histology shows eosinophilic intranuclear inclusions (Cowdry type A). Multinucleated giant cells on Tzanck smear. Neonatal herpes if HSV-2 in genital tract during delivery → severe neurological disability or death. Risk highest with first-time maternal infection during pregnancy.

🔬 HPV — Clinical Presentation

Pathogenesis

  • Non-enveloped, dsDNA — transmitted via skin-to-skin contact (abrasions)
  • Targets basal cells of stratified squamous epithelium
  • L1 binds HSPGs on basement membrane
  • Uses host RNA polymerase for mRNA synthesis
  • Minimal cell death and inflammation → ↓ immune responses

Clinical Features

  • Benign squamous cell papillomas (condylomata acuminata)
  • Anogenital warts
  • HPV-6 and HPV-11 → warts + laryngeal papillomas
  • High-risk types (HPV-16, HPV-18) → squamous cell carcinoma of cervix, head/neck, anogenital region
  • Koilocytes on biopsy (small nucleus + large perinuclear vacuole)

🔬 HIV — Clinical Presentation

Early Acute Stage (resolves in ~2 wks)

  • Fever, lethargy, sore throat, generalized lymphadenopathy
  • Maculopapular rash on trunk, arms, legs — but NOT on palms and soles

Middle/Latent Stage (years)

  • Often asymptomatic
  • CD4 counts gradually decline

Late Stage (AIDS)

  • Opportunistic infections due to immunodeficiency
  • p24 serves as a serologic marker
  • Disrupts cell-mediated immunity
Distinguish from Syphilis: HIV acute rash is on trunk/arms/legs but spares palms/soles. Secondary syphilis rash includes palms/soles. This is a classic exam differentiator.

🔬 Chlamydia trachomatis — Clinical Presentation

In Females (PWU)

  • Often asymptomatic
  • Cervicitis: mucopurulent discharge, easily induced endocervical bleeding
  • Endometritis, salpingitis, proctitis, perihepatic inflammation
  • Complications: PID and salpingitis

In Males (PWP)

  • Urethritis, epididymitis with clear discharge
  • Prostatitis
  • Also: proctitis, pharyngitis, conjunctivitis
LGV (Lymphogranuloma Venereum): Caused by C. trachomatis L serotypes (L1-L3). Primary = small papule that heals spontaneously. Second stage (2–6 wks later): tender local lymphadenopathy, fever, headache. Untreated → fibrosis/strictures. Inflammatory response is granulomatous and neutrophilic.

🔬 Neisseria gonorrhoeae — Clinical Presentation

In Females (PWU)

  • Often asymptomatic
  • Cervicitis with purulent vaginal discharge
  • Intermenstrual bleeding
  • Complication: ascending infection → salpingitis → PID

In Males (PWP)

  • Urethritis with purulent discharge
  • Epididymitis, prostatitis, urethral stricture
Key Distinction: Gonorrhea = purulent discharge. Chlamydia = clear/mucopurulent discharge. Disseminated gonococcal infection can cause tenosynovitis, macular/pustular rash, and migratory arthritis (Practice Case #2).

🔬 Treponema pallidum — Stages of Syphilis

Primary Syphilis (~3 wks post-exposure)

  • Chancre: single, firm, painless red raised lesion
  • High bacterial load in chancre
  • Heals with or without treatment
  • Bacteria disseminate via blood/lymphatic system

Secondary Syphilis (2–10 wks after chancre)

  • Generalized papular rash including palms and soles
  • Condylomata lata = flat-topped, moist papules on genitals
  • Mild fever, malaise, weight loss, lymphadenopathy
  • Patchy alopecia may occur

Latent Period

  • Early latent (1–2 yrs): can relapse to secondary symptoms, still infectious
  • Late latent (years): asymptomatic, not infectious

Tertiary Syphilis (after 5+ years)

  • 1/3 of untreated patients
  • Neurosyphilis: meningeal, meningovascular, general paresis, tabes dorsalis
  • Gummas: nodular lesions from delayed hypersensitivity (bones, skin, mucous membranes)
  • Cardiovascular syphilis: syphilitic aortitis → progressive dilation → aortic valve insufficiency and aneurysm of ascending aorta
Congenital Syphilis: Crosses placenta after 3rd month. Findings: Hutchinson's teeth, mulberry molars, saber shins, saddle nose, rhagades, snuffles, frontal bossing, hepatosplenomegaly, interstitial keratitis, 8th nerve deafness. All pregnant women should be tested ≥2x during pregnancy.

🔬 Trichomonas vaginalis — Clinical Presentation

In Females

  • Often asymptomatic
  • Yellow, frothy vaginal discharge
  • Vulvovaginal discomfort
  • Dysuria, dyspareunia
  • "Strawberry cervix" — fiery red from dilated mucosal blood vessels

In Males

  • Usually asymptomatic (~10% symptomatic)
  • Itching, irritation, dysuria, discharge
Pregnancy Risk: In pregnant patients, trichomoniasis increases risk for preterm delivery. Diagnosis: wet mount shows pear-shaped trophozoites with typical jerky motion.

🔬 Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID)

Infectious/inflammatory disorder of the upper female genital tract (uterus, fallopian tubes, adjacent pelvic structures). Can spread to abdomen including perihepatic structures (RUQ pain).

Presentation

  • Lower abdominal pain (dull, aching, crampy, bilateral)
  • Abnormal vaginal discharge (~75%)
  • Postcoital bleeding (~40%)
  • Fever >38°C (~30%, late finding)
  • Gonococcal PID: more abrupt, more toxic

Complications

  • Salpingitis
  • Perihepatitis (Fitz-Hugh-Curtis)
  • Repeated episodes → infertility and ectopic pregnancy
LO 4 Identify and describe the preventative measures for STIs

🛡️ Preventative Measures

StrategyDetails
VaccinationGardasil 9-valent HPV vaccine for preteens (routine at age 11–12, can start at 9); Hep B vaccination
Reduce number of sex partnersMonogamy decreases exposure risk
Use protectionCondoms, barriers; PrEP/PEP for HIV
Get testedRegular screening; make sure partners are aware of test results
HPV Vaccine Schedule: Age 9–14 at initial vaccination → 2-dose series (0, 6–12 months). Age ≥15 at initial vaccination → 3-dose series (0, 1–2 months, 6 months). Special situations: immunocompromised (including HIV) → 3-dose series even if age 9–14. Pregnancy: delay vaccination until after delivery.
CASES Practice Cases from Lecture

📝 Case #1 — Trichomoniasis

28 y/o with vaginal itching, frothy yellow discharge, dysuria, multiple sexual partners. PE: strawberry cervix.

Dx: Trichomonas vaginalis. Classic triad: frothy yellow discharge + dysuria + strawberry cervix. Not viral — the frothy discharge and strawberry cervix distinguish it from HSV, chlamydia, and gonorrhea.

📝 Case #2 — Disseminated Gonococcal Infection

23 y/o woman with finger tenosynovitis → painful rash on extremities (macular + pustular) → ankle pain → temp 39.4°C.

Dx: Disseminated N. gonorrhoeae. Triad of migratory polyarthralgia/tenosynovitis + dermatitis (pustules/macules) + fever. Think gonorrhea when you see joint involvement + skin lesions in a young sexually active patient.

📝 Case #3 — Syphilis (Primary + Secondary overlap)

39 y/o with nonhealing, nonpainful ulcer on upper lip + nonpruritic rash on trunk. Oral sex with partner who had penile lesion.

Dx: Treponema pallidum (syphilis). Painless ulcer = primary chancre (can be extragenital). Rash on trunk = secondary syphilis. Key: nonpainful + nonpruritic. This patient may have overlapping stages.

📝 Case #4 — LGV (Chlamydia L serotype)

26 y/o male with tender, swollen groin. Unilateral firm inguinal lymphadenopathy. No previous ulcer, discharge, or rash.

Dx: Chlamydia trachomatis L1-L3 (LGV). The primary papule healed spontaneously (patient didn't notice it). Now in second stage with tender lymphadenopathy. No rash or discharge rules out secondary syphilis and gonorrhea.

📝 Case #5 — HSV-2 Reactivation

32 y/o male with recurrent painful vesicles on penile shaft + tender groin adenopathy. Similar episode 1 year ago. 3 female sexual partners.

Dx: HSV-2 reactivation. Painful vesicles that rupture + recurrent episodes = classic HSV. Latent virus in sacral ganglia reactivates periodically. Partners can be asymptomatic shedders.
