📋 Approaching Patients with Suspected STI — Sexual History

Dr. Grlickova-Duzevik emphasized these principles before discussing any pathogen:

Be considerate of the spectrum of human sexuality
Sexual history is an important part of the medical history
Be considerate of all patients' needs when taking sexual history
Use gender neutral terms — partner, significant other
Ask open ended questions — allow your patient to describe their identity, anatomy, behaviors, sexual partners
Sample Questions: Are you having sex? How many partners in the past year? · Who are you having sex with? What types of sex? · How do you protect yourself from STIs? · What STIs have you had in the past? · Has your partner(s) been diagnosed with any STIs? · Do you use alcohol or drugs when you have sex? · Do you exchange sex for money, drugs, or a place to stay?

LO 2 Identify pathogen virulence factors & describe mechanisms of host defense evasion

🦠 Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV) — Structure, Virulence & Evasion

Structurally, herpesviruses contain four layers: (1) dsDNA genome, (2) protein capsid, (3) tegument (amorphous protein matrix), (4) lipid envelope with viral glycoproteins and host cellular proteins.

HSV-1 vs HSV-2: The two glycoproteins on the lipid bilayer envelope define them. Glycoprotein G1 → HSV-1. Glycoprotein G2 → HSV-2. Although each is a distinct virus, they share some antigenic components.
Virulence / StructureImmune Evasion Mechanism
Entry via receptor-mediated endocytosis or endosome formation (two steps: binding → fusion)Establishes latency in sensory neurons — hides from immune system
Replicates in dermis/epidermis → cellular destruction → inflammationDuring latency: only LAT transcripts produced, no viral proteins → invisible to immune cells
Both lytic (productive) and latent (dormant) life cyclesVP16 tegument transcription factor active only in lytic stage, not in neurons during latency
High viral load in vesiclesReactivates periodically due to host factors → returns to primary site → lytic replication until immune response forces it back into latency
Host Defense: Neuronal antiviral response is driven by IFN signaling (stimulates innate immunity, inhibits viral spread). TLRs bind HSV-1 proteins or viral nucleic acid and activate innate immune response by inducing chemokines and proinflammatory cytokines. The IFN response is critical for establishing lifelong latent infection.

🦠 Human Papillomavirus (HPV) — Structure, Virulence & Evasion

dsDNA virus that lacks envelope. L1 and L2 encode for the major and minor capsid proteins. HPV reaches its target tissue — the basal cells of stratified squamous epithelium — only through micro-abrasions or epithelial trauma.

Entry Mechanism: After basement membrane is exposed, HPV L1 major capsid protein binds heparin sulfate proteoglycans (HSPGs) on basement membrane → conformational changes of L1 → releases proteinase activity of L2 → further rearrangements → viral movement to keratinocyte zone → endocytosis.
Virulence FactorMechanism
Early genes (E1–E7): virus replicationE1 = helicase; E2 = transcriptional activator; E4 = viral release; E5 = immune evasion
Late genes (L1/L2): structural proteinsL1 = major capsid protein (self-assembles into capsids; immunogen in HPV vaccine)
E6 oncogeneMediates degradation of p53 → uncontrolled proliferation, resistance to apoptosis. Also stimulates TERT (telomerase) expression.
E7 oncogeneBinds Rb → frees E2F transcription factor → unrestricted entry into S-phase
Immune Evasion — "Invisible infection": Very low viral protein production, no viremia. No cell death → no inflammation → no danger signal. The virus downregulates keratinocyte innate immune sensors. E6 and E7 downregulate type I interferon responses. No activation of Langerhans cells or dendritic cells.

Infectious cycle timing: Virus and cell replicate together in basal layer (~50–100 copies). When host cell stops dividing and begins to differentiate, the virus activates all genes → amplifies genome to thousands of copies. In the top layers, L1/L2 are expressed, genomes are encapsidated, and infectious particles exit. The cycle from infection to new virus takes at least 3 weeks. HPV has no blood-borne phase and does not cause cell death.

🦠 Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV) — Virulence & Pathogenesis

Retrovirus with 2 strands of ssRNA. Transmission: blood, contact of semen or vaginal fluid with mucous membranes with or without micro-abrasions. The presence of other STIs increases the probability for transmission.

Virulence FactorRole
gp120 / gp41Viral attachment to host cells
Host receptor: CD4Primary receptor on T-helper cells, macrophages, DCs
Reverse transcriptaseSynthesizes DNA from genome RNA
IntegraseIntegration of proviral DNA into host genome
p24Nucleocapsid antigen — important serologic marker
Entry mechanisms at mucosal sites: (a) Direct infection of epithelial cells, (b) Transcytosis across epithelial cells or M cells, (c) Epithelial transmigration of infected donor cells, (d) Uptake by intra-epithelial Langerhans cells, (e) Access via abrasions in epithelial tissue. Once past the epithelium, virus reaches DCs, CD4+ T cells, and macrophages in stromal tissue → trafficking and trans-infection → seroconversion.

🦠 Chlamydia trachomatis — Virulence & Pathogenesis

Most common reported STI in the US. Gram (−), obligate intracellular. Transmitted by direct contact with EB (elementary body).

Virulence FactorMechanism
Elementary Body (EB)Infectious, non-dividing form — survives extracellularly, initiates infection by attaching to host plasma membrane
Reticulate Body (RB)Non-infectious, proliferative form — multiplies inside host cell by binary fission
T3SS effectorsInjected into cytoplasm upon EB entry; modifies inclusion membrane to prevent lysosomal degradation
Inclusion formationRBs transported to nutrient-rich environment near Golgi; inclusion expands, fills cytoplasm
Life Cycle (simplified): 1. EB attachment to host cells → 2. EB enters vacuole, differentiates into larger RB → 3. RB proliferates by binary fission, differentiates back to EB → 4. Cycle completes with release of intracellular chlamydiae → host cell death.

🦠 Neisseria gonorrhoeae — Virulence & Pathogenesis

Nonmotile Gram-negative diplococci (coffee beans). Most common among adolescents and young adults. Transmitted by contact with exudate from infected individuals.

Virulence FactorMechanism
PiliAdherence to urogenital epithelium; antigenic variation helps escape immune response
Opacity (Opa) proteinsMediates uptake into cells; evades immune response
Porin proteinsPrevents phagolysosome formation
Reduction modifiable proteins (Rmp)Blocks host bactericidal antibodies
Pathogenesis cascade: Colonizes epithelium → invasion and transcytosis → releases peptidoglycan, LOS, and OMVs → activates TLR and NOD signaling in epithelial cells, macrophages, DCs → cytokine/chemokine release → massive neutrophil influx → neutrophil-rich, purulent exudate that then facilitates transmission.

🦠 Treponema pallidum — Virulence & Pathogenesis

Gram-negative, spiral shaped bacteria. Usually visualized with silver stain or dark field microscopy. Transmitted through spirochete-containing lesions of skin or mucous membranes by intimate contact.

Virulence FactorMechanism
TP0453 (Tpp17)Spirochetes adhere to epithelial cells and extracellular matrix
Flagellar rotation + TP0751 proteasePenetrates extracellular matrix; passes through intercellular junctions by motility
TprKAntigenic variation → immune evasion of spirochetes
Key Concepts: T. pallidum produces no important toxins or enzymes. The organism infects the endothelium of small blood vessels, causing proliferative endarteritis — this occurs in all stages of syphilis. Pathology results from ischemia produced by vascular lesions. Chancres and rashes are superficial sites of infection from intense inflammatory response. Immune response reduces bacterial burden but does not eliminate systemic infection.

🦠 Trichomonas vaginalis — Structure & Virulence

Flagellated protozoan, extracellular. Pear-shaped with a central nucleus and four anterior flagella. Has an undulating membrane extending ~⅔ its length. It exists only as a trophozoite — there is no cyst form. Transmitted by sexual contact (no need for environmental durability). Primary locations: vagina and prostate. Found only in humans, no animal reservoir.

Virulence FactorMechanism
LPG (lipophosphoglycan)Binds host receptor galectin-1 → adhesion to epithelium
Membrane proteinsAdditional adhesion factors required for parasite survival near host cells
ProteasesReleased by parasite; may be associated with pathogenesis
LO 3 Describe pathogenesis, clinical presentation, and possible complications

🔬 HSV — Clinical Presentation & Complications

Clinical Features

  • Fever, malaise, tender inguinal lymph nodes, 3–7 days after exposure
  • Red papules → vesicles → painful ulcers
  • Vesicles contain large number of viral particles
  • Lesions heal spontaneously in 1–3 weeks
  • Cervical/vaginal lesions → whitish mucopurulent discharge, pelvic pain, painful urination

Cytopathic Effect & Dx

  • Eosinophilic intranuclear inclusions (Cowdry type A) = viral replication proteins and virions pushing chromatin to edges of nucleus
  • Cell fusion → inclusion-bearing multinucleate syncytia
  • Tzanck smear: cells from base of vesicle stained with Giemsa → multinucleated giant cells
  • DFA (direct fluorescent antibody) test on vesicular fluid
Neonatal Herpes: HSV-2 in genital tract during delivery → severe neurological disability or death. Risk is highest when the mother is infected for the first time during pregnancy. Most infections are asymptomatic despite active shedding, leading to undetected transmission.

🔬 HPV — Clinical Presentation

Benign Manifestations

  • 4 known HPV types cause benign squamous cell papillomas
  • HPV-6 and HPV-11 → anogenital warts (condylomata acuminata) and laryngeal papillomas

Malignant Manifestations

  • High-risk HPV-16 and HPV-18 → squamous cell carcinoma of the cervix, head and neck, anogenital region
  • Viral DNA integrates into cell DNA → large amounts E6/E7 → inactivate p53 and Rb → malignant transformation
  • No late proteins, no progeny virions produced in cancer cells
Histology: Koilocytes = small condensed nucleus + large perinuclear cytoplasmic vacuole (seen in biopsy of cervical intraepithelial neoplasia). Acetowhite lesion on colposcopy — acetic acid causes swelling of abnormal squamous epithelial areas and reversible coagulation of nuclear proteins/cytokeratins.

🔬 HIV — Clinical Presentation

Early Acute Stage (resolves in ~2 wks)

  • Fever, lethargy, sore throat, generalized lymphadenopathy
  • Maculopapular rash on trunk, arms, legs — but NOT on palms and soles

Middle/Latent Stage (years)

  • Asymptomatic
  • CD4 counts gradually decline

Late Stage (AIDS)

  • Opportunistic infections due to immunodeficiency
HIV vs Syphilis Rash: HIV acute rash = trunk/arms/legs, spares palms and soles. Secondary syphilis rash = includes palms and soles. Classic exam differentiator.

🔬 Chlamydia trachomatis — Clinical Presentation

People with Uteruses (PWU)

  • Often asymptomatic
  • Cervicitis: mucopurulent discharge, easily induced endocervical bleeding
  • Pelvic inflammatory disease, perihepatic inflammation

People with Penises (PWP)

  • Epididymitis and prostatitis (clear discharge)
  • Urethritis, proctitis, pharyngitis, conjunctivitis
LGV (Lymphogranuloma Venereum): Caused by C. trachomatis L1–L3 serovars. Primary lesion = small papule in genital area that heals spontaneously. Days to weeks later → second stage: tender local lymphadenopathy + fever, headache, myalgia. If untreated → fibrosis and strictures in anogenital tract. LGV lesions show granulomatous and neutrophilic inflammatory response. Infection leads to antibody and cell-mediated reactions but NOT to resistance to reinfection.

🔬 Neisseria gonorrhoeae — Clinical Presentation

PWU

  • Infections often asymptomatic
  • If symptomatic: cervicitis with purulent vaginal discharge; intermenstrual bleeding
  • Complication: ascending infection, salpingitis

PWP

  • Usually present with urethritis with purulent discharge
  • May develop epididymitis, prostatitis, urethral stricture
Key: Gonorrhea = purulent (thick, creamy) discharge. Chlamydia = clear/mucopurulent discharge. Neonatal gonococcal conjunctivitis can occur during delivery.

🔬 Treponema pallidum — Stages of Syphilis

Primary Syphilis (~3 wks post-exposure)

  • Chancre: single, firm, non-tender, red raised lesion where treponema invaded the skin
  • High level of bacteria present in the chancre
  • Heals with or without treatment
  • Bacteria disseminate via blood or lymphatic system

Secondary Syphilis (2–10 wks after primary chancre)

  • Painless superficial lesions on skin and mucosa; palms and soles of the feet
  • Condylomata lata = moist papules on the genitals (rich in spirochetes, highly infectious)
  • Mild fever, malaise, weight loss and lymphadenopathy is common in this stage
  • Patchy alopecia may also occur
  • Can last for several weeks; lesions heal spontaneously

Latent Period

  • ~⅓ will "cure" without treatment, ~⅓ remain latent
  • Early latent (1–2 yrs): secondary symptoms can reappear, patients still infectious
  • Late latent (many years): no symptoms, patients not infectious

Tertiary Syphilis (after 5+ years latent period)

  • 1/3 of untreated patients develop this stage
  • Neurosyphilis — Early: asymptomatic, meningeal, meningovascular. Late: general paresis, tabes dorsalis
  • Benign tertiary syphilis — Gummas: nodular lesions most probably due to delayed hypersensitivity to bacteria (bones, skin, mucous membranes of mouth/upper respiratory tract)
  • Cardiovascular syphilis: syphilitic aortitis → progressive dilation of aortic root/arch → aortic valve insufficiency and aneurysms of the proximal aorta. Intense inflammatory infiltrate but treponemas are rare.
Congenital Syphilis: Organism crosses placenta typically after the third month of pregnancy. Neonatal findings: Hutchinson's teeth, mulberry molars, saber shins, saddle nose, rhagades, snuffles, frontal bossing. Also: hepatosplenomegaly, interstitial keratitis, eighth nerve deafness. Can result in stillbirth. All pregnant women should be tested for syphilis at least twice during pregnancy.

🔬 Trichomonas vaginalis — Clinical Presentation

In Females (PWU)

  • Can be asymptomatic
  • If symptomatic: yellow frothy vaginal discharge, vulvovaginal discomfort, dysuria and dyspareunia
  • PE: "Strawberry cervix" — fiery red appearance due to dilation of cervical mucosa blood vessels
  • In pregnant patients: risk for preterm delivery

In Males (PWP)

  • Usually asymptomatic (~10% have urethritis)
  • Itching, irritation, dysuria, discharge
Dx: In a wet mount of vaginal discharge, the pear-shaped trophozoites have a typical jerky motion. Trichomoniasis is one of the most common infections worldwide — roughly 25–50% of women in the US harbor the organism.

🔬 Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID) — N. gonorrhoeae & C. trachomatis

Salpingitis and pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) are common ascending infections in PWU. PID is an infectious and inflammatory disorder of the upper female genital tract — uterus, fallopian tubes, and adjacent pelvic structures. Infection and inflammation may spread to the abdomen, including perihepatic structures.

Presentation

  • Lower abdominal pain (dull, aching, crampy, bilateral, constant)
  • Abnormal vaginal discharge (~75%)
  • Postcoital bleeding (~40%)
  • Fever >38°C (~30%, late finding)
  • Gonococcal PID: more abrupt onset, more toxic symptoms
  • Symptoms often near end of menses or first 10 days after menstruation
  • Pain usually lasts <7 days (if >3 weeks, PID less likely)

Complications

  • Perihepatitis (RUQ pain)
  • Repeated episodes of salpingitis and PID can lead to infertility and ectopic pregnancy
LO 4 Identify and describe the preventative measures for STIs

🛡️ HPV Vaccination — Prevention

Gardasil 9-valent HPV vaccine is currently approved in the US.

CategoryDetails
Routine vaccinationAge 11–12 years; can be started at age 9
Catch-up vaccinationAge 13–26 years, if not adequately vaccinated
Shared clinical decision-makingSome adults age 27–45 years, if not adequately vaccinated
Schedule: Age 9–14 at initial vaccination: 2-dose series at 0 and 6–12 months. Age 15+ at initial vaccination: 3-dose series at 0, 1–2 months, and 6 months. Special situations: Immunocompromising conditions (including HIV): 3-dose series even for ages 9–14. History of sexual abuse/assault: start at age 9. Pregnancy: HPV vaccination not recommended until after pregnancy; no intervention needed if vaccinated while pregnant.

Case #1

A 28-year old patient complains of vaginal itching and a frothy, yellow discharge. She also complains of painful urination.

The patient has multiple sexual partners. And on physical exam the finding shows the following: strawberry cervix.
Does any of these symptoms appear to be associated with any of the viral pathogens discussed before? What would be the next step?

Dx: Trichomonas vaginalis

None of the viral pathogens discussed (HSV, HPV, HIV) produce this presentation. The classic triad of frothy yellow discharge + dysuria + strawberry cervix points to T. vaginalis. Strawberry cervix = fiery red appearance from dilation of cervical mucosa blood vessels. Confirmed by wet mount showing pear-shaped trophozoites with jerky motion.

Case #2

23-year-old woman with a one-week history of swelling and pain in her right third finger was seen by an orthopedist, who prescribed cephalexin and indomethacin for suspected tenosynovitis.

Over the next several days, a painful rash developed on the patient's extremities. She presented to the emergency department the day after pain developed in her right ankle with generalized malaise.

Physical examination showed a temperature of 39.4°C (103°F) and a rash that was composed of both macular lesions and pustular lesions.
What will you do next for this patient?

Dx: Disseminated Neisseria gonorrhoeae infection

Classic triad: migratory polyarthralgia/tenosynovitis + dermatitis (macular and pustular lesions) + fever in a young, sexually active patient. The initial finger tenosynovitis that didn't respond to cephalexin should raise suspicion. Disseminated gonococcal infection requires blood cultures and joint fluid analysis; treatment is IV ceftriaxone.

Case #3

A 39-year old patient presents with nonhealing ulcer over the upper lip for one week and new onset rash on her trunk.

The patient had oral sex with the partner who had a lesion on his penis.

The physician examines the nonpainful ulcer and nonpruritic rash on the trunk of this patient.
What is on differential for this patient?

Dx: Treponema pallidum (Syphilis) — overlapping primary and secondary stages

The nonpainful ulcer on the upper lip = primary chancre (can occur at any site of inoculation — extragenital chancres are possible from oral sex). The nonpruritic rash on the trunk = secondary syphilis. Key features: nonpainful + nonpruritic. The partner's penile lesion was likely his own chancre. This patient has overlapping primary and secondary stages.

Case #4

A 26-year-old male visits his doctor for a tender, swollen groin. Upon examination, the doctor notes unilateral, firm inguinal lymphadenopathy. The patient reports no previous ulcer, discharge, or rash.
What is the most probable pathogen causing this change?

Dx: Chlamydia trachomatis L1–L3 (Lymphogranuloma Venereum)

LGV presents in stages. The primary papule healed spontaneously — the patient likely never noticed it. Now in the second stage with tender local lymphadenopathy and systemic symptoms. No rash or discharge rules out secondary syphilis and gonorrhea. The unilateral inguinal lymphadenopathy pattern is classic for LGV.

Case #5

A 32-year-old man presents with complaints of multiple painful vesicles on the shaft of his penis associated with tender groin adenopathy. The vesicles broke 2 days ago and the pain has increased.

He had similar lesions 1 year ago but never reached for healthcare.

He has 3 different female sexual partners, but he is not aware of them having any sores or diseases.
What is the most likely diagnosis?

Dx: HSV-2 Reactivation

Painful vesicles that rupture into ulcers + recurrent episodes = classic HSV. The virus established latency in sacral ganglia after the initial infection a year ago and has now reactivated. Key: lesions are painful (unlike syphilis chancre which is painless). His partners can be asymptomatic shedders — most HSV infections are transmitted by individuals who are unaware they are infected.
