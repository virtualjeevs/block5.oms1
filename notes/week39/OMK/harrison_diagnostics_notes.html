Part I · Detecting Proteins
01 🧠 First Principles — Harrison's Framework
🎯 Harrison's Core Message: If you learn first principles, you can figure out any new diagnostic test — even ones that didn't exist when you were in med school. Ask two questions about every assay, and you can reason through its strengths and limitations.
🔑 For ANY diagnostic assay, always ask: (1) What is the specimen? and (2) What, in the specimen, are we detecting? — e.g., are we detecting patient antibody (serology) or pathogen antigen/DNA?
Why First Principles Matter ★
  • Arm clinicians with fundamental knowledge to critically assess results — what are the likely errors? How accurate is the method?
  • Clinical practice is rapidly evolving — how much will change by the time you graduate or finish residency?
  • Lab techniques are tested on board exams!
Two Types of Antibody-Based Tests
Test FOR Antibody (Serology) Detects patient antibody in serum
The antibody IS the analyte
Lab-made antigen is on the plate
→ Tells you: patient was exposed (past or current infection)
Test USING Antibody (ELISA) Uses commercial antibody as a tool
Detects pathogen protein/antigen
Sample antigen is on the plate
→ Tells you: pathogen is present in the specimen
Harrison's "flipped" concept: Serology has lab antigen on plate & detects patient Ab. ELISA has sample antigen on plate & uses commercial Ab.
02 🧪 ELISA — Enzyme-Linked Immunosorbent Assay ★
💡 ELI5: Stick the target protein to the bottom of a tiny well. Add an antibody with an enzyme glued to it. If the antibody sticks, the enzyme makes a color (or light). More color = more target. It's like a color-changing dip test for proteins.
ELISA Protocol Steps ★
  1. Adsorb — Sample containing pathogen-specific antigen sticks to the bottom of a microwell plate
  2. Add Antibody — Antigen-specific antibody (conjugated to an enzyme) is added to the plate
  3. Wash — Unbound antibody is washed away (removes non-specific noise)
  4. Detect — Bound antibody detected by enzyme activity → produces a colored compound (colorimetric) or emits light (chemiluminescence)
Enzyme Detection Systems
A. Colorimetric Enzyme converts substrate → colored product
Measured by optical density (OD) at specific wavelength
Common enzymes: Peroxidase, Phosphatase
B. Chemiluminescence Enzyme converts substrate → emits light
Detected by luminometer
Often more sensitive than colorimetric
Direct vs. Indirect Assays ★★
FeatureDirect AssayIndirect Assay
Antibodies used ONE antibody (directly labeled) TWO antibodies (1st Ab + labeled 2nd Ab)
Detection system on... Primary antibody itself Secondary antibody
Advantage Faster, simpler protocol Signal amplification (multiple 2nd Abs bind each 1st Ab)
Label type Enzyme-linked or fluorophore on 1st Ab Enzyme-linked or fluorophore on 2nd Ab
🧬 ELISA vs. IHC: Same first principle (antibody-based detection), different "detection surface." ELISA → microwell. IHC → tissue slide.
Antibody Terminology
Polyclonal Antibody One antigen can have multiple epitopes
Polyclonal = mix of antibodies targeting different epitopes on the same antigen
Monoclonal Antibody Binds to one specific epitope on the antigen
Higher specificity, less batch variation
Key for precise diagnostic assays
Fluorophore Detection

Instead of an enzyme, the antibody can be conjugated to a fluorophore (fluorescent molecule). Sometimes manufacturers lazily call these "probes" — Harrison warns this terminology can be confusing. Think back to first principles: what is the detection system?

03 🩸 Serological Tests & Measuring Titre ★★
💡 ELI5: Serology = "ELISA flipped around." Instead of detecting pathogen, you immobilize lab-made antigen on the plate and look for patient antibody in the sample. If antibody is present → patient was exposed (past or current infection).
Serial Dilution & Titre ★★
🔑 Titre = "The highest dilution factor that still yields a positive reading." A higher titre number means MORE analyte in the original sample (it took more diluting to make the signal disappear).
Serial dilution: each tube is more dilute. The titre is the highest dilution that still gives a positive signal above the control/background.
⚠️ When plotting serial dilution data, the x-axis gets "bunched up" on a linear scale. Solution: log-transform the x-axis. This spreads the data points evenly and makes the titre easier to read from the graph.
Clinical Significance of Titre Changes ★
ObservationInterpretationClinical Meaning
Titre increases (e.g., 1:200 → 1:3200) More analyte in sample If measuring pathogen → worsening infection. If measuring Ab → mounting immune response
Titre decreases (e.g., 1:10000 → 1:500) Less analyte in sample If measuring pathogen → successful treatment. If measuring Ab → resolving infection or waning immunity
Titre Question from Lecture ★
📝 Given an ELISA titre graph with OD vs. log dilution factor and a control (background) line: the titre is the dilution at which the sample curve drops to meet the control line. The answer is expressed as a ratio (e.g., 1:128), NOT as an OD value or concentration.
04 🧫 Dot Blot & Western Blot ★
Dot Blot
  • Detects specific proteins OR nucleic acids
  • Does NOT involve fractionation — sample is applied directly to membrane without electrophoresis
  • Uses a serial dilution of known concentration as a reference → compare dot intensity of unknown samples to estimate concentration
  • Example: Tick-borne pathogen tests (Lyme disease)
Western Blot — Protein Detection ★★
💡 ELI5: Like a dot blot but with an extra step: you first separate the proteins by size (SDS-PAGE), THEN transfer to a membrane, THEN probe with antibodies. This tells you both whether the protein is there AND how big it is.
  1. Denature — Complex sample (e.g., serum) is denatured with SDS
  2. Separate — Proteins separated by SDS-PAGE (electrophoresis by size)
  3. Transfer — Separated proteins "blotted" onto a membrane
  4. Probe — Membrane treated with labeled antibodies to detect specific proteins
Reading a Western Blot
Band Labels gp = glycoprotein
p = protein
Numbers = molecular weight in kilodaltons (kDa)
e.g., gp160 = 160 kDa glycoprotein
Clinical Use Historically: HIV seroconversion confirmation
Currently: confirmatory test for Lyme disease (two-tiered testing)
Bands show which proteins the patient's antibodies recognize
Lyme Disease Two-Tiered Testing ★
  1. First Test: EIA (enzyme immunoassay) or IFA — if positive or equivocal → proceed
  2. Second Test: Symptoms ≤30 days → IgM AND IgG Western Blot. Symptoms >30 days → IgG Western Blot ONLY
Key Comparison: Dot Blot vs. Western Blot
FeatureDot BlotWestern Blot
FractionationNo — sample applied directlyYes — SDS-PAGE first
Size infoNo molecular weight dataYes — bands at specific kDa

Part II · Detecting Nucleic Acids
05 🧬 DNA Probe Tests & FISH ★
1. DNA Probe Tests (ASO) ★
🎯 First Principle: Relies on the specificity of Watson-Crick base pairing (hybridization). A labeled probe complementary to the target sequence binds if the target is present; it does not bind if the sequence doesn't match.
  • Also called: Allele-Specific Oligonucleotide Test (ASO) / Molecular Probe Test
  • Example: Probe specific to cystic fibrosis allele — binds sequence #1 (complementary) but not sequence #2 (non-complementary)
2. Fluorescence In-Situ Hybridization (FISH) ★
  • Fluorescent probes stain oligonucleotide sequences in situ (inside cells, on chromosomes)
  • Sometimes called "chromosome painting"
🔑 Board-Tested Example: BCR-ABL fusion (Philadelphia translocation)
ABL gene (chr 9) labeled RED · BCR gene (chr 22) labeled GREEN · Fused gene = YELLOW (mix)
Found in: CML (chronic myelogenous leukemia), ALL, AML
FISH for BCR-ABL: Normal cells show separate red and green dots. CML cells show yellow fusion signal from the Philadelphia translocation.
06 🔄 PCR — Polymerase Chain Reaction ★★★
⭐ Harrison: "It is very important that you understand these. I'll be available at the review sessions to talk more about this."
NAAT Terminology ★

You'll see many overlapping terms. All these describe nucleic acid amplification:

Terms You'll Encounter NAT — Nucleic Acid Test
NAAT — Nucleic Acid Amplification Test
DNA Probe Test
Molecular Probe Test
NAAT Methods PCR — Polymerase Chain Reaction
SDA — Standard Displacement Amplification
TMA — Transcription-Mediated Amplification
LCR — Ligase Chain Reaction
✅ All NAATs share 3 things: (1) Require DNA polymerases, (2) Amplify a specific template from the clinical sample, (3) Use a synthetic probe/primer (often proprietary).
Why Are Primers ~20 Bases Long? ★★
🧮 The Math: A 1-base primer would match 1 in 4 bases. A 2-base primer: (1/4)² = 1/16. A 20-base primer: probability of a random match on the human genome (2×10⁹ bases) = (2×10⁹) × (1/4)²⁰ ≈ 1 in 5,000. This makes the primer highly specific to its pathogen target.
The 3 Steps of Each PCR Cycle ★★★
PCR Cycling: (1) Denature at 95°C → (2) Anneal primers at ~60°C → (3) Elongate with DNA polymerase. Repeat 35 cycles.
🧮 Amplification Math: Starting with 1 copy of target DNA, after 35 cycles: 2³⁵ = 34,359,738,368 copies (~34 billion). This is why PCR can detect even a single pathogen molecule!
End-Point PCR vs. Quantitative PCR (qPCR) ★★
FeatureEnd-Point PCRQuantitative PCR (qPCR)
Result type Positive or Negative (presence/absence) Relative or Absolute measurement
What it tells you Is the pathogen there? How MUCH pathogen? (viral load, response to treatment)
Detection Run gel after PCR, look for band Measures DNA during cycling in real time
Clinical example Is N. gonorrhoeae DNA present? What is the HIV viral load? Is Chlamydia responding to treatment?
Reading a qPCR Curve ★★★
🔑 Detection threshold = "Where the amplification curve first straightens" (enters the exponential phase). Read down from that point to the X-axis to get the cycle number (Ct/Cq).
Lower Ct = More Starting DNA Fewer cycles needed → sample had lots of template
e.g., Ct = 14 → HIGH pathogen load
Higher Ct = Less Starting DNA More cycles needed → sample had little template
e.g., Ct = 33 → LOW pathogen load
Standard Curve for Absolute Quantification

Run qPCR on samples with known DNA concentrations (1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32 ng). Plot Ct vs. amount of DNA. This creates a standard curve. Then read unknown samples' Ct values against the curve to determine their concentration (e.g., viral load in copies/mL or ng of DNA).

★ Integration Question from Harrison's Slide ★
📝 Q: Which results confirm reduced pathogen after antibiotics?
✅ Before: PCR Ct = 5, titre = 1:10,000 → After: PCR Ct = 36, titre = 1:500
Why? Ct went up (less DNA → needed more cycles) AND titre dropped (less antigen → signal disappears at lower dilution). Both indicate pathogen reduction.
⚠️ Terminology Trap: qPCR is sometimes called "RT-PCR" (real-time PCR). Don't confuse with Reverse-Transcription PCR (also RT-PCR!) which uses RNA as template. Combining both gives the delightful RT-RT-PCR.
Part III · STI-Specific Diagnostics
07 🦠 Gonorrhea & Chlamydia Diagnostics ★★
🎯 Key Point: Both gonorrhea and chlamydia have similar symptoms and are often tested together. NAAT is the recommended method for both — more sensitive and specific than any other available test.
Gonorrhea (N. gonorrhoeae) ★
MethodDetailsLimitations
Gram Stain + Culture Look for Gram-negative diplococci from urethral sample Not reliable in women — normal female genital flora look similar. Also can't rule out in asymptomatic men.
NAAT ★ (Recommended) Detects N. gonorrhoeae genetic material; can use vaginal swab or urine from both sexes Most sensitive and specific. Eliminates need for pelvic exam in women.
Chlamydia (C. trachomatis)
  • Signs/symptoms overlap heavily with gonorrhea → often tested simultaneously
  • NAAT is the standard diagnostic method
  • Samples: vaginal swab (women), urine (both), urethral swab (men)
Screening Recommendations (Both)
Women Yearly testing if:
• Sexually active <25 years old
• ≥25 and at increased risk
• Pregnant or considering pregnancy
Men Yearly testing if:
• Men who have sex with men (MSM)

Both sexes:
• When symptomatic
• Repeat testing ~3 months post-treatment
NAAT Sensitivity Comparison
📊 NAAT is orders of magnitude more sensitive than other methods. Detection thresholds: NAAT → ~1 organism · Signal Amplification → ~10³ · Direct Probe → ~10⁴ · Enzyme Immunoassay → ~10⁵. NAAT testing dramatically increases detection rates.
08 🔴 Syphilis Diagnostics — Two-Test Algorithm ★★★
💡 ELI5: Syphilis uses antibody tests (serology), NOT PCR. There are two types of antibody tests, and you always need BOTH to make a diagnosis. One type stays positive forever; the other goes away after treatment. That difference is the key to interpreting results.
Two Types of Syphilis Tests ★★★
FeatureTreponemal TestNontreponemal Test
What it detects Antibodies specifically targeting T. pallidum Antibodies against biomarkers from cellular damage (indirect)
Examples FTA-ABS, TP-PA, EIA (immunoassay) RPR, VDRL
Specificity Highly specific for syphilis Less specific (can have false positives)
After treatment Stays positive for LIFE Disappears ~3 years after adequate treatment
Key clinical use Confirms syphilis exposure occurred Distinguishes active vs. treated infection
⭐ HIGHEST YIELD: Falling titres on nontreponemal tests indicate SUCCESSFUL TREATMENT. A positive treponemal test + negative nontreponemal test = past infection, successfully treated.
Why You Need Both Tests

Since treponemal antibodies stay positive for life, a positive treponemal screening alone can't distinguish between active infection and one that was treated years ago. The nontreponemal test resolves this — if it's also positive, the infection is active (or reinfection). If negative, the past infection was successfully treated.

FTA-ABS (Fluorescent Treponemal Antibody Absorption)
  • Useful after first 3-4 weeks following exposure
  • Can test blood AND CSF (for neurosyphilis diagnosis)
  • The "absorption" step removes cross-reactive antibodies to improve specificity
Testing Algorithm (Know Conceptually, Not Memorize)
⚠️ Harrison says you don't need to memorize the exact algorithm — but know that (1) the algorithm is constantly evolving, (2) both traditional and "reverse" algorithms exist, (3) a second confirmatory test of a different type is always required.
Screening Recommendations
  • When symptoms are present
  • Risk of exposure (MSM, multiple partners, partner diagnosed)
  • HIV infection (screen routinely)
  • Pregnant women — always screen
  • When another STD is diagnosed
09 🔬 Multiplexing & Other NAATs in STI Diagnostics
Multiplex PCR ★
💡 ELI5: Multiplex = run multiple primer sets in a single PCR reaction tube. One patient sample → simultaneously test for gonorrhea, chlamydia, trich, HSV, and more. Saves time, sample volume, and cost.

Commercial multiplex STI panels can detect 7-14 pathogens in 30 minutes to 4-5 hours, using real-time PCR with fluorescence detection. Detection targets include: C. trachomatis, N. gonorrhoeae, T. vaginalis, M. genitalium, U. urealyticum, U. parvum, M. hominis, HSV1, HSV2, H. ducreyi, T. pallidum, and more.

PCR Applications by STI

Chlamydia NAAT Often co-tested with gonorrhea
HSV PCR Can differentiate HSV-1 vs HSV-2
HIV Immunoassay (preferred for detection); qPCR for viral load qPCR determines viral load; diagnostic algorithm beyond this lecture's scope

10 📋 PLO 5 & 6 Quick Review — Gel Electrophoresis & Blotting (SNoW DRoP) ★★
Gel Electrophoresis (PLO 5)
  • Separates DNA (or protein) fragments by SIZE
  • Samples loaded into wells; electric current pulls them through gel
  • DNA is negatively charged → migrates toward positive electrode (anode)
  • Larger fragments = slower, closer to wells. Smaller fragments = faster, travel further
  • For proteins: SDS-PAGE is the gel electrophoresis method
Blotting Techniques (PLO 6) — SNoW DRoP ★★★
BlotTargetMethodologyApplications
Southern DNA Gel electrophoresis → membrane → labeled DNA probe Gene mapping, gene rearrangements, specific DNA sequence ID
Northern RNA Electrophoresis → membrane → labeled complementary nucleic acid Gene expression analysis, RNA size determination
Western Protein SDS-PAGE → membrane → specific antibodies Protein ID, expression analysis, post-translational modifications
🧠 Mnemonic: SNoW DRoP — Read down: Southern, Northern, Western = DNA, RNA, Protein. All three share the same steps: (1) separate by size, (2) transfer to membrane (with blocking), (3) probe with specific detection molecule.
