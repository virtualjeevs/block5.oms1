LO 1

Pediculosis Pubis (Pubic Lice)

Cause, clinical presentation, and recommended treatment

🦠 Causative Organism

Pthirus pubis (the pubic/crab louse) — an ectoparasite spread by person-to-person sexual contact

🩺 Clinical Presentation

  • Pruritus (itching) is the most common symptom
  • Visible lice or nits (eggs) in pubic/body hair
  • Skin irritation or excoriation from scratching
  • Can also involve axillary hair, eyebrows, eyelashes

Treatment

First-Line (OTC) — Preferred agents
Drug Permethrin 1% cream rinse (Nix) OR Pyrethrins + piperonyl butoxide (Rid)
MOA Permethrin/pyrethrins: interfere with insect sodium transport protein → neurotoxicity and death. Piperonyl butoxide: potentiates pyrethrins by inhibiting the arthropod enzyme that metabolizes them (no independent insecticidal activity)
Route Topical; OTC — patient applies themselves
Re-Eval Evaluate and retreat at 9–10 days if lice or eggs persist
Alternative Regimens (Rx) — If first-line fails or not tolerated
Malathion Organophosphate; irreversible AChE inhibitor; Rx only; less preferred due to odor and long application time. Not recommended in pregnancy.
Ivermectin Binds glutamate-gated Cl⁻ channels (invertebrate-specific); given orally for pubic lice; alternative when topicals fail
Lindane ⚠️ Second-line only / no longer available in US — CNS toxicity (neurotoxic); FDA designated second-line. Contraindicated in pregnancy, infants, elderly, seizure disorders, <110 lb.
Additional management: Machine wash & dry (hot cycle) or dry-clean clothing & bedding, OR remove from body contact ≥72 hours. Treat sexual partners from the previous month simultaneously.
🤰 Pregnancy: Permethrin or pyrethrins + piperonyl butoxide are recommended. Ivermectin classified as "human data suggest low risk." Malathion is not recommended.

Granuloma Inguinale (Donovanosis)

Treatment — Klebsiella granulomatis
DOC Azithromycin (macrolide)
Alternatives Doxycycline, ciprofloxacin, erythromycin, TMP-SMX

LO 3

Clinical Presentations of Each STI

Key signs & symptoms for vignette diagnosis — memorize distinguishing features

Gonorrhea (N. gonorrhoeae)

  • Men: urethral discharge (purulent), dysuria
  • Women: often asymptomatic; cervicitis, vaginal discharge, pelvic pain
  • Rectal: discharge, pain
  • Pharyngeal: sore throat
  • Neonates: ophthalmia neonatorum (via delivery)
  • Disseminated: septic arthritis, skin lesions

Chlamydia (C. trachomatis)

  • Often asymptomatic (especially men)
  • Similar to gonorrhea: urethritis, cervicitis
  • Women: mucopurulent cervical discharge, pelvic pain
  • Neonates: conjunctivitis (up to 50%) and pneumonia (up to 16%) via birth
  • Also causes LGV (L-types): genital ulcer, lymphadenopathy, proctocolitis

Syphilis (T. pallidum)

  • Primary: painless chancre at inoculation site; heals spontaneously
  • Secondary: diffuse maculopapular rash (palms/soles), condylomata lata, flu-like sx
  • Latent: no symptoms; early (<1 yr) vs. late
  • Tertiary: gummas, cardiovascular (aortitis), neurosyphilis (dementia, tabes dorsalis)
  • Congenital: can cause fetal death, prematurity, birth defects

Chancroid (H. ducreyi)

  • Painful genital ulcer(s) — distinguishes from syphilis (painless)
  • Regional lymphadenopathy (inguinal buboes)
  • Ulcer has ragged, undermined edges
  • Negative for T. pallidum on testing
  • Risk factor for HIV acquisition/transmission

Granuloma Inguinale (Donovanosis)

  • Painless ulcerative lesions on genitals/perineum
  • Beefy red appearance; can bleed
  • No regional lymphadenopathy (unlike chancroid)
  • Subcutaneous granulomas may occur
  • Rare in US; sporadic in India, South Africa, South America

Bacterial Vaginosis

  • Malodorous vaginal discharge — "fishy" smell (esp. after sex)
  • Thin, grayish-white discharge
  • Positive whiff test (amine/KOH)
  • Clue cells on wet prep (epithelial cells coated with bacteria)
  • Vaginal pH >4.5
  • Often mildly symptomatic or asymptomatic

Trichomoniasis (T. vaginalis)

  • Women: malodorous yellow-green discharge, vaginal irritation/pruritus
  • Cervical petechiae ("strawberry cervix") on exam
  • Men: often asymptomatic
  • Dysuria possible in both sexes
  • ~3–5 million US cases annually

Herpes Genitalis (HSV-2 > HSV-1)

  • Primary: painful vesicles/ulcers on genitals; fever, malaise, lymphadenopathy; most severe episode
  • Recurrent: shorter, milder; often preceded by prodrome (tingling, burning, shooting pain in legs/buttocks)
  • Latent in sacral dorsal root ganglia
  • Neonatal herpes from delivery during active infection

Genital Warts (HPV 6 & 11)

  • Flesh-colored, soft, cauliflower-like warts on external genitalia, perineum, or perianus
  • Usually painless; may cause itching/bleeding
  • Can appear on vaginal wall, cervix, anal canal
  • Most common viral STI in US

Pubic Lice (Pthirus pubis)

  • Intense pruritus in the pubic area — most common symptom
  • Visible lice or nits attached to hair shafts
  • Small bluish macules (maculae ceruleae) at bite sites
  • Spread by sexual contact and close physical contact
🧠 Vignette tip (Dr. Ochs): Painful ulcer + lymphadenopathy + T. pallidum negative → think chancroid. Painless ulcer (chancre) → think syphilis. Painless beefy-red ulcer → think granuloma inguinale. Vesicles with prodrome → think herpes. Cauliflower warts → HPV. Fishy discharge, clue cells → BV. Yellow-green discharge, strawberry cervix → Trichomoniasis.

Laboratory Tests Used for Diagnosis

Primary diagnostic test for each STI

STI Diagnostic Test(s) Key Notes
Gonorrhea Nucleic Acid Amplification Test (NAAT); Gram stain (Gram-neg diplococci in PMNs); culture NAAT is preferred. Gram stain of urethral discharge in men is sensitive; less so in women
Chlamydia NAAT (highly sensitive/specific — gold standard) Cannot be cultured on standard media. Obligate intracellular — NAAT is the standard of care
Syphilis Serologic: RPR or VDRL (non-treponemal, screening) + FTA-ABS or TPPA (treponemal, confirmatory). Direct visualization: dark-field microscopy Non-treponemal titers track treatment response. Test ALL syphilis patients for HIV.
Chancroid Culture on special media (selective agar); NAAT (no FDA-approved test — some labs have developed their own) No FDA-approved NAAT exists. Diagnosis often clinical (painful ulcer + negative syphilis/HSV test)
Granuloma Inguinale Visualization of Donovan bodies on tissue preparation or biopsy Donovan bodies = intracytoplasmic clusters of bacteria in macrophages; no reliable culture
Bacterial Vaginosis Amsel criteria: (1) thin homogeneous discharge, (2) vaginal pH >4.5, (3) positive whiff test, (4) clue cells on wet prep. ≥3 of 4 = diagnosis Clue cells (epithelial cells stippled with bacteria) are hallmark. Also: Nugent score on Gram stain
Trichomoniasis Wet prep (motile trichomonads); NAAT (most sensitive); culture Wet prep: visualize motile flagellated protozoa. NAAT increasingly standard
Herpes Genitalis PCR (most sensitive); viral culture; Tzanck smear (less sensitive); type-specific serology (HSV-1 vs HSV-2 IgG) PCR of lesion swab is gold standard. Tzanck shows multinucleated giant cells (not type-specific)
Genital Warts (HPV) Primarily clinical diagnosis (visual); biopsy if uncertain; HPV DNA testing (for cervical screening) Routine HPV genotyping not needed for diagnosis of warts; Pap smear/colposcopy for cervical HPV
Pubic Lice Clinical diagnosis — visual identification of lice and/or nits on pubic/body hair; magnification may help No lab test required; can use dermatoscopy to confirm if uncertain

LO 5

Mechanisms of Action of Recommended Drugs

MOA organized by drug class — high-yield for exam questions

🧱 Beta-Lactams (Penicillins & Cephalosporins)

Penicillins (Benzathine PCN G) · Cephalosporins (Ceftriaxone)
MOA Bind to penicillin-binding proteins (PBPs) (= transpeptidases) → inhibit the transpeptidase reaction that cross-links the peptidoglycan component of the bacterial cell wall → cell wall lysis and death. Time-dependent killing.
Used for Penicillins → syphilis (all stages). Ceftriaxone → gonorrhea (DOC), syphilis (alternative)

🔬 Tetracyclines (Doxycycline)

Doxycycline — Chlamydia DOC; syphilis alternative
MOA Bind to the 30S ribosomal subunit → inhibit protein synthesis (bacteriostatic). Memory trick: "Tetracyclines → Thirty S"
Spectrum Broad spectrum; includes aerobic Gram+/Gram−, Rickettsia, Mycoplasma, Chlamydia, CA-MRSA

🔬 Macrolides (Azithromycin, Erythromycin)

Azithromycin / Erythromycin — Chlamydia (pregnancy/infant); Granuloma inguinale DOC; Chancroid
MOA Bind to the 50S ribosomal subunit → inhibit protein synthesis (primarily bacteriostatic; azithromycin can be bactericidal at higher concentrations)

🔬 Fluoroquinolones (Ciprofloxacin)

Ciprofloxacin — Chancroid treatment option; gonorrhea alternative (resistance concerns)
MOA Inhibit two bacterial DNA topoisomerases: Type II (DNA gyrase) — primary target for Gram-negative organisms; Type IV topoisomerase — primary target for Gram-positive organisms. → Inhibit DNA replication and transcription. Concentration-dependent killing; bactericidal; long post-antibiotic effect (PAE).

🧫 Nitroimidazoles (Metronidazole, Tinidazole)

Metronidazole / Tinidazole — BV and Trichomoniasis DOC
MOA Prodrug: chemically reduced under anaerobic conditions → forms a reactive nitro radical anion → disrupts DNA/nucleic acid synthesis → cell death. Concentration-dependent killing.

🦠 Nucleoside Antivirals (Acyclovir, Famciclovir, Valacyclovir)

Acyclovir / Valacyclovir / Famciclovir — Herpes genitalis
MOA
  1. Viral thymidine kinase (HSV/VZV) converts the prodrug to its monophosphate form (selective activation — only in infected cells)
  2. Host cell kinases continue phosphorylation → active nucleoside triphosphate
  3. Competitively inhibit viral DNA polymerase
  4. Acyclovir only: also causes chain termination — lacks the 3'-hydroxyl group needed to attach the next nucleoside
Prodrugs Valacyclovir → metabolized to acyclovir. Famciclovir → metabolized to penciclovir (does NOT cause chain termination — has 3'-OH group)
Palliative NOT curative — virus remains latent in sacral dorsal root ganglia

🌿 HPV Wart Treatments

DrugMechanism

ImiquimodImmune response modifier — stimulates production of interferon-α and other cytokines (activates innate & cell-mediated immunity)
Sinecatechins (green tea)Unclear exact mechanism; likely immune modulation and direct antiviral/anti-proliferative effects

LO 6

Treatment Recommendations in Pregnancy

Critical distinctions — these are tested extensively

🔑 Master rule for pregnancy: Avoid tetracyclines/doxycycline (teeth discoloration), fluoroquinolones (cartilage damage), podofilox/podophyllin/imiquimod/sinecatechins (HPV warts — avoid in pregnancy). Beta-lactams and macrolides are generally safe.
STI Pregnancy Treatment Key Points
Gonorrhea Ceftriaxone IM (same dose) Treatment is the same as non-pregnant. If chlamydia not excluded → add azithromycin (NOT doxycycline). Cephalosporins are safe in pregnancy.
Syphilis Benzathine Penicillin G — NO alternative PCN is DOC at all stages in pregnancy. If PCN allergic: no substitution — perform allergy skin test and desensitize, then give PCN. T. pallidum crosses the placenta → fetal death, prematurity, congenital syphilis.
Bacterial Vaginosis Metronidazole (same regimen) Same regimen as non-pregnant. BV in pregnancy increases risk of premature labor & delivery complications → treat even if asymptomatic if detected.
Trichomoniasis Metronidazole 500 mg BID × 7 days Same regimen including pregnant women. If lactating: wait 12–24 hrs after dose or pump & dump.
Herpes Genitalis Oral acyclovir (1st episode or severe recurrent) Acyclovir is preferred — best safety track record. Famciclovir/valacyclovir safety not fully established (use only if needed). IV acyclovir for severe/disseminated infections. Suppressive therapy near term → reduces risk of active lesions at delivery → may prevent need for C-section.
Genital Warts Cryotherapy or TCA/BCA or surgical removal Avoid: podofilox, podophyllin, imiquimod, sinecatechins. Safe options: cryotherapy, TCA/BCA, surgical excision.
Pubic Lice Permethrin or Pyrethrins + piperonyl butoxide Both OTC options are recommended. Ivermectin — classified as "human data suggest low risk." Malathion: not recommended. Lindane: contraindicated.
Granuloma Inguinale Macrolide regimen (Azithromycin) Azithromycin is safe in pregnancy and remains the recommended treatment.
Chancroid Consider azithromycin or ceftriaxone Ciprofloxacin should be used with caution in pregnancy (fluoroquinolone — potential cartilage risk). Prefer azithromycin single dose or ceftriaxone single dose.
💡 Neonatal prophylaxis: Erythromycin ophthalmic ointment in both eyes at birth — prophylaxis for gonorrheal and chlamydial ophthalmia neonatorum (neonatal conjunctivitis). MOA: inhibits 50S ribosome.

LO 7

Common & Serious Adverse Effects

Organized by drug class — high-frequency exam material

Penicillins
  • Drug-induced hypersensitivity reactions (most important)
    • Penicillin degraded → pennicilloic acid → combines with body proteins → antigen formation → antibody response
    • Type I (IgE-mediated): urticaria or anaphylactic shock
    • Serum sickness (Type III)
  • Interstitial nephritis
  • Rash (maculopapular)
Cephalosporins
  • Generally well-tolerated
  • ~5% "cross-sensitivity" with penicillins (highest with 1st gen; <1% with 3rd gen ceftriaxone)
  • GI upset, diarrhea
  • Hypersensitivity reactions (rare with ceftriaxone)
Tetracyclines (Doxycycline)
  • Discoloration of teeth — avoid in pregnancy and children <8 years
  • Photosensitization — easy sunburn
  • Esophageal ulcers — take with full glass of water, remain upright
  • GI upset
Macrolides (Azithromycin, Erythromycin)
  • GI: diarrhea, nausea, abdominal cramps (especially erythromycin)
  • QT interval prolongation (cardiac risk)
  • Cholestatic hepatitis (rare, especially erythromycin estolate)
  • Clarithromycin: potential increased CV risk/death in heart disease patients (FDA warning)
Fluoroquinolones (Ciprofloxacin)
  • Tendonitis & tendon rupture (Achilles; classic board question)
  • Glucose excursions — hypoglycemia OR hyperglycemia
  • Phototoxicity
  • Peripheral neuropathy
  • QT prolongation
  • CI: pregnancy & pediatrics (cartilage damage — though no longer absolute)
Nitroimidazoles (Metronidazole, Tinidazole)
  • Anorexia, nausea, vomiting
  • Metallic taste (highly testable)
  • Take with food to reduce GI upset
  • Disulfiram-like reaction with ethanol — largely disproved by controlled trials; CDC no longer recommends avoiding alcohol; still appears on practice boards questions (Dr. Ochs will not test this)
Nucleoside Antivirals (Acyclovir)
  • GI upset, headache (oral)
  • Phlebitis with IV administration (~15%)
  • Crystalline nephropathy (IV) — risk ↑ with rapid infusion, dehydration, high dose
HPV Treatments
  • Podofilox/Podophyllin: mild–moderate pain, local irritation
  • Imiquimod: local inflammatory reactions — redness, irritation, induration, ulceration/erosions, vesicles
  • Sinecatechins: erythema, pruritus/burning, pain, ulceration, edema, vesicular rash; may weaken condoms/diaphragms
  • TCA/BCA: local irritation; use sodium bicarbonate to remove excess acid
  • Cryotherapy: pain during/after application, necrosis, possible blistering; hypo/hyperpigmentation
Lindane
  • Neurotoxicity — can be toxic to brain and nervous system
  • FDA designated as second-line only due to toxicity
  • No longer available in US
  • Contraindicated: premature infants, seizure disorders, pregnancy/breastfeeding, irritated skin, infants, children, elderly, <110 lb
⚠️ Jarisch-Herxheimer Reaction: Occurs within 24 hours of starting syphilis treatment (treating spirochetes causes this — also with Lyme disease). Symptoms: fever, headache, myalgia. Resolves within 24 hours. Counsel patients before leaving your office!

LO 8

Major Drug Interactions

Tetracyclines, Macrolides, and Fluoroquinolones

Tetracyclines (Doxycycline) — 30S inhibitor; bacteriostatic
Major DI Divalent and trivalent cations (Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺, Al³⁺, Fe²⁺) → chelation → significantly decreased absorption of tetracycline
Sources Dairy products (Ca²⁺), antacids containing magnesium/aluminum, calcium supplements, iron supplements
Counseling Take on empty stomach or at least 2 hours apart from cation-containing products. Swallow with a full glass of water (also prevents esophageal ulcers)
Macrolides (Erythromycin > Clarithromycin; NOT Azithromycin) — 50S inhibitor
Major DI CYP3A4 inhibitors — Erythromycin and clarithromycin strongly inhibit CYP3A4 (most common cytochrome P450 enzyme for drug metabolism)
Result ↑ plasma concentrations of CYP3A4 substrates (e.g., statins, warfarin, immunosuppressants, benzodiazepines, many others) → toxicity risk
Key note Azithromycin does NOT inhibit CYP3A4 — this interaction is primarily a concern with erythromycin (and clarithromycin, which is rarely used now)
Also QT prolongation — avoid with other QT-prolonging agents (e.g., fluoroquinolones, antiarrhythmics)
Fluoroquinolones (Ciprofloxacin) — Topoisomerase II & IV inhibitor; bactericidal
DI #1 Divalent/trivalent cations (same as tetracyclines) — dairy products and cation-containing antacids reduce absorption of fluoroquinolones via chelation
DI #2 Caffeine: Ciprofloxacin is a CYP1A2 inhibitor → inhibits caffeine metabolism → ↑ caffeine levels/toxicity (e.g., nervousness, insomnia, tachycardia)
DI #3 QT prolongation — additive risk with macrolides, antiarrhythmics, and other QT-prolonging drugs
Novel dacins Zoliflodacin: significant interaction with CYP3A4 inducers (e.g., rifampin, carbamazepine) → reduced drug levels
