The Six Pharyngeal Arches

Development of each arch and its cranial nerve, muscles, skeletal elements, and other derivatives

Overview 1st Arch 2nd Arch 3rd Arch 4th Arch 6th Arch Pouches Clefts Ear Tongue Thyroid Neural Crest Table Quiz (24 Qs)

📋 Overview

During weeks 4–5, neural crest cells migrate into the head and neck region and pile up between the surface ectoderm (outside) and the pharyngeal endoderm (inside). This creates paired swellings called pharyngeal arches (also called branchial arches).

Each arch contains four core components:

🦴 Skeletal / Cartilage

Bones, cartilages, and ligaments

💪 Muscles

Striated muscles that migrate with their nerve

🧠 Cranial Nerve

Motor, sensory, and/or autonomic innervation

❤️ Artery

An aortic arch artery
Key concept: As muscles of each arch migrate, they always drag their cranial nerve with them. Know the arch → know the nerve.

Neural Crest Origin of Arch Skeletal Derivatives

The skeletal/cartilaginous elements of each pharyngeal arch are derived from cranial neural crest cells (not from mesoderm). The muscle core, arterial core, and nerve are mesodermal/neuroectodermal. Neural crest cells migrate out of specific rhombomeres (R1–R7) of the hindbrain and populate the arches:

Mes NC
→ Fronto-nasal prominence
R1+R2
→ 1st Arch (BA1)
R4
→ 2nd Arch (BA2)
R5+R6
→ 3rd Arch (BA3)
R6+R7
→ 4th / 6th Arches

NC also contributes to: odontoblasts (dentin), dermis/hypodermis of face and neck, corneal endothelium, and the truncoconal septum of the heart.

When Do They Appear?

Day 23: 1st pharyngeal arch forms (most cranial position)
Day 24: 2nd arch appears → 1st groove forms between them
Day 26: 3rd arch develops → 2nd groove appears
Day 28: 4th arch (last visible externally). 5th and 6th develop internally — 5th almost entirely disappears
Important: The 2nd arch overgrows the 3rd and 4th, pulling them inward → smooth neck contour. This buries a temporary cervical sinus.

1 First Pharyngeal Arch

1st Arch — The Mandibular Arch

Nerve: CN V — Trigeminal  |  Cartilage: Meckel's

Two parts: maxillary prominence (upper jaw) and mandibular prominence (lower jaw). Meckel's cartilage disappears — the mandible forms lateral to it via intramembranous ossification.

🦴 Skeletal

  • Meckel's cartilage → disappears; perichondrium → sphenomandibular ligament + anterior lig. of malleus
  • Malleus & incus (middle ear ossicles)
  • Mandible (intramembranous ossification)
  • Maxilla, zygomatic bone, part of temporal bone

💪 Muscles

  • Muscles of mastication — temporalis, masseter, med. & lat. pterygoids
  • Mylohyoid (floor of mouth)
  • Anterior belly of digastric
  • Tensor veli palatini
  • Tensor tympani

🧠 CN V (Trigeminal)

  • V2 (Maxillary) — purely sensory, upper jaw
  • V3 (Mandibular) — sensory + motor, lower jaw
  • Motor to all muscles of mastication
  • Sensory: face, mouth, nose, touch/proprioception to anterior ⅔ of tongue

❤️ Artery

Terminal maxillary artery (branch of external carotid)

⚙️ Autonomic (CN V as post-ganglionic highway)

CN V does not carry pre-ganglionic parasympathetics, but its branches serve as the post-ganglionic highway for all four cranial parasympathetic ganglia:

  • V1 (short ciliary nn.) → Ciliary ganglion (pre-ganglionic: CN III) → pupil constrictor, ciliary muscle (accommodation)
  • V2 (zygomatic, greater/lesser palatine, nasopalatine nn.) → Pterygopalatine ganglion (pre-ganglionic: CN VII via greater petrosal nerve) → lacrimal gland, mucosa of nasal cavity & palate
  • V3 (auriculotemporal n.) → Otic ganglion (pre-ganglionic: CN IX via lesser petrosal nerve) → parotid gland
  • V3 (lingual n.) → Submandibular ganglion (pre-ganglionic: CN VII via chorda tympani) → submandibular & sublingual glands

2 Second Pharyngeal Arch

2nd Arch — The Hyoid Arch

Nerve: CN VII — Facial  |  Cartilage: Reichert's

Unlike Meckel's, Reichert's cartilage ossifies (endochondral ossification) to form bony structures.

🦴 Skeletal

  • Stapes (3rd ossicle) — dorsal Reichert's
  • Styloid process of temporal bone — dorsal Reichert's
  • Stylohyoid ligament — middle Reichert's
  • Lesser horn + upper body of hyoid — ventral Reichert's

💪 Muscles

  • Muscles of facial expression — frontalis, orbicularis oculi/oris, buccinator, etc.
  • Stapedius
  • Stylohyoid
  • Posterior belly of digastric
  • Platysma
  • Occipitalis, auricular muscles

🧠 CN VII (Facial)

  • Motor to all muscles of facial expression
  • Chorda tympani → crosses tympanic membrane → taste to anterior ⅔ of tongue

❤️ Arteries

  • Stapedial artery — regresses before birth
  • Hyoid artery → corticotympanic artery

⚙️ Autonomic (CN VII parasympathetics)

  • Greater petrosal nerve → pterygopalatine ganglion → lacrimal gland, nasal & palatal mucosa (post-ganglionic via V2)
  • Chorda tympani → submandibular ganglion → submandibular & sublingual salivary glands (post-ganglionic via lingual n. / V3)

3 Third Pharyngeal Arch

3rd Arch

Nerve: CN IX — Glossopharyngeal  |  Cartilage: unnamed

A smaller arch — only one muscle.

🦴 Skeletal

  • Greater horn (cornu) of hyoid
  • Lower body of hyoid

💪 Muscles

  • Stylopharyngeus — just one!

🧠 CN IX (Glossopharyngeal)

  • Motor to stylopharyngeus
  • Taste + general sensation to posterior ⅓ of tongue
  • Tympanic nerve (Jacobson's nerve) → middle ear plexus → lesser petrosal nerve → otic ganglion → post-ganglionic via auriculotemporal n. (V3) → parotid gland

❤️ Artery

Common carotid + proximal internal carotid

⚙️ Autonomic (CN IX parasympathetics)

  • Tympanic nerve → Jacobson's plexus (middle ear) → lesser petrosal nerve → exits skull via foramen ovale → otic ganglion
  • Post-ganglionic fibers ride auriculotemporal nerve (V3) → parotid gland

4 Fourth Pharyngeal Arch

4th Arch

Nerve: CN X — Vagus (Superior Laryngeal Branch)

Derivatives are mostly cartilaginous (laryngeal cartilages), not bony.

🦴 Skeletal

  • Thyroid cartilage (upper portion)
  • Corniculate & cuneiform cartilages
  • Epiglottic cartilage

💪 Muscles

  • Cricothyroid
  • Pharyngeal constrictors
  • Levator veli palatini
  • Striated muscles of esophagus (superior/upper portion)

🧠 CN X, Superior Laryngeal

  • Motor to cricothyroid, constrictors, levator palatini
  • Sensory to root of tongue

❤️ Arteries

  • Right: Proximal subclavian
  • Left: Aortic arch
5th Pharyngeal Arch: Forms briefly then regresses. No significant adult derivatives.

6 Sixth Pharyngeal Arch

6th Arch

Nerve: CN X — Vagus (Recurrent Laryngeal Branch)

Key difference from 4th: innervated by recurrent laryngeal (not superior).

🦴 Skeletal

  • Cricoid cartilage
  • Arytenoid cartilages
  • Lower thyroid cartilage

💪 Muscles

  • All intrinsic laryngeal muscles EXCEPT cricothyroid
  • Thyroarytenoid, cricoarytenoids, transverse arytenoid, vocalis

🧠 CN X, Recurrent Laryngeal

  • Motor to intrinsic laryngeal muscles (except cricothyroid)
  • Sensory: pharynx, larynx, esophagus, tympanic membrane, external ear
  • Parasympathetic to thoracic/abdominal viscera

❤️ Arteries

  • Right: Proximal pulmonary arteries
  • Left: Ductus arteriosus → becomes ligamentum arteriosum after birth; recurrent laryngeal nerve hooks beneath it on the left

⚙️ CN XI (Accessory Nerve) — Special note

CN XI is associated with the somitic mesenchyme of arch 6 (not neural crest). It innervates sternocleidomastoid and trapezius — muscles derived from occipital somites that migrate into arch territory.

🫁 Pharyngeal Pouches

Pouches = internal (endoderm-lined) outpocketings between adjacent arches. Each pouch is separated from its corresponding cleft by a pharyngeal membrane (ectoderm + mesoderm + endoderm).

1st Pouch

→ Tubotympanic recess → tympanic cavity (middle ear) + pharyngotympanic (Eustachian) tube.

1st membrane → tympanic membrane: ectoderm (lateral) + mesoderm (middle fibrous layer) + endoderm (medial). This is the only membrane that does not degenerate.

2nd Pouch

→ Palatine tonsil. Endoderm lining invaginates and proliferates to form tonsillar crypts. Mesenchyme condenses around the crypts into lymphoid nodules. The endodermal surface of the 2nd pouch also forms the supratonsillar fossa and epithelium of tonsillar sinus.

Histology: Tonsillar crypts lined by stratified squamous epithelium; surrounding lymphoid nodules with germinal centers.

3rd Pouch

Splits into two wings — and the parathyroid numbering flips (3rd pouch travels farther):

  • Dorsal wing → INFERIOR parathyroid gland (Parathyroid III) — migrates farther caudally with the thymus, overshoots, ends up inferior
  • Ventral wing → Thymus — descends into anterior mediastinum by week 7; endoderm → epithelial reticular cells + Hassall's corpuscles
  • Failure of descent → ectopic/undescended thymus or aberrant parathyroid gland III (may sit near common carotid artery or in chest)

4th Pouch

  • Dorsal wing → SUPERIOR parathyroid gland (Parathyroid IV) — shorter migration → stays superior
  • Ventral wing → Ultimobranchial body (also called ultimopharyngeal body) → migrates into thyroid → becomes parafollicular C cells (secrete calcitonin to lower serum Ca²⁺)
  • Clinically: Aberrant parathyroid IV can occur near thyroid gland

🧠 Parathyroid Trick

3rd pouch → Inferior (migrates far)  |  4th pouch → Superior (stays put)
"3 and 4 flip!"

〰️ Pharyngeal Clefts (Grooves)

Clefts = external (ectoderm-lined) grooves between adjacent arches. There are 4 clefts corresponding to the 4 external grooves visible on the embryo's neck.

1st Cleft

Only cleft that persists → external acoustic meatus (external ear canal). The ectodermal cells at the deep end proliferate to form the meatal plug, which later recanalizes.

Auricle (pinna): Arises from 6 mesenchymal hillocks (auricular hillocks of His): hillocks 1–3 from 1st arch, hillocks 4–6 from 2nd arch. The tragus = 1st arch; helix and lobe = 2nd arch.

Auricular pits (preauricular sinuses) = remnants of hillocks that fail to fuse → triangular area anterior to helix; can form preauricular cysts.

2nd–4th Clefts

All buried and obliterated by the rapidly proliferating 2nd pharyngeal arch, which overgrows the 3rd and 4th arches. The resulting buried space = cervical sinus of His → normally pinches off and degenerates completely by late fetal life.

Branchial Cysts, Sinuses & Fistulae

Branchial cyst = cervical sinus fails to degenerate → lined by squamous or columnar epithelium → lateral neck mass anterior to SCM, often presenting in adolescence when it enlarges after a URI.

Branchial sinus = opening on the external surface of the neck only (incomplete fistula).

Branchial fistula (complete) = both the 2nd cleft and the 2nd pouch membrane break down → full tract from the tonsillar sinus (internal opening) through the neck between the internal and external carotid arteries, exiting anterior to the SCM at its lower third (external opening) → weeps mucus.

3rd pouch fistula: A rarer internal sinus (branchial sinus type) can arise from persistent 3rd pouch tissue, passing posterior to the common carotid artery.

🦻 The Ear — Full Integration

🦻 Embryological Origins — Summary

  • External ear canal → 1st cleft (ectoderm); meatal plug recanalizes
  • Auricle / pinna → hillocks 1–3 = 1st arch; hillocks 4–6 = 2nd arch
  • Tympanic membrane → 1st pharyngeal membrane: ectoderm (outer) + fibrous mesoderm (middle) + endoderm (inner mucosal layer)
  • Tympanic cavity + Eustachian (pharyngotympanic) tube → 1st pouch (endoderm)
  • Malleus & Incus → 1st arch (Meckel's cartilage)  |  Stapes → 2nd arch (Reichert's cartilage)
  • Tensor tympani → 1st arch (CN V3)  |  Stapedius → 2nd arch (CN VII)
  • Inner ear (cochlea, semicircular canals, vestibule) → otic vesicle (from otic placode — NOT from pharyngeal arches)
  • CN VIII (Vestibulocochlear) → cochlear root (hearing) + vestibular root (balance) — associated with otic vesicle, not arches
Clinical pearl: In first arch syndrome (Treacher Collins, Pierre Robin), both malleus/incus and the auricle (anterior portion) can be malformed because they share 1st arch origin.

👅 Tongue Development

The tongue develops from multiple pharyngeal arches merging on the floor of the primitive pharynx during weeks 4–8. It has a dual origin: the mucosa comes from arch endoderm/ectoderm; the muscles come from occipital myotomes (not the arches), which is why tongue muscles are innervated by CN XII (hypoglossal), not the arch nerves.

Swellings That Form the Tongue

🌱 Anterior ⅔ (Body)

  • Median tongue bud (tuberculum impar) — single midline swelling from 1st arch; transient, quickly overgrown
  • Lateral lingual swellings (×2) — one on each side from the 1st arch; grow rapidly and fuse in the midline, covering the tuberculum impar
  • The lateral lingual swellings form the bulk of the anterior ⅔ of the tongue
  • Surface mucosa: ectoderm/endoderm from 1st arch → innervated by CN V3 (general sensation) and CN VII via chorda tympani (taste)

🌱 Posterior ⅓ (Root)

  • Copula (hypobranchial eminence, part 1) — midline swelling from 2nd arch; normally overgrown by 3rd arch tissue and contributes nothing to the adult tongue
  • Hypobranchial eminence (part 2) — midline swelling from 3rd arch; grows forward and overgrows the copula, forming the posterior ⅓
  • Surface mucosa: endoderm from 3rd arch → innervated by CN IX (general sensation + taste)
  • 4th arch contributes to the most posterior tongue/epiglottis region → innervated by CN X (superior laryngeal branch)
Fusion line = sulcus terminalis. The V-shaped groove on the dorsal tongue surface marks where the anterior and posterior portions fused. At the apex of the V sits the foramen cecum — the origin point of the thyroglossal duct and thyroid descent.

And remember: the 2nd arch (copula) is overgrown → contributes nothing to the adult tongue mucosa, even though it's present embryologically.

Ankyloglossia (Tongue-Tie)

The lingual frenulum (midline fold attaching tongue to floor of mouth) normally recedes as the tongue lifts away from the floor. If the lateral lingual swellings fuse too anteriorly or the frenulum fails to thin, it tethers the tongue tip → ankyloglossia. Mild cases: no treatment. Severe: frenulotomy or frenuloplasty.

Macroglossia

Overgrowth of the tongue. Causes include Down syndrome (trisomy 21 — relative macroglossia due to midface hypoplasia), Beckwith-Wiedemann syndrome, congenital hypothyroidism (myxedema), and lymphangioma/hemangioma. Relevant arch connection: the tongue musculature comes from occipital somites but hypertrophy of the arch-derived mucosal/connective tissue contributes to the overall enlargement.

🦋 Thyroid Gland Formation

The thyroid gland develops from a median endodermal thickening at the base of the tongue (between the 1st and 2nd arch swellings). Its origin site is marked in the adult by the foramen cecum — a blind pit at the junction of the anterior ⅔ and posterior ⅓ of the tongue.

Step-by-Step Descent

Week 4: Thyroid diverticulum forms as a downgrowth from the foramen cecum. It is bilobed from the start.
Weeks 4–7: Thyroid descends through the neck, connected to its origin by the thyroglossal duct. It passes anterior to (or through) the hyoid bone.
Week 7: Thyroid reaches its final position anterior to the trachea. Thyroglossal duct normally obliterates. Only the foramen cecum remains as a surface dimple.
Adult: The pyramidal lobe of the thyroid gland represents a remnant of the caudal portion of the thyroglossal duct in ~50% of individuals.

Thyroglossal Duct Cyst

If the thyroglossal duct fails to obliterate → thyroglossal duct cyst: midline neck mass, usually at or below the hyoid bone. Moves upward with swallowing and tongue protrusion (because it is tethered to the tongue via the duct tract). May become infected. Tx: Sistrunk procedure (excise cyst + middle third of hyoid bone + duct tract to foramen cecum).

Ectopic Thyroid Gland

If thyroid descent is incomplete, thyroid tissue can remain anywhere along the thyroglossal tract — from the base of the tongue (lingual thyroid) to the suprahyoid region. If the thyroid fails to descend entirely, no thyroid tissue reaches the neck.

Case 1 (NEJM 2012, Aalaa & Mohajeri-Tehrani): 12-year-old girl with midline neck mass, hypothyroidism on levothyroxine. Technetium-99 scan → uptake in the mass, no uptake in the normal thyroid location. Dx: Ectopic thyroid at a thyroglossal tract site — the only functional thyroid tissue this patient had.

Case 2 (NEJM 2010, Ghonge & Sahu): 15-year-old boy with lifelong bilateral neck swellings. CT showed enhancing lesions in the floor of the mouth, between hyoid and thyroid cartilage, and at foramen cecum. Subclinical hypothyroidism (TSH 12.6 μIU/mL). No normal thyroid in usual location. Dx: Multiple ectopic thyroid deposits along entire thyroglossal tract. Parents declined intervention; patient remained asymptomatic for >1 year.

Key test point: Before surgically excising a "thyroglossal duct cyst" or midline neck mass, always confirm the presence of normal thyroid tissue — the mass may be the patient's only thyroid.

🧬 Neural Crest & Head/Neck Development

What Neural Crest Contributes to the Head & Neck

Cranial neural crest (NC) migrates from the dorsal neural tube and gives rise to the connective tissue and skeletal elements of the pharyngeal arches. Paraxial mesoderm provides the muscle; neural tube/placode provides the nerves.

NC Contributions Beyond Pharyngeal Arches

🦷 Teeth

  • Odontoblasts (dentin) → NC
  • Enamel → ameloblasts (ectoderm)
  • Cementum, periodontal ligament, alveolar bone → NC

👁️ Eye & Skin

  • Corneal endothelium & stroma → NC
  • Dermis & hypodermis of face and neck → NC
  • Melanocytes (throughout body) → NC

❤️ Cardiovascular

  • Truncoconal (conotruncal) septum → NC; divides the outflow tract into aorta + pulmonary trunk
  • NC defects → conotruncal heart defects (ToF, TGA, truncus arteriosus)
  • DiGeorge syndrome (22q11 deletion) = NC migration failure → absent thymus (3rd pouch), absent parathyroids, conotruncal defects, arch abnormalities

🦴 Skull — NC vs. Mesoderm

  • Neural crest → intramembranous bones: frontal, parietal (anterior), nasal, lacrimal, zygomatic, maxilla, mandible, incisive
  • Paraxial mesoderm → endochondral/membranous: parietal (posterior), squamous temporal, occipital, petrous temporal
  • Hyoid bones, laryngeal cartilages → NC

DiGeorge Syndrome (22q11.2 Deletion)

Caused by haploinsufficiency of genes at chromosome 22q11.2 → failure of NC cell migration into 3rd and 4th pharyngeal pouches. Results in: absent or hypoplastic thymus (T-cell immunodeficiency), absent or hypoplastic parathyroids (hypocalcemia, tetany in neonate), conotruncal heart defects (interrupted aortic arch, truncus arteriosus, ToF), and dysmorphic facies. Recall the mnemonic: CATCH-22 — Cardiac defects, Abnormal facies, Thymic hypoplasia, Cleft palate, Hypocalcemia, 22q11.
