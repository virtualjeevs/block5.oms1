Migraine Headache

Common Migraine (without aura) · Classic Migraine (with aura)
A chronic condition punctuated by acute attacks of episodic, severe, unilateral head pain that is throbbing in nature. Divided into "Common" (no aura, 75% of cases) and "Classic" (with aura) subtypes.
Epidemiology
  • Affects up to 12% of the population
  • More frequent in women than men
  • Migraine without aura is the most common type (75% of cases)
  • Peak prevalence in ages 30–39
  • Often an inherited disorder, though specific loci/genes are not yet identified
Pathophysiology
  • Neuronal dysfunction in the cerebral cortex
  • Cortical spreading depression — a wave of neuronal depolarization sweeps across the cortex
  • This depolarization triggers activity in trigeminovascular sensory fibers → perceived as pain
  • The trigeminovascular system = meningeal branches of CN V providing sensory innervation to meninges and head vessels
  • Sensitization of primary afferent fibers and of the trigeminal complex in the brainstem plays a central role
Key Mechanism: Cortical spreading depression → trigeminovascular activation → meningeal nociceptor sensitization → headache pain
Clinical Presentation

Without Aura (most common):

  • Severe, unilateral frontal or temporal pain — throbbing in quality
  • Associated symptoms: nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, tremors, photophobia, phonophobia, sweating, chills

With Aura (precedes headache):

  • Visual disturbance (scotomata)
  • Tremor, pallor, vertigo
  • Unilateral numbness or weakness
  • Transient aphasia, thick speech

Episodes can be separated by weeks or months.

Diagnosis
  • › Based on clinical presentation
  • › History, physical, and neurological exam are critical
  • › Neuroimaging not routinely necessary but consider for:
Unexplained abnormal neuro exam finding · Atypical headache features · Red flags: "worst headache of my life," change in pattern, always same side, new-onset after age 50
Complications: Status migrainosus · Medication overuse/addiction. No curative medications — lifestyle changes may be most important in modifying the disorder.
Differential Diagnosis
Tension headache
Cluster headache
Trauma / infection
Intracranial lesion
TIA (for aura DDx)
Seizure / syncope
Vestibular disorders

Tension-Type Headache

TTH · Most common primary headache disorder
A primary headache disorder characterized by bilateral, non-throbbing headache of mild to moderate intensity, typically without other associated features. The most common headache and most prevalent neurologic disorder in the population.
Epidemiology
  • One-year prevalence of episodic TTH in the U.S.: 38% (ages 18–65)
  • Likely underreported — most with mild/infrequent TTH don't seek care
  • High burden of disability due to sheer prevalence
Etiology & Risk Factors

Episodic TTH — environmental factors (stress, muscle tenderness) most impactful

Chronic TTH — genetic factors play a key role

Risk Factors:

  • Female sex
  • Younger age
  • Fatigue · Sleep disorders
  • Migraine history
  • Depression history
Pathophysiology
  • Peripheral activation/sensitization of myofascial nociceptors — major contributor to episodic TTH
  • Continuous nociceptive input from peripheral myofascial structures → induces central sensitization → conversion of episodic → chronic TTH
  • Heightened sensitivity of central pain pathways plays a critical role
  • Elevated levels of TGF (Transforming Growth Factor) and TNF (Tumor Necrosis Factor) in peripheral blood
Episodic → Chronic Conversion: Peripheral myofascial nociception → continuous input → central sensitization → chronic TTH
Clinical Presentation
  • Mild to moderate intensity, bilateral, non-throbbing
  • Pain quality: dull, pressure, head fullness, band-like
  • Pericranial muscle tenderness
  • Triggered by stress and mental strain
  • No nausea/vomiting · No more than one of photophobia or phonophobia
  • Not aggravated by routine physical activity
Diagnostic Criteria

Episodic TTH — ≥10 episodes, each lasting 30 min–7 days:

At least 2 of:

  • ✓ Bilateral location
  • ✓ Pressing or tightening quality
  • ✓ Mild or moderate intensity
  • ✓ Not aggravated by routine physical activity

Both of:

  • ✓ No nausea or vomiting
  • ✓ No more than one of photophobia or phonophobia

Chronic TTH — ≥15 days/month for >3 months, same criteria above.

Clinical Pearl: Because TTH symptoms overlap heavily with secondary headaches (medication overuse, structural brain lesions), always consider secondary causes when patients present.

Medication overuse HA
Hemicrania continua
Hypnic headache
External pressure HA
Brain malignancy

Idiopathic intracranial HTN

Temporomandibular Joint Disorder

TMD · TMJ Dysfunction
A collective term encompassing pain involving the muscles of mastication, the temporomandibular joint, and other orofacial structures. Population studies find that 40–75% of adults have at least one sign of TMJ dysfunction.
Etiology
  • Trauma
  • Muscle pain / Overuse syndrome
  • Emotional-psychological issues
  • Malocclusion
  • Poorly fitting or recently changed dental prosthesis
Pathophysiology

Intra-capsular TMJ Pain Disorders:

  • Osteoarthritis — most common intra-capsular cause
  • Other arthritic etiologies: RA, psoriatic arthritis, hyperuricemia
  • Inflammatory response within the joint

Masticatory Muscle Pain Disorders:

  • Sensitization of muscle nociceptors and/or sensitization of brainstem circuits controlling muscles of mastication
  • Bruxism (subconscious loading of teeth) — strong emotional component

Pathology:

  • Altered position of the interarticular disc
  • Elongation of disc-supporting ligaments
  • Erosion of articular cartilage
Updated Understanding: TMJ pain of muscle origin was long thought to be myospasm — now understood to be most commonly due to a sensitization process at the peripheral nerve or brainstem level.
Clinical Presentation

Primary Symptoms:

  • Facial muscle pain
  • Preauricular (TMJ) pain
  • TMJ sounds: clicking, popping, catching, or locking
  • Limited mouth opening
  • Increased pain associated with chewing

Secondary Symptoms:

  • Earache · Headache · Neckache
Screening Exam
  • 1 Palpate for pain/tenderness in masseter and temporalis
  • 2 Palpate for pain/tenderness in the preauricular area
  • 3 Measure ROM of mandible — note incoordination or asymmetry
  • 4 Auscultate and palpate for TMJ sounds during movement
  • 5 Note excessive occlusal wear, tooth mobility, buccal mucosal ridging, lateral tongue scalloping
  • 6 Inspect symmetry and alignment of face, jaw, and dental arches
Differential Diagnosis
Dental pain
Sinusitis
Pulpitis
Headache
Otitis media
Trigeminal neuralgia

Giant Cell Arteritis

Temporal Arteritis · Horton Disease · GCA
A chronic, systemic vasculitis involving large and medium-sized arteries, most commonly affecting the temporal artery as well as the aorta and/or its major branches. The most important vasculitis to recognize in this block due to the risk of permanent vision loss.
Epidemiology
  • Most common in adults >50 years old
  • More common in people of northern European descent
  • Females affected 2–3× more than males
  • Incidence increases with age, peaking at ages 70–80
Etiology & Risk Factors
  • Exact cause unknown — thought to be inflammatory (based on response to glucocorticoids)
  • Older age · Female sex
  • History of or current smoking
  • HLA-DRB1*04 alleles
Pathophysiology

Complex inflammatory cascade → loss of smooth muscle → breakdown of elastic fibers → vessel remodeling and occlusion.

Cellular Mechanisms:

  • Likely a T-cell mediated response to an unidentified antigen in the vessel wall
  • Dendritic cells in the vessel wall become activated → recruit T cells and monocytes
  • Dendritic cells produce IL-1, IL-6, IL-21; together with IL-12 and IL-18 → promote differentiation of T lymphocytes into Th1 and Th17 subsets
  • Macrophages coalesce → form multinucleated giant cells → granuloma formation
  • Giant cells secrete metalloproteinases and reactive oxygen species → compromise structural integrity
  • Intimal proliferation → reduced blood flow → partial or complete ischemia
Pathology Findings: Focal inflammatory lesions · Intimal thickening (possible thrombosis) · Granulomatous inflammation centered on internal elastic membrane · Elastic lamina fragmentation · Multinucleated giant cells in 75% of cases · Adventitial thickening and fibrosis
Clinical Presentation
  • New headache or change in characteristics of preexisting headache
  • Abrupt onset of visual disturbances — especially transient/permanent monocular visual loss
  • Jaw claudication
  • Unexplained fever or other constitutional symptoms
  • Signs of vascular abnormalities:
    • Limb claudication · Asymmetric blood pressures · Abnormal radial pulse
    • Vascular bruits
    • Temporal artery abnormalities: tenderness to palpation, decreased pulse amplitude, nodules
Diagnosis

Should not be based on symptoms alone.

Initial Lab Testing:

  • • Complete blood count
  • • ESR and CRP
  • • Complete metabolic panel
  • • Urine dipstick
  • • Serum protein electrophoresis

Confirmatory Testing:

  • • Temporal artery biopsy
  • • Temporal artery CDUS (Color Doppler Ultrasound)
Critical: Do NOT delay treatment to schedule biopsy/CDUS. Start appropriate-dose glucocorticoids immediately when GCA is suspected.
Differential Diagnosis

Systemic Vascular:

Takayasu arteritis
Small/medium vessel vasculitides
Primary CNS angiitis

Non-vasculitis:

Idiopathic aortitis
VEXAS syndrome
NAION
Endocarditis / infections
