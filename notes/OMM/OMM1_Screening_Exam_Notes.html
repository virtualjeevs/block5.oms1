Tricky Billing Questions

⚡ Know This
  • OA finding → Head (M99.00) — it's primarily an occiput structure
  • Cervicothoracic or Thoracolumbar junction → bill the region where the finding is most significant, or both regions
  • Clavicle → Upper Extremity (M99.07)
  • Psoas → Lumbar (M99.03)
  • Piriformis → Pelvis/Sacrum (M99.04 or M99.05)
  • Left hamstring imbalance + R tibia IR + R ankle inverted → 3 billable LE regions: all are Lower Extremity, but may argue 1 region

The 4 Tenets of Osteopathic Medicine

  1. The body is a unit; the person is a mind, body, and spirit.
  2. The body is capable of self-regulation, self-healing, and health maintenance.
  3. Structure and function are reciprocally interrelated.
  4. Rational treatment is based on an understanding of body unity, self-regulation, and the interrelationship of structure and function.

Ed Stiles' Sequencing Principles

"When you step on a cat's tail, where does the sound come out?"
— George A. Laughlin, DO (AT Still's grandson), on treating away from the CC
"During a 20-minute treatment, you are better off spending 15 minutes examining and finding the Area of Greatest Restriction (AGR), then an effective 5-minute treatment will do more than an ineffective 20-minute treatment."
— Perrin T. Wilson
🧅 Sequencing Principles (Stiles)
  • The body is a functional unit — a tensegrity system.
  • Pain is usually not at the AGR; it's in an area of ineffective adaptation.
  • Treatment is like peeling an onion: outside-in, never reversed.
  • Each patient is treated uniquely.
  • Find the AGR → establish segmental Dx → start treatment there → move to next regional AGR → continue until treated area won't release (you're done for that visit).

🔬 Normal vs Dysfunctional Barrier
Normal: Elastic physiological barrier — yield, spring, soft end feel
Dysfunctional: Restrictive barrier — guarding, loss of tissue yield, no joint spring, hard end feel

Why It Works: Tensegrity

Engineering Concept (Donald Inger, MD, PhD)
  • Non-touching rods (bones) + tension wires (muscles & fascia) = self-supporting structure
  • Tension continuously transmitted across all structural members. ↑ tension in one member → ↑ compression in all others.
  • Therefore: Somatic dysfunction and OMT have impact both locally and distally.

Historical Footnote — Why OMT Billing Codes Exist

"Whenever a diagnosis comes up with 'somatic dysfunction,' the length of stay, the number of complications and the amount of drugs used is far less in Waterville Osteopathic Hospital than any of the surrounding hospitals, so you're saving us a lot of money."
— Head of Medicare in Maine to Edward G. Stiles, DO (1973), leading to the first five-level OMT coding system with Medicare

Documentation Quiz

Click a choice to see if you're correct, then read the explanation.

Question 1
A 25-year-old male presents for an annual physical. He reports ankle pain after a fall while walking to the clinic. In which section of the SOAP note should this be recorded?
  • A. Objective
  • B. Subjective
  • C. Plan
  • D. Assessment
  • E. Diagnostics
B — Subjective. The patient reports ankle pain — this is information the patient tells you, which belongs in the Subjective section. The HPI includes mechanism of injury (the fall).
Question 2
A 70-year-old female has mid-back pain. You find prominent transverse processes on the LEFT from T2–T6 that do NOT change in flexion or extension. What is the osteopathic finding?
  • A. T4 FRSl
  • B. T2–T6 NSrRl
  • C. T2–T6 FRSr
  • D. T2–T6 NSlRl
  • E. T4–T5 ESRl
B — T2–T6 NSrRl. Does NOT change in F/E = Type I = Neutral (N). Prominent TPs on the LEFT = rotated Left (Rl). In Type I (Fryette's Law I): sidebending and rotation are opposite → sidebent Right (Sr). So: N·Sr·Rl. Also, this is a group curve (T2–T6), which is characteristic of Type I.
Question 3
Same patient — 70F, mid-back pain, T2–T6 NSrRl. What is the Primary Diagnosis (Assessment)?
  • A. Cervicalgia
  • B. Sacroiliitis
  • C. The Dwindles
  • D. Back Pain
  • E. Muscle Strain
D — Back Pain. The primary diagnosis reflects the chief complaint. The patient came in for mid-back pain → primary billing diagnosis is Back Pain (or dorsalgia). The osteopathic finding (T2–T6 NSrRl) becomes the secondary/osteopathic diagnosis, not the primary.
Question 4
Same patient — 70F, T2–T6 NSrRl. What is the Secondary / Osteopathic Billing Diagnosis?
  • A. Cervicalgia
  • B. T2–T6 NSrRl
  • C. Somatic Dysfunction: Thoracic
  • D. Somatic Dysfunction: Lumbar
  • E. Muscle Strain
C — Somatic Dysfunction: Thoracic (M99.02). Billing diagnoses use regional categories, not the specific 3-plane notation. T2–T6 is in the thoracic region. The 3-plane notation (T2–T6 NSrRl) goes in the PE/OSE, not the billing code. D is wrong because T2–T6 is thoracic, not lumbar.
Question 5
After performing OMT during a clinic visit, where should patient tolerance of the procedure be recorded in the SOAP note?
  • A. Plan
  • B. Assessment
  • C. Objective
  • D. Subjective
  • E. History of Present Illness
A — Plan. The OMT procedure note lives in the Plan section (part 3 of the 5-part plan). Patient tolerance, complications, and post-treatment disposition are all documented within the OMT procedure note in the Plan.
