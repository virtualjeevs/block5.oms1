1Communication, Consent & Professional Touch

💬
Core LO These three principles apply before every exam and treatment encounter — not just the upper cervical spine.

Lay Terms vs. Medical Terminology

With patients (lay terms)

  • "I'd like to gently move your head and neck to check for tightness or restricted motion."
  • "Some of these muscles may be tender — please let me know if anything feels uncomfortable."
  • "I'm going to put my hands under the base of your skull — is that OK?"
  • "There may be some muscle soreness for 24–48 hours after treatment — that's normal."

With colleagues (medical terms)

  • "Somatic dysfunction of the head/neck — OA extended, sidebent R, rotated L."
  • "TART findings: tissue texture change, asymmetry, restricted ROM, tenderness at suboccipital region."
  • "Treated with ME at OA and AA; post-isometric relaxation achieved ×3."

Obtaining Consent

  1. Ask relevant screening questions: dizziness, vision changes, weakness, numbness/tingling, neck pain, recent trauma or surgery.
  2. Explain what you plan to do in language the patient understands.
  3. Explain potential risks (temporary soreness) and benefits.
  4. Confirm understanding and obtain explicit verbal consent before touching.
  5. Reassess throughout the procedure — "Are you OK? Any dizziness or tingling?"
  6. If the patient refuses at any point — stop. Patient refusal is an absolute contraindication to all OMT.

Professional Touch — The 3 C's

Consensual

Always obtain permission before physical contact. Re-confirm if the technique or position changes.

Considerate

Use minimum necessary force (2–3 oz for ME). Be aware of patient discomfort. Protect sensitive structures (jugular foramen, vertebral artery, occipitomastoid suture).

Cerebral

Apply clinical reasoning to every touch. Use palpatory findings to guide treatment. Be present — efferent action provides afferent data.

2Anatomy & Pertinent Landmarks

🦴
Key concept: C1 and C2 are atypical cervical vertebrae. Their architecture is fundamentally different from C3–C6 (typical) and even C7 (atypical but different).

Occiput (C0)

  • External occipital protuberance (Inion / EOP) — palpable midline landmark at the superior nuchal line. Starting point for CS tender point palpation.
  • Superior nuchal line — ridge running laterally from the inion; muscular attachment site.
  • Inferior nuchal line — runs parallel below; attachment for suboccipital muscles (rectus capitis posterior, obliquus capitis).
  • Occipital condyles — paired, convex articular surfaces that articulate with the concave superior surfaces of C1. They converge anteriorly and sit anterior to the foramen magnum — this convergence governs OA coupling mechanics.
  • Foramen magnum — central large opening for the spinal cord / brainstem.
  • Condylar canal — anterosuperior to the condyles. Slide correction: originally mislabeled as "hypoglossal canal"
  • Jugular foramina — bilateral, just lateral to the condyles at the occipitomastoid suture junction. Contains CN IX, X, XI and the jugular vein. Avoid compression of this area during hand placement.
  • Hypoglossal canal — transmits CN XII; anterosuperior, near condyles.
  • Occipitomastoid (OM) suture — junction of occipital and temporal bones; avoid compressing during exam.

C1 – Atlas (Atypical)

C1 does NOT have a vertebral body, a true spinous process, or uncovertebral joints. The commonly-cited image showing a large "spinous process" on C1 is incorrect.
  • No vertebral body — replaced by anterior tubercle and the space for the dens.
  • Anterior arch — houses the fovea dentis (synovial joint with the dens of C2).
  • Posterior tubercle — replaces the spinous process; major muscular attachment point. First bony midline structure palpated inferior to the occiput posteriorly is the C2 spinous process, NOT C1.
  • Lateral masses — bear the articular surfaces.
  • Superior articular surfaces — concave, convergent medially; receive the convex occipital condyles ("bowl in a bowl").
  • Inferior articular surfaces — convex; articulate with the convex superior surfaces of C2 (convex-on-convex).
  • Transverse processes — with transverse foramina (vertebral artery passes through C1–C6).
  • Groove for vertebral artery — on superior surface of posterior arch; clinically important landmark.
  • No disc between C1 and C2.
  • Palpation landmark: The C1 transverse process is located between the angle of the mandible (index finger) and the mastoid process (ring finger) — your middle finger will land on it.

C2 – Axis (Atypical)

  • Dens (odontoid process) — vertically oriented process projecting superiorly from the body; pivot point for C1 rotation. Vascular watershed: apex supplied by internal carotid artery branches; base by vertebral artery branches — affects healing of Type II odontoid fractures.
  • Superior articular surfaces — convex; face anterolaterally.
  • Typical inferior articular surface — articulates with C3 as a typical cervical facet joint.
  • Bifid spinous process — often bifid; the first large midline bony prominence felt below the occiput.
  • Transverse foramina — bilateral, for vertebral artery.
  • No disc between C1 and C2.
  • Three synovial joints at C1–C2: 2 facet joints + 1 anterior atlantoaxial joint (dens–anterior atlas arch). This makes the AA joint reactive in systemic inflammatory diseases (RA, etc.).

Muscles, Nerves & Neurovascular Structures

Suboccipital muscles (deep layer)

These muscles have the highest density of muscle spindles in the body (~200+ per gram vs. ~100/g in extraocular muscles). They are critical proprioceptors for head-in-space awareness.

  • Rectus capitis posterior minor — C1 posterior tubercle → inferior nuchal line (medial)
  • Rectus capitis posterior major — C2 SP → inferior nuchal line (lateral)
  • Obliquus capitis superior — C1 transverse process → inferior nuchal line
  • Obliquus capitis inferior — C2 SP → C1 transverse process (forms inferior boundary of suboccipital triangle)
  • Rectus capitis lateralis — C1 transverse process → jugular process of occiput; stabilizes OA, assists sidebending

Posterior cervical muscles (superficial → deep)

  • Superficial: Upper trapezius (very commonly tight; attaches to occiput/nuchal ligament)
  • Intermediate: Splenius capitis, splenius cervicis — extend head bilaterally; unilateral = SB + rotation together
  • Deep: Semispinalis capitis — key attachment along inferior nuchal line; major counterstrain target
  • Note: Above C2 there are no multifidi, allowing freer motion of C1 on C2.

Anterior cervical muscles

  • Longus capitis / longus colli — deep anterior stabilizers; prevent the head from "cantilevering" backward. Think of Fallingwater — without internal structural support, the cantilever collapses.
  • Sternocleidomastoid — attaches mastoid process to clavicle; shoulder pathology can affect the cervical spine through this connection.
  • Suprahyoid and infrahyoid muscles connect skull → hyoid → clavicle/scapula (omohyoid).

Nerves

  • Dorsal ramus C1 (Suboccipital N) — motor to suboccipital muscles; deep proprioception from OA–C2 facets.
  • Dorsal ramus C2 (Greater occipital N) — motor & proprioception from muscles and local joints; motor to semispinalis capitis, longissimus capitis, splenius capitis.
  • CN IX, X, XI — pass through jugular foramen (lateral to condyles). Compression → dysphagia, autonomic dysfunction, SCM/trapezius weakness.
  • CN X (vagus) — OA somatic dysfunction can impair vagal tone (see §9).
  • CN XII (hypoglossal) — exits hypoglossal canal; affects tongue motion and swallowing.

Vertebral Artery

Passes through transverse foramina of C1–C6 (NOT C7). Has a groove on the superior surface of C1's posterior arch. A loop passes just posterior to the occipital condyles — avoid excessive cervical extension during exam and treatment to protect this artery.

3Biomechanics, Motion Barriers & Characteristics

OA Joint (C0–C1) — The "Yes" Joint

Primary motion: Flexion / Extension (sagittal plane) — accounts for ~50% of total cervical F/E.
Minor motions: Sidebending & Rotation — coupled and opposite to each other (NOT Fryette mechanics).

Flexion

Occipital condyles glide posteriorly on the superior articular surface of C1 (atlas).

The chin moves toward the chest. Posterior suboccipital space increases.

Extension

Occipital condyles glide anteriorly on C1. Because the condyles converge anteriorly, extension causes wedging — the most common motion restriction at the OA is extension.

OA Coupling Rule (not Fryette!)

Translation of the occiput to the LEFT = OA sidebent RIGHT, rotated LEFT.

The surface geometry (higher laterally, lower medially) creates a "slipping" translation. Sidebending and rotation occur in opposite directions. Do NOT force rotation separately — it follows the translation automatically.

Memory aid: STRAW = Sidebend Towards Rotate AWay (for counterstrain positioning at OA)

AA Joint (C1–C2) — The "No" Joint

Primary motion: Axial Rotation — accounts for ~50% of total cervical rotation.
Minimal sidebending/flexion-extension. Diagnosis = rotation only.

C1 rotates around the dens (pivot point). The reference point is the anterior aspect of the C1 anterior tubercle. If the anterior tubercle moves to the right → AA rotated right.

C1 has convex inferior surfaces on C2's convex superior surfaces — a convex-on-convex articulation. Combined with the dens and the transverse ligament, this creates a stable pivot.

The lower cervical spine must be locked out (neck flexed to ~45°) to isolate rotation at C1–C2 during testing. Diagnosis is named for freedom.

📐
Normal ROM reference (full cervical spine): Flexion/Extension: 45–50° each; Rotation: 80–90° each side; Sidebending: 45° each side. Upper cervicals contribute ~50% of F/E and ~50% of rotation to these totals.

4Biomechanical Forces Creating & Maintaining Upper Cervical SD

Common Mechanisms

Acute stressors

  • Whiplash (MVA, sports trauma)
  • Fall / direct impact
  • Surgical positioning (neck extended for intubation → common post-op source of OA SD)
  • Birth trauma — condylar compression in neonates (jugular foramen, hypoglossal canal)

Chronic / postural stressors

  • Forward head posture → compensatory OA extension (to maintain visual horizon)
  • Prolonged looking down at devices without chin tuck → increased loading
  • Stomach sleeping → constant cervical rotation for 6–8 hours
  • Viscerosomatic reflexes (upper viscera → cervical SD)

How SD Maintains Itself — the OA/AA Cycle

  1. Hypertonic suboccipital muscles compress soft tissues surrounding neurovascular-lymphatic bundles.
  2. Compression → passive congestion → local swelling.
  3. Swelling further compresses the neurovascular bundle, including the vagus nerve (CN X).
  4. Impaired vagal tone → increased heart rate, reduced HRV, potential GI/autonomic dysfunction.
  5. Pain and reflex muscle guarding perpetuate the restricted barrier.

Pediatric Considerations

👶
Pediatric spine is not a small adult spine. At birth, multiple ossification centers remain cartilaginous. C2 dens does not fuse until ~age 6. C1 lateral masses fuse at ~age 7. Treat with age-appropriate gentleness; use cranial and indirect techniques preferentially in neonates/infants.

5Osteopathic Structural Exam — Upper Cervical Spine

Patient Preparation

  • Patient supine, physician seated at head of table.
  • Complete screening history: dizziness, vision changes, weakness, numbness/tingling, prior trauma, systemic disease (RA, Down syndrome, prior cervical fracture/surgery).
  • Obtain consent.

Step 1 — Tissue Texture & Tenderness (Palpation)

  • Cradle the head; fingers slide just inferior to the occiput (posterior-medial to the mastoid process).
  • Assess suboccipital muscles: hypertonicity, asymmetry, tenderness.
  • Note: do NOT compress the occipitomastoid suture / jugular foramen area.

Step 2 — OA Translation Test (C0 on C1)

Procedure: With both hands cradling the occiput (thumbs or index fingers at the inferior nuchal line region), apply a small lateral translation of the head (not the whole neck) — left then right. Test in neutral, flexion (chin tuck), and extension (slight chin lift).

  • Translation LEFT = OA sidebends RIGHT & rotates LEFT (coupled, follows automatically).
  • Find the plane (flexion vs. extension) in which translation is most restricted.
  • The motion that moves more freely = the freedom.
Example interpretation

If translation RIGHT is restricted in flexion → OA is in a flexed somatic dysfunction, sidebent L, rotated R.

If translation RIGHT is restricted in extension → OA is in an extended somatic dysfunction, sidebent L, rotated R.

Step 3 — AA Rotation Test (C1 on C2)

Setup: Flex the head/neck to 45° to lock out the lower cervical spine. This is critical — without it, you are testing the whole cervical spine, not just AA.

Landmark: Place index finger on angle of jaw, ring finger on mastoid process; middle finger lands on the C1 transverse process bilaterally.

Procedure: Maintain 45° flexion; rotate the head left and right, feeling the C1 transverse processes. The direction of easier, freer rotation = the freedom = the diagnosis.

AA Diagnosis rule

Rotates more freely to the RIGHT → AA rotated RIGHT.

Only diagnose rotation — no sidebending or F/E component for AA.

Signs of Cervical Instability — Stop & Re-evaluate

🚨
Use medical judgment / clinical correlation if any of these are present:
  • Severe muscle spasm; patient refuses to move head (esp. flexion)
  • "Lump in throat" sensation; lip or facial paresthesia
  • Severe headache; dizziness; nausea/vomiting
  • Soft end-feel on vertebral palpation; nystagmus; pupillary changes
  • Positional neurologic symptoms during exam → STOP immediately

6TART Findings, Documentation & Diagnosis

T — Tissue texture change

Increased tone, ropiness, or bogginess in suboccipital muscles. Doughy quality in acute; dense/fibrous in chronic.

A — Asymmetry

Unequal position or level of transverse processes of C1. Head visually tilted or rotated at rest.

R — Restricted ROM

Identified via translation test (OA) or rotation at 45° flexion (AA). Document as restricted barrier, not as where the patient "sits."

T — Tenderness

Pain/tenderness on palpation of suboccipital muscles, transverse processes, or spinous process of C2.

Documentation Template (SOAP)

OSE: Hypertonicity in right suboccipital muscles. Head/C1 neutral, sidebent left, rotated right. AA rotated right.

Assessment: Neck Pain — Cervical muscle strain. Somatic dysfunction: Head (OA — extended, SBR, RL); Neck (AA — rotated R).

Plan: Explained Dx, risks and benefits. Pt understands and consents. OA and AA treatment with ME. Post-treatment: increased left rotation without pain, less tender. Heat/cold Q2h PRN; gentle stretching; APAP/NSAID PRN. RTO 1 week PRN.

ICD Diagnosis Mapping

  • M99.00 Segmental dysfunction — cervical region (somatic dysfunction, head)
  • M99.01 Segmental dysfunction — cervical region (neck)
  • Always add the primary diagnosis (e.g., cervicalgia, cervical muscle strain) alongside the SD code.

7Muscle Energy — Upper Cervical Spine

Classification

Direct Active

Physician engages the restrictive barrier; patient applies gentle force toward the restrictive barrier (isometric contraction against physician resistance).

Force used

2–3 ounces — approximately the force needed to slide an empty teacup across a dry saucer (just enough to overcome the coefficient of static friction).

ME Indications & Contraindications

Indications

  • Somatic dysfunction in the absence of contraindications
  • Examination suggesting primarily a muscular cause for SD

Absolute Contraindications

  • Patient refusal
  • Significantly injured or painful muscles at the site
  • Low vitality / unable or unwilling to comply
  • Significant pathology at the site

Relative Contraindications

  • Undiagnosed joint swelling
  • Vascular compromise (vertebral artery disease, active DVT)
  • Open wound, infection
  • Bone pathology: infection, osteoporosis, metastatic process, instability
  • RA, Down syndrome (ligamentous laxity)
⚠️
OCG Reflex contraindications (additional): Recent eye surgery or trauma; fracture/dislocation or moderate-severe segmental instability of the cervical spine; evocation of neurological symptoms/signs on rotation of the neck.

Oculocephalogyric (OCG) Reflex

Voluntary eye motion reflexively contracts functional cervical/truncal muscle groups, allowing very gentle post-isometric relaxation or reciprocal inhibition. Used as the activating force for upper cervical ME instead of having the patient push with the head/neck.

Mechanism: Primitive visual tracking reflex — stabilizes retinal image by moving the head to follow the eyes.

Force: Patient uses only the gentlest eye movement. Can be performed up to 3 times per session.

OA Flexed Somatic Dysfunction

Diagnosis: OA prefers flexion; restricted in extension. Freedom = flexion. Example: OA flexed, SBR, RL.
  1. Position: Patient supine, physician at head of table cradling the occiput.
  2. Engage barrier: Bring the OA into the restrictive barrier — extension, with sidebending and rotation to the barrier.
  3. OCG activation: Ask the patient to look gently downward toward their feet (eyes toward feet = extension activation; will try to extend the head into the barrier).
  4. Hold: Patient maintains eye position for 3–5 seconds.
  5. Relax: Patient looks straight up; physician takes up the slack to the new restrictive barrier (post-isometric relaxation).
  6. Repeat up to 3 times total.
  7. Retest: Reassess translation symmetry in neutral and flexion/extension.

OA Extended Somatic Dysfunction

Diagnosis: OA prefers extension; restricted in flexion. Freedom = extension. Example: OA extended, SBR, RL.
  1. Position: Patient supine, physician cradling the occiput.
  2. Engage barrier: Bring the OA into the restrictive barrier — flexion (chin tuck/nod), with sidebending and rotation to the barrier. Not full cervical flexion — isolate to the OA.
  3. OCG activation: Ask the patient to look gently upward toward the physician (eyes up = flexion activation; tries to flex the head into the barrier).
  4. Hold: 3–5 seconds.
  5. Relax: Post-isometric relaxation; engage new barrier (more flexion, SB, rotation).
  6. Repeat up to 3 times.
  7. Retest.

AA Rotational Somatic Dysfunction

Diagnosis: AA rotated R or rotated L (rotation only — no other components). Diagnosis named for freedom.
  1. Position: Patient supine; physician at head of table with ring fingers on mastoid processes, middle fingers on C1 transverse processes, index fingers on angle of jaw.
  2. Lock out lower cervicals: Flex the head/neck to 45° (a "slice of pie"). Maintain this throughout.
  3. Engage restrictive barrier: Rotate the head to the restrictive direction (opposite to freedom) to the feather edge.
  4. Patient activation: "Gently try to turn your head back to where you started" (toward freedom) against the physician's resistance — 2–3 oz force at the zygoma/cheekbone. 50% of what seems intuitive.
  5. Hold: 3–5 seconds.
  6. Relax: Post-isometric relaxation; engage new restrictive barrier (more toward restriction).
  7. Repeat up to 3 times. Do not release the 45° flexion between repetitions.
  8. Retest: Re-examine rotation symmetry at 45° flexion.
💡
If the patient "pushes too hard": Remind them it's only about twice the force needed to slide a teacup. Too much force = injury risk and fails post-isometric relaxation mechanism.

8Counterstrain — Posterior Cervical Points

Classification

Indirect Passive

Physician positions the patient to minimize tenderness at the counterstrain point (CSP). Patient is passive. Hold 90 seconds.

Mechanism

Positioning approximates the origin and insertion of the affected muscle / relieves articular stress → reduces aberrant proprioceptive input from muscle spindles → allows relaxation of facilitated segment.

CS General Principles

  1. Find the CSP. Establish baseline tenderness: "If 0 = no pain and 10 = most intense pain, rate this."
  2. Move the patient into a position that reduces tenderness to ≤ 30% (ideally 0–1/10).
  3. Hold 90 seconds without moving. Fine-tune as needed.
  4. Return to neutral SLOWLY.
  5. Recheck tenderness at the same point. Should be reduced ≥ 70%.

CS Indications & Contraindications

Indications

  • SD with identified CSP, absence of contraindications
  • Headache (cervicogenic, suboccipital)
  • Post-surgical recovery (post-anesthesia OA restriction)

Absolute Contraindications

  • Patient refusal
  • Fracture or ligament tear directly affected by positioning
  • Exacerbation of life-threatening problem by positioning

Relative Contraindications

  • CV: active MI, DVT, vertebral artery disease
  • Neuro: symptoms provoked by positioning (dizziness, numbness → STOP)
  • General: gravely ill, cannot relax/remain passive, RA, Down syndrome, joint replacement, severe osteoporosis, recent surgery, pregnancy
💡
Post-treatment reactions (myalgia, arthralgia, fatigue) are common, self-limited, and well-tolerated. Counsel patients in advance.

PC1 Inion — Detailed Technique

  1. Find the inion (EOP). Palpate gently with index and middle fingers bilaterally just lateral to midline — note which side is more tender.
  2. Establish baseline: "Rate this tenderness 0–10."
  3. With both hands cradling the occiput (forearms supported on the table), introduce marked flexion — a chin tuck/nod motion. You may need to slightly lift the occiput off the table.
  4. Fine-tune: slight sidebend toward the tender side; slight rotation away from tender side.
  5. Ask: "How is that tenderness now?" Target ≤ 1/10.
  6. Hold 90 seconds without movement. Sense for tissue texture change.
  7. Slowly return to neutral. Recheck tenderness.

PC1 Lateral Occiput & PC2 Occiput — Technique

  1. Palpate 1–3 cm lateral to midline along the inferior nuchal line on each side. Feel for tenderness at splenius capitis (more lateral) vs. semispinalis capitis (more medial).
  2. Baseline tenderness scale.
  3. Introduce mild extension — small, local; do not let the patient's gaze go above the ceiling. Support forearms on the table.
  4. Add sidebend away from the tender point; rotate away from the tender point.
  5. Recheck: target ≤ 1/10.
  6. Hold 90 seconds. Return slowly to neutral. Recheck.

PC3 Maverick — Technique

  1. Palpate the inferior-lateral aspect of the C2 spinous process (first large bony midline structure below the occiput). Feel left and right for greater tenderness.
  2. Baseline scale.
  3. Introduce flexion to ~45° (not global cervical flexion — localize to the intersegmental level at C2–C3).
  4. Add sidebend away from the tender side; rotate away from the tender side. Movements are small — intersegmental.
  5. Recheck: target ≤ 1/10 (may need more rotation to achieve this).
  6. Hold 90 seconds. Return slowly to neutral. Recheck.

PC4–PC8 — Technique

  1. Palpate lateral to the spinous processes of C3–C7 (lateral articular pillar) and on the inferior surface of each spinous process (for midline points).
  2. Confirm tenderness and establish baseline.
  3. Introduce local/segmental extension: fingerpads under the tender articular pillar, apply gentle anterior pressure to extend that segment locally. Chin remains tucked; the rest of the cervical spine remains relatively neutral.
  4. Option: Bring the head slightly off the table (supported on physician's thigh/pillow) to allow further segmental drop. Keep chin tucked — patient should still see the ceiling.
  5. Add sidebend away and rotate away from the tender side.
  6. Hold 90 seconds. Return to neutral slowly. Recheck.

9Clinical Correlations & Evidence

Upper Cervical SD → Autonomic Impact

OA somatic dysfunction impairs vagal tone through the following cascade:

  • Hypertonic suboccipital muscles → compression of neurovascular-lymphatic bundles (including CN X)
  • → Passive congestion and local swelling → further impairs vagal function
  • → Increased heart rate, reduced HRV, GI dysfunction (paralytic ileus post-surgery)

Clinical implication: ME or OA condylar decompression improves HRV (parasympathetic activity markers RMSSD, pNN50 all significantly increased post-OMT in published studies).

Headache

  • OA SD → increased muscle tone + dural tension + sensitization of the trigeminal-cervical complex → cervicogenic headache
  • PC1 and PC2 points associated with frontal/periorbital headache patterns
  • OMT (ME, soft tissue, OA release) contributes to headache resolution

Surgical Patients

Endotracheal intubation requires neck extension → frequently creates OA SD post-operatively. Addressing this supports vagal tone and GI recovery (passing gas, appetite return — key discharge criteria).

Neonatal / Pediatric

  • Birth trauma → condylar compression → jugular foramen compression → CN IX/X/XI effects → vomiting, irritability, torticollis, swallowing difficulties
  • Treat with age-appropriate, gentle techniques. Ossification not complete until ~age 6–7.

Quick Reference — Safety Summary

🛑
Absolute contraindications to ALL upper cervical OMT:
  • Patient refusal at any time
  • Active cervical fracture or significant instability (RA atlantoaxial instability, Down syndrome)
  • Active vertebral artery dissection
  • Provocation of neurologic symptoms (dizziness, weakness, vision changes) on positioning or rotation

10UNE / COMLEX-Style MCQ Review

These questions are built from the learning objectives in this note and emphasize the style of clinical reasoning commonly expected on UNE and COMLEX-style assessments.

Question 1

A 29-year-old man presents 2 days after a motor vehicle collision with upper neck pain and occipital headache. During screening before any upper cervical exam, he reports dizziness and blurred vision when he lightly turns his head to the right. What is the best next step?

  1. Proceed with the AA rotation test because it localizes the problem
  2. Use posterior cervical counterstrain because it is indirect
  3. Stop the exam and re-evaluate for cervical instability or vascular/neurologic compromise
  4. Continue with OA translation testing only in neutral
  5. Begin muscle energy using very low force

Answer: C. Stop the exam and re-evaluate for cervical instability or vascular/neurologic compromise.

Rationale: The note is explicit that positional neurologic symptoms such as dizziness, weakness, or vision changes are a stop sign. Provoked symptoms during positioning or rotation are a contraindication to continuing the exam or treatment.

Question 2

A 47-year-old woman with rheumatoid arthritis has acute upper cervical pain after a minor fall. Injury to which ligament would most directly destabilize the atlantoaxial junction by allowing anterior translation of the atlas on the dens?

  1. Alar ligament
  2. Apical ligament
  3. Transverse ligament
  4. Posterior atlanto-occipital membrane
  5. Ligamentum nuchae

Answer: C. Transverse ligament.

Rationale: The transverse ligament is the primary stabilizer of the AA junction and limits anterior atlas translation. That is why instability from inflammatory disease such as RA is such an important upper cervical safety concern.

Question 3

During OA translation testing, the physician finds that translation to the right is most restricted when the OA is placed in flexion. Based on the interpretation rule used in this note, which diagnosis is correct?

  1. OA flexed, sidebent left, rotated right
  2. OA flexed, sidebent right, rotated left
  3. OA extended, sidebent left, rotated right
  4. OA extended, sidebent right, rotated left
  5. AA rotated right

Answer: A. OA flexed, sidebent left, rotated right.

Rationale: In this note, translation right corresponds to OA sidebent left and rotated right. The example under the OA translation section states that if translation right is restricted in flexion, the diagnosis is OA flexed, sidebent left, rotated right.

Question 4

To isolate the atlantoaxial joint, a physician flexes the patient's head and neck to 45 degrees and places the index finger at the angle of the jaw and the ring finger at the mastoid process. The middle finger rests on the structure being monitored during rotation testing. Rotation is freer to the right. Which statement is correct?

  1. The physician is palpating the C2 spinous process; diagnose AA sidebent right, rotated right
  2. The physician is palpating the C1 transverse process; diagnose AA rotated right
  3. The physician is palpating the occipital condyle; diagnose OA flexed, sidebent right, rotated left
  4. The physician is palpating the C1 transverse process; diagnose AA rotated left
  5. The physician is palpating the articular pillar of C2; diagnose OA extended, sidebent left, rotated right

Answer: B. The physician is palpating the C1 transverse process; diagnose AA rotated right.

Rationale: The note uses the angle of the jaw and mastoid to locate the C1 transverse process with the middle finger. AA is diagnosed by rotation only, and the diagnosis is named for the direction of freer motion.

Question 5

A patient is diagnosed with an OA extended somatic dysfunction. Which muscle energy setup is most appropriate when using the oculocephalogyric reflex?

  1. Engage extension, then ask the patient to look down toward the feet
  2. Engage flexion with a chin tuck, then ask the patient to look up toward the physician
  3. Engage flexion, then ask the patient to look down toward the feet
  4. Keep the OA neutral and ask the patient to shrug the shoulders
  5. Flex the lower cervical spine to 45 degrees and have the patient rotate against resistance

Answer: B. Engage flexion with a chin tuck, then ask the patient to look up toward the physician.

Rationale: OA extended means extension is the freedom and flexion is the restrictive barrier. The note pairs that setup with OCG activation by having the patient look upward toward the physician.

Question 6

A patient has an AA rotated right somatic dysfunction. Which muscle energy sequence is correct?

  1. Maintain 45 degrees of flexion, rotate right to the feather edge, and ask the patient to look down
  2. Maintain 45 degrees of flexion, rotate left to the feather edge, and ask the patient to gently turn the head right against resistance using 2-3 ounces of force
  3. Keep the neck neutral, sidebend left, and ask the patient to rotate left
  4. Extend the neck to isolate AA, rotate left, and ask the patient to push firmly
  5. Maintain 45 degrees of flexion, rotate left, and hold passively for 90 seconds

Answer: B. Maintain 45 degrees of flexion, rotate left to the feather edge, and ask the patient to gently turn the head right against resistance using 2-3 ounces of force.

Rationale: AA rotated right means right rotation is the freedom, so the restrictive barrier is to the left. Lower cervicals must stay locked out in 45 degrees of flexion, and the force should remain very gentle.

Question 7

A patient has a very tender counterstrain point at the inion and describes suboccipital headache. Which positioning is most appropriate?

  1. Marked flexion with slight sidebending toward and rotation away
  2. Marked extension with sidebending toward and rotation toward
  3. Flexion to 45 degrees with rotation toward only
  4. Mild extension with sidebending away and rotation away
  5. Neutral positioning with direct pressure on the point

Answer: A. Marked flexion with slight sidebending toward and rotation away.

Rationale: PC1 Inion is a posterior cervical maverick point. The note emphasizes marked flexion rather than extension, then fine-tuning with slight sidebending toward and rotation away.

Question 8

While treating a PC4 lateral tender point with posterior cervical counterstrain, which positioning cue best protects the vertebral artery?

  1. Use global cervical extension until the patient sees the physician's face
  2. Use segmental extension with the chin tucked so the patient still sees the ceiling
  3. Maximally rotate toward the tender point before extending
  4. Let the head hang off the table unsupported
  5. Add repeated active extension against resistance during the hold

Answer: B. Use segmental extension with the chin tucked so the patient still sees the ceiling.

Rationale: The note gives this exact safety cue for PC2-PC8 treatment: extension should be local and segmental, not global, and the patient should be able to see the ceiling rather than the physician's face.

Question 9

A patient is on postoperative day 1 after a difficult intubation and has suboccipital hypertonicity, tachycardia, and delayed return of bowel function. Which osteopathic explanation best fits the note?

  1. OA dysfunction increases lumbar sympathetic tone and directly causes ileus
  2. OA dysfunction may impair vagal tone, contributing to increased heart rate, reduced HRV, and GI dysfunction
  3. AA dysfunction primarily compresses the phrenic nerve and causes abdominal distention
  4. PC2 tender points indicate a primary trigeminal motor lesion
  5. Upper cervical dysfunction is unrelated to intubation positioning

Answer: B. OA dysfunction may impair vagal tone, contributing to increased heart rate, reduced HRV, and GI dysfunction.

Rationale: The clinical section specifically links OA somatic dysfunction to impaired vagal tone and notes that intubation-related neck extension commonly creates postoperative OA dysfunction.

Question 10

A newborn after a difficult delivery has poor latch, irritability, and swallowing difficulty. Upper cervical condylar compression is suspected. Which cranial nerves are most likely affected as they pass near the jugular foramen?

  1. II, III, IV
  2. V, VI, VII
  3. IX, X, XI
  4. XII only
  5. III, VII, IX

Answer: C. IX, X, XI.

Rationale: The neonatal and anatomy sections both emphasize that condylar compression can affect CN IX, X, and XI near the jugular foramen, producing feeding, swallowing, autonomic, and SCM/trapezius-related symptoms.
