Epidemiology Neck Pain Overview

📊 Scope of the Problem
Chronic pain affects over 100 million Americans over age 18, costing $600 billion/year in healthcare and lost productivity. Back and neck pain are the two leading causes of chronic pain and disability.

Key History Questions

🗣️ History Taking Pearl
Always categorize: type, location, duration, severity, aggravating/relieving factors, prior treatments, prior imaging, and trajectory (better/worse/same). Ask specifically if the patient has had these symptoms before and how they resolved.

Biomechanics Cervical Spine Motion

Upper Cervical Unit

JointNicknamePrimary MotionNotes
O-A (C0–C1)"Yes" JointFlexion / ExtensionNodding motion; sidebending and rotation coupled in opposite directions
A-A (C1–C2)"No" JointRotation (~50% of total cervical rotation)10–12° flexion/extension; only 1–2° sidebending

Lower Cervical Unit (C2–C7)

Sidebending and rotation are coupled in the same direction due to:

🏔️ Structure Saddle-shaped vertebral body Guides coupled motion
📐 Facets Direction of facet facings Constrains coupled movement
🔗 Joints Uncovertebral Joints of Luschka Between bodies of adjacent cervical vertebrae; locks in Type 2 mechanics
⚠️ Coupling Summary
Upper Unit (OA) → S & R opposite directions (Type 1 mechanics)
Lower Unit (C2–C7) → S & R same direction (Type 2 mechanics)
🧪 Implication for Motion Testing
Because of the reciprocal nature of upper vs. lower cervical motion, regional motion testing is insufficient to identify specific areas of restriction. Segmental assessment is required.

Cases Clinical Presentations

📋 Case 1 — Radiculopathy Pattern

42 F — Shoveling wet snow → next-day neck stiffness + intense right arm pain. No weakness. PMHx non-contributory.

PE: Depressed C5 reflex (R) · Spurling's (+) right · OAESSl · C5–7ErRSR · Elevated 1st rib (R) · Paraspinal hypertonicity C–T spine
🔍 Key Finding
Spurling's maneuver = foraminal compression test. Positive when reproduces radicular pain. Combined with depressed C5 reflex → C5 radiculopathy pattern. Consider imaging (low threshold with radiculopathy).
📋 Case 2 — Myelopathy Pattern

68 M — Gradual onset neck pain. No radiculopathy. Reporting increased falls, unsteady gait. PMHx: Cervical DDD.

PE: Myelopathic gait pattern · Brisk UE & LE reflexes · Hoffman's sign (+) · Spurling's (–) · Intact strength x4 extremities · OASSl · C2–3RSl · T4–7RrSl · L3–5RlSr · Left-on-left torsion
⚠️ Hoffman's Sign
Flick the nail of the middle finger. Positive: flexion of the distal thumb phalanx + 2nd/3rd finger phalanges. Indicates upper motor neuron lesion. HVLA is contraindicated in this patient — myelopathy signals cord compression.
📋 Case 3 — Somatic Dysfunction Pattern

32 F — "Woke up wrong," progressive over weeks. Worse with left rotation. No radiculopathy. PMHx: DDD at C5–6.

PE: NAD · Paraspinal hypertonicity · OAEssl · C2–3FRSl · C5Esrr · Locked 1st rib (L) · T3–5Rrls · T8–10RRSl · Tender points in rectus capitis & semispinalis capitis reproducing primary pain · Spurling's (–) · Non-antalgic gait
✅ OMT Candidate
Negative Spurling's, no neurologic deficits, identifiable somatic dysfunctions at multiple levels. Good candidate for cervical HVLA with attention to C2–3 and C5 diagnoses.

📝 AOA Position (2019)
AOA Position Paper concludes that cervical HVLA is safe and effective and should be taught in medical school.
💊 NSAID Comparison
NSAIDs cause 103,000 hospitalizations and at least 16,500 deaths/year in the USA from GI toxicity — the 15th most common cause of death. 81% of GI bleeds from NSAIDs occur without prior symptoms. Cervical manipulation complications are rare by comparison.

Vertebral Artery Accidents (VBA)

🩸 VBA Key Facts
  • Rare on its own — accounts for 1.3 in 1,000 stroke cases
  • 75% of VBA stroke patients have good functional recovery
  • Risk factors: migraine, HTN, oral contraceptives, smoking, hypermobility
  • Average age for VBA injury (all causes): 37 years
  • Haldeman et al.: VBA following manipulation is "idiosyncratic and rare"
  • 25% who suffered VBA after manipulation had sudden-onset new/unusual headache + neck pain (possibly dissection already in progress)

Cervical Artery Dissection (CAD)

📌 CAD Facts
Rate: 2.9 per 100,000 in the general population. 89% have no symptoms or disability within 3 months. Most common complaint: headache + neck pain.

Difficult to determine if manipulation caused CAD or if dissection was already present at time of treatment — the "chicken and egg" problem.

Safety Contraindications

Absolute Contraindications

🚫 Absolute — Do Not Manipulate
  • Structural disruption (fracture, instability)
  • Fragile bony or ligamentous structures
  • Neoplastic degradation of tissues (local)
  • Advanced RA in upper cervical spine
  • Patient refusal

Relative / Specific Contraindications

Vascular / Neurologic

  • Vascular insufficiency (cervical)
  • Acute CVA (stroke)
  • Severe vertigo
  • Acute herniated nucleus pulposus
  • Down Syndrome (Trisomy 21)

Structural / Systemic

  • Primary or metastatic bone tumor
  • Severe osteoporosis
  • Severe rheumatoid arthritis
  • Osteomyelitis
  • Anticoagulation / bleeding disorder
  • Metabolic bone disease
  • Hypermobility syndromes (e.g., Ehlers-Danlos)
  • Ocular lens implant (post-op period)
  • Fever
📝 General Contraindication Categories
  • Osteolytic conditions
  • Osteoporosis
  • Neoplastic degradation
  • Hyper-irritability of neurosensory unit
  • Patient apprehension
  • History of cervical fusion

Technique Diagnosis & HVLA Setup

Soft Tissue Preparation

🖐️ Posterior Cervical Soft Tissue Technique
Patient supine. One hand on forehead (counterforce). Other hand fingertips rhythmically stretch and compress soft tissue between spinous and articular processes while gently sidebending, rotating, and extending the C-spine. Applied bilaterally from OA to T1.
Note: Do not drag fingers across the patient's skin.

Diagnosis of Lower Cervical Spine (C2–C7)

🔎 Segmental Motion Testing
  1. Patient supine; operator at head of table
  2. Neck relaxed; index fingertip along articular pillars bilaterally
  3. Apply medial translatory force to test sidebending at each segment
  4. Test from C7 → C2, noting restrictions
  5. Perform in both flexion and extension
  6. R→L translation = right sidebending; L→R = left sidebending

Lower Cervical HVLA Setup

⚙️ HVLA Technique — Lower Cervical
Key principle: All lower cervical dysfunctions (flexed or extended) are treated in flexion for patient safety.
  1. Patient supine; physician at head of table
  2. Place index finger (DIP or PIP joint) over the articular pillar on the side of restricted sidebending
  3. Other hand cradles occiput, cups around ear, thumb gently on mandible
  4. Sidebend head toward the fulcrum (index finger)
  5. Rotate away from fulcrum; add flexion to localize at restricted segment
  6. HVLA thrust: 50% sidebending + 50% rotation toward the opposite axilla
  7. Recheck diagnostic findings
Optional: Use isometric contraction or respiratory cooperation to relax patient and better localize forces before thrust delivery.
🔢 Diagnosis Shorthand Examples
C3 restricted L sidebend → Finger on L articular pillar C3 → Rotate R, sidebend L, flex → Thrust toward R axilla
C5 restricted R sidebend (worse in flexion) → Treat in flexion → Finger on R articular pillar C5 → SB R, rotate L, flex

Question 1

A 25 yo with neck pain requests OMT. Which history is MOST contraindicated for HVLA?

  • A. Joint instability
  • B. Fibromyalgia
  • C. HNP without radiculopathy
  • D. Osteoarthritis
  • E. Mild osteoporosis
A — Joint instability is an absolute structural contraindication to HVLA. Fibromyalgia, HNP without radiculopathy, OA, and mild osteoporosis are relative/specific contraindications or not contraindications at this severity.
Question 2

A 34 yo presents post-MVA with C3 restricted in left sidebending, worse in extension. To apply HVLA you must:

  • A. Extend C-spine, index finger on right articular pillar C4
  • B. Flex C-spine, index finger on left articular pillar C4
  • C. Extend C-spine, index finger on left articular pillar C3
  • D. Flex C-spine, index finger on left articular pillar C3
  • E. Flex C-spine, index finger on right articular pillar C3
D — Treat all lower cervical in flexion for safety. Restricted left sidebending → finger on left articular pillar of C3 (the dysfunctional level, not the level below). Sidebend L, rotate R toward opposite axilla.
Question 3

A 24 yo has C5 worse in flexion, unable to translate R→L. HVLA setup includes:

  • A. Extension, right SB and right rotation
  • B. Flexion, right SB and right rotation
  • C. Flexion, right SB and left rotation
  • D. Flexion, left SB and right rotation
  • E. Extension, left SB and left rotation
C — C5 worse in flexion (extended dysfunction) but still treat in flexion for safety. Restricted R→L translation = restricted right sidebending → sidebend right. For lower cervical (C2–C7) Type 2 mechanics: SB right + rotate left (same direction coupling is for the barrier; thrust rotates opposite to sidebend direction). Thrust toward the left axilla.
Question 4

A 74 yo with neck pain has dizziness on passive head rotation + extension. X-ray: moderate DJD. Compression of which structure is most likely?

  • A. Brachiocephalic vein
  • B. Cervical ganglia of sympathetic trunk
  • C. Herniated NP at C4–5
  • D. Vertebral artery
  • E. Joints of Luschka
D — Vertebral artery. Dizziness provoked by rotation + extension in an elderly patient with DJD is classic for vertebral artery compression (vertebrobasilar insufficiency). This pattern is a contraindication to HVLA.
Question 5

A 62 yo requests OMT for neck pain. Which history is MOST contraindicated to HVLA?

  • A. Fibromyalgia
  • B. Cervical disk protrusion
  • C. Rheumatoid arthritis
  • D. Osteoarthritis
  • E. Cervical facet restriction C5–6
C — Rheumatoid arthritis. Especially in older patients, advanced RA in the upper cervical spine (atlantoaxial instability from pannus) is an absolute contraindication. Osteoarthritis and fibromyalgia are generally not contraindications. Cervical facet restriction is an indication for HVLA.
