Coupling rule: Translate occiput LEFT → OA SB right, R left. Sidebending and rotation are obligatorily opposite.

Atypical Vertebrae & Vasculature
  • C1 (Atlas): NO body, NO true SP, NO uncovertebral joints. Anterior arch → fovea dentis. Posterior tubercle replaces SP. First palpable midline bony prominence below occiput is C2 SP, not C1.
  • C2 (Axis): Dens (pivot for AA). Bifid SP (first large midline below occiput). Vascular watershed at dens base (vertebral a.) vs apex (carotid branches) → Type II odontoid fx healing risk.
  • Vertebral artery: Through transverse foramina C1–C6 only (NOT C7). Loops posterior to occipital condyles → avoid extreme extension.
  • Suboccipital muscles: Highest density of muscle spindles in the body (~200/g) → critical proprioceptors for head-in-space.

TART · Notation · ICD-10

criteria + documentation

T — Tissue Texture

Bogginess, ropiness, hypertonicity. Doughy = acute. Dense / fibrous = chronic.

A — Asymmetry

Positional offset. Lower cervical: prominent posterior articular pillar = SAME side as rotation.

R — Restriction

Identified via translation testing. Document the restrictive barrier, not the position of ease.

T — Tenderness

Palpation pain at articular pillar / SP / TVP / muscle. Patient-reported, not just provoked.

Three-Positional Diagnosis

  • Cannot tolerate HVLA, or HVLA contraindicated

ME Contraindications

Absolute

  • Patient refusal
  • Acute fracture / dislocation at site
  • Severe instability (RA, Down syndrome AA instability)

Upper Cervical ME — OCG Reflex

Oculocephalogyric (OCG) Reflex

Voluntary eye motion reflexively activates cervical muscle groups → very gentle activating force for upper cervical ME (replaces head/neck push). Mechanism: primitive visual tracking reflex stabilizing retinal image.

👁️↓
Eyes look DOWN (toward feet) = Extension activation
👁️↑
Eyes look UP (toward physician) = Flexion activation
⚠️
Additional OCG contraindications: recent eye surgery / trauma · cervical fracture / dislocation / instability · evocation of neuro symptoms with rotation
OA Flexed SD

Prefers flexion · restricted in extension

  1. Patient supine; physician cradles occiput
  2. Engage barrier: EXTENSION + SB & R to barrier (opposite directions)
  3. OCG: "Look DOWN toward feet" (eyes = extension force)
  4. Hold 3–5 sec → relax → re-engage new barrier
  5. Repeat ×3 → retest
OA Extended SD

Prefers extension · restricted in flexion

  1. Patient supine; physician cradles occiput
  2. Engage barrier: FLEXION (chin tuck) + SB & R to barrier
  3. OCG: "Look UP toward me" (eyes = flexion force)
  4. Hold 3–5 sec → relax → re-engage
  5. Repeat ×3 → retest
AA Rotational SD

Rotation only — no SB / FE

  1. Flex head/neck to 45° (lock-out)
  2. Engage restrictive rotation to feather edge
  3. "Gently turn back toward start" — 2–3 oz at zygoma
  4. Hold 3–5 sec → relax → new barrier
  5. Maintain 45° flex throughout
☕
2–3 oz teacup rule — force needed to slide an empty teacup across a dry saucer. 50% of what feels intuitive.

Lower Cervical ME — Direct, Patient-Activated

Extended Dysfunction (e.g. C5 ERSl)

Restricted in flexion + opposite SB/R · bring TO flexion barrier · patient pushes back

  1. Localize: monitoring fingers bilateral on articular pillars at the segment
  2. Flexion barrier: scoop & chin tuck → flex DOWN to the segment
  3. SB/R barrier: translate toward restriction (e.g., ERSl → translate R→L) to feather edge
  4. Patient cue: "Push the back of your head into the table" — 3–5 sec
  5. Relax 2–3 sec → take up new slack in all 3 planes
  6. Repeat ×3–5 → recheck translation
🎯
Confirm force at the segment: if not felt, switch cue to "push your whole neck back into my hand."
Flexed Dysfunction (e.g. C6 FRSr)

Restricted in extension + opposite SB/R · bring TO extension barrier · patient tucks chin

  1. Localize: monitoring fingers at C6 articular pillars
  2. Extension barrier: ring & pinky just INFERIOR to segment, lift toward ceiling → local extension
  3. SB/R barrier: translate toward restriction (e.g., FRSr → translate L→R) to feather edge
  4. Patient cue: "Tuck your chin" / "Lift your head slightly" — 3–5 sec
  5. Relax → re-engage all 3 planes
  6. Repeat ×3–5 → recheck
⚡
Don't confuse with HVLA: HVLA treats ALL lower cervical SD in flexion for safety. ME is position-specific — extended → flex barrier; flexed → ext barrier.

CS General Principles — 5 Steps
  1. Find the tender point (TP). Establish baseline tenderness 0–10.
  2. Position patient to reduce tenderness ≤ 30% (ideally 0–1/10) — fine-tune all 3 planes.
  3. Hold 90 seconds without moving. Sense for tissue texture change.
  4. Return to neutral SLOWLY & PASSIVELY — patient does NOT move themselves.
  5. Recheck the same TP — should be reduced ≥ 70%.
CS Indications
  • SD with identifiable TP, no contraindications
  • Acute / chronic cervical SD
  • Patients who can't tolerate direct techniques (post-trauma, hypersensitive)
  • Cervicogenic / suboccipital headache
  • Post-anesthesia OA restriction; post-op
  • Restricted rotation — esp. AC1 (dramatic recovery)
  • First-line before ME (may resolve segmental SD pre-treatment)
CS Contraindications

Absolute

  • Patient refusal
  • Fracture / ligament tear directly affected by positioning
  • Positioning would exacerbate life-threatening problem

Relative

  • Active MI · DVT · vertebral artery disease
  • Symptoms provoked by positioning → STOP
  • RA · Down syndrome · joint replacement · severe osteoporosis
  • Recent surgery · pregnancy · gravely ill
  • Cannot relax or remain passive

Post-Tx myalgia / arthralgia / fatigue is common, self-limited — counsel in advance.

STAR

AC1 — Rotation-Only Maverick

Use no flexion or extension. Rotate away markedly to about 90 degrees, then add slight side bending away. Fine-tune with a slight caudal force on the contralateral parietal bone.

Cervical HVLA — Lower Cervical Setup & Safety

Dr. Collins · all SDs treated in flexion
Soft Tissue Prep — Posterior Cervical

Always before HVLA. Patient supine.

  1. One hand on forehead = counterforce
  2. Other hand: fingertips along paraspinal trough between SP and articular pillars
  3. Rhythmic stretch + compress — gently SB, rotate, extend
  4. Bilateral, OA → T1
✋
Do not drag fingertips across skin. Tissue moves under your hand — your hand does not slide on the patient.
Lower Cervical Segmental Dx (C2–C7)
  1. Patient supine, operator at head of table
  2. Index fingertip pads on articular pillars bilaterally
  3. Apply medial translatory force at each level to test SB
  4. Test C7 → C2, note restrictions
  5. Repeat in flexion AND extension to find positional preference
↔
Translation rule: R→L translation induces R sidebending. Restriction = barrier on the side you're pushing toward.
Lower Cervical HVLA Setup — Universal Recipe

Key safety principle: All lower cervical dysfunctions — flexed OR extended — are treated in flexion. Flexion locks out the upper cervical and protects the vertebral artery.

  1. Patient supine, physician seated at head of table
  2. Place index DIP/PIP joint on articular pillar on the side of restricted SB
  3. Other hand cradles occiput, cups around ear, thumb gently on mandible
  4. Sidebend toward the fulcrum (your index)
  5. Rotate away from fulcrum; add flexion to localize at restricted segment
  6. Final thrust = 50% SB + 50% rotation, toward the opposite axilla
  7. Recheck
Optional: respiratory cooperation or brief isometric contraction before thrust to relax patient and tighten localization.

4 Tenets of Osteopathic Medicine
  1. The body is a unit — mind, body, and spirit.
  2. The body has capacity for self-regulation, self-healing, and health maintenance.
  3. Structure and function are reciprocally interrelated.
  4. Rational treatment is based on the first three tenets.

Ed Stiles' Sequencing Principles — How to Run a Treatment
  • The body is a functional unit — a tensegrity system.
  • Pain is usually NOT at the AGR — pain is at the area of ineffective adaptation.
  • Treatment is like peeling an onion: outside-in, never reversed.
  • Each patient is unique — there's no canned protocol.
  • Workflow: find AGR → segmental Dx at AGR → treat there → move to next regional AGR → continue until treated area won't release → stop for the visit.
🐈
"When you step on a cat's tail, where does the sound come out?" — Laughlin (A.T. Still's grandson). Translation: treat away from the chief complaint; the loud part isn't always the broken part.
⏱️
"In a 20-min treatment, you're better off spending 15 min finding the AGR — 5 effective minutes > 20 ineffective minutes." — Perrin T. Wilson

Why OMT Codes Exist — Quick Hit

In 1973 the head of Medicare in Maine told Edward G. Stiles, DO that any chart with "somatic dysfunction" in the assessment had measurably shorter LOS, fewer complications, and less drug use at Waterville Osteopathic Hospital than at neighboring hospitals.

Result: the first 5-level OMT coding system was negotiated with Medicare. The M99.0x codes you bill today exist because OMT documentably saved money at one hospital in Maine.

📂
Your 2° Dx of M99.0x isn't bureaucracy — it's the evidence trail that justifies billing the technique you just performed.

Osteopathic Structural Exam — Cervical Protocol

lab-day reference
Upper Cervical OSE

Position: Patient supine, physician seated at head of table.

Step 1 — TT & Tenderness

  • Cradle head; fingers slide just inferior to occiput, posterior-medial to mastoid
  • Assess suboccipital muscles for hypertonicity / asymmetry / tenderness
  • Do NOT compress occipitomastoid suture / jugular foramen (CN IX, X, XI)

Step 2 — OA Translation Test (C0 on C1)

  • Both hands cradle occiput; thumbs/fingers @ inferior nuchal line region
  • Apply small lateral translation of head only (not whole neck) L & R
  • Test in neutral · flexion · extension
  • Translate L = OA SB right + R left (coupled, automatic)
  • Find which plane (F vs E) translation is most restricted

Step 3 — AA Rotation Test (C1 on C2)

  • Flex head/neck to 45° (lock out lower cervicals)
  • Index → angle of jaw; ring → mastoid; middle finger lands on C1 TVP
  • Maintain 45° flexion; rotate head L & R
  • Direction of freer rotation = diagnosis (rotation only)
Lower Cervical OSE (C2–C7)

Position: Patient supine, head on table (not held up). Physician seated at head.

Step 1 — Translatory Motion Screen

  • Middle/index fingers bilateral on articular pillars at each level
  • Brace head with palms; translate R→L, then L→R
  • Compare ease — the resistant, "stuck" side identifies SD
  • Cannot translate R→L = SB right + R right
  • Work systematically C2 → C7 (count from C7 vertebra prominens)
  • Note tenderness for TART

Step 2 — F/E Discrimination

  • Flexion test: scoop & chin tuck → flex DOWN to segment, retest translation. Improves in flex → EXTENDED dysfunction
  • Extension test: ring & pinky just inferior to segment, lift toward ceiling. Improves in ext → FLEXED dysfunction
  • Side of resistance = direction of SB & R (always same)
📐
Logic check: Cannot translate R→L in EXTENSION → restricted in R SB/R in extension → stuck in flexion → FRSr.

🦴 1. Biomechanical

Restore position, motion, function. Posture, joint mechanics, fascia, muscle imbalance. Joint restriction, postural imbalance, kinetic chain.

🫁 2. Resp / Circ

Enhance venous + lymphatic drainage; arterial supply. Respiratory motion drives lymph flow. Edema, poor wound healing, recurrent infection, post-op swelling.

⚡ 3. Neurologic

↓ nociception · ↓ segmental facilitation · restore autonomic balance. T2–T8 → arm; T10–L2 → leg. Chronic pain, hyperalgesia, CRPS.

⚗️ 4. Metabolic / Energetic

Optimize energy through posture, economy of motion, nutrition. Dysfunctional tissue is energetically expensive. Chronic fatigue, post-illness deconditioning.

🧠 5. Behavioral / Biopsychosocial

Optimize ergonomics, sleep, nutrition; reduce mechanical + psychosocial stressors; rest + graded rehab. Occupational injury, allostatic load, work-disability.

Treatment Principle

Proximal Before Distal

Thoracic Inlet / 1st Rib → T-spine → Shoulder → Elbow → Wrist/Hand. Improves drainage, ↓ upstream nerve tension, ↓ facilitation, makes distal Tx more effective.

Case 1 — CTS & Medial Epicondylitis

Classic Pattern

  • Median distribution numbness: thumb, index, middle, radial half ring
  • Night pain wakes patient · flick sign for relief
  • Phalen's, Reverse Phalen's, Tinel's @ wrist, Durkan's all positive

Why Treat Proximal First?

  1. T2–T8 sympathetics → facilitation = ↑ pain + vasoconstriction
  2. Median roots C5–T1 — cervical/inlet restriction → "double crush"
  3. Thoracic outlet = terminal lymphatic/venous drainage of arm
  4. Reducing facilitation proximally improves distal Tx effectiveness
Case 2 — Plantar Fasciitis & MTPS

High-Yield Triad

  1. First-step morning pain — fascia tightens overnight
  2. Medial calcaneal tubercle TTP — plantar fascia origin
  3. Positive Windlass test (great toe DF tensions fascia)

Pes Planus → Kinetic Chain

  • Pes planus → ↑ pronation → flattened arch → overstretches plantar fascia → plantar fasciitis
  • Pes planus → ↑ tibial IR → overstretches deep posterior compartment → medial tibial pain (shin splints)

Risk Factors

  • Sudden activity ↑, overuse, poor footwear (> 300–500 mi)
  • Hard surfaces, poor stretching, pes planus

Distinguishing Features

  • Focal calcaneal TTP = PF
  • Diffuse medial tibial TTP = MTPS (NOT focal — that would be stress fx)
  • Negative hop test rules OUT stress fracture
  • Negative SLR rules out radiculopathy
  • Negative Tinel's @ tarsal tunnel rules out tarsal tunnel synd

Conservative Tx

  • Stretching: plantar fascia, gastroc-soleus, hip flexors
  • Icing post-activity · cross-train · new shoes (< 300–500 mi)
  • Night splinting (maintains DF) · NSAIDs sparingly

Force / Hold Numbers

  • Upper cervical ME force: 2–3 oz (teacup rule)
  • Lower cervical ME force: 20% effort
  • ME hold: 3–5 sec × 3–5 reps
  • CS hold: 90 sec
  • CS target: ≥ 70% ↓ tenderness, ≤ 1/10
  • AA lockout: 45° flexion

Direction Logic

  • OA coupling: SB & R OPPOSITE
  • Lower cervical: SB & R SAME
  • Translate L = SB R (bow-and-arrow)
  • Worse in flex = Extended (E prefers ext)
  • Worse in ext = Flexed (F prefers flex)
  • HVLA always treats lower cervical in flexion; ME is position-specific

OCG Reflex (Eyes for Force)

  • Eyes down → extend (treat OA flexed SD: barrier in ext, eyes down)
  • Eyes up → flex (treat OA extended SD: barrier in flex, eyes up)
  • Up to 3 reps per session
  • CI: recent eye sx/trauma · cervical fx/instab · neuro sx with rotation

Anatomy Quick Hits

  • Vertebral a. through TVF C1–C6, NOT C7
  • Suboccipital muscles: highest spindle density in body
  • C1: no body, no SP, no uncovertebral
  • C2 SP: first large midline below occiput
  • C7 SP: vertebra prominens (counting landmark)
  • Above C2 = no multifidi → freer C1/C2 motion

Sympathetic Roots

  • T2–T8 → Upper extremity
  • T10–L2 → Lower extremity
  • OA SD → ↓ vagal tone via CN X compression at jugular foramen
  • Post-intubation OA SD common → impairs GI recovery

Special-Test High-Sens Standouts

  • Lachman — most sens for ACL
  • Durkan's — highest sens + spec for CTS
  • Hawkins > Neer's for impingement sens
  • FADIR — most sens for FAI
  • Windlass — most sens for plantar fasciitis
  • Spurling's — high specificity for radiculopathy

HVLA Cheat Numbers

  • All lower cervical SDs treated in flexion (safety)
  • Fulcrum = side of restricted SB
  • Setup = SB toward fulcrum · rotate away · flex
  • Thrust = 50% SB + 50% rotation, toward opposite axilla
  • Adverse outcome 1 in 400k–4M
  • Spontaneous VBA risk 2× manip-related risk

Tx Planning — One-Liner

  • Pain ≠ AGR — pain is at the area of failed adaptation
  • Peel the onion — outside-in, never reversed
  • Stiles workflow: AGR → seg Dx → treat → next regional AGR
  • Tensegrity: local SD has distal effects (and vice versa)
  • 15 min finding AGR > 20 min ineffective treatment
  • Treat away from the CC ("step on cat's tail…")
§ 13

Red Flags & Stop Signs

stop · re-evaluate · refer
🚨 Cervical Instability — STOP & Re-evaluate
  • Severe muscle spasm; refusal to move (esp. flex)
  • "Lump in throat" sensation
  • Lip / facial paresthesia
  • Severe HA · dizziness · N/V
  • Soft end-feel on vertebral palpation
  • Nystagmus · pupillary changes
  • Positional neuro sx during exam → STOP IMMEDIATELY
🚨 Cervical Pain Red Flags (History)
  • Severe trauma (MVA, fall, assault)
  • Bladder / bowel changes
  • Weakness / paralysis
  • High fever (meningitis)
  • Cannot flex C-spine (meningismus)
  • UE numbness / tingling / pain
  • Progressive spastic paralysis
  • Unexplained weight loss
  • Cancer hx (breast/lung/prostate/lymphoma/myeloma/thyroid/kidney)
  • Osteoporosis · steroid use · immunocompromise
  • Sudden severe onset (?fracture)
🚨 Physical Exam Red Flags
  • Diminished DTRs
  • Sensory loss in dermatomal pattern
  • Myotomal weakness
  • Spastic paralysis
  • Fever
  • Intolerance of C-spine flexion
  • Lethargy / AMS
  • Carotid bruit
  • Thyroid nodules / enlargement
Absolute Contraindications — ALL Upper Cervical OMT
  • Patient refusal at any time
  • Active cervical fracture or significant instability (RA atlantoaxial instab, Down syndrome)
  • Active vertebral artery dissection
  • Provocation of neuro sx (dizziness, weakness, vision Δ) on positioning / rotation
CTS Case — When to Reconsider / Refer
  • Bilateral numbness → cord compression / UMN disease
  • Neck pain + dermatomal radiation → more likely radicular
  • Systemic sx (fever, weight loss, night sweats) → infectious / inflammatory / neoplastic
  • Rapidly progressive weakness → EMG/NCS urgently
⚠️ HVLA Relative Contraindications — Slow Down
  • Vascular insufficiency · acute CVA · severe vertigo · acute HNP
  • Down syndrome (atlantoaxial laxity)
  • Primary or metastatic bone tumor · osteomyelitis
  • Severe osteoporosis · severe RA · metabolic bone disease
  • Anticoagulation / bleeding disorder
  • Hypermobility syndromes (Ehlers-Danlos)
  • Recent ocular lens implant · fever
  • Cervical fusion history · hyper-irritable neurosensory unit
  • Patient apprehension — never thrust through fear
🛑 Myelopathy Triad — NEVER HVLA
  • Hoffman's (+) — UMN sign in UE
  • Brisk DTRs in UE and LE
  • Myelopathic gait (wide-based, spastic, falls)
  • Add: increased clumsiness, dropping things, B/B changes
📞
Order MRI cervical spine. Refer to neurosurgery. Document the contraindication in your note. The patient with myelopathy who gets thrust will leave the room worse than they came in.
