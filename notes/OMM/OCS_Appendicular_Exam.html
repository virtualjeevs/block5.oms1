LO 1 — Communication & Consent

Patient Communication & Professionalism

Lay Language vs. Medical Terminology

A core osteopathic clinical skill is code-switching — explaining the same finding clearly to a patient in plain language, then documenting it precisely in medical terms for colleagues. The same carpal tunnel finding becomes:

🗣️ To the Patient (Lay Terms)

"The nerve that runs through a small tunnel in your wrist is being pinched. That's why your thumb and index finger feel numb and tingly, especially at night."

📋 To Colleagues (Medical Terms)

"Median nerve compression at the carpal tunnel with thenar atrophy, positive Phalen's and Durkan's, and diminished two-point discrimination in the median nerve distribution."

Informed Consent Before Every Examination

⚠️ HIGH YIELD — OSCE CRITICAL
Consent must be obtained before examination AND before treatment. This is non-negotiable in clinical practice and on OSCEs. Touching a patient without consent — even for a reflex hammer — is a boundary violation.

The four elements of informed consent discussion are:

✅
Benefits
Pain reduction, improved mobility, better circulation
⚠️
Risks
Temporary soreness, rare symptom worsening, low risk of neurovascular irritation
🔄
Alternatives
Medications, splinting, physical therapy, activity modification, no treatment
🤝
Verbal Consent
Patient verbalizes understanding, has opportunity to ask questions, gives verbal consent

Consensual Touch & Professionalism

Osteopathic manipulative medicine is built on therapeutic touch. This requires:

  • Careful touch — use appropriate force, never cause unnecessary pain
  • Considerate touch — be aware of patient comfort, anxiety, and autonomy
  • Consensual touch — re-confirm consent before each new technique or region
  • Professional demeanor — explain what you are doing before you do it; narrate your findings

Range of Motion — Upper Extremity

📐 NORMAL ROM VALUES

LO 4 — TART & Somatic Dysfunction

Interpreting & Documenting Somatic Dysfunction

TART Criteria

Somatic dysfunction is diagnosed by identifying at least ONE of the four TART findings:

🌡️
T — Tissue Texture Changes
Hypertonia, boggy edema, fibrosis, skin drag
Acute: warm, edematous, tender. Chronic: cool, ropy, fibrotic
🔁
A — Asymmetry
Positional asymmetry compared to contralateral
Posterior radial head, anterior fibular head, innominate rotation
🚫
R — Restriction of Motion
Decreased ROM in one or more planes
Name the direction of FREE motion (osteopathic convention)
😣
T — Tenderness
Pain with palpation at the dysfunctional tissue
Counterstrain tender points, trigger points, enthesopathy

Documenting Somatic Dysfunction

📝 DOCUMENTATION RULE
Name the somatic dysfunction by its position of ease / freedom (the way it is found), NOT the direction of restriction. Example: A radial head that sits posterior and moves freely posteriorly but is restricted anteriorly = "Posterior Radial Head Dysfunction."

LO 5 — Five Osteopathic Models

Five Pathophysiologic Models of Osteopathic Care

The five models provide an integrated framework for understanding why and how OMM works in a given clinical scenario. Use multiple models simultaneously — not in isolation.

🦴

1. Biomechanical Model

Restore normal anatomical position, motion, and function of dysfunctional tissue. Addresses posture, joint mechanics, fascia, muscle imbalance. Applied when: joint restriction, postural imbalance, kinetic chain dysfunction.

🫁

2. Respiratory / Circulatory Model

Enhance venous and lymphatic drainage; improve arterial supply. Respiratory motion drives lymphatic flow via pressure differentials. Applied when: edema, poor wound healing, recurrent infection, post-surgical swelling.

⚡

3. Neurologic Model

Decrease nociceptive input, reduce segmental facilitation and central sensitization, restore autonomic balance. T2–T8 controls sympathetic tone to the arm; T10–L2 to the leg. Applied when: chronic pain, hyperalgesia, CRPS.

⚗️

4. Metabolic / Energetic Model

Optimize energy metabolism through improved posture, economy of motion, and nutrition. Dysfunctional tissue is energetically expensive. Applied when: chronic fatigue, metabolic disease, post-illness deconditioning.

🧠

5. Behavioral / Biopsychosocial Model

Optimize ergonomics, reduce mechanical and psychosocial stressors, optimize nutrition and sleep, rest injured tissue and incorporate graded rehabilitation. Applied when: occupational injury, allostatic load, work-disability.

🔗 APPLYING THE MODELS — KEY CONCEPT
In the CTS case, the dental hygienist has ALL FIVE models at play: Biomechanical (posterior radial head, carpal restriction), Resp/Circ (impaired lymphatic/venous drainage from inlet restriction), Neurologic (T2–8 sympathetic facilitation → vasoconstriction; median nerve nociception → cord facilitation), Metabolic (repetitive strain depletes tissue energy), Behavioral (ergonomic strain at work, allostatic load from inability to work).

The Integrated Osteopathic Impression

💡 CLINICAL INTEGRATION PEARL
In osteopathic thinking, proximal dysfunction drives distal symptoms. In the upper extremity: thoracic outlet → scalenes → first rib restriction → impairs neurovascular flow to the arm → amplifies distal entrapment (CTS). In the lower extremity: sacral/pelvic dysfunction → altered gait mechanics → kinetic chain loading → plantar fascia overload.
LO 6 — OMM Treatment

Treatment of the Appendicular Skeleton

Treatment Principle: Proximal Before Distal

Thoracic Inlet / 1st Rib → Thoracic Spine → Shoulder Girdle → Elbow → Wrist & Hand

Treating proximally first: improves drainage and circulation, reduces upstream nerve tension, decreases facilitation, and makes distal treatment more effective.

Case 1 — Carpal Tunnel Syndrome

Case 1: CTS & Medial Epicondylitis

Chief Complaint

"I've been having numbness in my hand and pain in my elbow for the past few months."

Patient Profile

45-year-old right hand-dominant female dental hygienist. 4 months progressive numbness/tingling/pain in right hand (thumb and index finger) and medial elbow. Worse at night, wakes from sleep. Dropping objects, decreased grip strength. Worse with typing and smartphone use. Gradual onset, no acute trauma.

Key Risk Factors

Repetitive use, female, age >40, ergonomic strain, frequent fine motor use, repetitive wrist flexion/gripping

Key Physical Exam Findings

👁️
Inspection
Thenar atrophy
Chronic median nerve compression → APB muscle wasting
👆
Sensation
Diminished median distribution
Thumb, index, middle, radial half of ring finger
💪
Strength
4/5 grip & thumb abduction
Recurrent branch of median → thenar weakness
✅
Positive Tests
Phalen's, Reverse Phalen's, Tinel's (wrist), Durkan's
All confirm median nerve compression at carpal tunnel
❌
Negative Tests
Spurling's, L'Hermitte's, Adson's
Rules out radiculopathy, myelopathy, and TOS

Osteopathic Structural Exam Findings

OSE FINDINGS
Posture: Anterior head carriage, protracted/rounded shoulders, increased thoracic kyphosis, right forearm held in slight protective flexion.

Head/Neck: OA ESrRl; HTN right paraspinals and scalenes; C5–C7 FSrRr; CT junction restricted all planes.

Thorax: T4 FSrRr, T5–8 NSrRl; increased paraspinal HTN right; R 1st rib inhalation dysfunction; decreased rib excursion right upper thorax.

Upper Extremity: HTN upper trap and lev scap; HTN and fascial restriction forearm flexors; posterior radial head; TP at medial epicondyle; restriction of trapezoid and capitate; increased tension in transverse carpal ligament.

Why Treat the Proximal Structures First?

  1. T2–T8 sympathetic innervation to the upper extremity — facilitation → ↑ pain sensitivity + vasoconstriction. Treatment restores autonomic balance and improves respiratory excursion → better lymphatic/venous drainage.
  2. Median nerve roots C5–T1 — cervical/inlet restriction can irritate or compress the nerve proximally, adding to total nerve tension (double-crush phenomenon).
  3. Thoracic outlet is the terminal lymphatic and venous drainage point for the arm — inlet restriction impairs drainage, worsening edema and inflammation at the carpal tunnel.
  4. Reducing facilitation proximally improves the effectiveness of distal treatment by lowering background sensitization.

Red Flags — When to Reconsider

🚨 RECONSIDER OR REFER
  • Bilateral numbness → cord compression or upper motor neuron disease
  • Neck pain with dermatomal radiation → more likely radicular
  • Systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss, night sweats) → infectious, inflammatory, or neoplastic process
  • Rapidly progressive weakness → EMG/NCS urgently

Case 2: Plantar Fasciitis & Medial Tibial Pain Syndrome

Chief Complaint

"My heel and inner shin have been hurting, especially when I get out of bed."

Patient Profile

38-year-old male recreational runner. 3 months progressive left heel (plantar, sharp, worst first steps AM) and medial shin (aching, diffuse). Increased from 10→25 mi/wk, runs on road, worn-out shoes (300 mi). No acute injury. Relief with rest and ice.

❌

Plantar Fasciitis — Classic Features

⭐ HIGH YIELD TRIAD
  1. First-step morning pain — fascia tightens during sleep; first weight-bearing stretches it acutely
  2. Medial calcaneal tubercle tenderness — plantar fascia origin = diagnostic palpation point
  3. Positive Windlass test — dorsiflexion of great toe tensions the fascia, reproducing pain

How Pes Planus Contributes

Pes Planus → ↑ Pronation → Flattened arch overstretches plantar fascia → Plantar fasciitis
Pes Planus → ↑ Tibial internal rotation → Overstretches deep posterior compartment → Medial tibial pain (shin splints)

How Sacral/Lumbar/Pelvic Dysfunction Contributes

  • Altered gait mechanics → less economy of motion → more tissue strain per step
  • Changed load distribution through the foot → asymmetric calcaneal loading
  • Decreased venous and lymphatic drainage of the lower extremity and pelvis
  • Facilitation → increased pain sensitivity and vasoconstriction in the foot

Osteopathic Structural Exam Findings

OSE FINDINGS
Posture: Anterior pelvic tilt, increased lumbar lordosis, bilateral pes planus, bilateral valgus knees, left leg externally rotated.

Spine/Pelvis: L5 FSlRl; L1–4 NSrRl; L/L sacral torsion; Left anterior innominate, right posterior innominate, left pubic symphysis superior shear.

Lower Extremity: Left hip flexed/externally rotated; HTN posterior tibialis and soleus; left posterior fibular head; anterior talus (talo-calcaneal restriction); cuboid inverted; navicular inferiorly displaced; tight plantar fascia.

Treatment Plan

Conservative / Self-Care

  • Stretching: plantar fascia, gastroc-soleus, hip flexors
  • Icing after activity
  • Rest from running; cross-train
  • New shoes (<300–500 miles); arch support
  • Night splinting (maintains dorsiflexion)
  • NSAIDs sparingly

OMM Treatments

  • CS: Medial/lateral hamstring, medial/lateral ankle, flexed calcaneus
  • ST: Fibularis kneading, popliteal fossa, plantar fascia release, longitudinal arch spring, supine traction
  • ME: Psoas/hamstring/quad imbalance, anterior/posterior fibular head, gastroc/soleus
  • Art: Anterior/posterior fibular head, cuboid dysfunction
  • HVLA: Longitudinal arch, navicular dysfunction, talotibial dysfunction
