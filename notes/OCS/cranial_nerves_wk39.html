Neurologic Terminology

💡
Why these matter All of these terms may appear in exam questions, SOAP notes, or patient encounters. Know the precise definition — especially the paired terms (paresis vs. paralysis, dizziness vs. vertigo, anesthesia vs. paresthesia).
Acuity Keenness or sharpness of sensation — most commonly refers to visual acuity (e.g., 20/20).
Ptosis Drooping of the upper eyelid due to weakness of the levator palpebrae superioris (innervated by CN III).
Nystagmus Involuntary rhythmic eye movements. Jerk nystagmus = fast + slow phases (named for fast phase). Pendular = equal amplitude both directions.
Paralysis / Plegia Complete loss of voluntary muscle function in a region.
Paresis Partial/incomplete loss of motor function; weakness without complete loss.
Tremor Involuntary, relatively rhythmic, purposeless oscillatory muscle movements.
Tic (Habit Spasm) Quick, irregular but repetitive movements or vocalizations; more common in children; can be motor or vocal.
Fasciculation Brief spontaneous flickering of a muscle fasciculus (bundle); visible under the skin; does not typically move joints.
Dizziness Nonspecific symptom describing spinning, faintness, unsteadiness, or weakness — must be further characterized by the patient.
Vertigo False sensation of self or environmental motion (spinning, whirling, tilting, falling) — vestibular system dysfunction.
Pre-syncope Sensation of impending loss of consciousness; lightheadedness, often with diaphoresis and graying of vision; global cerebral hypoperfusion.
Syncope Transient complete loss of consciousness and postural tone from global cerebral hypoperfusion with spontaneous recovery.
Seizure Transient sudden episode of uncontrollable motor activity (focal or generalized), usually with loss of consciousness; from uncontrolled neuronal firing.
Paresthesia Abnormal tingling, burning, or pins-and-needles sensation — sensation without appropriate stimulus.
Anesthesia Loss of sensation (numbness); absence of normal sensory perception in a region.
Atrophy Decrease in muscle bulk due to disuse or denervation.
Hypertrophy Increase in muscle bulk (enlargement of existing fibers, not new cells).
Cephalgia Headache — pain in any part of the head.
Strabismus Misalignment of the eyes; includes esotropia (inward deviation) and exotropia (outward deviation).
Diplopia Double vision — seeing two images of a single object; from EOM palsy (CN III, IV, or VI).

Extraocular Muscles: LR₆(SO₄)AR₃

Mnemonic breakdown

  • LR₆ — Lateral Rectus → CN VI (Abducens)
  • SO₄ — Superior Oblique → CN IV (Trochlear)
  • AR₃ — All the Rest → CN III (Oculomotor)
All the Rest (CN III): Superior rectus, inferior rectus, medial rectus, inferior oblique, levator palpebrae

Cardinal directions & muscles (right eye)

  • Up + lateral → Superior rectus (III)
  • Lateral → Lateral rectus (VI)
  • Down + lateral → Inferior rectus (III)
  • Up + medial → Inferior oblique (III)
  • Medial → Medial rectus (III)
  • Down + medial → Superior oblique (IV)

Obtain informed consent and explain each test in lay terms before beginning. Drape appropriately.

Lay Terminology for Patient Explanation (Consent)

  • "I'm going to check how well you can smell by holding a small item near your nose with your eyes closed."
  • "I'll be checking your eye movements by asking you to follow my finger without moving your head."
  • "I'm going to lightly touch the surface of your eye with a small piece of cotton — this checks a reflex. You may blink automatically."
  • "I'd like you to make several faces for me — raise your eyebrows, close your eyes tight, and smile big. I'll be checking muscle strength."
  • "I'm going to gently touch different parts of your face to see if sensation feels the same on both sides."

Illness Scripts: Headache Syndromes

🧠 Migraine (with or without Aura)

Epidemiology & Risk

  • Peak 25–55 years; F > M (3:1); strong family history
  • Triggers: hormones, stress, sleep deprivation, certain foods, EtOH, weather
  • Strong genetic component; often positive FHx

Symptoms

  • Unilateral, pulsating/throbbing headache
  • Moderate to severe intensity
  • Duration 4–72 hours if untreated
  • Nausea/vomiting common
  • Photophobia AND phonophobia
  • Aura (in ~30%): transient focal neuro sx, often visual (scintillating scotoma, zigzag lines) lasting ~20 min before headache

Signs on Exam

  • Neuro exam typically NORMAL between episodes
  • Patient may appear pale, uncomfortable, prefer dark quiet room
  • CN exam normal (vs. CN III palsy — think cluster/PComm aneurysm)

Key Differentiators

  • UNILATERAL pulsating (vs. bilateral pressure of tension)
  • N/V + photo/phonophobia (vs. GCA with jaw claudication)
  • Aura (vs. no aura in tension)
  • Does NOT have autonomic features (vs. cluster HA)
🚨 Red flags: "Thunderclap" onset, fever + meningismus, neuro deficits, vision loss → urgent workup
💢 Tension-Type Headache (TTH)

Epidemiology & Risk

  • Most common primary headache disorder
  • Stress, poor sleep, eye strain, dehydration, musculoskeletal tension
  • Associated with pericranial muscle tenderness

Symptoms

  • BILATERAL, pressure/tightening ("band around head")
  • Mild to moderate intensity
  • Duration 30 min – 7 days
  • No significant nausea or vomiting
  • Mild photo OR phonophobia (NOT both)
  • NOT aggravated by routine physical activity

Signs on Exam

  • Pericranial muscle tenderness on palpation
  • Normal neurologic exam
  • Normal visual fields, EOMs, CN exam

Key Differentiators

  • BILATERAL (vs. unilateral migraine/cluster)
  • No N/V, no autonomic features
  • NO aura
  • Not disabling (can continue activity)
OMT pearl: suboccipital release, OA decompression often therapeutic
⚠️ Giant Cell Arteritis (Temporal Arteritis)

Epidemiology & Risk

  • Age >50 (typically >70); F > M
  • Granulomatous vasculitis of medium/large vessels
  • Associated with polymyalgia rheumatica
  • Elevated ESR (>50mm/hr), CRP

Symptoms

  • New unilateral temporal headache, severe
  • Jaw claudication — pain/fatigue with chewing that stops at rest ← HIGH SPECIFICITY
  • Scalp tenderness (tender temporal artery)
  • Systemic: fever, malaise, weight loss, fatigue
  • Visual symptoms: amaurosis fugax → sudden painless permanent monocular blindness if untreated

Signs on Exam

  • Tender, nodular, thickened, non-pulsatile temporal artery
  • Elevated ESR/CRP; confirm with temporal artery biopsy
  • CN II affected: ischemic optic neuropathy

Key Differentiators

  • Age >50 + jaw claudication = GCA until proven otherwise
  • Elevated inflammatory markers
  • Vision loss risk — ophthalmic emergency
🚨 TREAT IMMEDIATELY with high-dose steroids before biopsy results to prevent bilateral blindness!
🦷 TMJ Dysfunction (Temporomandibular Joint Disorder)

Epidemiology & Risk

  • Typically 20–40 years; F slightly > M
  • Risk: bruxism, malocclusion, stress, trauma, arthritis
  • Can involve muscles of mastication (CN V3) or joint itself

Symptoms

  • Jaw pain, especially with chewing or wide mouth opening
  • Clicking, popping, or crepitus with jaw movement
  • Limited mouth opening or deviation of jaw on opening
  • Referred pain to temple, ear, neck (may mimic headache)
  • Ear fullness or tinnitus

Signs on Exam

  • Tenderness on palpation of TMJ and muscles of mastication (masseter, temporalis)
  • Palpable click or pop on jaw opening
  • Restricted or deviated mandibular opening
  • Normal CN exam (no facial weakness, normal corneal reflex)

Key Differentiators

  • Jaw pain with chewing (vs. GCA jaw claudication — both jaw pain but different character, age, systemic sx)
  • Clicking/mechanical symptoms
  • Young patient, no systemic signs
  • No elevated ESR, no vision loss risk

Subjective History for CN Complaint

🗣️
Patient-Centered Language Use open-ended questions first ("Tell me more about what you're experiencing"). Avoid leading questions. Acknowledge patient emotions. Use lay terms to explain ("double vision" rather than "diplopia" when speaking to the patient).

HPI — OPQRST + Modifying Factors for CN Complaints

OnsetWhen did it start? Sudden vs. gradual? Any precipitating event (trauma, illness)?
Provocation/PalliationWhat makes it better or worse? Activity, rest, position, medications, food?
QualityDescribe it in your own words. Burning, pressure, throbbing, sharp, electric?
RadiationDoes it spread anywhere? Jaw, neck, behind the eye, down the arm?
SeverityOn a scale of 0–10, how would you rate it? How does it affect daily activities?
TimingConstant vs. episodic? Duration of episodes? Frequency? Time of day? Progressive?
Associated SxN/V, photophobia, phonophobia, aura, vision changes, hearing changes, facial drooping, dizziness?
ContextSick contacts, travel, recent illness, stress, sleep, hydration, medications?

PMHx

  • Prior headache diagnoses or neurologic conditions
  • HTN, DM, afib (stroke risk)
  • Cancer history (metastasis/increased ICP)
  • Autoimmune disease (vasculitis, MS)
  • Prior head trauma or surgeries
  • HIV/immunosuppression (opportunistic CNS)

PSHx / FHx / SHx

  • PSHx: Head/neck/orbital surgeries; prior CNS procedures
  • FHx: Migraine (strong genetic link); aneurysm; GCA; neuro conditions
  • SHx: Tobacco (vascular risk); alcohol; recreational drugs; occupational exposures; stress; travel; sexual Hx if relevant

Medications & Allergies

  • List ALL medications including OTC, supplements, herbals
  • Hormones/OCPs (migraine trigger, stroke risk)
  • Steroids (consider for GCA)
  • Anticoagulants (bleeding risk)
  • Medication overuse? (rebound HA)
  • Document ALL allergies + reaction type (NKDA = No Known Drug Allergies)

Review of Systems (ROS) for CN Complaints

🔍
ROS Rationale The goal is neuroanatomical localization — use the anatomy of the CN to determine which systems to screen. A chief complaint of diplopia drives you to ask about vascular, neuro, MSK. A complaint of jaw pain should prompt screening for constitutional sx (GCA) and dental/GI.

📝
Total Neurologic ROS = 23 elements Highlighted CN-related items: anosmia, loss/change of vision, diplopia, facial pain, facial weakness/asymmetry, change in hearing, change in taste, dysphagia, dysarthria. These directly map to CN pathology and must be asked when the chief complaint involves any cranial nerve system.

Rationale for System Selection

For a chief complaint involving CN (e.g., diplopia, facial droop, headache), the physical exam must address systems that could produce or explain the CN deficit. The anatomy drives the rationale.

Visual Field Defects — Localization

  • Monocular blindness → ipsilateral optic nerve (CN II) lesion anterior to chiasm
  • Bitemporal hemianopsia → optic chiasm (crossing nasal fibers) — think pituitary adenoma!
  • Homonymous hemianopsia (right or left) → optic tract (contralateral side)
  • Quadrantanopsia (upper or lower) → optic radiation (parietal vs. temporal lobe)
💡
Memory tip Pituitary tumors compress the chiasm from below, damaging the crossing nasal fibers → bitemporal hemianopsia (you lose the outer/temporal vision in both eyes)

Normal Exam Documentation

Dr. Filippakis' Normal Neurological Exam Template

Mental Status: Awake, alert, attentive, oriented to self, location, and date. Speech is fluent and spontaneous without aphasia, dysarthria, or paraphasic errors. Memory intact to recent events. Follows complex commands.

Cranial Nerves: Pupils equal and reactive. Optic discs sharp without pallor or papilledema. No APD or red desaturation. Visual fields are full to confrontation. VA is *** OD and *** OS. Extraocular movements are intact. Sensation intact to light touch in V1/V2/V3. Face symmetric with activation. Hearing intact to finger rub. Palate elevates symmetrically. Tongue protrudes midline and has 5/5 strength. Trapezius and SCM 5/5 strength bilaterally.

Motor: Normal bulk and tone. No adventitious movements. Strength is 5/5 throughout, proximally and distally. DTRs are 2+/4 throughout. No sustained ankle clonus. Plantar response is flexor bilaterally.

Sensory: Intact to temperature and vibration throughout. No sensory level. Negative Romberg.

Coordination: FTN/HTS intact without ataxia or dysmetria.

Gait: Steady and narrow-based, tandem gait performed without difficulty.
📝
Documenting Abnormal Findings Be specific: state which CN, which eye/side, what deficit was found, and what was intact. Example: "CN VI (abducens) — right eye esotropia at rest with inability to track laterally to the right. CN II, VII, VIII, IX, X, XII intact to observation and testing."

Differential Diagnosis Framework

Diplopia / EOM Palsy

CN III Palsy

  • Posterior communicating artery aneurysm (pupil-involving!)
  • Diabetic CN III infarct (pupil-sparing)
  • Uncal herniation (compression)

CN IV Palsy

  • Head trauma (most common cause of isolated CN IV)
  • Microvascular disease (DM, HTN)
  • Congenital

CN VI Palsy

  • Most common isolated EOM palsy
  • Increased ICP (false localizing sign)
  • Microvascular (DM, HTN); petrous ridge tumor
  • Trauma (in children)

Facial Nerve (CN VII) Palsy

Lower Motor Neuron (ALL face involved)

  • Bell's palsy (idiopathic, HSV reactivation)
  • Ramsay Hunt syndrome (VZV reactivation — + vesicles + ear pain)
  • Lyme disease (bilateral possible)
  • Trauma, tumor (parotid), sarcoidosis

Upper Motor Neuron (forehead SPARED)

  • Stroke (contralateral cortex or subcortex)
  • Tumor, MS, hemorrhage

Vertigo

Peripheral (CN VIII / inner ear)

  • BPPV — positional, brief, Dix-Hallpike positive
  • Vestibular neuritis — sudden severe, viral
  • Menière's disease — triad: vertigo + SNHL + tinnitus
  • Ramsay Hunt (CN VIII involvement)

Central (brainstem / cerebellum)

  • Posterior circulation stroke (AICA, PICA)
  • Cerebellar tumor
  • Multiple sclerosis

Dysphagia / Hoarseness

  • CN X palsy — lung/mediastinal tumor, aortic aneurysm
  • Bulbar palsy (ALS, MS, GBS)
  • Posterior fossa stroke (Wallenberg = PICA)
  • Myasthenia gravis — fatigable CN IX/X weakness
  • Jugular foramen syndrome (CN IX, X, XI)

SOAP Note & 5-Part Medical Plan

S
SUBJECTIVE
  • Chief complaint (CC) in patient's own words
  • HPI: OPQRST + associated symptoms
  • PMHx, PSHx, FHx, SHx
  • Medications & Allergies
  • ROS: General + relevant systems (see table above)
O
OBJECTIVE
  • Vital signs (T, HR, RR, BP, O₂ sat, Ht/Wt)
  • General appearance (NAD, distress level)
  • Relevant PE systems (HEENT, Neuro, Neck, CV, Resp, MSK)
  • Full CN exam with findings documented
  • OSE findings (TART: Tissue texture, Asymmetry, Restriction, Tenderness)
A
ASSESSMENT
  • Primary diagnosis with ICD code
  • Differential diagnosis (rank by likelihood)
  • Somatic dysfunction diagnosis (if present)
  • Synthesize S + O to justify diagnosis
P
PLAN (5 Parts)
  1. Diagnostics — labs (ESR/CRP for GCA, MRI/CT for acute neuro, MRA for aneurysm, LP if needed), imaging, referrals for testing
  2. Therapeutics / Referrals — medications (triptans for migraine, steroids for GCA), referral to neurology/ophthalmology/ENT/vascular surgery as indicated
  3. OMT — osteopathic manipulative treatment techniques; document technique, region, and goal (e.g., OA decompression, suboccipital release, cervical myofascial release)
  4. Patient Education — explain diagnosis, red flags to return for, medication side effects, lifestyle modifications (triggers, sleep hygiene, hydration)
  5. Follow-up — specific timeframe, what to monitor, criteria for urgent return (sudden vision loss, worst headache of life, new neuro deficits)

OSE Documentation — Somatic Dysfunction

Use TART criteria. Document the region, then the diagnosis using standard osteopathic nomenclature.

Example: "OA: ESrRl (occipitoatlantal joint, extended, sidebent right, rotated left). Thoracic inlet: sidebent right, rotated right. Temporal bone: right external rotation."

TART: T = Tissue texture change (boggy/ropy), A = Asymmetry, R = Restriction of motion, T = Tenderness

Osteopathic Considerations for CN Disorders

Biomechanical Model

Bone structures to assess:

  • Sphenobasilar synchondrosis (SBS)
  • Cranial base; OA joint; upper cervical (C1–C3)

Muscles to assess:

  • Muscles of mastication (CN V3)
  • Facial muscles (CN VII)
  • Suboccipital, SCM, trapezius, scalenes
  • Suprahyoid/infrahyoid muscles

Neurologic Model

Facilitation / viscerosomatic reflexes:

  • Sympathetic: T1–T4 (screen for TART)
  • Parasympathetics: OA, AA (vagal tone, CN IX/X)

Cranial field considerations:

  • OA/C-spine dysfunction → alters foraminal spaces → affects CN traversal
  • Temporal bone dysfunction → CN VII, CN VIII
  • Jugular foramen restriction → CN IX, X, XI
  • Fascial strain patterns may restrict CN pathways

Common OMT Applications

  • Headache: suboccipital release, OA decompression, cervical myofascial release, craniosacral therapy
  • Vertigo/tinnitus: temporal bone techniques, OA balanced ligamentous tension
  • Facial pain: V5 technique, maxillary technique, TMJ soft tissue
  • Dysphagia/dysphonia: hyoid bone release, thoracic inlet technique
  • Respiratory/circulatory: thoracic inlet, CT junction assessment

No cervical HVLA for patients with history of stroke or carotid stenosis
