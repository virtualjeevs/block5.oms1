Neurologic Terminology — Week 40

💡
Exam priority Know the bold terms precisely — they distinguish cerebellar disease from basal ganglia disease from peripheral neuropathy on OSCE and boards.
Nystagmus Involuntary rhythmic eye movements. Jerk type = fast + slow phases (named for fast direction). Can be caused by vestibular or cerebellar dysfunction.
Dysarthria Disturbance of speech (slow, ataxic, slurred, jerky) from incoordination of the muscles of phonation. Can result from cerebellar, CN, or upper motor neuron pathology.
Hypotonia Muscle flaccidity — reduced tension or loss of muscle tone, decreased resistance to passive movement. Classic sign of cerebellar dysfunction (cerebellum maintains tone).
Ataxia "Without order" — loss of control of coordinated voluntary movement. Medium-large amplitude involuntary oscillatory movements superimposed on intended movement trajectory.
Dysdiadochokinesis Inability to perform rapid alternating movements (RAM). Agonist/antagonist switching is slow, irregular, and clumsy. Classic sign of cerebellar disease.
Dysmetria Errors in judging distance, speed, power, and direction of movement → under- or overshooting of target. Tested by FTN and HTS. Typically cerebellar.
Dyssynergia Decomposition of movement — jerky, erratic, awkward, disorganized execution. The essential disturbance in cerebellar disease; movements lose their smooth synergy.
Tremor Involuntary, relatively rhythmic, purposeless, oscillatory muscle movements. Classified by when they occur: resting, postural, or intention (see tremor section).
Tic (Habit Spasm) Quick, irregular but repetitive movements (motor tic) or utterances (vocal tic); seemingly purposeful acts. More common in children. Often worsened by stress.
Fasciculation Brief, spontaneous flickering of a muscle fasciculus (bundle). Visible under skin with oblique lighting. Suggests LMN denervation (e.g., ALS, radiculopathy).
Paralysis / Plegia Complete loss of voluntary muscle function. Paresis = partial/incomplete loss (weakness). "-plegia" = complete; "-paresis" = partial. (Review from Wk 39).
Bradykinesia Slowness of movement; a cardinal feature of Parkinson disease from basal ganglia dopaminergic loss. Distinguished from weakness — force is intact, speed is not.
Hypomimia "Masked face" — facial immobility and reduction of normal facial expressiveness. Classic Parkinson disease finding; basal ganglia lesion reduces automatic facial movement.
Micrographia Abnormal reduction in handwriting size; letters may be consistently small or progressively diminish during the writing task. A hallmark of Parkinson disease.
Festination Tendency to walk with increasingly rapid but progressively smaller steps; center of gravity falls forward over the stepping feet. Parkinson disease gait pattern.
Cogwheel Rigidity Jerky quality to increased muscle tone; passive movement meets rhythmic "catches" as if attached to a heavy cogwheel. Pathognomonic of Parkinson disease.

The Coordination Network

🧠
Key concept Coordination = "awkwardness of movement" — NOT weakness. Coordination requires four integrated systems. A lesion in any one causes a distinct pattern of incoordination.
⚡ Motor System (Output)
Carries out voluntary movement; provides the "efferent action." Damage to the corticospinal tract (pyramidal) → weakness. Damage to basal ganglia → rigidity, bradykinesia, involuntary movements.
🌊 Vestibular System (Input)
Balance, and coordination of eye, head, and body movements. Dysfunction → vertigo, disequilibrium, vestibular nystagmus. Integrated with CN VIII input and cerebellar output.
👁️ Sensory System (Input)
Proprioception (joint position sense) and vibration travel via dorsal columns. Loss → sensory ataxia: worsens dramatically with eyes closed (Romberg positive). "Searching" oscillatory movement.
🔄 Cerebellar System (Integration)
Integrates motor + sensory + vestibular input; modulates response to produce smooth, rhythmic movement and steady posture. Damage → cerebellar ataxia: clumsy regardless of eyes open/closed, + nystagmus, dysarthria, hypotonia.

Two Primary Coordination Pathways: Lesion Localization

Intended/Voluntary Movement → Cerebellum

  • Awkwardness during active movement (reaching, walking)
  • Examples: intention tremor, ataxia, dysmetria, dyssynergia
  • Wide-based gait, dysdiadochokinesis
  • Abnormalities persist whether eyes open or closed

Involuntary Movement (At Rest) → Basal Ganglia

  • Abnormal movements occurring without voluntary intent
  • Examples: resting tremor, chorea, athetosis, hemiballismus
  • Parkinson disease: pill-rolling tremor, bradykinesia, cogwheel rigidity
  • Huntington disease: chorea (dance-like writhing movements)
⚠️
Critical distinction: Cerebellar lesions are ipsilateral (cerebellum does not cross); corticospinal and sensory tracts are contralateral to the lesion above their decussation.

Differentiating Tremors

Resting (Static) Tremor
When: At rest; decreases with movement
Most prominent when the affected part is fully supported and relaxed.

Classic example: "Pill-rolling" tremor of Parkinson disease (~5 Hz). Thumb rolls across fingertips.

Lesion: Basal ganglia (dopaminergic neurons in substantia nigra).

Associated: Bradykinesia, cogwheel rigidity, hypomimia, micrographia, festination.
Postural Tremor
When: Maintaining a posture against gravity
Appears when the affected part is held actively against gravity (e.g., hands outstretched).

Classic examples:
  • Essential (benign familial) tremor — most common movement disorder; bilateral; may improve with alcohol; no Parkinson features
  • Hyperthyroidism tremor
  • Anxiety/fatigue-related tremor
  • Medication-induced (e.g., lithium, valproate)
Lesion: Various (often no discrete single lesion; enhanced physiological tremor or essential tremor networks).
Intention Tremor
When: During movement; worsens near target
Absent at rest; appears with voluntary movement and characteristically worsens as the limb approaches its target.

Classic examples:
  • Cerebellar disease (most common cause)
  • Multiple sclerosis (demyelination of cerebellar tracts)
  • Posterior fossa stroke
Lesion: Cerebellum or its related tracts (dentate nucleus, superior cerebellar peduncle).

Associated: Dysmetria, dysdiadochokinesis, wide-based gait, nystagmus.

Coordination Physical Exam

📋
Remember "The Big Five" Coordination falls under category 4 (sensory system and coordination) and category 5 (gait and station) of the neurologic exam. Always explain each test in lay terms and obtain consent before performing.

RAM abnormalities from agonist/antagonist switching failure

Interpreting Ataxia Type — Movement Pattern

Sensory Ataxia

  • "Searching" movement — limb approaches target with meandering oscillation
  • Dramatically WORSE with eyes closed (Romberg positive)
  • Positive: loss of proprioception / vibration
  • Steppage gait (foot stomping); decreased reflexes
  • Causes: dorsal column disease, peripheral neuropathy (B12 deficiency, DM, tabes dorsalis)

Cerebellar Ataxia

  • Oscillatory movement perpendicular to plane of movement (side to side)
  • Worsens as limb approaches target; NOT significantly changed by eye closure
  • Associated: nystagmus, dysarthria, hypotonia, dysdiadochokinesis, dysmetria
  • Appendicular: extremity ataxia. Truncal: gait instability
  • Causes: cerebellar stroke, MS, spinocerebellar ataxia, chronic EtOH

Lay Terminology for Patient Explanation (Consent Examples)

  • "I'd like to check your coordination by having you touch my moving finger and then touch your nose alternately several times — almost like a game of 'follow the target.'"
  • "I'm going to ask you to flip your hand back and forth on your leg as fast as you comfortably can. This helps me check how well your brain and muscles are coordinating."
  • "Please place your heel on your opposite knee and slide it down your shin to your big toe. I'll be watching for how smoothly and accurately you can do this."
  • "I'm going to ask you to stand up from the chair without using your hands — cross your arms across your chest for me. I just want to watch how you get up from sitting."
  • "I'll ask you to walk normally, then walk heel to toe in a straight line — like walking a tightrope. Then I'd like to see you walk on your toes, then on your heels."

Gait, Stance & Romberg Sign

Gait Assessment — Step by Step

  1. Rise from chair without hands — difficulty suggests proximal hip extensor weakness (extensors of hip) or quadriceps weakness (extensor of knee)
  2. Baseline walk — walk across room and back; observe posture, base width, arm swing, stride length, turning. Normally: symmetric arm swing, smooth turns
  3. Tandem (heel-to-toe) walk — may reveal ataxia not obvious in baseline; sensitive for subtle cerebellar dysfunction
  4. Toe walking — tests plantarflexion strength (S1 myotome, gastrocnemius) and balance
  5. Heel walking — tests dorsiflexion strength (L4/L5 myotome); inability = sensitive test for corticospinal tract damage

Romberg Sign

How to Perform

  • Patient stands with feet together, arms at sides (or crossed)
  • Observe for 30–60 seconds with eyes open (should be stable)
  • Ask patient to close eyes; observe for imbalance
  • Stand close to catch patient if they fall

Interpretation

  • Positive Romberg = marked worsening of balance with eyes closed → suggests loss of proprioception or vestibular dysfunction (sensory ataxia)
  • Key principle: patient uses vision to compensate for proprioception loss; removing visual cue (closing eyes) exposes the deficit
  • Patient with severe cerebellar dysfunction cannot stand even with eyes open (this is NOT a positive Romberg — Romberg specifically tests proprioception)
⚠️
Romberg ≠ cerebellar test! Romberg tests proprioception. Cerebellar patients may be unable to maintain stance even with eyes open — this is a different finding. Don't confuse them.
💡
Documentation example: "Negative Romberg" (normal) vs. "Positive Romberg with notable sway and near-fall upon eye closure, consistent with sensory ataxia."

Illness Scripts: Week 40

🤧 Sinusitis (Acute Rhinosinusitis)

Epidemiology & Risk

  • Extremely common; most cases viral (rhinovirus)
  • Acute <4 weeks; subacute 4–12 weeks; chronic >12 weeks
  • Risk: allergic rhinitis, nasal polyps, immune suppression, anatomic variants (deviated septum), recent URI
  • Bacterial superinfection: S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae, M. catarrhalis

Symptoms

  • Facial pain, pressure, or fullness over affected sinus
  • Maxillary: cheek and upper teeth pain
  • Frontal: forehead pain, worse leaning forward
  • Nasal congestion; purulent (yellow-green) nasal discharge
  • Post-nasal drip; hyposmia/anosmia (CN I)
  • Fever, malaise, fatigue; headache

Signs on Exam

  • Tenderness on direct palpation over sinuses (maxillary/frontal)
  • Nasal turbinate erythema and edema
  • Purulent drainage visible in nasal cavity or posterior pharynx
  • Transillumination of sinuses reduced (clinical adjunct)

Key Differentiators

  • Facial pain + purulent discharge + worsening after initial URI improvement = bacterial sinusitis (PODS: Persistent >10d, Onset of severe symptoms, Double sickening)
  • No vesicular rash (vs. HZO), no electric shock quality (vs. TN), no facial motor weakness (vs. Bell's)
🚨 Red flags: periorbital edema/erythema (orbital complication), meningismus, severe headache → CT urgently
⚡ Trigeminal Neuralgia (Tic Douloureux)

Epidemiology & Risk

  • Age >50 (peak 60–70); F > M; often unilateral
  • Most common: vascular compression of CN V root (superior cerebellar artery)
  • Secondary causes: MS (demyelination), tumor, structural compression

Symptoms

  • Paroxysmal, electric shock-like or stabbing facial pain
  • Extremely severe; lasts seconds (up to 2 min)
  • V2 (cheek) and V3 (jaw) most commonly affected; V1 rare
  • Triggered by light touch: eating, chewing, talking, toothbrushing, wind on face, even a light breeze
  • Pain-free intervals between attacks
  • No sensory deficit or motor weakness (distinguishes from other CN V lesions)

Signs on Exam

  • Neurologic exam typically NORMAL between episodes
  • No facial numbness (sensory intact)
  • No jaw weakness (motor intact)
  • May trigger during exam with light touch to trigger zone

Key Differentiators

  • PAROXYSMAL (seconds) — not constant like sinusitis
  • TRIGGERED by light touch — not by head movement
  • Normal neuro exam — no facial weakness, no rash
  • No vesicles (vs. HZO); no facial paralysis (vs. Bell's)
Treatment: Carbamazepine (first-line); microvascular decompression surgery for refractory cases
😐 Bell's Palsy (Idiopathic Peripheral CN VII Palsy)

Epidemiology & Risk

  • Most common cause of peripheral facial paralysis; any age
  • Presumed HSV-1 reactivation in the geniculate ganglion of CN VII
  • Risk: pregnancy, immunosuppression, diabetes, recent viral illness
  • Acute onset; often maximal within 48–72 hours

Symptoms

  • Unilateral facial weakness involving ALL muscles — forehead AND lower face (key: LMN = all face)
  • Cannot close the eye completely (lagophthalmos) → corneal drying/abrasion
  • Pain behind or around the ear (otalgia) — often precedes palsy
  • Altered taste (anterior 2/3 tongue — chorda tympani via CN VII)
  • Hyperacusis (CN VII innervates stapedius muscle — dampens loud sounds)
  • Decreased lacrimation; drooling

Signs on Exam

  • ALL ipsilateral facial muscles weak — forehead INCLUDED (vs. central/UMN which spares forehead)
  • Cannot raise eyebrow; cannot close eye (Bell's phenomenon: eye rolls up on attempted closure)
  • No vesicles in ear (vs. Ramsay Hunt which = Bell's + VZV + vesicles)
  • Normal strength and sensation otherwise

Key Differentiators

  • LMN: all face involved including forehead ← HIGH YIELD
  • UMN (stroke): forehead SPARED (bilateral cortical representation of frontalis)
  • No vesicles in EAC → Bell's; vesicles present → Ramsay Hunt
🚨 Eye care essential: lubricating drops, protective glasses, taping eyelid closed at night to prevent corneal damage
👁️ Herpes Zoster Ophthalmicus (HZO)

Epidemiology & Risk

  • VZV reactivation in the CN V1 (ophthalmic) branch of the trigeminal ganglion
  • Age >50; immunosuppressed patients at higher risk
  • 10–20% of all herpes zoster cases affect V1

Symptoms

  • Prodrome: burning, tingling, or pain in forehead/periorbital region 1–5 days before rash
  • Unilateral vesicular rash in the V1 distribution: forehead, scalp, periorbital region, upper eyelid
  • Eye involvement: red, painful eye; decreased vision; photophobia
  • Pain often severe, out of proportion to rash appearance
  • Constitutional symptoms: fever, malaise

Signs on Exam

  • Vesicular/crusted rash strictly following V1 distribution — does NOT cross midline
  • Hutchinson's sign: vesicles on tip, side, or root of nose → nasociliary branch of V1 → high risk for ocular involvement (corneal/iris)
  • Conjunctival injection, corneal staining with fluorescein (keratitis)
  • Decreased corneal sensation (V1 afferent of corneal reflex)
  • CN II/III involvement possible in severe cases

Key Differentiators

  • Vesicular rash in V1 distribution + ocular pain = HZO until proven otherwise
  • Hutchinson's sign = immediate ophthalmology referral
  • No facial motor weakness (CN VII intact — vs. Ramsay Hunt which also has facial palsy + CN VIII involvement)
🚨 Ophthalmology emergency: can cause keratitis, uveitis, retinal necrosis, vision loss. Start antivirals immediately (acyclovir/valacyclovir).

Subjective History for Coordination Complaint

🗣️
Patient-Centered Approach Open-ended first: "Tell me more about the shaking/coordination difficulty." Avoid leading. Use the patient's language. Acknowledge functional impact: "How has this affected your daily activities?"

PMHx

  • Prior neurologic diagnoses (Parkinson, MS, stroke, cerebellar disease)
  • DM (peripheral neuropathy → sensory ataxia)
  • Thyroid disease (hyperthyroid tremor)
  • Autoimmune disease (MS, vasculitis)
  • Cancer history (paraneoplastic cerebellar degeneration)
  • Chronic liver disease (hepatic encephalopathy, Wilson disease)

PSHx / FHx / SHx

  • PSHx: CNS surgery, prior trauma; deep brain stimulation
  • FHx: Essential tremor (autosomal dominant), Parkinson disease, Huntington disease, spinocerebellar ataxia (many genetic), Wilson disease
  • SHx: Alcohol (cerebellar degeneration with chronic use); heavy metals (mercury — Minamata disease, manganese — welders); occupational exposures; recreational drugs

Medications & Allergies

  • Anticonvulsants (phenytoin, carbamazepine — cerebellar toxicity at high levels)
  • Antipsychotics / metoclopramide (drug-induced parkinsonism, tardive dyskinesia)
  • Lithium, valproate — tremor
  • Aminoglycosides — vestibular/cerebellar toxicity
  • Amiodarone — tremor, peripheral neuropathy
  • Always ask about herbals, supplements, OTCs

Normal Coordination Exam Documentation

Dr. Filippakis' Normal Neurological Exam Template (Coordination-Relevant)

Mental Status: Awake, alert, attentive, oriented to self, location, and date. Speech is fluent and spontaneous without aphasia, dysarthria, or paraphasic errors. Memory intact to recent events. Follows complex commands.

Cranial Nerves: Pupils equal and reactive. Visual fields full. VA *** OD and *** OS. Extraocular movements intact without nystagmus. Sensation intact to light touch V1/V2/V3. Differential Diagnosis — Coordination Complaints

Cerebellar Disorders

  • Acute: cerebellar stroke (PICA/AICA), Wernicke encephalopathy (thiamine deficiency), medication toxicity (phenytoin, alcohol)
  • Subacute: MS (demyelinating plaques), paraneoplastic cerebellar degeneration (anti-Yo, anti-Hu), alcohol-related degeneration
  • Chronic/progressive: Spinocerebellar ataxias (SCA 1, 2, 3 — autosomal dominant), Friedreich ataxia (autosomal recessive, onset <25y, loss of proprioception + cerebellar)

Basal Ganglia / Movement Disorders

  • Parkinson disease: resting tremor + bradykinesia + rigidity + postural instability (TRAP)
  • Drug-induced parkinsonism: antipsychotics, metoclopramide — reversible
  • Essential tremor: postural; bilateral; familial; improved by EtOH; no TRAP features
  • Huntington disease: autosomal dominant; chorea + cognitive decline + psychiatric
  • Wilson disease: autosomal recessive; liver + neuropsychiatric + KF rings; treatable

Sensory Ataxia

  • Dorsal column disease: B12 deficiency (subacute combined degeneration), tabes dorsalis (syphilis)
  • Peripheral neuropathy: DM, chronic EtOH, B12 deficiency, chemotherapy-induced
  • Friedreich ataxia (mixed cerebellar + sensory)
  • Key finding: Romberg positive; DTRs reduced/absent; loss of vibration and proprioception

Other Coordination DDx

  • Vestibular dysfunction: BPPV, vestibular neuritis (unsteadiness, vertigo-predominant)
  • Corticospinal tract: weakness mimicking coordination issue — test for spasticity and hyperreflexia
  • Functional/psychogenic: variable, inconsistent pattern; relieved by distraction; usually younger patients
  • Toxin/medication: EtOH intoxication, heavy metal poisoning, drug toxicity

SOAP Note & 5-Part Plan — Coordination

S
SUBJECTIVE
  • CC: patient's own words ("my hand shakes")
  • HPI: OPQRST — onset, quality (rest vs. movement), which limb, progression
  • PMHx, PSHx, FHx (essential tremor? Parkinson? SCA?)
  • SHx: alcohol, toxin exposures, occupational
  • Medications (drug-induced tremor/parkinsonism)
  • ROS: all systems — especially neuro (TRAP features, sensory sx, falls)
O
OBJECTIVE
  • Vital signs + BMI; orthostatic blood pressures if autonomic dysfunction suspected
  • General: appearance, affect, gait observed entering room
  • Neurologic: full exam including CN, motor (bulk/tone/strength), DTRs, sensory, coordination (FTN/HTS/RAM), gait, Romberg
  • Specific coordination documentation: which limbs, severity, whether eyes-open vs. eyes-closed changes finding
  • OSE with TART findings
A
ASSESSMENT
  • Primary diagnosis with justification from S+O
  • DDx ranked by likelihood with distinguishing features
  • Example: "Right-sided resting tremor with bradykinesia, cogwheel rigidity, hypomimia, and festinating gait — most consistent with Parkinson disease. DDx includes essential tremor (absence of bradykinesia/rigidity argues against), drug-induced parkinsonism (no offending agent identified)."
  • Somatic dysfunction diagnosis (e.g., C4 ERSl; thoracic inlet SlRr)
P
PLAN (5 Parts)
  1. Diagnostics — Brain MRI (rule out structural, demyelination), lab work (B12, TFTs, copper/ceruloplasmin for Wilson, metabolic panel, ESR/CRP if inflammatory), DAT scan (dopamine transporter SPECT for Parkinson confirmation)
  2. Therapeutics / Referrals — Neurology referral; pharmacotherapy per diagnosis (levodopa/carbidopa for Parkinson; propranolol/primidone for essential tremor; carbamazepine for TN); physical/occupational therapy
  3. OMT — Cervical and thoracic somatic dysfunction treatment; OA decompression; myofascial release of suboccipital and upper cervical muscles; address somatic dysfunction found on OSE
  4. Patient Education — Explain diagnosis and prognosis; fall prevention strategies; medication expectations; support resources (Parkinson Foundation if applicable); driving safety discussion; occupational modifications
  5. Follow-up — Specify timeframe; monitor medication response; assess for disease progression; return precautions (sudden worsening, new falls, dysphagia, acute cognitive change)

Osteopathic Considerations

Biomechanical Model

  • Upper cervical (OA, AA, C2-C3): proprioceptive afferents → affects vestibular and cerebellar integration
  • Thoracic inlet dysfunction → affects upper extremity nerve root conduction and vascular flow
  • Kyphotic posture (common in Parkinson) → secondary C4/thoracic somatic dysfunction
  • Sacral somatic dysfunction may affect lower extremity proprioception pathways
  • From the case: C4 ERSl, Thoracic inlet SlRr, mildly increased thoracic kyphosis, L3-5 NSlRr, Sacrum right-on-right torsion

Neurologic & Circulatory Models

  • Sympathetics T1–T4: facilitation may contribute to upper extremity tone and tremor
  • OA/AA dysfunction: affects posterior fossa circulation (vertebral artery) → cerebellar perfusion
  • Thoracic inlet: impacts jugular venous drainage → can elevate intracranial venous pressure
  • Vagal tone affected by OA/AA somatic dysfunction

OMT Applications for Coordination Disorders

  • OA decompression, suboccipital inhibition: improve posterior fossa circulation and proprioceptive input to cerebellum
  • Cervical myofascial release: address rigidity and neck tension (Parkinson)
  • Thoracic kyphosis treatment: muscle energy / HVLA (if appropriate) or indirect techniques
  • Thoracic inlet technique: improve upper extremity lymphatic/venous drainage
  • Craniosacral therapy: address rhythmic palpatory findings
  • Note: No HVLA in patients with severe osteoporosis, vertebral artery disease, advanced Parkinson instability
