W42 core frame

What this week is really testing

For this block, think in three layers: (1) take a strong, compassionate subjective history, (2) perform and describe a normal screening eye exam, and (3) recognize the big abnormal patterns for conjunctivitis, macular degeneration, glaucoma, cataract, and a few retinal/optic nerve findings.

High-yield orientation

  • Rubric wins. If Bates, the PowerPoint, and the rubric ever disagree, use the rubric as the tie-breaker.
  • For an eye complaint, think anterior to posterior and superficial to deep: lids/conjunctiva → cornea/anterior chamber → lens → vitreous → retina/macula → optic disc/nerve → visual pathways.
  • The screening eye exam this week centers on inspection, pupils, corneal light reflex, EOMs, accommodation/convergence, and basic ophthalmoscope use. Visual acuity and visual fields were taught earlier with cranial nerves.
  • Not all vision complaints are from the eyeball itself. Neuro red flags matter: headaches, mental status change, weight loss, night sweats, field cuts, or other focal deficits.
Clinical anchor
The slide deck keeps returning to a 68-year-old woman with vision changes. The pattern points you toward asking targeted ROS, then deciding whether the problem sounds conjunctival, lenticular, retinal/macular, optic nerve, or central neurologic.

Quick red flags

🚨
Pain + vision loss
Think urgent
Especially glaucoma, uveitis, corneal injury, optic neuritis, or retinal emergency.
🧠
Neuro symptoms
Broaden ROS
Ask about worsening headaches, mental status change, weight loss, and night sweats when something central is on the list.
✨
Flashes / floaters / curtain
Retinal concern
Do not brush these off as just "blurry vision."
🫥
Field defect
Localize it
Central loss suggests macula; temporal field loss bilaterally points you toward the chiasm.

Vision change?→ Central vs peripheral→ Sudden vs gradual→ Painful vs painless→ Eye vs optic nerve vs brain
Illness scripts

Conjunctivitis, macular degeneration, glaucoma, cataract

Know the pattern, the structure involved, the typical ROS clues, and what abnormal findings you expect on exam.

High-yield comparison

Condition Classic history Expected abnormal findings Where the problem lives Quick pearl
Conjunctivitis Red eye with irritation, discharge, and usually preserved visual acuity. Viral pattern = watery, itchy/gritty. Bacterial pattern = purulent discharge or crusting; "eyes stuck shut when I wake up" is a strong clue. Diffuse conjunctival injection. Viral: watery discharge. Bacterial: purulent discharge/crusting. Pupils and vision usually normal in uncomplicated cases. Conjunctiva Pain plus decreased vision should push you away from a simple conjunctivitis script.
Macular degeneration Gradual central vision trouble: difficulty reading, trouble in dimmer light, trouble recognizing faces. Risk factors from the slides include older age, smoking, and HTN. Central visual impairment or central scotoma. Fundus may show drusen and a bright, sharply demarcated atrophic macular area in nonexudative disease. Peripheral vision is relatively spared. Macula / fovea If the patient bumps into things on the sides but sees straight ahead, that is not the macular degeneration pattern.
Glaucoma Open-angle: often gradual, painless peripheral loss. Acute angle-closure: severe eye pain, headache, nausea, and vision loss. Pathologic optic cupping on fundus. On oblique light testing, a narrow anterior chamber angle can cast a shadow on the nasal iris and flags risk for acute narrow-angle glaucoma. Optic nerve and aqueous outflow/anterior chamber angle Open-angle preserves the normal iris-cornea spatial relationship. Angle-closure is the dramatic painful emergency script.
Cataract Gradual, painless cloudy or blurry vision, often with glare and worse night driving; can already be present in PMHx. Lens opacity. Fundal/retinal reflex may be dark, interrupted, absent, or sometimes white depending on the pathology in the visual axis. Lens If the ophthalmoscope reflex is not clean, think about opacity somewhere along the path of light, especially the lens.
Slide-based distinctions
The deck explicitly contrasts viral conjunctivitis (watery, gritty, normal vision), bacterial conjunctivitis (purulent / crusting), acute angle-closure glaucoma (pain + headache + nausea + vision loss), and macular degeneration (central vision loss affecting reading and face recognition).

Case pearls from the 68-year-old patient

  • Bacterial conjunctivitis clue: "My eyes are stuck shut when I wake up."
  • Macular degeneration support: few-year gradual course, trouble reading in darker environments, increasing difficulty recognizing faces, smoking history, HTN.
  • Brain tumor / central lesion ROS: ask about unintentional weight loss, worsening headaches, night sweats, and change in mental status.
Why the face-recognition clue matters
Macular disease hurts fine central vision, so the patient struggles with reading and recognizing faces even while gross peripheral navigation may be less affected.

Subjective

Entire subjective history + ROS, done compassionately

You are expected to define, obtain, and interpret the full subjective history: HPI, PMHx, PSHx, family history, social history, meds, allergies, and ROS.

What to collect for any chief complaint

Section What belongs here Why it matters
CC + HPI Onset, location/laterality, duration, timing, severity, character, aggravating/relieving factors, associated symptoms, prior episodes, and impact on function. Builds the illness script and lets you localize the problem.
PMHx / PSHx Existing eye disease, HTN, diabetes, migraine, autoimmune disease, surgeries, trauma. Risk factors and prior interventions change your differential.
Family history Glaucoma, macular degeneration, retinal disease, migraines, neurologic disease. Some eye disease clusters in families.
Social history Smoking, occupation, contact lens use, driving difficulty, visual demands, substance use, exposure risks. Smoking is relevant for macular degeneration; contacts raise infection questions.
Meds / allergies Drops, anticoagulants, steroids, glaucoma meds, relevant allergies. Meds can explain symptoms or change treatment decisions.
ROS Target eye ROS plus relevant general, neuro, vascular, respiratory, CV, GI, GU, derm, and MSK questions based on the complaint. Rules in and rules out related systems instead of treating the eye in isolation.
Patient-centered language
Introduce yourself and your role, confirm the patient's name, date of birth, pronouns, and what they prefer to be called. Ask questions in a non-judgmental way and avoid making the patient feel blamed for symptoms or exposures.

Target eye ROS from the slides

  • Eye pain
  • Eye redness
  • Eye drainage / pus / crusting (characterize it)
  • Eye dryness
  • Eye itching
  • Excessive tearing
  • Date of last eye exam
  • Loss of vision in part of field
  • Change in clarity / acuity
  • Double vision diplopia
  • Blind spots in field scotomas
  • Specks that move with gaze floaters
  • Photopsia flashing lights
  • Contacts or glasses use myopia/hyperopia context

Rationale: which extra ROS systems matter for an eye complaint?

When vision loss / field cut / neuro concern is present

  • General: weight loss, night sweats, fever
  • Neurologic: headache, weakness, numbness, mental status change, balance problems
  • Vascular / CV: HTN, vascular disease, stroke history
  • Endocrine / metabolic context: diabetes risk when retinal disease is on the table

When red eye or irritation is the main complaint

  • Derm / allergy: atopy, itching, rashes
  • Respiratory / infectious: recent URI symptoms for viral conjunctivitis
  • MSK / autoimmune context: inflammatory symptoms if considering uveitic or systemic disease
  • Social / exposure: contacts, trauma, irritants, sick contacts

Lay explanation + consent script before the exam

A simple version that sounds natural
"Next I want to examine your eyes. I am going to look at the outside of the eyes, check your pupils and eye movements, and use a light to look in the back of the eyes. The light may be bright, but it should not hurt. Is that okay with you?"

Draping / respect reminders

  • Explain what you are doing before you move clothing, gown, or drape.
  • Expose only the area being examined.
  • Step out while the patient changes when applicable.
  • Retie the gown when you are done if you loosened it.

General care reminders

  • Wash hands on entering or before the exam.
  • Watch the patient's face for discomfort.
  • Help reposition the patient safely on the table.
  • At the end, explain findings in plain language and thank them.
Anatomy + glossary

Structures and definitions you should be able to say out loud

Know the basic external structures, the posterior fundus landmarks, and the vocabulary that shows up in the history and eye exam.

Structures pertinent to the exam

  • Pupil: circular opening in the iris through which light enters.
  • Cornea: transparent anterior structure and the eye's major refractive surface.
  • Conjunctiva: mucous membrane over the anterior sclera and inner lids.
  • Sclera: tough white outer coat of the eye.
  • Anterior chamber: space between cornea and iris; angle matters for glaucoma risk.
  • Retina: light-sensing tissue lining the back of the eye.
  • Optic disc: where ganglion cell axons converge to form the optic nerve; normal blind spot.
  • Optic cup: central depression in the optic disc; becomes pathologically enlarged in glaucoma.
  • Macula: central retinal area for fine central vision.
  • Fovea: center of the macula with sharpest vision.
  • Extraocular muscles: muscles that move the globe; tested in the six cardinal fields.

Reflexes and retinal terms you must distinguish

Direct pupillary response
Light in eye A → eye A constricts
Response in the illuminated eye.
Consensual response
Light in eye A → eye B constricts
Response in the opposite eye.
Accommodation reflex
Near target → pupils constrict
Also involves lens accommodation for near focus.
Convergence
Eyes adduct for near target
Usually tracked to within about 6 to 8 cm of the nose.
Fundal / retinal reflex
Glow through the pupil with the ophthalmoscope
Absent, dark, or white reflex is abnormal.
Scleral icterus
Yellow sclera
Think bilirubin / jaundice.

Physical exam

Normal eye exam: what you do, what normal looks like, what abnormal means

Be able to describe the indications for the eye exam, perform it correctly, and report findings clearly in both lay and medical language.

Suggested sequence for the screening eye exam

  1. Inspect eyebrows, lids, lashes, conjunctiva, sclera, cornea, and general alignment.
  2. Pupils: size, symmetry, shape; test direct and consensual responses.
  3. Corneal light reflex: reflection should be symmetric and slightly nasal to the center of each pupil.
  4. EOMs: trace an H or Z pattern through the six cardinal directions.
  5. Accommodation and convergence: shift focus from distance to a near target.
  6. Anterior chamber depth: use oblique light when indicated.
  7. Ophthalmoscopy: obtain fundal reflex, then identify optic disc, cup, vessels, and macula region as able.
Normal wording
PERRLA = pupils equally round and reactive to light and accommodation.
EOMI = extraocular movements intact.
Normal sclera = white. Normal conjunctiva = pink.

Important normal findings

  • Sclera white; conjunctiva pink
  • Pupils equal, round, reactive to light and accommodation
  • Corneal light reflex symmetric
  • EOMs intact without nystagmus or pain
  • Convergence preserved
  • Disc margins sharp on funduscopic exam
  • No hemorrhages, exudates, or AV nicking
The slides also remind you that color vision and contrast sensitivity are not part of this week's screening eye exam, and visual fields/acuity were taught earlier with CNs.

Ophthalmoscope

Using the ophthalmoscope + recognizing fundus abnormalities

Know the setup, the body positioning, the fundal reflex, and the big funduscopic abnormalities that show up in clinical reasoning.

Ophthalmoscope steps from Bates, simplified

  1. Darken the room and turn the lens disc to 0 diopters.
  2. Use your right hand/right eye for the patient's right eye and left hand/left eye for the left eye.
  3. Brace the scope against your bony orbit and have the patient look slightly up and over your shoulder.
  4. Start about 15 inches away and about 15 degrees lateral to the patient's line of vision.
  5. Find the fundal / retinal reflex first. Any interruption, darkness, or abnormal whiteness matters.
  6. Then move closer and identify the optic disc, cup, vessels, and posterior pole structures as able.
Fundal reflex pearl
A normal reflex is the glow seen through the pupil with the ophthalmoscope. An abnormal or absent reflex can point toward cataract, retinal abnormality, strabismus, or other pathology in the visual axis.

Key abnormal retinal / optic nerve findings

  • Papilledema: swelling of the optic disc due to increased ICP.
  • Pathologic cupping: enlarged cup-to-disc ratio, classic glaucoma association.
  • AV nicking: venous narrowing at AV crossings, associated with HTN/vascular disease.
  • Cotton wool spots: nerve fiber layer ischemic injury; think HTN or diabetes, among others.

How to talk through fundus findings

Finding What it literally looks like What it makes you think about
Papilledema Swollen optic disc with loss of crisp margins and bulging of the physiologic cup region. Raised intracranial pressure until proven otherwise.
Pathologic cupping Large central cup relative to disc size. Glaucomatous optic neuropathy.
AV nicking Artery indents and displaces a vein at a crossing. Chronic hypertension / vascular disease.
Cotton wool spots Soft white superficial retinal lesions from microinfarcts. HTN, diabetes, and other ischemic/inflammatory states.
Abnormal fundal reflex Dark, absent, interrupted, or white reflex. Cataract, retinal detachment, retinoblastoma, and other pathology depending the context.

OSE + SOAP

Osteopathic considerations, documentation, and how to report your findings

The osteopathic portion is about whether structure is contributing to ocular symptoms and how you document that alongside the standard history and physical exam.

What the osteopathic eye exam is assessing

  • Whether dysfunction of the cranial base or upper cervical spine may be contributing to eye pain, visual strain, blurred vision, diplopia, or headaches.
  • Whether altered neural input, autonomic tone, venous drainage, lymphatic drainage, or fascial/mechanical tension may be worsening symptoms.
  • Whether there are TART findings involving the OA joint, C1-C3, frontal bone, sphenoid, temporal bones, orbits, suboccipital muscles, SCM, or cervical paraspinals.
  • Whether dysfunction around the pathways of CN II, III, IV, and VI could be relevant.
Important scope point
OSE does not replace the standard eye exam. It complements it by asking whether somatic dysfunction may be contributing to symptoms or symptom persistence.

Positive vs negative OSE framing

Positive findings
TART / restriction / hypertonicity
Example: OA asymmetry, upper cervical restriction, suboccipital tightness, cranial base restriction.
Negative findings
No major contributory SD appreciated
Used when the osteopathic screen does not show meaningful somatic dysfunction related to the complaint.
Technique choice
Gentle first in acute complaints
Slides favor indirect approaches like MFR, BLT, and counterstrain when symptoms are acute or severe.
Treatment goal
Optimize structure + drainage
Aim to reduce postural strain, fascial tension, and impaired head/orbit drainage.

SOAP note skeleton for an eye complaint

Subjective

  • CC in the patient's words
  • HPI with chronology and associated eye symptoms
  • PMHx / PSHx / FamHx / SocHx / meds / allergies
  • Relevant ROS from eye + other indicated systems

Objective

  • Vitals and general appearance
  • HEENT exam with specific eye findings
  • Neuro findings when indicated, especially CNs and fields/acuity context
  • OSE findings and somatic dysfunction diagnosis

Assessment

  • Primary ophthalmic diagnosis or differential
  • Relevant secondary considerations
  • Somatic dysfunction diagnosis when present

Plan - 5 required parts

  1. Diagnostics - what testing, if any, is needed
  2. Therapeutics / referrals - meds, ophthalmology referral, etc.
  3. OMT - what you did or would consider
  4. Patient education - what you explained in plain language
  5. Follow-up - when / return precautions

Documentation language: normal and abnormal examples

Normal HEENT eye wording
Visual acuity 20/20 bilaterally. Sclera white, conjunctiva pink. Pupils equally round and reactive to light and accommodation. Disc margins sharp; no hemorrhages, exudates, or AV nicking.
Abnormal case wording from the deck
Fundoscopic exam reveals a large, sharply demarcated bright area and several small, punctate patchy yellow lesions - a pattern that fits nonexudative age-related macular degeneration.
Lay communication version
"The back of your eye shows changes in the center part that helps with sharp central vision. That matches the kind of trouble you have been having with reading and recognizing faces."

Built for fast review: history → exam → fundus → OSE → SOAP. Rehearse saying the normal exam out loud, because that is usually where performance becomes much smoother.

UNE / COMLEX review

Ten board-style eye MCQs

These questions are built from the learning objectives in this note: pattern recognition for common eye complaints, focused history, normal screening exam, ophthalmoscopy, fundus findings, and osteopathic documentation.

Question 1

A 68-year-old woman reports a few years of gradually worsening trouble reading in dimmer light and increasing difficulty recognizing faces. She has a smoking history and hypertension. Which diagnosis best fits this pattern?

  1. Bacterial conjunctivitis
  2. Nonexudative age-related macular degeneration
  3. Open-angle glaucoma
  4. Cataract
  5. Optic neuritis
Answer
B. Nonexudative age-related macular degeneration. The note ties gradual central visual difficulty, trouble reading, and difficulty recognizing faces to macular disease, with smoking and HTN as supporting risk factors.

Question 2

A patient presents with a severely painful red eye, headache, nausea, and decreased vision. On oblique light testing, a shadow is cast on the nasal iris. Which diagnosis is most likely?

  1. Viral conjunctivitis
  2. Acute angle-closure glaucoma
  3. Cataract
  4. Nonexudative macular degeneration
  5. Simple refractive error
Answer
B. Acute angle-closure glaucoma. The deck's emergency glaucoma script is pain, headache, nausea, and vision loss. The nasal iris shadow on oblique light testing supports a narrow anterior chamber angle.

Question 3

A college student has a red eye with watery discharge, itching, and a gritty sensation after recent URI symptoms. Visual acuity is preserved and the pupil exam is normal. Which diagnosis is most likely?

  1. Viral conjunctivitis
  2. Bacterial conjunctivitis
  3. Acute angle-closure glaucoma
  4. Macular degeneration
  5. Retinal detachment
Answer
A. Viral conjunctivitis. Watery discharge, gritty irritation, URI context, and preserved acuity match the note's viral conjunctivitis pattern rather than deeper painful pathology.

Question 4

A 59-year-old patient describes gradual, painless cloudy vision with increasing glare and worse night driving. During ophthalmoscopy, the fundal reflex is dark and interrupted. Which structure is most likely responsible for this patient's symptoms?

  1. Conjunctiva
  2. Anterior chamber angle
  3. Lens
  4. Macula
  5. Optic disc
Answer
C. Lens. The cataract script in the note is gradual, painless cloudy vision with glare, and the ophthalmoscope reflex may be dark, interrupted, absent, or otherwise abnormal because of opacity in the visual axis.

Question 5

A patient reports loss of vision in part of the field and you are concerned about a central neurologic lesion rather than an isolated surface eye problem. Which additional review-of-systems cluster is most appropriate based on this note?

  1. Ear fullness, sore throat, and sinus pressure only
  2. Weight loss, night sweats, headaches, and mental status change
  3. Abdominal pain, dysuria, and constipation only
  4. Knee swelling and low back pain only
  5. Exercise tolerance and calf cramps only
Answer
B. Weight loss, night sweats, headaches, and mental status change. The subjective section specifically recommends broadening ROS this way when field loss or a central lesion is in the differential.

Question 6

Which finding is normal when testing the corneal light reflex during a screening eye exam?

  1. The light reflex is absent unless the patient accommodates
  2. The reflection is symmetric and slightly nasal to the center of each pupil
  3. The reflection is symmetric and directly over the lateral limbus bilaterally
  4. The reflection is visible in only one eye if visual acuity is normal
  5. The reflection should move from nasal to temporal when the patient closes one eye
Answer
B. The reflection is symmetric and slightly nasal to the center of each pupil. That is the normal corneal light reflex position described in the exam section; asymmetry raises concern for misalignment such as strabismus.

Question 7

A patient cannot abduct the right eye during cardinal gaze testing. Which extraocular muscle and cranial nerve are most likely affected?

  1. Medial rectus, CN III
  2. Superior oblique, CN IV
  3. Lateral rectus, CN VI
  4. Inferior oblique, CN III
  5. Superior rectus, CN III
Answer
C. Lateral rectus, CN VI. The note's memory hook is LR6 SO4 AO3: lateral rectus is innervated by CN VI, superior oblique by CN IV, and all the rest by CN III.

Question 8

Which setup is correct for basic ophthalmoscopy according to the simplified Bates steps in this note?

  1. Bright room, 20 diopters, approach straight on from 2 inches away
  2. Darken the room, set to 0 diopters, use right hand/right eye for the patient's right eye, and start about 15 inches away and 15 degrees lateral
  3. Darken the room, set to -10 diopters, and always use your dominant eye for both eyes
  4. Room lighting does not matter as long as the patient looks directly into the light
  5. Test the left eye first with your right hand and remain centered on the patient's visual axis
Answer
B. Darken the room, set to 0 diopters, use right hand/right eye for the patient's right eye, and start about 15 inches away and 15 degrees lateral. That is the exact simplified technique sequence listed in the ophthalmoscope section.

Question 9

Funduscopic exam shows a swollen optic disc with loss of crisp margins and bulging of the physiologic cup region. Which condition does this finding most strongly suggest?

  1. Chronic open-angle glaucoma
  2. Raised intracranial pressure
  3. Simple cataract
  4. Uncomplicated bacterial conjunctivitis
  5. Presbyopia
Answer
B. Raised intracranial pressure. This describes papilledema, which the note explicitly links to increased ICP. In contrast, glaucoma is associated with pathologic cupping.

Question 10

A student writes a SOAP plan for an eye complaint that includes diagnostics, ophthalmology referral, and OMT. The student also documents return precautions. Which required plan component from this note is still missing?

  1. Chief complaint
  2. Family history
  3. Patient education in plain language
  4. PERRLA documentation
  5. Somatic dysfunction diagnosis
Answer
C. Patient education in plain language. The note lists five required plan parts: diagnostics, therapeutics/referrals, OMT, patient education, and follow-up. The missing element here is patient education.
