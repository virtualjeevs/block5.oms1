Internal Anatomy (Cross-Section)

From front to back: Cornea → Aqueous humor → Iris/Ciliary body → Lens (held by zonules) → Vitreous humor → Retina → Choroid → Sclera. The optic nerve exits posteriorly, surrounded by dura mater, and carries visual signals to the brain.

🎯 Clinical Pearl — Dr. Wortham
When you're looking into the right eye with the ophthalmoscope, the optic nerve is always nasally located — aim about 15° toward the nose from the patient's line of sight to bring it into view.

🎯 Diplopia Decision Tree — Dr. Wortham
Binocular diplopia (goes away when either eye is covered) → misalignment → suspect acute cranial neuropathy (CN III, IV, or VI).
Monocular diplopia (persists with one eye covered) → distortion inside that eye → cataract, corneal irregularity, vitreous bleed.

Past Ocular History

Developmental & Surgical

  • Gestational age / birth weight — premature infants at risk for retinopathy of prematurity
  • History of "lazy eye" (amblyopia) or "crossed eyes" (strabismus) → can explain lifelong poor vision in one eye
  • Glasses, contacts, or refractive surgery (LASIK)
  • Prior eye/facial trauma
  • Prior eye surgery or procedures (laser, injections)
  • Problems with anesthesia (local or general)
  • Glaucoma diagnosis

Current Eye Medications

  • OTC: artificial tears, Clear Eyes, vitamins
  • Rx drops: record name AND the color of the bottle cap (standardized by drug class)

Past Medical History

  • Diabetes — duration matters; microvascular disease → retinal bleeding, vitreous hemorrhage
  • Hypertension
  • Cancer (metastatic disease can affect the eye)
  • Thyroid disease (Graves' ophthalmopathy)

Family History

👨‍👩‍👧 Why Family Hx Matters
Ask about: blindness, glaucoma, retinal detachment, macular degeneration, and poor vision at a young age in close relatives. Pedigree analysis can help identify inheritance patterns of retinal dystrophies. If a child has parents with early vision loss, that child is at much higher risk — you can save them from a lifetime of poor vision by early screening.
💬 Dr. Wortham's Clinical Insight
"90% of all diagnoses come from a good history." — Make the history thorough before picking up the ophthalmoscope.

03 Visual Acuity

Snellen Chart — Distance Vision (20 feet)

The Snellen chart tests distance acuity. The numerator is the testing distance (20 ft); the denominator is the distance at which a normal eye can read that line.

📏 Interpretation
20/20 = average human vision (based on distribution of cones in the fovea).
20/200 = what a patient sees at 20 ft, a normal eye can see at 200 ft — legally blind threshold.

Documentation Rules

  • Always document how vision was tested (chart type, distance, correction status)
  • Check with glasses/contacts on if the patient wears them — document as "corrected." Otherwise document as "uncorrected."
  • Test and record each eye separately (right eye = OD, left eye = OS)
  • If patient cannot read any line, try: count fingers → hand motion → light perception → no light perception

Near Card — Near Vision (14 inches)

Tested at 14 inches using a near card (e.g., Rosenbaum pocket screener). Patients over age 40 commonly need reading correction at near even if distance vision is fine.

📖 Presbyopia
Loss of near accommodation that occurs with aging (typically noted after age 40). The lens becomes stiffer and loses the ability to change shape for near focus. Patients will describe needing reading glasses even if they had good vision before.

04 Visual Fields

Confrontational Visual Field Testing

A bedside screening method. Examiner faces the patient at ~arm's length, covers one of their own eyes, and tests the corresponding eye of the patient by presenting finger movement at the periphery in each quadrant.

Cover patient's L eye → Test R eye (examiner covers own R eye) → Move fingers in 4 quadrants → Repeat for L eye Patient must fixate on examiner's face/nose throughout
📐 Normal Visual Field
Peripheral field extends roughly 90°–100° from fixation (varies with lid anatomy). Central vision (fovea/macula) provides high-acuity central field.

🧠 Visual Pathway Anatomy
Retina→ Optic Nerve (CN II)→ Optic Chiasm→ Optic Tract→ LGN→ Optic Radiations→ Visual Cortex (occipital)
Nasal fibers cross at the chiasm; temporal fibers stay ipsilateral. A chiasmal lesion selectively damages crossing nasal fibers → bitemporal hemianopia.

When to Use Automated Perimetry

Confrontational testing is a good screen but misses subtle defects. Refer for formal automated visual field testing (Humphrey or Goldmann perimetry) when the defect is complex, subtle (e.g., inferior quadrant loss), or needs precise characterization for follow-up (e.g., glaucoma monitoring).

Practical Tips from Dr. Wortham

  • Always test each eye separately — hold the occluder firmly, watch for peeking
  • For central loss: ask the patient to look at your face — can they see your nose? Your ears? This quickly distinguishes central vs. peripheral loss
  • For peripheral loss: begin fingers far out at the periphery where they cannot see, then move inward until they first detect movement
  • Dense peripheral loss → patient will not see any fingers until they are well within the central field; watch their gaze to be sure they're fixating on you

05 External Exam

General Observation

Before touching anything, step back and observe. A seasoned clinician does this in seconds. Look for:

  • Facial asymmetry — everyone has some degree; note eyelid, eyebrow, and ear position
  • Eye position in the orbits — are both eyes at the same level?
  • Pupil symmetry — equal size and position?
💬 Dr. Wortham
"Don't take every little asymmetry as pathological — raise your hand if you have facial asymmetry. Everyone should raise their hand. Normal facial asymmetry can translate into slightly different ocular anatomy that is not pathologic."

06 Conjunctiva & Cornea

Conjunctival Anatomy

Bulbar Conjunctiva

Covers the exposed surface of the globe up to the limbus. Thin and transparent — blood vessels visible when inflamed.

Tarsal (Palpebral) Conjunctiva

Lines the inner surface of the eyelids. Accessed by lid eversion (flipping the lid). Inspect for foreign bodies, follicles, papillae, scarring.

🎯 Viral Conjunctivitis Trick
Feel for a pre-auricular lymph node (just in front of the ear). Tender, palpable node = viral conjunctivitis ~90% of the time. This does NOT occur with bacterial conjunctivitis.

Subconjunctival Hemorrhage

  • Sharply demarcated bright red patch; resolves in ~1–2 weeks
  • Usually benign: Valsalva (coughing, straining), hypertension, anticoagulants
  • If diffuse, bilateral, or recurrent → investigate for bleeding disorder or trauma

Lacrimal / Nasolacrimal System

Lacrimal gland (superior-temporal) → Tear film over globe → Puncta (upper + lower, medial canthus) → Canaliculi (8 mm) → Lacrimal sac (10 mm) → Nasolacrimal duct (12 mm) → Valve of Hasner → nasal cavity
💧 Epiphora (Tearing) Pearls
  • ~90% of tears drain through the inferior punctum — blockage here → constant tearing
  • In neonates, nasolacrimal duct obstruction is common — Valve of Hasner fails to open → treated with massage, then probing at ~12 months
  • If tearing + NO redness → think lacrimal system obstruction
  • Causes of blockage: sinusitis, nasal polyps, inflammation, trauma, chronic infection

Corneal Assessment

When evaluating the cornea, clarity is the key criterion. If you can visualize fine detail of the iris through the cornea, it is clear.

FindingDescriptionCauses
Corneal abrasionLoss of epithelial cells; detected with fluorescein staining (fluoresces green under cobalt blue light)Foreign body, contact lens wear, trauma
Corneal ulcerStromal infiltrate + epithelial defect; white/opaque spot on corneaBacterial keratitis (Staph, Pseudomonas), fungal, herpes simplex
Corneal edemaDiffuse clouding; iris detail obscuredAcute angle-closure glaucoma, endothelial failure (Fuchs')

🔵 Fluorescein Staining Technique
Wet a fluorescein strip → touch to the inferior conjunctival fornix (lower lid) → patient blinks to distribute dye → examine with cobalt blue light. Any disruption of the corneal epithelium glows bright green. Do NOT touch the strip to the cornea — it will artificially stain an area and confuse the picture.

07 Anterior Chamber

Assessing Depth (Oblique Flashlight Test)

Shine a penlight tangentially from the temporal side across the iris. In a deep chamber, light illuminates the entire iris. In a shallow chamber, the nasal side of the iris is in shadow (the bulging iris blocks light passage).

Deep Anterior Chamber (Normal)

  • Light illuminates both sides of iris uniformly
  • Found in myopic (nearsighted) eyes — longer axial length
  • Low risk for angle-closure glaucoma

Shallow Anterior Chamber (Risk)

  • Nasal side of iris is in shadow
  • Found in hyperopic (farsighted) eyes — shorter axial length
  • Higher risk for acute angle-closure glaucoma
  • Also at risk: severe premature infants

08 Pupils

Documenting the Pupils

  • Document size at rest (in mm) for each eye
  • Document response to direct light and near stimulus
  • Document any anisocoria (unequal pupil size) — if found, review old photos and history to determine if chronic or new

Relative Afferent Pupillary Defect (RAPD) — Marcus Gunn Pupil

🔍 How to Detect RAPD
  1. Shine light in the good eye (L) → both pupils constrict
  2. Quickly swing light to the affected eye (R) → both pupils dilate (the affected eye sees less light, so the brain reduces the constriction drive)
  3. Swing back to good eye → both constrict again
RAPD = optic nerve disease until proven otherwise (if vision loss is acute and isolated).
🧠 RAPD vs. CN III Palsy
A CN III palsy dilates the pupil because the efferent constriction pathway (CN III → pupillary sphincter) is damaged — the dilated pupil still responds to consensual light from the opposite eye. A RAPD dilates because the afferent sensory signal from that eye is weak — the problem is the optic nerve bringing the signal in, not the muscle squeezing the pupil.

Have the patient look far away, then at a near target. Normal near reflex produces:

Fixed Pupil Differentials

⚠️ Fixed Dilated Pupil — Think:
  • Acute angle-closure glaucoma — mid-dilated, fixed, painful red eye
  • CN III palsy — "surgical" cause (posterior communicating artery aneurysm compresses CN III externally → pupil first!); vs. "medical" (microvascular diabetic CN III → pupil spared)
  • Trauma — direct iris sphincter damage
  • Pharmacologic — atropine, scopolamine, nebulizer mist (anticholinergic)
  • Iris adhesions (synechiae) — chronic iritis/uveitis scars the pupil margin onto the lens → irregular, non-reactive pupil

Practical Shortcut — Dr. Wortham's Method

💡 One-Second Bilateral Pupil Check
Set the ophthalmoscope to +1 (green side) with a large beam. Hold at arm's length. Both eyes are illuminated simultaneously — compare pupil size, symmetry, and retinoscopic reflex in one glance. You've simultaneously screened pupils AND checked for an asymmetric red reflex (cataract, vitreous bleed, etc.).

09 Extraocular Motility (EOM) Exam

Technique

  • Use a specific target — a small light or toy is better than a finger
  • Ask the patient to follow the target with their eyes while keeping their head still
  • Test 9 positions of gaze: primary position, 4 cardinal positions (R, L, up, down), and 4 diagonal positions
  • At each position, ask about diplopia
  • Note any limitation of movement, lag, or nystagmus

📐 CN IV Palsy — Head Tilt Sign
A patient with a CN IV (superior oblique) palsy will tilt their head toward the opposite shoulder to reduce vertical diplopia. If they tilt right → suspect left CN IV palsy, and vice versa. This is the only explanation for head tilt to eliminate vertical double vision — it's pathognomonic.
🧩 Oblique Muscles — Summary
  • When the eye is abducting (looking out): SR = up, IR = down
  • When the eye is adducting (looking in toward nose): IO = up, SO = down
  • The obliques act through the trochlea (SO) or the orbital floor; their action changes with eye position, making them clinically subtle

Methods for Assessing Alignment

1. Corneal Light Reflex (Hirschberg Test)

Shine a penlight or hold the ophthalmoscope at arm's length at the patient's eyes. Observe where the light reflex falls on each cornea:

  • Centered on both corneas = orthophoric (well-aligned)
  • Reflex displaced nasally on one eye = that eye is deviated outward (exotropia)
  • Reflex displaced temporally on one eye = that eye is deviated inward (esotropia)
  • Each 1 mm of displacement ≈ 22 prism diopters of deviation

2. Cover-Uncover Test

Have the patient fix on a target. Cover one eye and observe whether the uncovered eye moves to take up fixation. If it moves, a tropia (manifest strabismus) is present. Then uncover and watch for a movement back — this reveals a phoria (latent deviation).

💡 Practical Shortcut — Ophthalmoscope Screen
Set the ophthalmoscope to +1, large beam, arm's length. While looking above the scope, compare the corneal light reflexes of both eyes simultaneously. Also peer through the scope to simultaneously screen for pupil symmetry and asymmetric red reflex — all in one moment.

Pseudostrabismus

⚠️ Common Pitfall
Broad nasal bridges (common in infants and in some Asian populations) can make eyes appear crossed (esotropia). The corneal light reflex test reveals the reflexes are actually centered — this is pseudostrabismus, not a true deviation. Dim lighting makes the test harder; turn up the scope light and be quick.

What to Examine (in order)

Red reflex (from arm's length) → Track vessels to optic nerve → Assess optic nerve (disc, cup, margins, color) → Ask patient to look at light → view fovea → Patient looks in different directions → peripheral retina

Red (Funduscopic) Reflex

From arm's length with +1 lens and a large beam, the reflection of light off the vascular retina produces an orange-red glow from each eye. Assess both eyes simultaneously and compare.

Reflex AppearancePossible Cause
Symmetric bright orange-red glowNormal
Asymmetric (one eye dull/dark)Corneal opacity, hyphema/hypopyon, cataract, vitreous hemorrhage, retinal tumor (retinoblastoma), large retinal lesion
White/yellow reflex (leukocoria)Retinoblastoma (urgent in children), congenital cataract, severe retinal detachment, persistent fetal vasculature
Absent reflexDense cataract, vitreous hemorrhage, complete retinal detachment
⚠️ Leukocoria (White Reflex) in a Child = Urgent
Absent or white funduscopic reflex in a child requires immediate ophthalmology referral to rule out retinoblastoma. This is a life-threatening intraocular tumor that can metastasize. Never dismiss an asymmetric or absent red reflex in a child.

Assessing the Optic Nerve (Disc)

Glaucoma — Progressive Optic Cup Enlargement

🎯 Cup-to-Disc Ratio
  • Normal C:D ≤ 0.50 — average 0.25
  • 0.6–0.7 — suspicious; compare with fellow eye; check IOP
  • 0.8–0.9 — highly suspicious for glaucoma; urgent referral
  • Note: large cups can be familial (especially in highly myopic patients) — not always glaucoma, but must rule it out
  • Glaucoma = progressive peripheral visual field loss (starts superior and inferior arcuate) as optic nerve fibers are destroyed

Disc Edema (Papilledema)

⚠️ Papilledema vs. Papillitis
Papilledema = disc swelling from raised intracranial pressure (bilateral, no pain, late vision loss). Signs: blurred disc margins, loss of cup, peripapillary hemorrhages, venous engorgement.

Papillitis = optic disc inflammation (optic neuritis) — unilateral, painful eye movement, early central vision loss, RAPD positive.

If you see blurred margins AND hemorrhages at the disc → papilledema until proven otherwise — urgent workup for elevated ICP.

Optic Nerve Pallor

A pale (white/grey) optic disc indicates atrophy — loss of axons. Causes:

  • Glaucoma (advanced) — with cupping
  • Ischemic optic neuropathy — sudden vision loss, often upper or lower visual field cut, pale disc with hemorrhages acutely
  • Optic nerve compression — tumor pressing on the nerve
  • Prior optic neuritis
  • Hereditary optic neuropathies (e.g., Leber's)

Assessing the Fovea

After locating and examining the optic disc, ask the patient to look directly at the light. Their eye will shift medially — you will now be looking at the fovea (about 2 disc diameters temporal to the disc).

🩺 Cataract — Effect on Red Reflex
A cataract (clouded lens) disrupts the funduscopic reflex. The reflex may appear dull, irregular, or partially blocked. Cataracts appear as dark opacities against the orange reflex. A dense nuclear cataract may reduce reflex to a dim amber glow.

Key Takeaways from Dr. Wortham

⭐ Top Clinical Pearls
  1. History first — 90% of diagnoses come from a good history. Ask systematically.
  2. Check each eye separately — always. For VA, fields, and pupils.
  3. Pre-auricular node = viral conjunctivitis until proven otherwise
  4. RAPD = optic nerve disease — until proven otherwise (if acute isolated finding)
  5. Asymmetric red reflex in a child = urgent ophthalmology referral (rule out retinoblastoma)
  6. Blurred disc margins + hemorrhages = papilledema → workup for raised ICP
  7. Flashes + floaters (acute onset) = retinal tear/detachment until ruled out → same-day referral
  8. Head tilt to eliminate vertical diplopia = CN IV palsy on the opposite side
  9. Sudden, severe eye pain + fixed mid-dilated pupil + cloudy cornea = acute angle-closure glaucoma → emergency
  10. Family history matters — glaucoma, macular degeneration, retinal detachment, early vision loss all carry strong hereditary risk
🛡️ Personal Safety — Dr. Wortham's Advice
If you have poor vision in one eye (amblyopia, trauma history, etc.) — wear eye protection. An injury to your only functional eye is catastrophic. Clear, lightweight safety glasses are widely available.
