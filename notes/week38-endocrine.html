PLO 1 ⚗️
Testicular Synthesis of Testosterone & Androgen Receptor Binding
Describe testicular synthesis of testosterone and testosterone binding to the androgen receptor
🔬 Synthesis — Basics
💡Leydig cells make testosterone. The sequence is hypothalamus → GnRH → pituitary → LH → Leydig cell. LH pushes cholesterol into the steroid pathway and turns on the enzymes that make testosterone.
  • LH stimulates mobilization & transport of cholesterol into the steroidogenic pathway
  • LH stimulates gene expression and activity of steroidogenic enzymes
  • Steroidogenic enzymes convert cholesterol → testosterone
📋 Synthesis Steps
  1. 1Hypothalamus secretes GnRH (gonadotropin-releasing hormone) → stimulates anterior pituitary to release LH
  2. 2LH binds to LH receptors on Leydig cells, activating the cAMP-PKA pathway → upregulates steroidogenic enzymes
  3. 3Cholesterol is transported into mitochondria via StAR protein (steroidogenic acute regulatory protein) — the rate-limiting step
🔄 Steroidogenic Pathway
Cholesterol → Pregnenolone (inner mitochondrial membrane) → Progesterone (smooth ER) → 17α-Hydroxyprogesterone → Androstenedione → Testosterone
📷 Steroidogenic Pathway — Cholesterol to Testosterone (with enzymes)

🔥 Androgen Potency (Relative): DHT (100%) > Testosterone (50%) > Androstenedione (8%) > DHEA (4%)

🎯 Androgen Receptor (AR) Binding
🔑Only free testosterone can enter target cells. In some tissues it is converted to DHT, which binds the androgen receptor more strongly. The hormone-receptor complex then changes gene transcription.
  1. 1Free testosterone diffuses across the plasma membrane into target cells
  2. 2In some tissues, testosterone → DHT by 5α-reductase (DHT has higher AR affinity)
  3. 3Testosterone or DHT binds to cytoplasmic AR → conformational change, dissociation of heat shock proteins (HSPs)
  4. 4Activated AR complex translocates to nucleus → binds androgen response elements (AREs) → activates gene transcription
✅ Effects of AR activation: Development of male reproductive organs · Spermatogenesis · Secondary sex characteristics (muscle mass, voice deepening, hair growth) · Libido & mood regulation

Central Regulation: GnRH, FSH, and LH
Describe the central regulation of testosterone synthesis — the role of GnRH, FSH, and LH
🔄Think of this as a hormone relay: hypothalamus → GnRH → pituitary → LH/FSH → testes. LH drives Leydig cell testosterone production, while FSH helps Sertoli cells support spermatogenesis. Rising testosterone then slows the system back down.

PLO 3 ⬅️
Control of Glandular Secretion by Negative Feedback
Discuss the control of glandular secretion by negative feedback
🔄There are two feedback brakes: testosterone/estradiol suppress GnRH and LH, while inhibin B specifically suppresses FSH.
Loop 1 — LH Feedback
  1. LH acts on Leydig cells → stimulates testosterone
  2. Testosterone partly converts to 17β-estradiol
  3. Testosterone + 17β-estradiol feedback on hypothalamus & pituitary → inhibit LH release → decrease amplitude of LH pulses
Loop 2 — FSH Feedback
  1. FSH acts on Sertoli cells → supports spermatogenesis
  2. Sertoli cells secrete Inhibin B in response to FSH
  3. Inhibin B acts at the pituitary level to specifically inhibit FSH secretion
🔑 Key distinction: Testosterone/estradiol inhibit both GnRH and LH/FSH. Inhibin B selectively inhibits FSH only — it does NOT affect LH or GnRH.

PLO 4 🩸
Significance of Hormone Binding to Plasma Proteins
Explain the significance of hormone binding to plasma proteins
📦Binding proteins act like a circulating reserve. They carry lipid-soluble hormones, protect them from fast breakdown, and reduce the immediately free fraction. The free hormone is the part that can enter cells and act.
EffectDetails
TransportFacilitates transport of lipid-soluble hormones (steroids, thyroid) through the aqueous bloodstream
ProtectionProtects hormones from degradation — prolongs half-life and ensures stability in circulation
InactivationBound hormones are inactive — cannot bind receptors on target cells. Only unbound (free) hormones are active
Feedback regulationIf liver ↑ production of binding proteins → ↓ free hormone → triggers feedback to release more hormone to restore balance
💜 Bioavailable hormones: Steroids bind weakly to albumin → dissociate quickly → available for cell uptake. These albumin-bound + free hormones = bioavailable fraction.

PLO 7 ⚡
Adrenal Medulla Catecholamines & Release Mechanism
Learn the catecholamines produced by the adrenal medulla and explain the mechanism by which they are released
⚡The adrenal medulla makes catecholamines from tyrosine. During stress, sympathetic firing releases ACh onto chromaffin cells, calcium rises, and epinephrine/norepinephrine are released into blood.
Catecholamine% ProducedFunction
Epinephrine (Adrenaline)~80%↑Heart rate, dilates airways, mobilizes glucose, ↑blood flow to muscles — fight-or-flight
Norepinephrine (Noradrenaline)~20%↑Blood pressure via vasoconstriction, maintains basal vascular tone
Dopamine<1%Precursor for norepinephrine; mild vasodilatory effects
🔁 Release Mechanism — Steps
  1. 1Body detects stress → activation of sympathetic nervous system
  2. 2Brain signals preganglionic sympathetic neurons to fire via the splanchnic nerve
  3. 3Neurons release ACh onto chromaffin cells in the adrenal medulla
  4. 4ACh binds to nicotinic receptors → triggering calcium influx into chromaffin cells
  5. 5↑Ca²⁺ causes vesicles to release epinephrine (80%) and norepinephrine (20%) into the bloodstream
📷 Catecholamine Synthesis Pathway & Chromaffin Cell Release Mechanism
🔥 PNMT: Phenylethanolamine-N-methyltransferase converts norepinephrine → epinephrine. Production of PNMT in chromaffin cells is stimulated and maintained by cortisol from the adrenal cortex.

HormoneSourceTargetFunctionRegulation

PLO 9 🌅
Diurnal Pattern of Cortisol Production
Describe the diurnal pattern of cortisol production and learn its physiologic basis
🕐Cortisol is highest in the early morning to help you wake up and handle daily stress, and lowest around midnight. The SCN and light-dark cues help set this rhythm.
Time of DayCortisol LevelPhysiologic Significance
Early Morning (4–8 AM, peak ~6–8 AM)HighestPrepares body for wakefulness, ↑alertness, mobilizes energy (gluconeogenesis)
Daytime (Midday to Afternoon)Gradual DeclineSupports metabolism, stress response, immune regulation
Evening (Late Afternoon to Early Night)LowAllows relaxation and transition to rest
Night (Midnight to ~4 AM)LowestFacilitates sleep, tissue repair, immune function
🎛️ What Controls the Rhythm
Light Exposure Morning light → ↑CRH & ACTH → high cortisol (wakefulness & energy)
Nighttime darkness → suppresses CRH → low cortisol (sleep & recovery)
SCN (Suprachiasmatic Nucleus) Detects light/dark cycles in the hypothalamus
Signals hypothalamus to release CRH → ↑ACTH → ↑cortisol
⚠️ Chronic stress can alter the rhythm → elevated evening cortisol. Sleep deprivation dysregulates cortisol → affects immune function and metabolism.

⚙️ Cellular Mechanism — Steps
  1. 1Cortisol enters cell by freely diffusing (lipophilic steroid hormone)
  2. 2Binds to inactive GRα in cytoplasm → conformational change → releases heat shock proteins (HSPs)
  3. 3Activated cortisol-GR complex translocates to nucleus → binds GREs (Glucocorticoid Response Elements) in DNA → acts as transcription factor
  4. 4↑Transcription of genes for gluconeogenesis, metabolism, anti-inflammatory proteins + suppresses pro-inflammatory genes

Synthetic Corticosteroids — Pharmacodynamics & Pharmacokinetics
Describe the pharmacodynamics (MOA) of synthetic corticosteroids (glucocorticoids and mineralocorticoids) and pharmacokinetics (ADME) of synthetic glucocorticoids
💊These are either natural adrenal cortex hormones or drug versions of them. Glucocorticoids mainly affect inflammation and metabolism; mineralocorticoids mainly affect salt, potassium, and water balance.
Drug ClassDrugsMOAOverall EffectAdverse EffectsExcretion
Glucocorticoids Cortisol · Prednisone · Triamcinolone · Dexamethasone Binds glucocorticoid receptors (GRs) in cytoplasm → complex → nucleus → regulates gene expression (activate or inhibit transcription) Anti-inflammatory · Immunosuppressive Adrenal atrophy · Osteoporosis · Diabetes · Cushingoid symptoms · Weight gain · Muscle wasting · HTN · Steroid psychosis Metabolized by CYP3A. CYP3A inducers ↓ levels
Mineralocorticoids Aldosterone · Fludrocortisone Binds mineralocorticoid receptors (MRs) in kidney, colon, sweat glands → ↑Na⁺ reabsorption + K⁺ excretion (upregulates Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase & ENaC in distal tubules) Electrolyte & fluid retention Fludrocortisone: Hypertension · Edema · Hypokalemia (due to Na⁺ retention and K⁺ excretion) Not extensively metabolized
⚠️ Key PK point: Glucocorticoids are metabolized by CYP3A. CYP3A inducers (rifampin, phenytoin, carbamazepine) decrease glucocorticoid levels → risk of treatment failure.

Superiority, Non-Inferiority & Equivalence Trials
Define the conditions for the application of superiority, non-inferiority, and equivalence trials
FeatureSuperiority TrialNon-Inferiority TrialEquivalence Trial
Characteristics Tests if new intervention is better than control Tests if new intervention is not unacceptably worse than standard Tests if new intervention is similar (neither significantly worse nor better)
Condition Novel vs non-standard intervention Novel vs standard intervention Novel vs standard (e.g., generic drug)
Control Placebo or non-standard Standard intervention Standard intervention
Hypothesis Type Two-tailed One-tailed Two-tailed
Statistical Range μ₁ − μ₀ ≠ 0 μ₁ − μ₀ > −Δ −Δ ≤ μ₁ − μ₀ ≤ Δ
Key to Hypothesis Effect size (d) Effect size (d) + clinically meaningful difference (Δ) Effect size (d) + clinically meaningful difference (Δ)
Analysis ITT (Intention-to-Treat) ITT + per-protocol ITT + per-protocol
Reporting P-value + CI Confidence Interval (CI) Confidence Interval (CI)
📘 Examples
Superiority Trial A pharmaceutical company develops a new BP medication and wants to test if it is more effective than an existing drug.
Non-Inferiority Trial A once-daily antibiotic vs a twice-daily standard antibiotic for pneumonia. Goal: new drug is not significantly worse, while offering better adherence and fewer side effects.
Equivalence Trial A generic inhaler manufacturer wants to show their drug delivers the same therapeutic effect as the brand-name inhaler within a predefined equivalence margin.
Key Distinction Superiority uses non-standard/placebo control
Non-inferiority & Equivalence use standard treatment control
PLO 12 📊
Plotting & Interpreting Trial Results
Describe the ways in which superiority, noninferiority, and equivalence trials are plotted and interpreted
📈Read the confidence interval (CI) against 0 and the trial margin Δ. Above 0 supports superiority, above −Δ supports non-inferiority, and entirely within −Δ to +Δ supports equivalence.
📷 CI Plots for Superiority, Non-Inferiority & Equivalence Trials
Superiority — CI vs 0
CI PositionInterpretation
CI entirely above 0New treatment is superior ✅
CI crosses 0No significant difference (superiority not proven) ❌
CI entirely below 0Control is superior ❌
Non-Inferiority — CI vs −Δ
CI PositionInterpretation
CI entirely above −ΔNon-inferiority is proven ✅
CI crosses −ΔNon-inferiority not proven ❌
CI entirely below −ΔNew treatment is inferior ❌
Equivalence — CI vs Both Boundaries (−Δ and +Δ)
CI PositionInterpretation
CI entirely within (−Δ, +Δ)Treatments are equivalent ✅
CI extends beyond −Δ or +ΔEquivalence is not proven ❌
🔑 Remember: For Superiority the boundary is 0. For Non-Inferiority the boundary is −Δ (one side only). For Equivalence the CI must fit inside both −Δ and +Δ.
