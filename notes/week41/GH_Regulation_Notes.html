Background

The Hypothalamic–Pituitary–GH Axis

The hypothalamus controls GH production by secreting two opposing signals to the anterior pituitary somatotrophs:

Hypothalamus
→ GHRH (+)
Anterior Pituitary
Somatotrophs
→ GH (+)
Liver & Tissues
→ IGF-1 (+)
Growth / Metabolism
Hypothalamus
→ Somatostatin (−)
Anterior Pituitary
Inhibits GH release
Neuron Locations

GHRH-secreting neurons → arcuate nuclei  |  Somatostatin neurons → periventricular nuclei
Ghrelin (gut hormone) also stimulates GH via the growth hormone secretagogue receptor (GHS-R)

GHRH Signal Transduction

GHRH binds to its receptor on somatotrophs → activates stimulatory G protein (Gαs) → ↑ adenylyl cyclase → ↑ cAMP → PKA activation → stimulates somatotroph proliferation + GH synthesis/secretion.

Somatostatin Receptors

5 known GPCR subtypes (adult pituitary expresses all except Type 4). Activation → ↓ intracellular cAMP and Ca²⁺ → net decrease in GH secretion.

GH Receptor

Member of the cytokine receptor superfamily; large extracellular and cytoplasmic domains. GH has two receptor-binding domains — one GH molecule binds one receptor, then the second binding site recruits a second receptor → homodimerization, which is essential for receptor activation. Downstream cascades include JAK2/STAT, MAPK, PI3K/IRS, and PLC pathways.

PLO 1 — Differentiate primary, secondary, and tertiary dysfunction in hypothalamic-pituitary signaling
PLO 1

Primary, Secondary & Tertiary Dysfunction

The level at which signaling breaks down determines the classification. Think of it as where in the axis the problem originates:

Level Site of Dysfunction GH Example Hormone Pattern
Primary The target endocrine gland itself (anterior pituitary / somatotrophs) Pituitary somatotroph adenoma (↑ GH); Pituitary damage → ↓ GH Abnormal end-hormone; feedback signals are high but do not suppress production
Secondary Anterior pituitary responding abnormally to hypothalamic input Pituitary fails to secrete GH despite normal GHRH; radiation-induced hypopituitarism ↓ GH; GHRH may be normal or elevated; IGF-1 low
Tertiary Hypothalamus fails to deliver appropriate releasing or inhibiting hormone Hypothalamic damage → ↓ GHRH → ↓ GH (or ↑ GHRH causing GH excess) ↓ GHRH → ↓ GH → ↓ IGF-1; or ↑ GHRH → ↑ GH → ↑ IGF-1

Clinical Pearl — Feedback Loops

In primary pituitary adenoma (acromegaly): negative feedback is intact — somatostatin ↑, GHRH ↓ — but the adenoma is autonomous and does not respond. GH remains elevated regardless.

Causes of Acquired Secondary/Tertiary GH Deficiency

  • Damage to pituitary and/or hypothalamus
  • Inadequate blood supply to anterior pituitary
  • Cranial irradiation
  • Intracranial tumors (e.g., craniopharyngioma)
  • Head trauma
  • Genetic disorders (congenital)

Potential Sources of GH Excess (Acromegaly/Gigantism)

  • (A) Normal axis — baseline
  • (B) Increased hypothalamic GHRH secretion
  • (C) Increased pituitary GH secretion (adenoma)
  • (D) Decreased somatostatin tone
  • Rarely: ectopic source of GH or GHRH
PLO 2 — Identify stimuli that evoke GH release and the effects of GH and IGF-1
PLO 2

Stimuli for GH Release & Physiologic Effects

Stimuli for GH Secretion

▲ Stimuli that INCREASE GH
  • Hypoglycemia / 2-deoxyglucose
  • Exercise
  • Fasting
  • ↑ Circulating amino acids (arginine infusion, protein meal)
  • Glucagon
  • Stressful stimuli / pyrogen / lysine vasopressin
  • Various psychologic stresses
  • Going to sleep (onset of deep sleep, stages 3/4)
  • L-Dopa & α-adrenergic agonists
  • Apomorphine & dopamine receptor agonists
  • Estrogens and androgens
  • Ghrelin (gut hormone, signals empty stomach)
▼ Stimuli that DECREASE GH
  • REM sleep
  • Glucose (hyperglycemia)
  • Cortisol
  • Free fatty acids (FFA)
  • Medroxyprogesterone
  • Growth hormone itself (negative feedback)
  • IGF-1 (long-loop negative feedback)
  • Somatostatin
Sleep Pattern

Adults have ~10 pulses of GH secretion per day, each lasting ~90 min, separated by ~128 min. Peak GH release occurs within 1 hour of onset of deep sleep (stages 3–4). PRL also rises after sleep onset but is not sleep-stage dependent.

Direct vs. Indirect (IGF-1 Mediated) Effects of GH

Direct Effects of GH

  • Na⁺ retention
  • Lipolysis (↑ free fatty acids)
  • ↓ Insulin sensitivity (anti-insulin)
  • Protein synthesis
  • Epiphyseal growth (direct)
  • Acts on cartilage stem cells → primes them to respond to IGF-1

Indirect Effects (via IGF-1)

  • Insulin-like activity (↑ glucose uptake)
  • Antilipolytic activity
  • Protein synthesis
  • Epiphyseal growth (major driver)
  • Balanced, symmetric growth (IGF-1 receptor expressed in many tissues)

Anti-Insulin (Diabetogenic) Effects of GH on Metabolism

GH antagonizes insulin's actions on peripheral tissues:

🧠 / 💪
Skeletal muscle
▲ Gluconeogenesis
▼ Glucose uptake
🫀
Liver
▲ Gluconeogenesis
▲ Glycogenolysis
▲ IGF-1 production
🫘
Adipose tissue
▼ Glucose uptake
▲ Lipolysis → ↑ FFA

Clinical nuance: GH itself raises plasma glucose (hyperglycemia), but GH also stimulates the liver to produce IGF-1, which increases glucose uptake in liver and muscle. Therefore a patient with chronically high GH levels may have near-normal plasma glucose because IGF-1 partially counteracts GH's diabetogenic effects.

IGF-1 Key Facts

  • Structure: Small peptide (MW 7647); closely related to insulin but C-chain is not separated and has a D-domain extension
  • Source: ~75% of circulating IGF-1 comes from the liver; also produced locally in tissues
  • Binding: >99% protein-bound in serum (unusual; extends half-life)
  • Regulation: Primarily regulated by GH; also by thyroxine (T4)
  • Receptors: Present in multiple cell types → accounts for symmetric, balanced growth
  • Feedback: IGF-1 feeds back negatively on both the hypothalamus (↑ somatostatin, ↓ GHRH) and directly on the pituitary
PLO 3 — Describe how stimulation tests are used to determine GH deficiency
PLO 3

Stimulation Tests for GH Deficiency

Because GH is secreted in pulses, a single random GH level is unreliable. Testing follows a stepwise approach:

  1. Measure serum IGF-1 — unlike GH, IGF-1 does not fluctuate hour-to-hour with food, exercise, stress, or sleep. It is the best screening test. A low IGF-1 (below gender/age-specific lower limit) in a patient with organic pituitary disease suggests GH deficiency.
  2. If IGF-1 is equivocal → proceed to a provocative (stimulation) test to confirm GH deficiency.
  3. Interpret results: A subnormal GH response to the provocative test confirms GH deficiency.

Commonly Used Provocative Tests

Test Mechanism Protocol Normal Response
Clonidine Stimulates GHRH release via α-adrenergic pathways Oral 5 mcg/kg (max 250 mcg); GH measured at 0, 30, 60, 90 min GH rises above threshold
Glucagon → transient hyperglycemia → endogenous insulin → controlled hypoglycemia → GH secretion IM injection; GH sampled serially GH rises in response to hypoglycemia
Arginine infusion Amino acid stimulates GHRH/GH release (inhibits somatostatin) IV 0.5 g/kg (max 40 g) over 30 min; GH at 0, 30, 60, 90, 120 min GH rises above threshold
Oral Glucose Tolerance Test (GH suppression — for excess) Hyperglycemia normally suppresses GH; used to confirm acromegaly/gigantism 75 g glucose; GH measured before and 2 hours after Normal: GH <1 ng/mL at 2 h
Acromegaly: GH stays >2 ng/mL in >85% of cases
Diagnosis of Acromegaly — Best Tests

Best single test: Serum IGF-1 (elevated in acromegaly). If equivocal, follow with an oral glucose tolerance test (GH suppression test). Failure of GH to suppress below 1 ng/mL at 2 hours = diagnostic of acromegaly. For pituitary gigantism, workup is the same; children who fail suppression → MRI to look for pituitary adenoma.

LO 1 — Differentiate consequences of decreased GH secretion before vs. after epiphyseal plate closure
LO 1

Decreased GH Secretion: Dwarfism vs. Adult GH Deficiency

🧒 BEFORE Epiphyseal Closure
GH Deficiency in Children — Dwarfism

Defining feature: Growth failure. Epiphyseal plates are still open → bones cannot elongate normally without adequate GH/IGF-1.

Causes

  • Damage to pituitary/hypothalamus
  • Inadequate blood supply to anterior pituitary
  • Genetic disorder (congenital)
  • Cranial irradiation
  • Tumor (e.g., craniopharyngioma)
  • Head trauma

Key Features

  • Growth failure / decreased height velocity — most important clinical sign
  • Delayed bone age
  • Congenital forms may not manifest until late infancy
  • Short stature (proportionate)
  • Possible associated hormone deficiencies (hypopituitarism)

Diagnosis

  • Serum IGF-1 (low)
  • Provocative tests if IGF-1 equivocal (clonidine, glucagon, arginine)
  • Growth charts — track height velocity carefully

Treatment

  • Synthetic human GH (recombinant; FDA approved 1985)
  • Trade names: Genotropin®, Humatrope®, Norditropin®, Nutropin®, Saizen®
  • Sogroya (somapacitan) FDA-approved 2020 for adults
🧑 AFTER Epiphyseal Closure
Adult GH Deficiency

Defining feature: No longitudinal bone growth possible (plates fused), but GH still plays important metabolic roles throughout life.

Note on Aging

GH production falls ~50% every 7 years after peak. IGF-1, testosterone, and DHEA all decline with age → contributes to sarcopenia and metabolic changes.

Key Clinical Features

  • Brain: ↑ Social isolation; ↓ quality of life, energy, drive, self-esteem
  • Cardiovascular: Abnormal cardiac structure/function; ↑ lipids & inflammatory markers; ↓ fibrinolysis; ↓ VO₂ max
  • Glucose metabolism: Insulin resistance
  • Adipose tissue: ↑ Fat mass & truncal obesity; ↓ lean body mass
  • Bone: ↑ Skeletal fragility & vertebral fractures; ↓ bone density
  • Skeletal muscle: ↓ Muscle mass (sarcopenia)
  • Hypothalamic-pituitary: ↓ Pituitary mass; secondary adrenal, thyroid, gonadal failure

Treatment

  • Recombinant GH replacement
  • Follow AACE/ACE guidelines for management
  • Note: also abused by athletes/bodybuilders for ↑ muscle mass, ↓ body fat
LO 2 — Differentiate consequences of increased GH secretion before vs. after epiphyseal plate closure
LO 2

Increased GH Secretion: Gigantism vs. Acromegaly

🧒 BEFORE Epiphyseal Closure
Pituitary Gigantism

GH excess occurring before fusion of epiphyseal growth plates → unrestrained longitudinal bone growth. By definition, only seen in growing children.

Causes (same as acromegaly)

  • Pituitary GH-secreting adenoma (most common)
  • Excessive GHRH production from hypothalamus
  • Rarely: ectopic source of GH or GHRH

Key Features

  • Abnormal height growth (typically precedes or concurrent with rapid weight gain)
  • Accelerated growth velocity
  • Most children with tall stature do not have pituitary gigantism — must distinguish
  • May also develop acromegalic features

Diagnosis

  • Serum IGF-1 (elevated)
  • GH suppression test (oral glucose tolerance test)
  • MRI of pituitary if biochemistry confirms excess

Treatment

  • Transsphenoidal surgery (initial therapy)
  • Pegvisomant (GH receptor antagonist)
  • Octreotide / Lanreotide (somatostatin analogs)
🧑 AFTER Epiphyseal Closure
Acromegaly

GH excess in adults (after plate fusion). No longitudinal growth possible → characteristic soft tissue and bone remodeling changes. Incidence: 6–8 per million/year; mean age at diagnosis: 40–45 years; avg time to diagnosis: ~12 years.

Most Common Cause

Somatotrophic macroadenoma of the anterior pituitary (~1/3 of all hormone-secreting pituitary adenomas). Onset is insidious; progression very slow.

Clinical Features

  • Facial: Prognathism (protruding lower jaw), maxillary widening, teeth separation & malocclusion, thickened nose/lips, frontal bossing
  • Hands/Feet: Enlargement, soft tissue thickening, increased bony tufts at distal phalanges — patients notice rings no longer fit
  • Bone/Cartilage: Hyperplasia of soft tissue & cartilage; increased osteoblast activity; increased protein synthesis → tissue/bone hyperplasia
  • Viscera: Thyroid, heart, liver enlargement
  • Skin: Thickening, soft tissue overgrowth (virtually all patients)
  • Endocrine: Menstrual dysfunction (most women); hyperprolactinemia in ~30% (cosecretion of PRL + GH by somatomammotroph); ↓ gonadotropins (macroadenoma compresses)
  • Metabolic: Insulin resistance; may have near-normal glucose (IGF-1 counteracts)
  • Feedback loop: ↑ Somatostatin + ↓ GHRH, but adenoma is autonomous — GH stays elevated

Diagnosis

  • Best single test: Serum IGF-1 (stable, not pulsatile)
  • If IGF-1 equivocal → Oral Glucose Tolerance Test (GH suppression test)
  • Normal: GH <1 ng/mL at 2 h post-glucose
  • Acromegaly: GH >2 ng/mL in >85% of patients

Treatment

  • 1st line: Transsphenoidal surgery
  • Pegvisomant (Somavert): GH receptor antagonist; efficacy monitored by IGF-1 (not GH)
  • Octreotide (Sandostatin): Somatostatin analog; longer half-life (2 h vs. 2 min for native SS); inhibits GH more effectively
  • Lanreotide (Somatuline): Also a somatostatin analog

Quick Reference

Drug Reference

For GH Excess (Acromegaly / Gigantism)

  • Pegvisomant (Somavert) — GH receptor antagonist; first-line medical option; monitor with IGF-1
  • Octreotide (Sandostatin) — Somatostatin analog; t½ = 2 h (vs. 2 min for native SS)
  • Lanreotide (Somatuline) — Somatostatin analog
  • Surgery: Transsphenoidal is preferred first-line for most patients

For GH Deficiency

  • Genotropin®, Humatrope®, Norditropin®, Nutropin®, Saizen®, Serostim® — recombinant human GH
  • Sogroya (somapacitan) — FDA approved Aug 2020 for adult GH deficiency; once-weekly dosing
  • Note: GH is commonly abused by athletes/bodybuilders — not an approved use
