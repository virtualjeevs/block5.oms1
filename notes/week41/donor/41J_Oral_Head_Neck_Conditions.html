Respiratory Diphtheria

An infectious disease caused by toxin-producing strains of the gram-positive bacillus Corynebacterium diphtheriae, leading to characteristic respiratory signs and potentially lethal systemic effects.

Epidemiology

  • Worldwide cases <8,000/year due to vaccination
  • Reemerging due to declining vaccine coverage
  • Four biotypes: gravis, intermedius, mitis, belfanti — all associated with endemic and epidemic disease
  • Primary transmission: respiratory secretions (droplets, fomites, skin lesions)
  • Peak transmission: winter and spring
  • Incubation: 2–5 days; can transmit for 2–6 weeks without antibiotics
  • Risk factors: travel to Latin America, Asia, Africa, Middle East; lack of vaccination

Etiology

  • Aerobic gram-positive rod
  • Humans are the sole reservoir
  • Only toxin-producing strains cause disease
  • Toxin gene carried by bacteriophage (β-phage)
  • Non-toxigenic strains cause local but not systemic disease

Pathophysiology

  • Localized inflammation of the respiratory tract with local and systemic toxin effects
  • Diphtheria toxin inactivates Elongation Factor-2 (EF-2) → blocks protein synthesis → cell death
  • Toxin binds membrane receptors → enters cells → ADP-ribosylates EF-2
  • Local effect: coalescing pseudomembrane of fibrin, dead cells, bacteria forms over tonsils/pharynx
  • Massive cervical swelling → "bull neck" appearance; respiratory stridor from airway compromise
  • Systemic dissemination → myocarditis (arrhythmia, heart block), neuropathy (cranial nerve palsies, demyelination), and renal tubular necrosis

Clinical Presentation

  • Early: Sore throat, malaise, low-grade fever, cervical lymphadenopathy
  • Progresses to: Gray-white exudates → confluent pseudomembrane that bleeds on removal
  • Massive edema of tonsils, uvula, submandibular and anterior neck → bull neck
  • Stridor and hoarseness from laryngeal involvement
  • Cardiac: arrhythmias, heart block (1–2 weeks post infection)
  • Neurologic: palatal palsy (nasal voice, regurgitation), cranial nerve palsies, peripheral neuropathy

Differential Diagnosis

  • Infectious mononucleosis
  • Group A streptococcal tonsillo-pharyngitis
  • Epiglottitis
  • Viral pharyngitis
  • Acute necrotizing ulcerative gingivitis
  • Oral candidiasis

Diagnosis

  • Throat culture identifying gram-positive rods in "Chinese character" arrangement on Gram stain
  • Loeffler's medium or tellurite agar preferred
  • Toxin testing (Elek test / PCR) differentiates toxigenic from non-toxigenic strains
  • Clinical diagnosis sufficient for immediate antitoxin treatment — do not wait for confirmation

Oral Leukoplakia

A firmly attached white plaque (hyperkeratotic tissue) of the oral mucosa — generally benign but classified as an Oral Potentially Malignant Disorder (OPMD) with variable risk of squamous cell carcinoma.

Epidemiology

  • Prevalence: 1.5–4.5% in the general population
  • More common in males and those over 40
  • Strongly associated with tobacco use (smoked and especially smokeless)
  • Also associated with alcohol consumption and HPV
  • Common (homogeneous) form: 3–15% risk of malignant transformation
  • Proliferative verrucous form: 70–100% risk of malignancy

Etiology & Risk Factors

  • WHO definition: "White plaques of questionable risk, having excluded other known diseases that carry no increased risk for cancer"
  • Risk factors mirror those for squamous cell carcinoma
  • Tobacco (especially smokeless), alcohol, trauma, HPV infection
  • Oral Hairy Leukoplakia (separate entity) = EBV in HIV-infected patients — not premalignant

Pathophysiology & Pathology

  • Irritation → hyperkeratosis (thickening of keratin layer) → white appearance
  • Also involves parakeratosis, acanthosis (thickened stratum spinosum), and epithelial atrophy
  • White color = hyperkeratosis of the epithelium (keratin blocks light transmission)
  • Low-grade dysplasia may be present
  • Histology: thickened stratified squamous epithelium with orderly cellular arrangement; expanded stratum spinosum
  • Genetic abnormalities found in both SCC and non-dysplastic leukoplakia — molecular changes precede histologic dysplasia

Clinical Presentation

  • White patches on oral mucosa cannot be wiped off with gauze (distinguishes from thrush)
  • Homogeneous: Uniformly white, thin, well-defined margins — lower malignant risk
  • Non-homogeneous: Speckled (erythroleukoplakia), nodular, or verrucous — higher malignant risk
  • Oral proliferative verrucous leukoplakia (OPVL): Rare, multifocal, aggressive — very high malignant potential
  • Generally asymptomatic; may occur anywhere in the oral cavity
  • High-risk sites: lateral border of tongue and floor of mouth

Differential Diagnosis

  • Frictional keratosis (mechanical irritation)
  • Oral lichen planus
  • Discoid lupus erythematosus
  • Submucous fibrosis
  • Oral candidiasis (thrush — can be wiped off)
  • Hairy leukoplakia (EBV/HIV)

Diagnosis

  • Clinical: attempt to wipe with gauze; if firmly adherent and unexplained — suspect leukoplakia
  • Eliminate mechanical causes for 6 weeks observation period
  • Definitive: Biopsy with histopathological grading of dysplasia
  • Dysplasia grade = primary predictor of malignant transformation risk

HSV Gingivostomatitis

Primary herpes simplex virus (HSV-1) infection of the oral mucosa and gingiva — the most common form of primary HSV in children, presenting as a painful vesiculoulcerative stomatitis with systemic prodrome.

Epidemiology

  • Primary herpetic gingivostomatitis: most common in children aged 6 months – 5 years
  • Second peak: early 20s
  • ~88% of HSV infections are asymptomatic
  • In adults, primary infection typically presents as pharyngitis
  • ~90% of oral cases caused by HSV-1
  • Lower socioeconomic status → seroconversion earlier in life
  • Worldwide seroprevalence of HSV-1 up to 80% in adults

Etiology

  • HSV-1: most common cause of oral herpes
  • HSV-2: primarily genital but can cause oral disease
  • Transmission: direct contact with oral secretions or lesions
  • Reactivation triggers: trauma, UV light, URTI, pregnancy, menstruation, immunosuppression, temperature extremes, allergies

Pathophysiology

  • HSV enters through breaks in mucocutaneous surfaces
  • Infects epithelial cells → causes cell lysis → vesicle formation
  • Retrograde axonal transport → virus travels along sensory nerves to trigeminal ganglion (for oral HSV-1)
  • Virus establishes latency in sensory ganglia — evades immune surveillance
  • Reactivation: virus travels anterograde back to mucocutaneous surface → recurrent lesions (herpes labialis)
  • Pathology: intraepithelial vesicles filled with clear serous fluid; rupture → shallow red-rimmed ulcers
  • Tzanck smear: eosinophilic intranuclear inclusions (Cowdry type A), multinucleated giant cells

Clinical Presentation

  • Incubation: 1–26 days
  • Prodrome (~4 days): Fever, malaise, decreased oral intake, cervical lymphadenopathy, oral pain
  • Eruption of microvesicular lesions throughout the oral cavity → rupture → painful shallow ulcerations
  • Inflamed, friable gingiva especially around the alveolar rim
  • Fever resolves in 3–5 days; lesions persist up to 21 days
  • Herpetic whitlow: vesicular lesions on fingers (autoinoculation)
  • Rare: HSV-1 spread to CNS → life-threatening viral meningoencephalitis

Differential Diagnosis

  • Aphthous ulcers
  • Recurrent herpes labialis (cold sores)
  • Herpes zoster
  • Epstein-Barr / CMV
  • Enterovirus (hand, foot & mouth — Coxsackievirus A)
  • Rubeola; HIV; oral syphilis

Diagnosis

  • Primarily a clinical diagnosis
  • PCR for HSV DNA (gold standard if lab confirmation needed)
  • Rapid direct immunofluorescence assay (DFA)
  • Serologic tests for IgG and IgM (useful for distinguishing primary vs. recurrent)
  • Tzanck test: smear of vesicle scraping — shows multinucleated giant cells and intranuclear inclusions

Tonsillitis

Inflammation of the palatine tonsils (GALT) due to viral or bacterial infection. The most clinically significant form is Group A beta-hemolytic Streptococcus (GABHS) tonsillitis due to its serious non-suppurative complications.

Epidemiology

  • Acute viral tonsillitis: peak in children <5 years and young adults
  • Acute bacterial tonsillitis (GABHS): peak 5–15 years; rare <2 years
  • Peak seasons: winter and spring
  • Extremely common — accounts for ~15% of all GP visits in children

Etiology

  • Viral (most common): Rhinovirus, RSV, adenovirus, coronavirus; less commonly EBV, CMV, Hep A, rubella, HIV
  • Bacterial: Group A beta-hemolytic Streptococcus (GABHS / S. pyogenes); also S. aureus, S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae
  • Unvaccinated children: Corynebacterium diphtheriae

Pathophysiology

  • Pathogen (viral or bacterial) infects tonsils → inflammatory response with cytokine upregulation
  • Viral: virus infiltrates tonsil epithelium → local inflammatory cascade
  • Bacterial (GABHS): penetrates mucosa → cytokine storm; M-protein of GABHS is key virulence factor (antiphagocytic)
  • Lymphoid hyperplasia → tonsillar enlargement
  • Desquamated epithelial cells + lymphoid debris collect in crypts → cryptic tonsillitis
  • Calcification of crypt debris → tonsillolith

Clinical Presentation

  • Acute bacterial (GABHS): Sudden onset fever, severe sore throat, dysphagia, pharyngeal/tonsillar erythema & edema, tonsillar exudates, cervical lymphadenitis — no cough
  • Acute viral: Cough, coryza, rhinorrhea, oral ulcers, anterior stomatitis, conjunctivitis, diarrhea — upper respiratory features
  • GABHS complications: Rheumatic fever (2–4 wks), glomerulonephritis (1–2 wks), scarlet fever, PANDAS, septic arthritis, peritonsillar abscess

Differential Diagnosis

  • Aphthous stomatitis (ulcers on anterior mucosa; no systemic symptoms)
  • Herpangina (posterior oropharyngeal lesions, Coxsackievirus)
  • Ludwig angina (submandibular space infection)
  • Infectious mononucleosis (EBV; splenomegaly; heterophile Ab+)
  • Peritonsillar abscess

Diagnosis — Modified Centor Score

  • +1: Age 3–14 yrs | Tonsillar exudate/swelling | Tender anterior cervical nodes | Temp >38°C (100.4°F) | Absence of cough
  • 0: Age 15–44 yrs | −1: Age >44 yrs
  • Score ≤1: No further workup needed
  • Score 2–3: RADT ± throat culture
  • Score ≥4: Empiric antibiotic therapy
  • RADT positive → initiate antibiotics; RADT negative → throat culture to rule out GABHS
Illness Script

Cleft Palate

A congenital craniofacial anomaly resulting from failure of midline fusion of embryonic facial processes during weeks 5–12 of gestation. May occur in isolation or as part of a broader syndrome.

Epidemiology

  • Cleft lip ± palate (CL[P]): ~1/1,000 live births (most common)
  • Isolated cleft palate (CP): ~1/2,000 live births
  • Unilateral CL/P more common than bilateral
  • No racial heterogeneity for isolated CP; CL[P] shows marked racial differences:
  • Asian & Native American: 1:500 | White: 1:1,000 | African descent: 1:1,500–2,000
  • CP more likely to be syndromic than CL[P]

Etiology & Risk Factors

  • Genetic: Multifactorial inheritance; defects in TGF-β transcription cascades; chromosomal: Trisomy 13 (Patau), Trisomy 18 (Edwards), Pierre Robin Sequence
  • Environmental / Teratogenic:
  • Nicotine and/or alcohol during pregnancy
  • Anti-epileptics: phenytoin, valproate, carbamazepine, phenobarbital
  • Folate antagonists: methotrexate, trimethoprim
  • Excessive Vitamin A intake during pregnancy

Pathophysiology — Embryological Basis

  • Face forms from fusion of 5 embryonic prominences (derived from 1st pharyngeal arch & neural crest cells) — weeks 5–12
  • Upper lip: Maxillary prominences fuse with lateral then medial nasal prominences (week 5) → failure = cleft lip
  • Lower lip/mandible: Fusion of right + left mandibular processes
  • Primary palate: Fusion of medial nasal prominences → failure = anterior cleft
  • Secondary palate: Two lateral palatine processes fuse on midline (weeks 8–12) → failure = cleft palate
  • Key: secondary palate formation requires palatal shelves to elevate horizontally — tongue descent is critical for this

Clinical Presentation

  • Submucosal cleft palate: Mucosa intact; underlying muscular/bony defect — may present later with speech abnormalities or OME
  • Incomplete cleft palate: Clefting of secondary palate only
  • Complete cleft palate: Entire hard palate, soft palate, and uvula; often with cleft lip
  • Clinical features: facial deformities, feeding difficulties (inability to create suction), speech difficulties (hypernasal voice, VPD), dentition defects, conductive hearing loss (Eustachian tube dysfunction → recurrent OME)

Associated Syndromes (DDx)

  • Pierre Robin Sequence: Cleft palate + glossoptosis + mandibular retrognathia
  • Trisomy 13 (Patau): Microcephaly, cleft lip/palate, microphthamia, polydactyly, cardiac defects
  • Trisomy 18 (Edwards): Prominent occiput, low-set ears, micrognathia, clenched fists, rocker-bottom feet, cardiac/renal defects

Diagnosis

  • Prenatal: Ultrasound detects CL/P after 12 weeks; amniocentesis for karyotyping
  • Postnatal: Clinical inspection of hard/soft palate; digital palpation for submucosal clefts
  • Later presentation of submucosal: abnormal speech, recurrent OME
  • Management: multidisciplinary team — surgery at 6–12 months, speech therapy, dental/orthodontic care, ENT
