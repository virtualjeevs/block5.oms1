Types of teeth & dental formulae

🦷
Incisors
8 permanent · 8 deciduous
Chisel-shaped single-rooted teeth at the front. Thin sharp cutting edge.
Function: cutting / biting
🦷
Canines
4 permanent · 4 deciduous
Single cusp, single root. Longest tooth. Located at the corners of the dental arch.
Function: tearing / piercing
🦷
Premolars
8 permanent only
Two cusps (bi-cuspid). 1–2 roots. Absent in the primary dentition — replace the primary molars.
Function: crushing / grinding
🦷
Molars
12 permanent · 8 deciduous
Multiple cusps. Upper: 3 roots; lower: 2 roots. Third molars = wisdom teeth (may be absent).
Function: grinding / chewing
Permanent: 2·1·2·3 / 2·1·2·3 × 2 = 32
Incisors · Canines · Premolars · Molars — upper jaw / lower jaw × bilateral

Primary (deciduous) dentition

  • 20 teeth total
  • Formula: 2·1·0·2 / 2·1·0·2
  • No premolars
  • Eruption begins ~6 months; complete ~2–3 years
  • Shed and replaced by age 12–13
  • Smaller, whiter, and less mineralised than permanent teeth

Permanent (secondary) dentition

  • 32 teeth total (28 if wisdom absent)
  • Formula: 2·1·2·3 / 2·1·2·3
  • First permanent tooth: first molar (~age 6)
  • Wisdom teeth: ~17–25 years (may be impacted or absent)
  • Larger, more yellow due to dentin showing through enamel
Crown vs root: The anatomical crown is the portion covered by enamel. The clinical crown is the portion visible above the gum. The root is the portion embedded in the alveolar socket, covered by cementum.

Microstructure of a tooth

A tooth is a mineralized structure consisting of four main tissues. Working from outside inward:

Enamel Crown only
Hardest biological substance known. Formed by ameloblasts (ectodermal origin). Acellular once tooth erupts — ameloblasts are lost at eruption, so enamel cannot regenerate. Composed of calcium hydroxyapatite (96–97% mineral). Formed in rods (prisms) running from the dentinoenamel junction (DEJ) to the tooth surface. The DEJ is scalloped, interlocking enamel and dentin.
Dentin Crown + Root
Less hard than enamel (~70% mineral). Formed by odontoblasts (mesodermal origin). Odontoblasts persist throughout life — they line the pulp cavity and continue to form dentin slowly. Contains dentinal tubules housing odontoblast processes (Tomes' fibres). Predentin = unmineralised dentin just inside the pulp. Types: primary (during development), secondary (lifelong deposition), tertiary/reparative (response to injury).
Cementum Root only
Bone-like tissue (~50% mineral). Formed by cementoblasts. Covers dentin of the root. Anchors the periodontal ligament (Sharpey's fibres) to the root surface. The cementoenamel junction (CEJ) marks the border between enamel and cementum — a key anatomical landmark. Acellular cementum (primary) near the crown; cellular cementum (secondary) near the root apex.
Pulp cavity Core
Contains loose connective tissue, blood vessels, lymphatics, and nerves — the only innervated region of the tooth. The pulp chamber occupies the coronal portion; root canals extend into each root. Neurovascular bundles enter through the apical foramen at the root tip. The pulp is supplied by branches of the inferior and superior alveolar nerves and vessels. All dentinal pain is sensed via pulp nerves (hydrodynamic theory).
Remember: Only the pulp is innervated. Enamel and dentin contain no nerve fibres. Pain from exposed dentin is explained by the hydrodynamic theory — fluid movement within dentinal tubules stimulates pulp nerve fibres.
Clinical pearl: Enamel cannot regenerate after eruption. Fluoride works by substituting fluorapatite for hydroxyapatite, making enamel more acid-resistant — this is why it prevents caries, not by regenerating enamel.
3

Surrounding structures

🦴
Alveolar bone (alveolar process)
The tooth socket — the bony housing of the tooth root. Contains the cribriform plate (bundle bone) which is highly vascular and perforated for neurovascular supply. Remodels throughout life in response to mechanical loading (basis of orthodontics). Resorbs when a tooth is lost.
🔗
Periodontal ligament (PDL)
Dense fibrous connective tissue occupying the periodontal space between cementum and alveolar bone. Contains Sharpey's fibres (principal fibres) that anchor into both cementum and bone. Provides cushioning and proprioception. Contains cementoblasts, osteoblasts, fibroblasts, and undifferentiated mesenchymal stem cells. ~0.15–0.38 mm wide.
🩸
Gingiva (gum)
Masticatory mucosa covering the alveolar ridge and surrounding the necks of teeth. Composed of keratinised stratified squamous epithelium. The gingival sulcus is the shallow groove between the free gingiva and the tooth (≤3 mm normal). Junctional epithelium attaches to the enamel/cementum at the base of the sulcus.
🔵
Apical foramen
Small opening at the tip of each root through which the neurovascular bundle enters the pulp cavity. Infection spreading beyond the apex causes periapical abscess. The foramen narrows with age as secondary cementum deposits.
Periodontium = the collective term for all supporting structures: gingiva + PDL + cementum + alveolar bone. Periodontitis is inflammation/destruction of the periodontium.

Tooth development (odontogenesis)

Begins as an interaction between the ectoderm of the alveolar ridge (dental lamina) and the underlying mesenchyme overlying the alveolar bone.

1. Initiation — Dental lamina forms
Ectoderm of alveolar ridge thickens to form the dental lamina. This is the origin of all teeth in the arch.
2. Bud stage
Dental lamina forms ectodermal buds that proliferate into the underlying mesenchyme. One bud per tooth.
3. Cap stage — Enamel organ forms
The bud expands to partially surround a condensation of mesenchyme (the dental papilla), forming a cap-shaped enamel organ. Three layers: outer dental epithelium, stellate reticulum, inner dental epithelium.
4. Bell stage — Differentiation of key cells
Inner dental epithelium differentiates into ameloblasts. The dental papilla cells adjacent to ameloblasts differentiate into odontoblasts. The two cell layers face each other across the future dentinoenamel junction (DEJ).
5. Apposition — Matrix secretion
Ameloblasts secrete enamel matrix inward (toward the DEJ). Odontoblasts secrete predentin outward. As mineral is deposited, the two rows of cells move apart — enamel grows outward, dentin grows inward.
6. Root formation — Root sheath & cementum
At the future CEJ, the enamel organ layers fuse to form Hertwig's epithelial root sheath, which grows inward around the root dentin. Connective tissue cells along the root sheath differentiate into cementoblasts, laying down cementum. The dental papilla becomes enclosed as the pulp.
7. Eruption
The tooth erupts through the gingiva. Ameloblasts are stripped off at eruption → enamel formation ends permanently. Deciduous teeth are eventually lost as permanent successors develop within the alveolar ridge.
Origin summary: Enamel = ectodermal origin (ameloblasts). Dentin, pulp, cementum, PDL, alveolar bone = mesodermal / neural crest (ectomesenchyme) origin.

10 Practice MCQs

1 Which cell type is responsible for enamel formation, and what happens to these cells upon tooth eruption?
  • A Odontoblasts; they persist lining the pulp cavity
  • B Ameloblasts; they are lost at eruption, so no new enamel can be formed
  • C Cementoblasts; they migrate to cover the crown
  • D Fibroblasts; they are replaced by keratinocytes
Ameloblasts (ectodermal origin, from inner dental epithelium) secrete enamel matrix during tooth development. At eruption, the ameloblast layer is stripped off — which is why enamel cannot be regenerated after a tooth erupts. This is a key exam fact!
2 A patient complains of pain in a lower molar triggered by cold water but no spontaneous pain. Histologically, which region of the tooth mediates this pain sensation?
  • A Enamel — direct nerve endings in enamel rods
  • B Dentin — nerve fibres within dentinal tubules
  • C Pulp — nerves stimulated via fluid movement in dentinal tubules (hydrodynamic theory)
  • D Cementum — nociceptors in Sharpey's fibres
The pulp is the only innervated part of the tooth. Enamel and dentin contain no nerves. Pain from dentinal stimuli is explained by the hydrodynamic theory: thermal or osmotic changes cause fluid movement in dentinal tubules → stimulates odontoblast processes → detected by pulp nerve fibres at the pulp-dentin boundary.
3 Which of the following correctly describes the cementoenamel junction (CEJ)?
  • A The junction between enamel and dentin on the crown
  • B The attachment of the periodontal ligament to alveolar bone
  • C The base of the gingival sulcus
  • D The border between enamel (crown) and cementum (root) — the anatomical crown-root junction
The CEJ (cementoenamel junction) is where enamel and cementum meet, marking the anatomical boundary between the crown and root. It is an important clinical landmark — gingival recession exposes cementum below the CEJ, which is much softer and more vulnerable to erosion than enamel.
4 During tooth development, the dental papilla is significant because it:
  • A Gives rise to odontoblasts (which form dentin) and ultimately becomes the pulp
  • B Differentiates into ameloblasts to form enamel
  • C Forms the periodontal ligament fibres
  • D Becomes the cementum of the root
The dental papilla is a condensation of mesenchyme enclosed within the enamel organ. Its peripheral cells differentiate into odontoblasts (which form dentin), while its central cells become trapped within the forming dentin and form the pulp tissue. The PDL and cementum arise from the dental follicle (sac), not the papilla.
5 How many teeth are present in the complete adult permanent dentition?
  • A 20
  • B 32
  • C 28
  • D 24
The complete permanent dentition has 32 teeth: 8 incisors + 4 canines + 8 premolars + 12 molars. If third molars (wisdom teeth) are absent or impacted and removed, this reduces to 28. The primary dentition has 20 teeth (no premolars, only 8 molars).
6 Sharpey's fibres are a key histological feature of which structure?
  • A Enamel, anchoring it to the dentin
  • B Pulp, forming its fibrous core
  • C Periodontal ligament, embedded into both cementum and alveolar bone
  • D Gingival epithelium, anchoring it to the tooth
Sharpey's fibres are bundles of collagen that are embedded (inserted) into both cementum on the root side and alveolar bone on the other side. They are the principal fibres of the periodontal ligament and are responsible for suspending and cushioning the tooth within its bony socket.
7 A patient requires a mandibular block for extraction of a lower first molar. Which nerve is primarily targeted?
  • A Posterior superior alveolar nerve (V2)
  • B Lingual nerve (V3) at its origin
  • C Mental nerve
  • D Inferior alveolar nerve (V3) at the mandibular foramen
The inferior alveolar nerve (branch of V3 — mandibular division) enters the mandibular canal via the mandibular foramen on the medial surface of the ramus. Blocking it here anaesthetises all ipsilateral lower teeth, lower lip, and chin. The posterior superior alveolar nerve (V2) supplies the upper molars. The mental nerve is the terminal branch of the inferior alveolar nerve, supplying the chin and lower lip only.
8 Which of the following tooth types is absent from the primary (deciduous) dentition but present in the permanent dentition?
  • A Premolars
  • B Canines
  • C Incisors
  • D First molars
Premolars do not exist in the primary dentition (formula 2·1·0·2). They erupt at around ages 10–12 and replace the primary molars. The primary dentition has 20 teeth; canines, incisors, and molars (though primary molars are replaced by premolars) are all present in both dentitions.
9 Hertwig's epithelial root sheath is important in tooth development because it:
  • A Secretes enamel matrix on the crown surface
  • B Determines root shape and induces cementoblast differentiation
  • C Forms the periodontal ligament principal fibres
  • D Produces the dental lamina for the permanent tooth bud
Hertwig's epithelial root sheath forms when the layers of the enamel organ fuse at the future CEJ and grow inward to mould the shape of the root(s). It induces the overlying mesenchymal cells to differentiate into cementoblasts, which deposit cementum. Gaps (fenestrations) in the sheath allow cementoblasts to reach the dentin surface. The shape of the sheath determines single vs. multi-rooted morphology.
10 On a histological section you observe a tissue lining the root canal containing loose connective tissue, collagen fibres, blood vessels, and nerve fibres. The peripheral cells appear tall and columnar with processes extending into adjacent mineralised tissue. What are these peripheral cells?
  • A Ameloblasts secreting enamel matrix
  • B Cementoblasts depositing cementum
  • C Odontoblasts lining the pulp cavity and sending processes into dentinal tubules
  • D Fibroblasts of the periodontal ligament
Odontoblasts are tall columnar cells lining the pulp cavity. Each sends a long cytoplasmic process (Tomes' fibre / odontoblast process) into a dentinal tubule within the mineralised dentin. They form dentin throughout life and persist in the pulp. The description — tall columnar cells with processes extending into an adjacent mineralised tissue, found lining a central cavity — is the classic histological description of odontoblasts.
