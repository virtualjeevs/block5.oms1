PLO: Function of taste and smell in monitoring the chemical environment

  • Perceive qualities & intensities of taste and odor

Olfaction — Smell

PLO: Describe mechanisms for how odors are transmitted from olfactory neuron cilia to brain perception
  • Olfactory epithelium: roof of nasal cavity, cribiform plate
  • Olfactory receptor cells = neurons → axons = CN I
  • Bowman's glands produce mucus; basal cells enable regeneration
  • ORC lifespan 4–8 weeks — one of very few CNS neurons that regenerate
  • Receptor cilia extend into mucus layer — site of odorant binding

Anatomy of the Olfactory Epithelium

The olfactory epithelium is located on the roof of the nasal cavity, on both sides of the nasal septum, and lies against the thin, perforated cribiform plate of the skull base.

🔑 Key Location Olfactory epithelium → roof of nasal cavity → cribiform plate → ventral forebrain (anterior fossa)

Histology of the Olfactory Epithelium

Olfactory Receptor Cells

Are neurons — their axons bundle to form CN I (olfactory nerve). Contain olfactory cilia extending into mucus, and a cell body in the nasal mucosa. Lifespan: 4–8 weeks.

Bowman's Glands

Olfactory glands that produce mucus — odorants must dissolve in this mucus to reach receptor proteins on the cilia.

Basal Cells

Stem cells that support lifelong renewal of the olfactory epithelium. ORCs are one of very few CNS neuron types capable of regeneration.

Supporting Cells

Provide structural and metabolic support to the olfactory receptor cells within the epithelium.

🏥 Clinical Pearl Because olfactory receptor cells regenerate (basal cells replace them every 4–8 weeks), anosmia from damaged ORCs can sometimes recover — unlike most CNS neuron damage. This is clinically significant for head trauma patients.
⚠️ Key Distinction Olfactory receptor cells = neurons (unique — most sensory receptors are NOT neurons). Taste receptor cells = NOT neurons (epithelial cells that synapse onto neurons). Know this distinction!
Exam Q

Where are olfactory receptor cell bodies located, and what structure do their axons form?

Answer: Cell bodies are in the nasal mucosa (olfactory epithelium). Their axons pass through the cribiform plate and bundle together to form CN I (the olfactory nerve), synapsing in the olfactory bulb.

Odor Signal Transduction

PLO: Transduction cascade from odorant binding to action potential in CN I
  • Odorant dissolves in mucus → binds GPCR on olfactory cilia
  • GPCR → Golf → adenylyl cyclase → cAMP (second messenger)
  • cAMP → opens cyclic nucleotide-gated Na⁺/Ca²⁺ channels → depolarization
  • Ca²⁺ → Ca²⁺-activated Cl⁻ channels → amplification of depolarization
  • Receptor potential → action potentials in CN I axons
  • Response adapts in ~1 minute even with continued odorant presence
💡 Core Principle There are specific GPCRs for detecting different odors. Each olfactory receptor cell expresses only ONE type of odorant receptor gene.

Step-by-Step Cascade

  1. 1Odorant dissolves in mucus (from Bowman's glands) and binds to GPCR (odorant receptor protein) on cilia membrane
  2. 2GPCR activates Golf (α-subunit) → stimulates adenylyl cyclase
  3. 3Adenylyl cyclase converts ATP → cAMP (second messenger)
  4. 4cAMP binds to cyclic nucleotide-gated (CNG) channels → opens channels → influx of Na⁺ and Ca²⁺
  5. 5Elevated intracellular Ca²⁺ opens Ca²⁺-activated Cl⁻ channels → Cl⁻ exits cell (amplification step)
  6. 6Combined ion flux causes membrane depolarization → graded receptor potential
  7. 7Sufficient depolarization → action potentials transmitted in CN I (olfactory neuron axons)
🔑 Amplification Cl⁻ acts as an amplifier of the olfactory stimulus. Ca²⁺ influx → Ca²⁺-activated Cl⁻ channels open → Cl⁻ efflux → additional depolarization. This boosts sensitivity significantly.

Why the Response Terminates

The olfactory response may terminate without producing an action potential for several reasons:

  • Diffusion: Odorants may diffuse away from the cilia
  • Enzymatic breakdown: Scavenger enzymes in the mucus degrade odorants
  • cAMP signaling: Elevated cAMP can activate other intracellular signaling pathways that terminate transduction
  • Adaptation (~1 min): Even with continued odorant exposure, receptor response adapts — explains why you stop smelling your own perfume
🏥 Clinical Relevance This adaptation (~1 minute) is clinically important: patients with continuous odor hallucinations (olfactory auras) are NOT experiencing adaptation — something is wrong centrally (e.g., seizure activity in olfactory cortex = uncinate fits).
Exam Q

What is the role of Cl⁻ channels in olfactory transduction, and which second messenger triggers the cascade?

Answer: cAMP is the second messenger. Cl⁻ channels are opened by elevated intracellular Ca²⁺ (which entered via CNG channels) and amplify the depolarizing signal — Cl⁻ exits, adding to membrane depolarization beyond what Ca²⁺/Na⁺ influx alone provides.

Odorant Receptor Specificity

Humans have ~400 functional odorant receptor genes (out of ~800 total — about half are pseudogenes). Each olfactory receptor neuron expresses only one type of receptor. The brain perceives odor identity by integrating the pattern of which glomeruli are activated.

Olfactory Pathway to the Brain

PLO: Olfactory pathway from receptor to cortical perception
  • 1°: Olfactory receptor neurons (SVA neurons) → CN I → olfactory bulb
  • 2°: Mitral cells in olfactory bulb → olfactory stria/tract
  • Targets: Pyriform cortex, olfactory tubercle, amygdala, entorhinal cortex
  • UNIQUE: reaches cerebral cortex WITHOUT synapsing in thalamus first
  • Glomeruli are receptor-type specific (one receptor gene per glomerulus)
  • Further connections: hypothalamus, hippocampal formation → odor-associated memory

Olfactory Bulb — The First Relay

The olfactory bulb is a relay structure that receives input from olfactory nerve axons and modulates their activity before sending signals to higher brain centers.

🔑 Glomerular Specificity Each glomerulus in the olfactory bulb receives input only from olfactory receptor cells expressing the same receptor protein gene. This is how odor identity is maintained — spatial mapping of receptor types onto bulb glomeruli.

Pathway Summary

1° → Olfactory receptor neurons (SVA neurons in nasal mucosa) → axons through cribiform plate → form CN I (olfactory nerve)
Relay → Synapse in olfactory bulb glomeruli (with mitral cells & tufted cells)
2° → Mitral/tufted cells → olfactory tract/stria → olfactory areas
Cortex → Pyriform cortex, periamygdaloid cortex, olfactory tubercle, amygdala → perception & emotion
Further → Thalamus (mediodorsal nucleus) → orbitofrontal cortex (conscious discrimination of odors)
Memory → Entorhinal cortex → hippocampal formation → odor-associated memory; Hypothalamus → ANS, feeding
⚡ Unique Feature — MUST KNOW Olfaction is unique among all sensory pathways: odor information reaches the cerebral cortex WITHOUT first synapsing in the thalamus. This is why smell is so powerfully linked to emotion and memory (direct access to amygdala and hippocampus).
Exam Q

How does the olfactory pathway differ from all other sensory pathways regarding thalamic relay?

Answer: All other senses (vision, hearing, touch, taste) relay through the thalamus before reaching the cortex. Olfactory signals from the olfactory bulb project directly to piriform cortex, amygdala, and other areas WITHOUT thalamic relay. This explains direct smell-emotion and smell-memory connections.

Anterior Olfactory Nucleus

Signals from the olfactory tract also synapse in the anterior olfactory nucleus, which sends projections to the contralateral olfactory bulb — allowing cross-hemisphere communication and modulation of olfactory sensitivity.

Olfactory Disorders

PLO: Clinical deficits in olfaction — causes and presentations
  • Anosmia: aging, nasal obstruction, epithelium damage, head trauma (CN I shear at cribiform), anterior cranial fossa tumors
  • Trauma: 7–30% of head injuries → shearing of olfactory cilia at cribiform plate
  • Uncinate fits: olfactory cortex lesion → seizures with olfactory auras + involuntary lip/tongue movements
  • Hyposmia: Parkinson's (90% affected, <15% aware), Alzheimer's, chorea
  • COVID-19: sudden onset anosmia is a characteristic symptom
Disorder Definition Causes / Notes
Anosmia Complete loss of odor sensation
  • Aging (most common overall cause)
  • Nasal obstruction (viral/allergic rhinitis, polyps, neoplasm)
  • Epithelial damage (surgery, topical drugs, pollutants)
  • Trauma: 7–30% of head injuries shear olfactory cilia (CN I) at cribiform plate
  • Tumors of anterior cranial fossa floor (orbital surface of frontal lobe) → pressure on olfactory bulb/tract
  • COVID-19 — sudden onset anosmia
Hyposmia Reduced odor perception Parkinson's Disease (90% of patients have demonstrable olfactory loss — <15% are aware of it until tested!), Alzheimer's, chorea — due to alterations in olfactory brain regions
Uncinate Fits Seizures with olfactory auras Lesions of olfactory cortex → seizures characterized by sensation of odors (olfactory auras) AND involuntary movements of lips & tongue
🏥 Parkinson's Pearl About 90% of Parkinson's Disease patients have a demonstrable olfactory loss, yet less than 15% are aware of the problem until specifically tested. Olfactory testing may serve as an early biomarker for PD.
⚠️ Trauma + Anosmia Head trauma causing anosmia indicates shearing of olfactory cilia at the cribiform plate — the axons of CN I are thin and unmyelinated, making them vulnerable to even mild trauma. Anterior cranial fossa fractures are a major concern. Test for anosmia after any significant head injury.
🦠 COVID-19 Sudden-onset anosmia became a hallmark symptom of COVID-19, likely due to damage to sustentacular (supporting) cells in the olfactory epithelium, which in turn damages olfactory receptor cells indirectly. Recovery is possible given regenerative capacity of the olfactory epithelium.
Exam Q

A patient sustains a head injury and subsequently cannot smell. What specific anatomical mechanism explains this?

Answer: Head trauma causes shearing of olfactory nerve fibers (CN I) at the cribiform plate. The thin, unmyelinated olfactory nerve axons pass through small foramina in the cribiform plate and are vulnerable to shearing forces during head injury, disconnecting the olfactory epithelium from the olfactory bulb. Occurs in 7–30% of significant head injuries.

Gustation — Taste

PLO: Anatomy and structure of the gustatory system
  • 5 primary tastes: bitter, sour, sweet, salty, umami (± rancid fat)
  • All tastes detectable over entire tongue — but regional threshold differences
  • Bitter: posterior 1/3; Sweet: anterior 2/3
  • CN VII (anterior 2/3 tongue) · CN IX (posterior 1/3, pharynx, palate) · CN X (epiglottis)
  • Papillae types: Fungiform, Foliate, Vallate (all have taste buds) · Filiform (NO taste buds — touch only)
  • Taste buds: ~2 week lifespan · 50–150 taste receptor cells/bud
  • Taste receptor cells are NOT neurons (unlike olfactory receptor cells)

The 5 Primary Taste Qualities

Bitter Alkaloids, toxic compounds Posterior 1/3 Type II (TAS2Rs GPCRs)
Sour H⁺ (acids) Sides Type III (proton channels)
Sweet Sugars, artificial sweeteners Anterior 2/3 (tip) Type II (TAS1R2/TAS1R3 GPCR)
Salty Na⁺ salts Anterior sides Type I (ENaC Na⁺ channels)
Umami Glutamate (MSG) Posterior/base Type II (TAS1R1/TAS1R3 GPCR)

📌 Important All tastes can be detected over the entire tongue surface. However, different regions have different thresholds (sensitivity) — the "tongue map" refers to sensitivity, not exclusive localization.

Taste is Not Just the Tongue

Taste receptors are also found in the:

  • Palate — CN IX
  • Pharynx — CN IX
  • Epiglottis — CN X
  • Nasal cavity: odors from food pass through pharynx → nasal cavity → detected by olfactory receptors → perceived as "taste" (actually retronasal olfaction)

Cranial Nerve Innervation

CN VII — Facial

Anterior 2/3 of tongue. Cell bodies in geniculate ganglion (medial wall tympanic cavity in temporal bone).

CN IX — Glossopharyngeal

Posterior 1/3 of tongue, pharynx, palate. Cell bodies in inferior (petrosal) ganglion (jugular fossa).

CN X — Vagus

Epiglottis region. Cell bodies in inferior (nodose) ganglion (jugular fossa).

Papillae of the Tongue

Papilla Type Taste Buds? Location Notes
Fungiform Yes (3–5 per papilla) Tip & sides (highest density) Scattered all over tongue
Foliate Yes (numerous) Sides, towards back of tongue Leaf-like folds
Vallate (Circumvallate) Yes (high number) Base of tongue (row of ~7–12) Largest papillae; nearer the throat
Filiform NO All over tongue Touch sensation only — most numerous

Taste Bud Structure

Taste buds are embedded in trenches around papillae. They contain:

Taste Receptor Cells

Produce receptor potentials (membrane voltage shifts) in response to tastants. Are NOT neurons. Lifespan: ~2 weeks. 50–150 per taste bud.

Basal Cells

Stem cells → continuously replace taste receptor cells. Explains fast TRC renewal (unlike neurons).

Supporting Cells

Structural support for the taste bud architecture.

Afferent Axon Synapses

Primary afferents (CN VII, IX, X) branch inside the taste bud and synapse at the basal region of several taste receptor cells.

💡 Taste Receptor Cell Properties Individual taste receptor cells can be influenced by multiple tastants but show a preference (bias) for one. The brain interprets taste quality from the pattern of activity across many taste cells and their afferent fibers — not a labeled-line code.

Taste Signal Transduction

PLO: Mechanisms of taste transduction by tastant class
  • Salt (Type I): Na⁺ → ENaC (amiloride-sensitive) → depolarization → Ca²⁺ → serotonin release
  • Sour (Type III): H⁺ → proton channels (otopterin 1) + block K⁺ channels → depolarization → serotonin
  • Bitter/Sweet/Umami/Kokumi (Type II): GPCR → PLC → IP3 → Ca²⁺ from stores → TRPM4/5 → Na⁺ → ATP release via CALHM1/3 → P2X2/X3 receptors on afferent axon
  • Amiloride blocks ENaC → reduces salt taste (diuretic side effect)

Type I — Salt Transduction

🧂 Salt (Na⁺) Na⁺ enters through ENaC (Epithelial Na⁺ Channel — amiloride-sensitive, voltage-insensitive) on apical membrane → membrane depolarization → opens voltage-gated Na⁺ & Ca²⁺ channels → ↑ intracellular Ca²⁺ → releases serotonin → activates afferent axon.
💊 Clinical: Amiloride Amiloride is a potassium-sparing diuretic that blocks ENaC. Side effects can include reduced salt taste perception — because the same channel mediates both renal Na⁺ reabsorption AND salt taste detection.

Type III — Sour Transduction

🍋 Sour (H⁺) H⁺ ions act via two mechanisms: (1) pass through proton channels (otopterin 1) on apical membrane, and/or (2) block potassium channels → prevents K⁺ efflux → membrane depolarization → ↑ Ca²⁺ → serotonin release → afferent activation.

Type II — Bitter, Sweet, Umami, Kokumi Transduction

These all use GPCRs — the most complex transduction cascade:

  1. 1Tastant binds to specific GPCR on apical membrane (Taste GPCR / T1R or T2R family)
  2. 2GPCR activates Phospholipase C (PLC) → cleaves PIP₂ → produces IP₃
  3. 3IP₃ triggers Ca²⁺ release from intracellular ER stores
  4. 4Ca²⁺ activates TRPM4/5 ion channels → Na⁺ influx → membrane depolarization
  5. 5Depolarization → ATP released through CALHM1/3 ATP-permeable channels
  6. 6ATP binds to P2X2/X3 receptors on afferent axon → action potential generated
🔑 GPCR Pairs to Know
  • Sweet: TAS1R2 + TAS1R3 heterodimer
  • Umami (savory/glutamate): TAS1R1 + TAS1R3 heterodimer
  • Bitter: TAS2R family (~25 types — broad detection for toxins)
  • Kokumi (texture enhancer): CaSR (Calcium-Sensing Receptor)

Exam Q

Why does bitter taste use so many different receptor subtypes (~25 TAS2Rs), while sweet uses only one receptor pair?

Answer: Bitter compounds are diverse and include many structurally unrelated toxic/poisonous molecules — having ~25 TAS2Rs allows detection of a broad range of potentially harmful compounds. Sweet compounds (sugars) are more structurally similar, so one heterodimeric receptor pair (TAS1R2/R3) can detect them with high sensitivity.

Gustatory Pathway to the Brain

PLO: Taste pathway from receptor cells to cortical perception
  • 1°: Pseudounipolar afferent neurons of CN VII, IX, X → ganglia → NTS (Rostral)
  • 2°: Rostral Nucleus of Solitary Tract (NST / "gustatory nucleus") → thalamus & limbic system
  • NST ↔ Hypothalamus (emotion & feeding) and Amygdala
  • 3°: VPM of thalamus → Insular cortex & frontal taste cortices (perception)
  • Taste pathways DO synapse in thalamus — unlike olfaction
1° → Pseudounipolar afferent neurons of CN VII, IX, X; cell bodies in sensory ganglia (geniculate / petrosal / nodose)
2° → Rostral Nucleus of the Solitary Tract (NST) — the "gustatory nucleus" in the brainstem medulla
Limbic ↔ NST ↔ Hypothalamus (emotion & feeding regulation) and Amygdala (emotional valence of taste)
3° → VPM (Ventral PosteroMedial) nucleus of thalamus
Cortex → Insular cortex (primary taste area) and frontal taste cortices → conscious taste sensation & perception
Taste Pathway

Synapses in VPM of thalamus before reaching cortex (like most senses)

Second-order neurons at rostral NST → thalamus → insular cortex

Also connects to hypothalamus & amygdala via NST

Olfactory Pathway

Does NOT synapse in thalamus — projects directly to piriform cortex & amygdala

Unique among all sensory systems

Thalamic connections are secondary (from orbitofrontal cortex feedback)

Exam Q

What is the second-order nucleus for taste, and what are its two major subcortical connections?

Answer: The Rostral Nucleus of the Solitary Tract (NST) — also called the "gustatory nucleus." Its two major subcortical connections are: (1) Hypothalamus — for feeding regulation and emotional aspects of taste, and (2) Amygdala — for emotional valence and aversive/appetitive associations with food.

Taste Disorders

PLO: Clinical taste deficits — causes and presentations
  • Patients complaining of taste loss often actually have olfactory deficits
  • Ageusia (rare): dental block of lingual nerve; Ramsey-Hunt syndrome (VZV/shingles)
  • Ageusia is usually unilateral — not noticed because saliva spreads tastants to unaffected side
  • Hypogeusia (common): smoking, aging, endocrine/inflammatory/metastatic disorders
  • Dysgeusia (most common): distorted taste — dental infection or drugs entering saliva from blood
  • Hypergeusia: heightened sour sensitivity — reported in breast cancer patients
  • Gustatory auras: seizures from insular cortex / frontal operculum
⚠️ Critical Clinical Point Patients complaining of "lack of taste sensation" often instead have olfactory deficits, but they perceive this as a reduced sense of taste ("I can't taste my food"). This is because much of what we call "flavor" is actually retronasal olfaction. Always test SMELL when a patient complains of lost TASTE.
Disorder Definition Causes / Notes
Ageusia Lack of taste sensation (rare)
  • Temporary partial ageusia: anesthetic dental block of lingual nerve
  • Ramsey-Hunt Syndrome: varicella-zoster virus (chickenpox → shingles later in life) damages cranial nerves mediating taste
  • Usually unilateral — patient often unaware because saliva spreads tastants to activate receptors on the unaffected side
Hypogeusia Lessened taste sensation (common) Smoking, aging, wide variety of endocrine, inflammatory, and metastatic disorders
Dysgeusia Distorted taste sensation — most common clinical taste problem Presence of noxious substances in the mouth from dental infection or drugs entering saliva from the bloodstream
Hypergeusia Heightened taste sensation Heightened sensitivity to sour reported in breast cancer patients
Gustatory Auras Taste sensations from seizures Epileptiform neural activity starting in secondary taste area (insula & frontal operculum)
🏥 Ramsey-Hunt Syndrome Varicella-zoster virus (VZV) lies dormant in sensory ganglia after chickenpox. Reactivation (shingles) affecting the geniculate ganglion of CN VII → Ramsey-Hunt syndrome: facial weakness + ear pain + vesicles + taste loss (anterior 2/3 tongue). Can affect other cranial nerve ganglia too.
Exam Q

Why are unilateral taste deficits often not noticed by patients?

Answer: Because saliva spreads dissolved tastants over the entire tongue surface, activating taste receptors on the unaffected side. Even if one side has a complete taste deficit, the intact side still generates normal taste perception. Bilateral testing or specific regional testing is needed to detect unilateral deficits.

Olfaction vs. Gustation — Master Comparison

Key Comparison Points (High-Yield)
  • Olfactory receptor cells = NEURONS; Taste receptor cells = NOT neurons (epithelial)
  • Olfaction: bypasses thalamus; Gustation: goes through VPM thalamus
  • Olfaction CN: CN I only; Gustation CNs: VII, IX, X
  • Olfactory 2°: olfactory bulb mitral cells; Gustatory 2°: Rostral NTS
  • Both use GPCR mechanisms for complex tastants/odorants; both show adaptation
Feature Olfaction (Smell) Gustation (Taste)
Receptor cells Olfactory receptor cells — ARE neurons Taste receptor cells — are NOT neurons
Cranial nerve CN I (olfactory) CN VII, IX, X
Receptor location Nasal epithelium (olfactory cilia) Taste buds (microvilli at taste pore)
Cell lifespan 4–8 weeks (regenerate from basal cells) ~2 weeks (replaced from basal cells)
Transduction GPCR → Golf → cAMP → CNG channels → Na⁺/Ca²⁺ → Cl⁻ (amplifier) Multiple: ion channels (salt/sour) or GPCR → IP₃ → Ca²⁺ (bitter/sweet/umami)
Second-order neuron Mitral/tufted cells (olfactory bulb) Neurons of rostral NST (medulla)
Thalamic relay? ❌ NO — unique! Direct to cortex ✅ YES — VPM of thalamus
Primary cortex Piriform cortex (primary olfactory cortex) Insular cortex (primary taste cortex)
Loss of function Anosmia (hyposmia, uncinate fits) Ageusia (dysgeusia, hypogeusia)
NT released by receptor cell Direct action potential (ORC is a neuron) Serotonin (Type I/III) or ATP (Type II)
Adaptation ~1 minute with continuous exposure Also occurs; faster for some tastants
🎯 The Big Distinctions
  1. ORC = neuron; TRC = epithelial cell (NOT a neuron)
  2. Olfaction bypasses the thalamus; taste does not
  3. Olfaction uses only one transduction mechanism (GPCR/cAMP); taste uses at least three different mechanisms depending on tastant type
High-Yield Integration

A patient has a lesion of the left insula. Which senses are affected and how?

Answer: The insular cortex is the primary taste cortex. A lesion here could produce gustatory auras or taste deficits (ageusia/dysgeusia). Note: olfaction would be largely unaffected, as the primary olfactory cortex is the pyriform cortex. However, the insula also receives secondary olfactory projections, so complex flavor perception may be altered.
