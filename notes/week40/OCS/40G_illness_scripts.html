Head & Neck Illness Scripts

Epidemiology, clinical presentation & pathophysiology of four key conditions

Bell's Palsy

CN VII — Facial Nerve
Epidemiology
Incidence

13–34 cases per 100,000 people per year

High-Risk Groups

Pregnancy (3× higher risk, especially 3rd trimester / 1st postpartum week); Diabetes (5–10% of patients)

Etiology

Most common: HSV reactivation. Also: HZV, CMV, EBV, adenovirus, rubella, mumps, influenza B, coxsackievirus

Pathophysiology

The facial nerve travels through a narrow bony channel — the facial canal in the temporal bone — positioned between the petrosal venous plexus cranially and the cervical venous plexus in the suboccipital region. Viral reactivation (most commonly HSV-1) causes inflammation and oedema of the nerve within this confined space. Because the canal cannot expand, swelling leads to axonal compression and progressive loss of nerve function.

Pathology on imaging: T2-weighted MRI shows signal enhancement of CN VII, particularly near the internal auditory meatus, consistent with oedema and inflammation around the nerve sheath.
Clinical Presentation
Motor Loss

Sudden unilateral facial paralysis: eyebrow sagging, inability to close eye, loss of nasolabial fold, drooping of mouth corner

Autonomic / Sensory

Decreased tearing (lacrimation), hyperacusis (stapedius paralysis), loss of taste from anterior ⅔ of tongue

Key Features

Acute onset; progressive to maximal weakness within 3 weeks; recovery expected within 6 months; LMN pattern (all facial muscles affected)

Diagnosis
MethodFinding / Purpose
Clinical examObserve: eye closure, brow elevation, frowning, showing teeth, lip puckering — all assess CN VII branches
EMGDetermines prognosis for nerve recovery
CT / MRIOrdered for atypical presentations to rule out tumour or central lesion
SerologyLyme disease titres if exposure is possible
Differential Diagnosis
  • Herpes zoster (Ramsay Hunt syndrome)
  • Otitis media spreading to facial canal
  • Lyme disease
  • Guillain-Barré syndrome
  • HIV infection, sarcoidosis, Sjögren syndrome
  • Tumour or stroke (central — spares forehead)
🩺 Clinical Pearl

Bell's palsy is by definition idiopathic. A lower motor neuron lesion of CN VII affects ALL muscles on the ipsilateral side (including the forehead), distinguishing it from an upper motor neuron stroke, which spares the forehead due to bilateral cortical representation of the frontalis.

Trigeminal Neuralgia

CN V — Trigeminal Nerve
Epidemiology
Incidence

4–13 per 100,000 per year; prevalence <0.1% — a rare condition

Demographics

Women > Men; incidence increases gradually with age

Etiology

Most commonly: neurovascular compression of CN V root near pons. Rarely: vestibular schwannoma, meningioma, epidermoid cyst, MS demyelination, or vascular malformation

Pathophysiology

The trigeminal nerve arises at the midlateral surface of the pons. Its sensory root is the largest in the nervous system, with over half of fibres in the nociceptive range. Compression of the nerve root — typically by a deviant arterial or venous loop within millimetres of the pons — causes focal demyelination.

Mechanism of pain: Demyelinated lesions generate ephaptic cross-talk — aberrant electrical coupling between light-touch fibres and pain-generating fibres. This means light tactile stimulation of the face can trigger severe pain attacks. Central sensitisation also contributes, accounting for refractory periods, delay from stimulus to onset, and summation of painful sensations.

Vessel contact causes ischaemia visible as a whitish-grey patch on the nerve. Demyelination can also occur in multiple sclerosis patients.

Clinical Presentation
Pain Character

Paroxysmal, severe, electric shock-like / stabbing / shooting. Lasting a fraction of a second to 2 minutes per attack

Distribution

Strictly unilateral. Limited to CN V distribution (V2/V3 most common; V1 rare). Most frequent trigger zone: perinasal / perioral region

Triggers & Features

Light touch of trigger zones (eating, talking, cold air, brushing teeth). Background dull persistent pain common. Associated: lacrimation, conjunctival injection, rhinorrhoea

Diagnostic Criteria (ICHD)
  • Recurrent paroxysms of unilateral facial pain in ≥1 CN V division, no radiation beyond
  • Pain: lasting fraction of a second – 2 minutes, severe intensity, electric/stabbing quality
  • Precipitated by innocuous stimuli in the affected trigeminal distribution
  • Not better explained by another diagnosis
Investigations
InvestigationPurpose
MRI + MR AngiographyPreferred — identify neurovascular compression or structural brain lesion
CT HeadAlternative, less sensitive for soft tissue detail
Differential Diagnosis
  • Acute herpes zoster / postherpetic neuralgia
  • Trauma to trigeminal nerve
  • Dental pain (referred)
🩺 Clinical Pearl

Although classified as episodic, most patients report an underlying constant dull ache between attacks. The trigger zone most frequently involves the perioral and nasal region (V2/V3 overlap). V1 involvement is rare and should raise suspicion for HZO.

Sinusitis

Paranasal Sinuses
Epidemiology
Prevalence

Affects 1 in 7 adults annually; higher rates in the South and Midwest; peak incidence fall–early spring

Demographics

Women > Men; most common in children <15 and adults 25–64 years old

Pathogens

Viral (most common): Rhinovirus, coronavirus, adenovirus, influenza/parainfluenza. Bacterial: S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae, M. catarrhalis, S. aureus. Rarely fungal.

Anatomy & Pathophysiology

The paranasal sinuses (frontal, maxillary, ethmoid, sphenoid) are hollow, mucous-membrane–lined spaces within the cranial bones, communicating with the nasal cavity through narrow openings called ostia. Normal drainage depends on three intact mechanisms:

1. Drainage Pathway

Mucosal swelling (viral infection, allergy, anatomic defect) obstructs the ostium, trapping secretions

2. Ciliary Function

Impaired mucociliary clearance (e.g., smoking, ICU positioning) prevents secretion removal

3. Mucus Quality

Altered viscosity causes stasis; stagnant secretions become a culture medium for bacterial superinfection

Key cascade: Viral URI → mucosal oedema → ostial obstruction → pressure rise → pain. Bacterial superinfection occurs when bacteria colonise the inflamed, isolated sinus. Untreated acute sinusitis can become chronic; cytokine-driven osteoclast activation can erode surrounding bone into the orbit or cranial cavity.
Clinical Presentation
TypeKey Features
Acute Viral Fever, headache, facial pain/pressure (maxillary most common), erythema/swelling over sinus, concurrent otitis media possible; resolves <10 days
Acute Bacterial Symptoms ≥10 days without improvement, OR severe symptoms at 3+ days, OR double-worsening (improves then worsens 5–6 days later)
Fungal – Noninvasive Allergic: asthma-like features. Mycetoma: unilateral maxillary complaints
Fungal – Invasive Epistaxis, dark necrotic ulcers on septum/palate/turbinates; acutely ill, altered mental status — EMERGENCY
Chronic ≥2 of: mucopurulent drainage, nasal congestion, facial pain, anosmia/hyposmia; present for months or insidious onset

🩺 Risk Factors to Know

Septal deviations or polyps; impaired mucociliary transport; immunodeficiency; prolonged supine ICU positioning; nasal cocaine abuse; nasogastric / nasotracheal tubes; prolonged oxygen use.

Herpes Zoster Ophthalmicus

CN V1 — Ophthalmic Division
Epidemiology
Incidence

4–4.5 cases of herpes zoster per 1,000 individuals globally; ~1 million US cases annually; lifetime risk 30%

HZO Specifically

10–20% of all herpes zoster presents as HZO; 50% of HZO cases involve the eye (anterior segment most common); 30% progress to postherpetic neuralgia

Risk Factors

Age >50; immunocompromise; physical or emotional stress; acute illness; prior varicella infection (universal prerequisite)

Pathophysiology

Varicella-zoster virus (VZV) is a double-stranded DNA herpesvirus that infects ~95% of humans in childhood as varicella (chickenpox). Initial infection is via the upper respiratory tract; viral particles disseminate and undergo retrograde axonal transport to take up lifelong latent residency in the nuclei of sensory and autonomic ganglion cells throughout the body.

Reactivation mechanism: Waning T-cell–mediated immunity (age, stress, illness, immunosuppression) allows VZV to exit latency. The virus replicates in the ganglion cell body, spreads anterogradely along axons, and erupts as the characteristic painful vesicular rash in a single dermatomal distribution.

When reactivation occurs in the trigeminal ganglion and involves the ophthalmic division (V1) — specifically its frontal, nasociliary, or lacrimal branches — the condition is termed Herpes Zoster Ophthalmicus. Nerve involvement distribution: thoracic 40%, cranial 30%, cervical 12%.

Clinical Presentation
Prodrome

Pain, burning, or tingling in the V1 dermatome precedes the rash by days. Constitutional symptoms: fever, malaise, fatigue, headache.

Rash

Vesicular rash on erythematous base in unilateral, single dermatomal (V1) distribution. Does not cross the midline.

Nasociliary Sign

Hutchinson's sign: vesicles on the tip/side of nose (nasociliary branch) → strongly predicts ocular involvement

Ocular Involvement
StructureManifestation
EyelidsBlepharitis (inflammation)

CorneaEpithelial keratitis; stromal keratitis → corneal oedema
Uveal tractUveitis (iris, ciliary body, choroid inflammation)
Long-termProlonged inflammation → permanent visual loss
Pathology & Complications
  • Intense local inflammation and tissue necrosis
  • Virus-induced perineuritis (neuritis)
  • Serious complications: meningoencephalitis, myelitis, vasculitis, cranial neuritis
  • Postherpetic neuralgia: chronic neuropathic pain persisting long after rash resolution (30% of HZO cases)
Diagnosis

Typically a clinical diagnosis based on the dermatomal vesicular rash in the V1 distribution. Molecular confirmation (PCR, antibody serology) available for ambiguous cases.

Differential Diagnosis
  • Herpes simplex keratitis / dermatitis
  • Bacterial or viral conjunctivitis
  • Allergic conjunctivitis
  • Acute angle-closure glaucoma
  • Corneal ulcer or abrasion
  • Contact dermatitis or insect bites
🩺 Clinical Pearl

HZO diagnosis does not automatically mean eye involvement — only ~50% of HZO cases affect the eye. However, nasociliary branch involvement (Hutchinson's sign) is the most important predictor of ocular disease and should prompt urgent ophthalmology referral. Early antiviral therapy reduces the risk of postherpetic neuralgia and ocular complications.
