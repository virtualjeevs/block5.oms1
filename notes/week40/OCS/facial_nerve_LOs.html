Rapid Orientation

Why CN VII Is a Favorite for Anatomy Questions

The facial nerve is a mixed nerve whose lesion pattern changes dramatically depending on where it is damaged. That makes it one of the most localizable cranial nerves in head and neck anatomy.

  • Motor: muscles of facial expression plus stapedius, stylohyoid, and posterior belly of digastric
  • Parasympathetic: lacrimal, submandibular, sublingual, nasal, and palatine glands
  • Taste and sensation: anterior two-thirds tongue taste, palate taste, and ear sensory fibers
LO 40D Focus

Course, Components, and Branch Distribution

Everything through the taste section belongs to the descriptive anatomy objective.

I

Overview: Five Fiber Types in One Facial Nerve

CN VII exits the brainstem at the pontomedullary junction and carries five distinct fiber types. The nerve is most famous for facial expression, but that is only one part of its job.

SVE

Motor to 2nd Arch Muscles

Supplies muscles of facial expression, stapedius, stylohyoid, and posterior belly of digastric.

GVE

Secretomotor Parasympathetics

Preganglionic fibers from the superior salivatory nucleus reach the lacrimal, submandibular, and sublingual glands.

SVA

Taste Fibers

Carry taste from the anterior two-thirds of the tongue and from the palate, with cell bodies in the geniculate ganglion.

GSA / GVA

General Sensory Fibers

Carry somatic sensation from the ear canal region and visceral sensation from the oropharyngeal and palatal mucosa.

Geniculate Ganglion

The geniculate ganglion is the sensory ganglion of CN VII. Taste and general sensory cell bodies sit here, while motor fibers and parasympathetic preganglionic fibers pass through without synapsing.

Fiber Type Main Function Peripheral Route Central Association
SVE Facial movement and stapedius control Main trunk, parotid plexus, stapedius branch Motor nucleus of VII
GVE Lacrimation and salivation Greater petrosal nerve and chorda tympani Superior salivatory nucleus
SVA Taste from tongue and palate Chorda tympani and greater petrosal pathways Geniculate ganglion to nucleus solitarius
GSA / GVA Ear and oropharyngeal sensation Posterior auricular, chorda, and palatal routes Geniculate ganglion to sensory nuclei
II

Course of the Facial Nerve

The facial nerve has an intrapontine segment, a cisternal and meatal segment, a long intratemporal segment, and then an extracranial motor distribution. Every major branch point matters clinically.

1

Pontomedullary Border

CN VII exits at the pontomedullary junction after its motor fibers loop around the abducens nucleus, creating the facial colliculus.

2

Internal Auditory Meatus

CN VII enters the internal auditory meatus with CN VIII and occupies the anterosuperior compartment.

3

Geniculate Ganglion and Genu

At the sharp bend in the facial canal, the geniculate ganglion appears and the greater petrosal nerve branches off first.

4

Facial Canal

The nerve continues through the petrous temporal bone, giving off the nerve to stapedius and then the chorda tympani.

5

Stylomastoid Foramen to Parotid

The main trunk exits the skull, gives off pre-parotid motor branches, then enters the parotid gland and divides into terminal facial-expression branches.

III

Motor Component: Facial Expression and Related Muscles

All muscles of facial expression are second pharyngeal arch derivatives. The main trunk exits at the stylomastoid foramen, gives off pre-parotid branches, then splits within the parotid gland into the five classic terminal branches.

Parotid Plexus Mnemonic

To Zanzibar By Motor Car = temporal, zygomatic, buccal, marginal mandibular, and cervical branches.

Before the Parotid

Pre-Parotid Motor Branches

Posterior auricular nerve
Supplies occipitalis and auricular muscles; also carries general sensory fibers from the postauricular region.
Nerve to stylohyoid
Supplies the stylohyoid muscle, which elevates the hyoid during swallowing.
Nerve to posterior digastric
Supplies the posterior belly of digastric, which helps elevate the hyoid and stabilize mandibular motion.
Parotid Plexus

Terminal Motor Branches

Temporal branch
Supplies frontalis and the upper orbicularis oculi region. Damage impairs forehead wrinkling and eyebrow elevation.
Zygomatic branch
Supplies the lower orbicularis oculi and midfacial elevator region. Damage weakens eye closure.
Buccal branch
Supplies buccinator and upper lip muscles. Damage causes food pocketing and weak cheek compression.
Marginal mandibular branch
Supplies lower lip depressors and mentalis. Damage causes lower lip asymmetry and poor lower tooth display.
Cervical branch
Supplies platysma and contributes to lower face and neck soft-tissue tension.

Stapedius

Intratemporal branch

The stapedius dampens ossicular movement. Loss of its function causes hyperacusis.

Frontalis

Temporal branch

Raises the eyebrows and wrinkles the forehead. It is preserved in many UMN lesions.

Orbicularis oculi

Temporal and zygomatic branches

Closes the eyelids. Weakness causes lagophthalmos and corneal exposure risk.

Buccinator

Buccal branch

Compresses the cheek against the teeth and prevents food from collecting in the vestibule.

UMN vs LMN Rule

UMN lesions spare the forehead because the upper facial motor nucleus receives bilateral cortical input. LMN lesions paralyze the entire ipsilateral face, including the forehead and eye closure.

IV

Parasympathetic Component: GSPN and Chorda Tympani

Preganglionic parasympathetic fibers arise in the superior salivatory nucleus and leave CN VII by two high-yield routes: the greater petrosal nerve to the lacrimal region and the chorda tympani to the submandibular region.

Greater Petrosal Nerve Pathway

  • Preganglionic fibers leave CN VII at the geniculate ganglion region as the greater petrosal nerve.
  • The greater petrosal nerve passes toward the foramen lacerum and joins the deep petrosal nerve.
  • The combined nerve of the pterygoid canal reaches the pterygopalatine ganglion, where parasympathetic fibers synapse.
  • Postganglionic fibers hitchhike on V2 and then the lacrimal nerve route to reach the lacrimal gland, and also supply nasal and palatine glands.

Chorda Tympani Pathway

  • The chorda tympani branches off near the distal facial canal, crosses the middle ear cavity, and exits through the petrotympanic fissure.
  • In the infratemporal fossa it joins the lingual nerve, a branch of V3.
  • Preganglionic parasympathetic fibers synapse in the submandibular ganglion.
  • Postganglionic fibers supply the submandibular and sublingual glands while taste fibers continue centrally without synapsing in the ganglion.
Branch Ganglion Target Important Hitchhiking Route
Greater petrosal nerve Pterygopalatine ganglion Lacrimal, nasal, and palatine glands Vidian nerve, then V2 and lacrimal communications
Chorda tympani Submandibular ganglion Submandibular and sublingual glands Joins the lingual nerve in the infratemporal fossa
V

Taste and General Sensory Components

The taste fibers of CN VII are classic hitchhikers: they travel out on the chorda tympani and lingual nerve peripherally, but their cell bodies remain in the geniculate ganglion and they project centrally to the rostral nucleus solitarius.

Taste Route

Anterior Two-Thirds of the Tongue

Peripheral receptor
Taste buds in fungiform and foliate papillae generate the signal.
Peripheral carrier
Taste fibers travel first with the lingual nerve, then leave it to run in the chorda tympani.
Ganglion
The geniculate ganglion holds the first-order cell bodies.
Central termination
Central processes enter the brainstem and terminate in the rostral nucleus solitarius.

GSA Territory

  • External auditory meatus and conchal bowl
  • External tympanic membrane region
  • Small postauricular skin distribution
  • Explains ear pain and vesicles in Ramsay Hunt syndrome

GVA Territory

  • Soft palate and oropharyngeal mucosa inputs
  • Palatal fibers can travel with the greater petrosal pathway
  • General visceral fibers project centrally to sensory nuclei
  • These fibers matter for lesion localization more than bedside testing
High-Yield Contrast

The lingual nerve carries general sensation from the anterior two-thirds of the tongue. The chorda tympani carries taste from that same territory and joins the lingual nerve in the infratemporal fossa.

LO 40E Focus

Clinical Patterns of Facial Nerve Injury

Use branch loss to move proximally: facial paralysis alone is distal, taste loss is higher, hyperacusis is higher still, and dry eye means the lesion is above the greater petrosal branch.

VI

Clinical Localization and Syndromes

Facial nerve lesions are localizable because each major branch leaves at a different point in the facial canal. Additive symptoms help you determine how proximal the lesion must be.

Core Principle

The facial nerve can be injured at several different points along its course, and the clinical picture depends on where the lesion sits. In other words, where the lesion is determines the symptoms. The farther proximal you move, the more branch functions you lose.

Location Motor Deficit Taste Loss Lacrimation Auditory Changes
Subarachnoid space (A) One-sided loss of facial expression Loss from the anterior two-thirds of the tongue Reduced on the affected side Hyperacusis only from stapedius loss; this pattern does not require CN VIII involvement
Internal auditory meatus (B) Same motor pattern as lesion A Same taste loss as lesion A Same tear loss as lesion A Hyperacusis plus hearing loss, imbalance, or dizziness because CN VIII travels here too
Facial canal above stapedius (C) Full ipsilateral hemifacial paralysis Loss of taste Preserved because the greater petrosal branch is already gone Hyperacusis from stapedius paralysis
Distal to stapedius but above chorda (D) Full ipsilateral hemifacial paralysis Loss of taste Preserved No auditory change because stapedius is already spared
Stylomastoid foramen (E) Weakness can include stylohyoid and posterior digastric along with facial expression Preserved because the lesion is distal to chorda tympani Preserved No auditory change
Parotid region (F) Hemifacial palsy of the terminal branches; platysma is affected, but stylohyoid and posterior digastric are spared Preserved Preserved No auditory change
Lesion Ladder

The farther down from the root you go, the fewer symptoms you see, because fewer branches remain to be interrupted. A parotid lesion hits the terminal motor branches, but it misses the proximal taste, lacrimal, and stapedius branches.

Brainstem

Pontine Lesion

Often involves neighboring tracts and nuclei

  • Complete ipsilateral LMN facial weakness
  • Possible CN VI palsy from nearby abducens involvement
  • Taste, parasympathetic, and stapedius fibers may all be affected
  • Contralateral long-tract deficits may coexist
IAM / CPA

Internal Auditory Meatus Lesion

Think vestibular schwannoma pattern

  • LMN facial weakness with ipsilateral SNHL
  • Tinnitus and vestibular symptoms are common
  • Dry eye and taste loss can be present
  • CN VIII findings are the major clue
Geniculate Ganglion

Ramsay Hunt Syndrome

VZV reactivation in the sensory ganglion

  • Painful vesicles in the ear canal or concha
  • Complete ipsilateral LMN facial palsy
  • Often includes taste loss and dry eye
  • May also involve vertigo or hearing loss
Proximal Canal

Above the Stapedius Branch

Very proximal intratemporal lesion

  • Complete ipsilateral LMN facial weakness
  • Hyperacusis because stapedius is denervated
  • Taste loss because chorda fibers are also affected
  • Dry eye because the lesion is above the GSPN branch
Bell's Palsy Region

Distal Canal or Bell's Palsy

Variable involvement depending on exact level

  • Sudden complete LMN facial weakness including forehead
  • Taste loss and hyperacusis may be present or absent
  • Hearing is usually normal because CN VIII is spared
  • Eye protection is essential to prevent keratitis
Stylomastoid / Parotid

Extracranial Lesion

Parotid tumor, surgery, or trauma

  • Facial weakness only, often branch-selective
  • Taste is preserved because chorda is proximal
  • Lacrimation is preserved because GSPN is proximal
  • Hyperacusis is absent because stapedius is already branched off

Bell's Palsy Essentials

  • Idiopathic LMN facial palsy, often linked to HSV-1 reactivation
  • Forehead involvement confirms a peripheral pattern
  • Postauricular pain can precede weakness
  • Artificial tears and night eye protection are mandatory

Schirmer Test Logic

  • Reduced lacrimation suggests injury above the greater petrosal branch
  • Normal tears suggest the lesion is distal to the geniculate ganglion
  • Useful when distinguishing proximal from distal intratemporal lesions
  • Pairs well with taste and hyperacusis findings
Localization Shortcut

Facial paralysis alone suggests a lesion at or distal to the stylomastoid foramen. Add taste loss and the lesion is above the chorda tympani. Add hyperacusis and the lesion is above the nerve to stapedius. Add dry eye and the lesion is above the greater petrosal nerve near the geniculate ganglion or more proximal.

Blink Reflex Logic

  • Afferent limb: CN V detects the corneal touch
  • Efferent limb: CN VII closes the eyelids
  • CN V lesion: touching that cornea gives no pain signal and no blink is triggered from that side
  • CN VII lesion: pain is still felt, but the affected eye cannot close even though the opposite eye can still blink

Bell's Palsy Essentials

  • Usually localizes within the facial canal as an acute peripheral CN VII palsy
  • Ear pain can precede weakness by about a day
  • Inflammation and edema compress the nerve within the canal
  • The forehead is weak and the patient may not be able to fully close the affected eye
Blink Reflex Pattern Expected Finding
Normal reflex Afferent via CN V and efferent via CN VII, so corneal touch produces a blink response
CN V damage No pain on corneal touch and no blink is triggered when the affected cornea is touched; the blink machinery itself is still intact
CN VII damage Pain is felt when the cornea is touched, but the affected eye cannot blink because the motor limb is out
Bell's Palsy Feature Clinical Pattern
Location Within the facial canal
Onset Acute and may be preceded by ear pain for about 24 hours
Pathogenesis Inflammation and edema cause compression of the nerve and can leave residual synkinesis
Appearance Hemifacial paralysis including the forehead, inability to grimace normally, and incomplete closure of the affected eye
Risk factors Diabetes and hypertension are classic associations
Parotid Pearl

The branches to stylohyoid and posterior digastric leave the facial nerve before the nerve enters the parotid gland. That is why a parotid-region lesion can paralyze the facial expression branches while sparing stylohyoid and posterior digastric.

Practice Questions

Ten board-style questions with balanced distractors. Click an option to reveal the answer and explanation.
1.A patient has isolated facial weakness after a parotidectomy, but taste, lacrimation, and hearing remain normal. Which lesion site best fits this pattern?
Lesion within the internal auditory meatus
Lesion near the geniculate ganglion
Lesion within the extracranial parotid plexus
Lesion within the proximal facial canal
An extracranial or parotid plexus lesion affects the motor branches after the greater petrosal nerve, stapedius branch, and chorda tympani have already left. That preserves lacrimation, taste, and hearing while producing facial weakness.
2.Loss of tearing on the affected side indicates that a facial nerve lesion lies proximal to which named branch?
Greater petrosal nerve from the genu
Posterior auricular nerve from the trunk
Marginal mandibular branch of the plexus
Cervical branch of the platysma
The greater petrosal nerve carries preganglionic parasympathetic fibers that ultimately reach the lacrimal gland. Dry eye means those fibers were interrupted before they branched off.
3.A lesion causing hyperacusis must be located proximal to which structure?
Buccal branch supplying the cheek
Stapedius branch within the canal
Chorda tympani near the fissure
Temporal branch above the zygoma
Hyperacusis occurs when stapedius is denervated, so the lesion must lie above the branch to stapedius within the facial canal.
4.Taste fibers from the anterior two-thirds of the tongue reach CN VII by first traveling with which nerve?
Greater auricular nerve in the neck
Lingual nerve in the fossa
Auriculotemporal nerve near the ear
Glossopharyngeal nerve in the pharynx
Taste fibers from the anterior tongue hitchhike first on the lingual nerve, then join the chorda tympani, and finally re-enter the facial pathway toward the geniculate ganglion.
5.Which ganglion contains the sensory neuron cell bodies for facial nerve taste fibers?
Pterygopalatine ganglion in the fossa
Submandibular ganglion on the lingual
Geniculate ganglion in the canal
Otic ganglion below the foramen
The geniculate ganglion is the sensory ganglion of CN VII and contains the first-order cell bodies for taste and general sensory afferents.
6.A patient has complete ipsilateral facial paralysis and hearing loss from a single lesion. Which site best explains both findings?
Parotid plexus in the gland
Stylomastoid foramen at the base
Internal auditory meatus region
Distal buccal branch in the face
CN VII and CN VIII travel together through the internal auditory meatus, so a lesion there can produce both facial weakness and sensorineural hearing loss.
7.Painful ear vesicles with LMN facial paralysis most strongly suggests which diagnosis?
Bell's palsy from canal edema
Ramsay Hunt syndrome from VZV
Trigeminal neuralgia from root compression
Parotid tumor causing branch loss
Ramsay Hunt syndrome reflects varicella-zoster reactivation in the geniculate ganglion, producing ear pain, vesicles, and peripheral facial paralysis.
8.Preganglionic facial parasympathetic fibers to the submandibular gland synapse in which ganglion?
Pterygopalatine ganglion in the fossa
Submandibular ganglion on the lingual
Geniculate ganglion in the canal
Otic ganglion near the mandibular
Chorda tympani carries preganglionic parasympathetic fibers to the submandibular ganglion, where they synapse before reaching the submandibular and sublingual glands.
9.A cortical stroke affecting the corticobulbar fibers to CN VII most classically causes which deficit?
Ipsilateral whole-face paralysis including forehead
Contralateral lower-face weakness with forehead sparing
Bilateral loss of taste from the tongue
Dry eye with preserved facial motion
UMN lesions spare the forehead because the upper facial nucleus receives bilateral cortical input. The lower facial muscles receive mainly contralateral cortical input, so the lower face weakens on the opposite side.
10.Which combination of findings best localizes a lesion above the chorda tympani but below the internal auditory meatus?
Facial weakness with hearing loss and vertigo
Facial weakness with taste loss alone
Facial weakness with dry eye alone
Facial weakness with forehead sparing
A lesion above the chorda tympani can remove taste, but if it is below the IAM there is no obligatory CN VIII involvement. Dry eye would require an even more proximal lesion above the greater petrosal branch.
