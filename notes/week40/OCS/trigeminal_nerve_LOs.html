Rapid Orientation

What Makes CN V High Yield

The trigeminal nerve is the largest cranial nerve. It carries general somatic sensation from the face, orbit, nasal cavity, oral cavity, teeth, meninges, and anterior two-thirds of the tongue, while its motor root supplies first pharyngeal arch muscles.

  • V1: sensory only, enters the orbit through the superior orbital fissure
  • V2: sensory only, exits the cranial cavity via foramen rotundum
  • V3: mixed division, exits via foramen ovale and carries the motor root
LO 40B Focus

Course and Distribution of the Trigeminal Nerve

Everything through the motor section belongs to the anatomy and branch-distribution objective.

I

Overview: From Brainstem to the Three Divisions

The trigeminal nerve arises from three sensory nuclei and one motor nucleus extending from the midbrain to the medulla. At the level of the pons, the sensory fibers form the large sensory root and the motor fibers form a smaller motor root that later joins only the mandibular division.

Key Fact

The trigeminal ganglion, also called the semilunar or Gasserian ganglion, lies in the trigeminal cave on the petrous temporal bone. It is the sensory hub from which V1 ophthalmic, V2 maxillary, and V3 mandibular emerge.

V1

Ophthalmic Division

Purely sensory. Travels in the lateral wall of the cavernous sinus and enters the orbit through the superior orbital fissure.

Skin
Forehead, scalp to the vertex, upper eyelid, dorsum and tip of nose.
Core Branches
Frontal, nasociliary, lacrimal.
Clinical Pearl
Afferent limb of the corneal reflex.
V2

Maxillary Division

Purely sensory. Exits the cranial cavity through foramen rotundum, crosses the pterygopalatine fossa, then continues as the infraorbital nerve.

Skin
Lower eyelid, cheek, lateral nose, upper lip, and upper teeth/gingiva.
Core Branches
Zygomatic, palatine, nasopalatine, superior alveolar, infraorbital.
Clinical Pearl
Midface fractures can injure the infraorbital nerve.
V3

Mandibular Division

Mixed division. Passes through foramen ovale into the infratemporal fossa and carries both sensory and motor fibers.

Skin
Lower lip, chin, cheek mucosa, anterior ear, temporal scalp, anterior 2/3 tongue sensation.
Core Branches
Buccal, lingual, inferior alveolar, auriculotemporal, motor branches.
Clinical Pearl
Jaw deviates toward a V3 motor lesion.

II

V1 Ophthalmic Nerve

V1 is the smallest trigeminal division and is entirely sensory. It passes through the lateral wall of the cavernous sinus, then enters the orbit through the superior orbital fissure where it divides into the frontal, nasociliary, and lacrimal nerves.

Largest V1 Branch

Frontal Nerve

Supraorbital nerve
Exits through the supraorbital notch or foramen and supplies the upper eyelid, conjunctiva, forehead, frontal sinus, and scalp up to the vertex.
Supratrochlear nerve
Courses medially above the trochlea and supplies the medial upper eyelid, conjunctiva, and lower forehead.
Medial Orbital Branch

Nasociliary Nerve

Anterior ethmoidal nerve
Enters the anterior ethmoidal foramen and contributes sensation to the anterior nasal cavity and external nose.
  • Medial nasal branches run along the septum.
  • Lateral nasal branches run along the lateral wall.
  • External nasal branch supplies the skin of the distal nose.
Long ciliary nerves
Carry sensory fibers from the cornea and sympathetic fibers to the dilator pupillae and ciliary body.
Infratrochlear nerve
Supplies the medial eyelids, lacrimal sac, caruncle, and side of the nose.
Lateral Orbital Branch

Lacrimal Nerve

Lacrimal nerve
Provides sensory supply to the lacrimal gland, conjunctiva, and lateral upper eyelid. Parasympathetic fibers to the lacrimal gland hitchhike from CN VII via the zygomatic nerve of V2 and then into the lacrimal nerve.
Autonomic Relationship

The trigeminal nerve carries fibers from parasympathetic ganglia, but it does not provide the preganglionic parasympathetic supply itself. In other words, CN V is the major distribution highway, not the origin of the parasympathetic signal.

III

V2 Maxillary Nerve

V2 is purely sensory. It exits the middle cranial fossa through foramen rotundum, crosses the pterygopalatine fossa, enters the orbit through the inferior orbital fissure, runs in the infraorbital groove and canal, and emerges on the face through the infraorbital foramen.

Early Branch

Branch in the Cranium

Meningeal branch
Returns to supply dura and associated vessels in the cranial cavity.
Pterygopalatine Fossa

Branches Related to the Pterygopalatine Ganglion

Zygomatic nerve
Splits into zygomaticotemporal and zygomaticofacial branches; also carries hitchhiking parasympathetic fibers toward the lacrimal nerve.
Greater and lesser palatine nerves
Greater palatine supplies hard palate and adjacent gingiva; lesser palatine supplies soft palate, uvula, and tonsillar region.
Nasopalatine nerve
Crosses the roof of the nasal cavity, descends on the septum, passes through the incisive canal, and supplies the anterior hard palate.
Posterior superior nasal and pharyngeal branches
Supply the lateral nasal wall, superior/middle conchae, and nasopharynx posterior to the auditory tube.
Posterior superior alveolar nerves
Descend toward the maxillary molars and posterior gingiva before V2 enters the infraorbital groove.
Infraorbital Canal

Branches Given Off as the Infraorbital Nerve

Middle superior alveolar nerve
Usually supplies premolars and adjacent gingiva.
Anterior superior alveolar nerve
Supplies incisors and canines and contributes branches to the anterior floor/lateral wall of the nasal cavity.
Terminal infraorbital branches
Emerge as inferior palpebral, lateral nasal, and superior labial branches to the lower eyelid, lateral nose, and upper lip.
IV

V3 Mandibular Nerve

V3 is the largest division and the only one that is mixed. It exits through foramen ovale into the infratemporal fossa. Its sensory fibers serve the lower face, mandibular teeth, and anterior two-thirds of the tongue, while its motor fibers supply the muscles of mastication and several additional first arch muscles.

Branches Before the Main Trunks

Direct Branches from the Mandibular Nerve

Meningeal branch
Re-enters the cranial cavity to supply dura in the middle cranial fossa.
Nerve to medial pterygoid
Motor supply to medial pterygoid and small branches to tensor veli palatini and tensor tympani.
Anterior Trunk

Mainly Motor with One Sensory Exception

Buccal nerve
The only purely sensory branch of the anterior trunk. Supplies cheek skin and buccal mucosa.
Masseteric, deep temporal, and lateral pterygoid nerves
Motor supply to masseter, temporalis, and lateral pterygoid.
Posterior Trunk

Mainly Sensory with a Mylohyoid Motor Branch

Auriculotemporal nerve
Supplies anterior ear, temporal scalp, TMJ, and carries postganglionic parasympathetic fibers from CN IX via the otic ganglion to the parotid gland.
Lingual nerve
Provides general sensation to the anterior two-thirds of the tongue, floor of mouth, and lingual gingiva. It is joined by the chorda tympani, which adds taste fibers and parasympathetic fibers for the submandibular ganglion.
Inferior alveolar nerve
Enters the mandibular foramen, runs in the mandibular canal, and supplies lower teeth.
  • Mylohyoid nerve: leaves just before the mandibular foramen and supplies mylohyoid plus the anterior belly of digastric.
  • Mental nerve: exits the mental foramen to supply lower lip and chin.
  • Incisive branch: continues anteriorly to incisor teeth.
V

Motor Root and Muscles of Mastication

The motor root runs inferior to the sensory root and joins V3 after the trigeminal ganglion. These fibers are branchial motor fibers to first pharyngeal arch derivatives.

Eight Muscle Checklist

Temporalis, masseter, medial pterygoid, lateral pterygoid, mylohyoid, anterior belly of digastric, tensor veli palatini, and tensor tympani.

Temporalis

Deep temporal nerves

Elevates and retracts the mandible. This is a key muscle to palpate during CN V motor testing.

Masseter

Masseteric nerve

Powerful elevation of the mandible. Easily palpated at the angle of the mandible while the patient clenches.

Medial pterygoid

Nerve to medial pterygoid

Elevates the mandible and contributes to grinding movements together with the lateral pterygoid.

Lateral pterygoid

Nerve to lateral pterygoid

Protracts and depresses the mandible. Weakness here produces jaw deviation toward the lesion.

Mylohyoid

Mylohyoid nerve

Elevates the floor of the mouth and assists with mandibular depression when the hyoid is fixed.

Anterior belly of digastric

Mylohyoid nerve

Depresses the mandible and elevates the hyoid during swallowing and early jaw opening.

Tensor veli palatini

Branch with medial pterygoid supply

Tenses the soft palate and helps open the auditory tube during swallowing or yawning.

Tensor tympani

Branch with medial pterygoid supply

Tenses the tympanic membrane to dampen loud sound transmission.

How to Test the Motor Root

  • Ask the patient to clench while palpating temporalis above the zygomatic arch and masseter over the mandibular ramus.
  • Ask the patient to open the mouth; the jaw deviates toward a weak ipsilateral lateral pterygoid.
  • Compare side-to-side resistance and look for wasting of temporalis or masseter in chronic lesions.

Clinical Presentation of Trigeminal Nerve Damage

Use symptom distribution to decide which division is affected and whether the lesion is sensory-only, mixed, proximal, or distal to branch junctions.

VI

Clinical Localization and High-Yield Syndromes

Sensory loss follows the dermatomal map of the affected branch. Motor deficits occur only when V3 motor fibers are involved. This makes CN V lesions especially localizable if you know the branch patterns.

V1 Ophthalmic

Ophthalmic Lesion Pattern

Forehead and orbit dominant

  • Loss of sensation over forehead, scalp to vertex, upper eyelid, dorsum of nose, and cornea.
  • Absent corneal reflex because the afferent limb is lost.
  • High risk of corneal drying and injury if protective sensation is absent.
  • No weakness of mastication because V1 has no motor fibers.
V2 Maxillary

Maxillary Lesion Pattern

Midface and upper jaw dominant

  • Loss of sensation over lower eyelid, cheek, lateral nose, upper lip, maxillary teeth, palate, and nasal mucosa.
  • Often seen with infraorbital nerve injury after zygomatic or Le Fort fractures.
  • Upper dental pain may disappear even while pathology continues silently.
  • No mastication weakness because V2 is purely sensory.
V3 Mandibular

Mandibular Lesion Pattern

Lower face plus motor signs

  • Loss of sensation over lower lip, chin, mandibular teeth, cheek mucosa, anterior ear, and anterior two-thirds of tongue.
  • Jaw deviates toward the side of the lesion on opening.
  • Temporalis and masseter contraction is reduced on the affected side.
  • Lingual lesions may remove general sensation from the tongue; taste is lost only if the lesion is proximal to chorda tympani entry.

Trigeminal Neuralgia

  • Usually caused by vascular compression of the trigeminal root, classically by the superior cerebellar artery.
  • Produces brief, excruciating, unilateral shock-like facial pain.
  • Often triggered by touch, chewing, talking, or cold air.
  • Most often affects V2 and V3 territories.
  • Carbamazepine is first-line therapy; microvascular decompression is the classic surgical option.

Inferior Alveolar Nerve Block

  • Local anesthetic is placed near the mandibular foramen before the inferior alveolar nerve enters the mandibular canal.
  • This numbs the lower teeth and frequently the lower lip and chin through the mental nerve.
  • The lingual nerve often becomes numb as well because it lies nearby in the infratemporal fossa.
  • That explains why the anterior two-thirds of the tongue may feel numb after dental anesthesia.
Localization Strategy

If forehead sensation is intact, V1 is likely spared. If cheek sensation is intact, V2 is likely spared. If lower teeth and chin sensation are intact but the anterior two-thirds of tongue loses general sensation, think isolated lingual nerve lesion. If taste is preserved, the lesion is distal to the chorda tympani junction.

Finding Localization Meaning
Absent corneal reflex afferent limb Suggests V1 or nasociliary dysfunction
Cheek and upper lip numbness Points toward V2 or infraorbital involvement
Jaw deviates toward weak side Indicates ipsilateral V3 motor lesion
Lower lip and chin numbness Suggests mental nerve or inferior alveolar lesion
Tongue touch lost but taste intact Points to lingual lesion distal to chorda tympani entry
Complete hemifacial anesthesia Implies major proximal CN V damage or root-level lesion

Practice Questions

1.A lesion affecting the maxillary division of CN V would most directly interrupt fibers that pass through which skull opening?
Passes through the superior orbital fissure
Passes through the foramen rotundum
Passes through the foramen ovale
Passes through the stylomastoid foramen
V2 exits the cranial cavity through the foramen rotundum. V1 uses the superior orbital fissure, V3 uses the foramen ovale, and the facial nerve exits the skull via the stylomastoid foramen.
2.During corneal reflex testing, failure to sense corneal touch with preserved facial motor function localizes best to which structure?
Afferent fibers in the ophthalmic division
Efferent fibers in the zygomatic branch
Motor fibers in the mandibular division
Taste fibers in the chorda tympani
The ophthalmic division, specifically the nasociliary branch, carries the afferent limb of the corneal reflex. Facial nerve branches close the eyelids, so preserved facial motor function makes an afferent V1 problem most likely.
3.A patient opens the mouth and the jaw deviates to the right. Which muscle is most likely weak on the right side?
Right temporalis retracts the mandible
Right lateral pterygoid opens the mandible
Right masseter elevates the mandible
Right buccinator compresses the cheek
The jaw deviates toward the side of a weak lateral pterygoid because the intact contralateral lateral pterygoid pushes the mandible across. Buccinator is a facial nerve muscle, not a trigeminal motor muscle.
4.Numbness of the lower lip and chin after a dental procedure most directly indicates anesthesia of which named nerve?
Infraorbital nerve of the maxilla
Buccal nerve of the cheek
Mental nerve of the mandible
Auriculotemporal nerve of the temple
The mental nerve is the terminal branch of the inferior alveolar nerve and supplies the lower lip and chin. This is why an inferior alveolar block often produces chin and lower-lip numbness.
5.A lesion distal to the chorda tympani junction would most likely produce which sensory deficit?
Loss of taste from the posterior tongue
Loss of touch from the anterior tongue
Loss of smell from the nasal roof
Loss of vision from the ipsilateral eye
A lesion of the lingual nerve distal to chorda tympani entry removes general sensation from the anterior two-thirds of the tongue while preserving taste, because the taste fibers have already joined more proximally.
6.Which branch of the trigeminal nerve carries postganglionic parasympathetic fibers to the parotid gland?
Lacrimal nerve near the orbit
Nasopalatine nerve of the septum
Auriculotemporal nerve of V3
Supraorbital nerve of V1
Postganglionic parasympathetic fibers from the otic ganglion hitchhike on the auriculotemporal nerve to reach the parotid gland. The trigeminal nerve is acting as the distribution route, not the source of the parasympathetic signal.
7.Which structure lies in closest relationship to the sensory neuron cell bodies of CN V?
Ciliary ganglion in the orbit
Trigeminal ganglion in the cave
Geniculate ganglion in the canal
Otic ganglion in the fossa
The primary sensory cell bodies for CN V are located in the trigeminal ganglion within the trigeminal cave. The ciliary, otic, and other named ganglia are autonomic ganglia, not the main sensory ganglion for CN V.
8.Loss of cheek sensation with numbness of the upper lip and preserved mandibular motor function localizes best to which division?
Ophthalmic sensory division only
Maxillary sensory division only
Mandibular mixed division only
Facial motor division only
The maxillary division carries sensation from the cheek and upper lip and has no motor component. Preserved mandibular motor function makes V3 motor involvement unlikely.
9.Which named branch is the only purely sensory branch arising from the anterior trunk of V3?
Lingual nerve of the tongue
Buccal nerve of the cheek
Mylohyoid nerve of the floor
Mental nerve of the chin
The buccal nerve is the sensory exception in the mainly motor anterior trunk of V3. Lingual, mylohyoid, and mental branches arise from different parts of the mandibular nerve.
10.Classic trigeminal neuralgia most commonly produces paroxysmal pain in which combination of trigeminal territories?
Forehead and corneal surface areas
Maxillary and mandibular facial areas
Occipital and cervical scalp areas
Auricular and vagal pharyngeal areas
Trigeminal neuralgia most often affects V2 and V3, producing severe episodic pain in the maxillary and mandibular territories. V1 involvement is less common but clinically important when present.
