The Parotid Gland

Anatomical relationships with CN VII & secretomotor innervation via CN IX

1 The Parotid Gland – Overview

The parotid gland is the largest of the three major salivary glands and occupies the parotid region of the face, anteroinferior to the ear. It is enclosed within a dense fascial capsule — the parotid fascia — and its duct (Stensen's duct) opens into the oral vestibule opposite the upper second molar.

Key anatomical point: The parotid gland is unique in that the facial nerve (CN VII) courses through the substance of the gland, dividing it into superficial and deep lobes. This has profound surgical significance — parotid surgery risks facial nerve injury.

The facial nerve enters the posterior aspect of the parotid gland after leaving the stylomastoid foramen. Once inside, it divides into ascending and descending branches from which all other facial branches are derived.

2 CN VII – Relationship with the Parotid

The facial nerve exits the skull through the stylomastoid foramen, then curves forward beneath the ear to enter the face. It then enters the posterior aspect of the parotid gland and radiates anteriorly from the gland's anterior margin.

Remember: CN VII innervates muscles of facial expression — those derived from the 2nd pharyngeal arch. Although skeletal in organisation, these muscles arise from branchial arch mesenchyme (not somites), through complex interactions between mesenchyme and neural crest cells.
🩺 Clinical Note

The nerve crosses over the masseter to reach the posterior margin of the muscles of facial expression. Because of its close relationship with the parotid gland, parotid tumours, abscesses, or surgical procedures can damage CN VII, resulting in ipsilateral facial paralysis (lower motor neuron lesion — all muscles on that side are affected).

3 CN IX – Secretomotor Innervation of the Parotid

The glossopharyngeal nerve (CN IX) provides the parasympathetic (secretomotor) innervation to the parotid gland via a lengthy, indirect pathway through the otic ganglion. This is one of CN IX's four major functional roles.

The parasympathetic pathway — step by step

Inferior salivatory nucleus (brainstem)
Preganglionic parasympathetic fibres originate here, travelling with CN IX from the medulla. CN IX exits through the jugular foramen.
Tympanic nerve (Jacobson's nerve)
Shortly after leaving the jugular foramen, the preganglionic fibres branch off as the tympanic nerve, entering the posterior temporal bone and reaching the tympanic cavity (middle ear).
Tympanic plexus (middle ear)
The tympanic nerve forms the tympanic plexus in the middle ear. From here the fibres also supply touch and pain sensation to the tympanic cavity and Eustachian tube.
Lesser superficial petrosal nerve
Preganglionic fibres consolidate into this nerve, which exits the temporal bone anteriorly to pass through or near the foramen ovale, joining the mandibular nerve (V3).
Otic ganglion
Located on the medial aspect of V3, just inferior to foramen ovale. Synapse occurs here. Postganglionic fibres travel with the auriculotemporal nerve.
Auriculotemporal nerve → Parotid gland
Postganglionic fibres "hitchhike" on the auriculotemporal nerve (a branch of V3) to reach and innervate the parotid gland, stimulating serous saliva secretion.
Mnemonic for the pathway: IX → Tympanic nerve → Tympanic plexus → Lesser petrosal nerve → Otic ganglion → Auriculotemporal → Parotid
🩺 Frey's Syndrome (Auriculotemporal Syndrome)

After parotidectomy or other trauma, regenerating parasympathetic fibres from the otic ganglion may aberrantly reinnervate sweat glands of the overlying skin instead of the parotid. This causes gustatory sweating — flushing and sweating of the cheek during eating.

4 Other Branches of CN IX — Full Picture

CN IX has five sets of branches, all of which arise as the nerve curves anteroinferiorly from the jugular foramen toward the oropharynx:

Branch Function Target
Tympanic (→ lesser petrosal) Parasympathetic secretomotor; also touch/pain Parotid gland (via otic ganglion); tympanic cavity & Eustachian tube
Carotid (sinus nerve) Visceral afferent — baroreceptor & chemoreceptor Carotid sinus & body (blood pressure monitoring)
Pharyngeal Somatic afferent — touch & pain Naso- & oropharyngeal mucosa (via pharyngeal plexus)
Muscular Somatic motor (SVE) Stylopharyngeus — the only muscle uniquely innervated by CN IX (3rd arch derivative)
Tonsillar Somatic afferent — touch & pain Bed of the palatine tonsil
Lingual Special afferent (taste) + somatic afferent Posterior ⅓ of tongue — foliate & circumvallate papillae

5 CN VII vs CN IX — Parotid Relationship Summary

Feature CN VII (Facial) CN IX (Glossopharyngeal)
Arch derivation 2nd pharyngeal arch 3rd pharyngeal arch
Relation to parotid Passes through the gland; divides it into lobes Does not traverse gland; innervates it from outside
Function at parotid No secretory function — purely structural/surgical relationship Secretomotor (parasympathetic) — stimulates serous saliva production
Fiber type to parotid Motor (SVE) to facial muscles; not glandular GVE (parasympathetic) preganglionic → synapse at otic ganglion → postganglionic via auriculotemporal
Exit from skull Stylomastoid foramen Jugular foramen (with CN X, XI)
Clinical concern Facial paralysis with parotid pathology or surgery Frey's syndrome post-parotidectomy; glossopharyngeal neuralgia
