PLO 1 — Define "Glycemic Index of the Food"

The glycemic index (GI) is a numerical value (0–100) that measures how much a particular food raises blood glucose levels within 2 hours after eating, compared to pure glucose (which has a GI of 100). It reflects how quickly digestible carbohydrates in a food are converted to glucose and enter the bloodstream.

Key distinction — Glycemic Index vs. Glycemic Load:

  • Glycemic Index (GI) = blood glucose rise per 100 g of a food compared to pure glucose
  • Glycemic Load (GL) = GI × digestible carbohydrate per serving ÷ 100

The GL takes serving size into account — this is why table sugar can have a low glycemic load (small serving × moderate GI ÷ 100 = low number), even though it has a high GI. Dr. G gave all points for the RAT question on this because GL was not taught in the pre-LOs.

PLO 3 — Carbohydrate Structures, Digestion & Influence on GI

Type Structure Digestion Speed GI Impact
Monosaccharides
(glucose, fructose, galactose)		 Single sugar unit — simplest form Immediate — no digestion needed, directly absorbed HIGH GI — rapid blood glucose spike (glucose itself = 100)
Oligosaccharides
(sucrose, lactose, maltose, raffinose)		 2–10 sugar units linked together Fast — quickly broken by disaccharidases at brush border Moderate-High GI — relatively fast glucose release
Polysaccharides
(starch, glycogen, cellulose/fiber)		 Long chains of hundreds–thousands of sugar units Slow — requires extensive enzymatic breakdown (amylase); fiber is indigestible LOW GI — delayed/sustained glucose release; fiber slows absorption further
Key principle: The more complex the carbohydrate structure → the longer it takes to digest → the slower the glucose release → the lower the glycemic index. Fiber (a polysaccharide) is not digestible by human enzymes and acts as a physical barrier that traps glucose, slowing its absorption. This is why high-fiber foods consistently have lower GI values.

LO 1 — Lifestyle & Nutritional Treatment for Prediabetes / Type 2 DM

Treatment requires a comprehensive, multi-pronged approach — not just "go eat healthy and exercise":

  • Medical Nutrition Therapy (MNT): First priority is improving nutrient quality — increase dietary fiber (whole grains, legumes, leafy vegetables, fruits), promote monounsaturated and polyunsaturated fats over saturated fats, reduce processed/high-GI carbohydrates, eliminate sugary beverages as a first step
  • Physical Activity: Introduced after initial weight loss is established for weight maintenance; resistance training builds muscle mass (improves glucose uptake via GLUT4); exercise activates AMPK → GLUT4 translocation to cell surface → insulin-independent glucose uptake; also improves cardiovascular health, HDL levels, stress reduction
  • Behavioral Therapy: Motivational interviewing to assess patient readiness; set SMART goals (specific, measurable, achievable, realistic, time-constrained); lifestyle changes must be realistic for the patient's circumstances
  • Medications: GLP-1 receptor agonists can help establish behavioral changes, BUT patients on these often don't want to eat → risk of malnutrition (protein, B12, calcium, vitamin D deficiency). Medications should accompany MNT, not replace it
  • Address Social Determinants: Screen for food insecurity, housing insecurity, financial barriers. Refer to community health workers and local resources. Work with what the patient can do

Initial goal: 5% weight loss to reduce adiposity → decreases insulin resistance → improves glycemia and dyslipidemia

LO 2 — Meal Planning Methods for Type 1 and Type 2 Diabetes

Method Best For How It Works Key Feature
Insulin-to-Carb Ratio Type 1 DM (especially insulin pump users) Count grams of carbohydrate in each meal; calculate insulin dose using patient-specific ratio (typically 1 unit insulin per 12–15 g CHO) + correction factor (typically 1 unit per 50 mg/dL above target) Most precise; requires carb counting & blood glucose monitoring before meals
Exchange Lists Type 1 & Type 2 DM Foods grouped by similar nutritional value (starch, meat, vegetable, fruit, milk, fat); foods within a group can be "exchanged" for variety. Prescribed servings per meal Focus on consistency of timing/amount (T1D) or caloric control (T2D). Older method

Comparison: For Type 1 DM (no endogenous insulin) → exogenous insulin must be precisely coordinated with carbohydrate intake → insulin-to-carb ratio is the best method. For prediabetes/Type 2 DM (insulin available but resistant) → carbohydrate amount is coordinated with endogenous insulin → Plate Method and Exchange Lists are appropriate; pair with DASH or Mediterranean dietary patterns for best results.

LO 4 — Lifestyle & Nutritional Recommendations for Obesity Treatment

  1. Motivational Interviewing — Assess where the patient is at; determine which changes are realistic (SMART goals)
  2. Phase 1 — Focus on Nutrient Quality for Weight Loss:
    • Increase plant-based food: 5 portions/day of fruits & vegetables
    • Increase dietary fiber: whole grains, vegetables, fruits (white bread → whole grain; rice → legumes)
    • Watch hidden dietary fats; choose cooking methods without added fat (frying → braising)
    • Switch to sugar-free beverages (eliminate soda as first step)
    • Promote MUFA & PUFA over saturated fats
    • Initial goal: 5% weight loss
  3. Phase 2 — Exercise for Weight Maintenance:
    • Introduce regular exercise once initial weight loss is established
    • Include resistance exercise to build muscle mass
    • Start small — even taking stairs instead of elevator is a first step
    • Benefits: cardiovascular health, stress release, muscle building, GLUT4 activation → improved glucose utilization, HDL increase
  4. Phase 3 — Ongoing Maintenance:
    • Continue nutrient quality focus + exercise habit
    • Use anthropometric measures beyond weight (waist circumference, body composition) to monitor progress
    • Consider medications (GLP-1 agonists) if lifestyle alone insufficient — but ensure patient maintains adequate nutrition alongside
    • Consider bariatric surgery in severe cases

Overall outcome: Reduced adiposity → management of a key risk factor for insulin resistance, hyperglycemia, and dyslipidemia

Prediabetes: Definition, Screening & Diagnosis

Prediabetes is the "in-between" stage where blood sugar is above normal but has not yet crossed the threshold for diabetes. Most patients are asymptomatic. This is a critical window — patients can still work hard to prevent full development of diabetes.

A1C reflects glucose management over ~3 months because erythrocytes (red blood cells) have a lifespan of about 90 days. This is why expected outcomes from medical nutrition therapy are seen between 6 weeks and 3 months after initiation.
"Everything below 100 for fasting glucose is going to be normal. Everything else beyond that is going to be higher."

Screening Guidelines (ADA 2022)

  • Patients with BMI >25 kg/m² with one or more risk factors
  • Patients with prediabetes → screened yearly
  • Women with gestational diabetes → screened every 3 years
  • All adults ≥35 years
  • Normal results → repeat every 3 years
  • People with HIV

Key Risk Factors for Diabetes

  • Physically active less than 3×/week
  • Family history of type 2 diabetes (first-degree relative)
  • Age ≥45 years
  • High blood pressure (≥140/90 mmHg)
  • History of gestational diabetes
  • Excess weight, especially abdominal/visceral fat
  • Polycystic ovarian syndrome (PCOS)
  • HDL <35 mg/dL and/or triglycerides >250 mg/dL
  • History of vascular disease
  • Acanthosis nigricans / severe obesity
  • Chronic stress, poor sleep hygiene
1 in 3 people in the US may have prediabetes. Socioeconomic factors (in red on Dr. G's slides) are major contributors — they drive chronic stress, poor sleep, hypertension, and limited access to nutritious food.

Type 1 Diabetes

  • Usually diagnosed earlier in life
  • Autoimmune destruction of pancreatic β-islet cells
  • No insulin production
  • Glucose cannot enter cells → cells starve
  • Requires exogenous insulin (pump or injection)
  • Insulin must be coordinated with carb intake

Type 2 Diabetes

  • Much more common; typically later onset
  • Insulin resistance — body has insulin, cells stop responding
  • Initially: hyperinsulinemia (body compensates by making more insulin)
  • Eventually: β-cells fatigue → insulin deficiency develops
  • Strongly associated with excess adiposity, especially visceral fat
  • Carb amount coordinated with endogenous insulin

Why Are the Symptoms So Nonspecific?

In both types, all cells are starving while glucose accumulates in the blood. The glucose is there doing damage (pro-inflammatory) but cannot get into cells:

  • Blurred vision — retinal cells starving, vascular damage
  • Fatigue — systemic cellular energy deprivation
  • Neuropathies (pain/tingling) — neurons not being fed
  • Polyuria, polydipsia, polyphagia — osmotic diuresis, dehydration, cellular hunger
  • Vascular abnormalities → tissue-based complications

Risk Factors: Modifiable vs Non-Modifiable

Non-Modifiable

  • Age
  • Genetic factors
  • Family history
  • History of gestational diabetes
  • Polycystic ovarian syndrome (PCOS)

Modifiable

  • Excess adiposity / weight (especially abdominal)
  • Physical activity levels
  • Hypertension

Socioeconomic Factors (HIGH RISK — in red on slide)

Socioeconomic status is associated with high risk for diabetes — contributes to chronic stress, poor sleep, hypertension, poor nutritional exposure (lack of funds or accessibility for nutritious food). These are interconnected and often compound each other.

"No one gets money from changing in the lifestyle, so that's not gonna be heavily advertised in the social media as a miraculous way to change things around."

Prevention & Treatment Goals for Type 2 Diabetes

The three core goals:

  1. Maintain normoglycemia — keep blood sugar as close to normal as possible
  2. Maintain functional β-cells of the pancreas — avoid exhausting them with constant insulin demand
  3. Control dyslipidemia — manage LDL, VLDL, and HDL levels

Food Insecurity & Type 2 Diabetes

Food insecurity = unreliable availability of nutritious food and the inability to consistently obtain food without resorting to socially unacceptable practices.
  • 13.5% of US households reported food insecurity in 2023 (~18 million people) — up from 12.8% in 2022
  • Risk for T2D is increased twofold in food-insecure individuals
  • Food insecurity is associated with severe uncontrolled hyperglycemia
  • Reasons: steady consumption of inexpensive carbohydrate-rich processed foods, binge eating, financial constraints filling prescriptions, anxiety/depression → poor self-care

Screening Tool (2-item, validated)

An affirmative response to either statement = sensitivity 97%, specificity 83%:

  1. "Within the past 12 months we worried whether our food would run out before we got money to buy more"
  2. "Within the past 12 months the food we bought just didn't last, and we didn't have money to get more"
"These are 18 million people. The country where I come from originally, there are 2 million people. It's a country. So these are like several small countries in Europe that do not have access to food in one of the most developed countries."

Tailoring Treatment for Social Context

  • Assess: food insecurity, housing insecurity/homelessness, financial barriers, social support
  • Refer: to appropriate local community resources
  • Connect: patients with community health workers for self-management support
  • Don't give up on them. Work with what they can do

Energy Balance Model vs. Carbohydrate-Insulin Model (CIM)

Energy Balance Model

Energy-dense, tasty, modern processed foods drive positive energy balance through increased intake → fat deposition. Simply: calories in > calories out.

Carbohydrate-Insulin Model (CIM)

Rapidly digestible carbohydrates, acting through insulin & other hormones, cause increased fat deposition → which then drives positive energy balance. The metabolic effect of what you eat matters, not just how much.

MNT for Hyperglycemia

Hyperglycemia is reduced by:

  • Decreased glycemic index of foods consumed → by increasing intake of dietary fiber and dietary fat (which slow glucose absorption)
  • Decreased total dietary carbohydrates — NOT elimination, but replacing high-GI with low-GI carbs, adding fiber
  • Exercise — activates AMPK pathway: ATP is used → AMP accumulates → AMPK activated → GLUT4 translocation to cell surface → insulin-independent glucose uptake
Exercise mechanism for hyperglycemia (CBL-relevant, Dr. Spicer material):
Exercise → ATP used → AMP↑ → AMPK activated → GLUT4 translocated to cell membrane → glucose uptake WITHOUT needing insulin. This is why exercise is beneficial in insulin-resistant states.

2. Carbohydrate Counting / Insulin-to-Carb Ratio

Best for: Insulin-dependent patients, especially those with insulin pumps

Key parameters (patient-specific, takes time to calibrate):

  • Insulin-to-Carb Ratio: typically 1 unit insulin per 12–15 g carbohydrate
  • Correction Factor: typically 1 unit insulin per 50 mg/dL glucose above target

Worked Example (from Slide 27)

Given:

• Pre-lunch glucose: 155 mg/dL

• Target post-meal: 130 mg/dL

• Correction factor: 1 unit / 50 mg/dL

• Insulin-to-carb ratio: 1 unit / 15 g CHO

• Lunch carbs: ~45 g CHO (grilled cheese, salad, apple, cup of milk)


Step 1 — Correction dose:

  155 − 130 = 25 mg/dL to correct

  25 ÷ 50 = 0.5 units


Step 2 — Meal dose:

  45 g CHO ÷ 15 g/unit = 3 units


Step 3 — Total:

  0.5 + 3 = 3.5 units of insulin

"I'm not gonna give you calculations on the exam — but understand the concepts."

Examples of 1 Carbohydrate Unit (15 g CHO):

  • Starch: 1 slice bread, ⅓ cup cooked rice/pasta, ½ small bagel
  • Milk: 1 cup milk, ⅔ cup fat-free yogurt
  • Fruit: 1 small piece of fruit, ¾ cup berries, ½ cup apple juice

Glycemic Index — Deep Dive

Delayed carbohydrate absorption = lower glycemic index. Factors that lower GI:

  • Dietary fiber — whole grains, legumes, leafy vegetables, fruits
  • Dietary fat — slows gastric emptying → slows glucose absorption
  • Carbohydrate processing/preparation — cooking method, temperature, what you eat it with

The Pasta Experiment (Fun Fact from Lecture)

Freshly cooked hot pasta = higher GI (starches are open, glucose readily released)

Same pasta cooled overnight in fridge, then eaten cold or reheated = lower GI

Why? The starches retrograde (reform/restructure) when cooled — they become like resistant starch (insoluble fiber) that traps glucose and passes through undigested. Less energy absorbed, but less glucose spike.

Important Nuances (from Dr. G)

  • We rarely eat foods in isolation — eating protein + fat + fiber with carbs lowers the overall GI of a meal
  • Different people have different gut microbiomes → process fiber and carbs differently
  • A candy bar (high sucrose, low glucose) may paradoxically have a lower GI than whole grain bread — the index doesn't account for all types of sugar processing
  • The GI is a research/nutritional science tool — useful but not perfect in clinical practice
Clinical advice for patients: "Put some protein with your carbohydrates, put some fats. Make sure your carbohydrates have fiber content. That should help slow down those spikes and drop the glycemic index."

MNT for Dyslipidemia

Decrease LDL Cholesterol — MNT

  • Fiber — decreases serum cholesterol, decreases insulin response, decreases HMG-CoA reductase activity, decreases reabsorption of bile acids
  • MUFA/SFA ratio — saturated fatty acids ↑ LDL; PUFAs ↓ LDL (but also ↓ HDL somewhat). Aim for MUFA:SFA > 1

Decrease Triglycerides (VLDL) — MNT

  • Limit high-glycemic-load carbohydrates → prevents hepatic fatty acid synthesis from excess carbs
  • Decreased adiposity (weight loss) → improves insulin suppression of FFA release from fat stores → decreases hepatic FFA uptake via portal vein from visceral fat → decreases hepatic TG synthesis → decreased VLDL
  • Decreased insulin resistance → increased activation of lipoprotein lipase (LPL)
Why does hyperglycemia worsen VLDL? In insulin resistance, cells can't use glucose → excess glucose is converted to fat by the liver → increased VLDL production. So controlling carbs also helps dyslipidemia.

Increase HDL — Exercise

MNT primarily targets LDL and VLDL. Exercise is the primary intervention for raising HDL. Exercise also improves insulin sensitivity via GLUT4 receptor activation.

Practice Cases

Case 1: Jennifer — 52 y/o Accountant

HPI: 28-year accountant, nocturia recently, former smoker (25 pack-years, quit 2 years ago), sedentary lifestyle, 2-hour daily commute, hasn't seen PCP in 20 years.

Vitals/Labs: BMI 29.5, waist circumference 37 inches, fasting glucose 118 mg/dL, A1C 6.1%, LDL 155 mg/dL (ref <130), HDL 35 mg/dL (ref >60), BP 120/80

Question 1: Based on her blood work, Jennifer has:

a) Normal glucose levels   b) Glucose levels indicative of prediabetes   c) Glucose levels indicative of type 2 diabetes

B — Prediabetes. Fasting glucose 118 mg/dL (100–125 = prediabetes range) and A1C 6.1% (5.7–6.4% = prediabetes range). Both values fall in the prediabetes category.

Question 2: Based on anthropometric measures, this patient has:

a) No risk   b) Increased risk   c) High risk   d) Very high risk   e) Extremely high risk

C — High risk for development of diabetes. BMI 29.5 alone = increased risk, but waist circumference of 37 inches for a female pushes her into high risk. The abdominal fat distribution carries more weight than BMI alone for diabetes risk assessment.
Waist circumference (abdominal/visceral fat distribution) is a better predictor of diabetes risk than BMI alone. If you have time in practice, measuring waist circumference is more valuable than just getting BMI.

Question 3: Which are risk factors for diabetes in this patient? (check all correct)

a) Hypotension   b) LDL <100   c) History of gestational diabetes   d) Sedentary lifestyle   e) Age

C, D, E.
✅ C — Gestational diabetes history is a risk factor
✅ D — Sedentary lifestyle is a modifiable risk factor
✅ E — Age ≥45 (she is 52) is a risk factor
❌ A — Hypotension is NOT a risk factor (hypertension IS)
❌ B — Low LDL is NOT a risk factor (high LDL and low HDL ARE)

Case 2: 57 y/o Patient — New Onset Symptoms

HPI: Increased thirst, increased urination, fatigue over 3 months. Fasting glucose 128 mg/dL, A1C 6.6%. Recommended MNT.

Note: This patient meets diabetes criteria — fasting glucose ≥126 and A1C ≥6.5%

Question 1: First priority of MNT in T2DM?

a) Improve serum glucose and lipid levels   b) Improve physical activity   c) Improve body image   d) Improve albumin levels

A — Improve serum glucose and lipid levels. MNT first priority is to improve the metabolic parameters — focus on food quality first, then exercise follows.

Question 2: When are expected outcomes from MNT seen?

a) 1 week   b) 2–3 weeks   c) 4–5 weeks   d) 6 weeks to 3 months

D — Between 6 weeks and 3 months. Because A1C reflects glucose management over the ~90-day lifespan of erythrocytes, meaningful changes in A1C require 6 weeks to 3 months to manifest.

Question 3: What is the purpose of MNT in this patient?

a) Control fat intake   b) Control carb and fat intake   c) Control glycemic index by controlling amount and type of carbs, balance fat and carb intake   d) Control water intake

C — Control the glycemic index by controlling the amount and type of carbohydrate intake, balance the fat and carbohydrate intake. This is the most comprehensive answer — MNT for hyperglycemia involves managing glycemic index through carb type/amount AND balancing fat intake.

Master Summary

MNT for Hyperglycemia

  • Control the amount and type of carbohydrate (favor low-GI, high-fiber)
  • Balance carbohydrate and fat intake (promote MUFA/PUFA over SFA)
  • Encourage regular exercise (AMPK → GLUT4 → insulin-independent glucose uptake)

MNT for Dyslipidemia

  • Moderate intake of low glycemic index carbohydrates (high fiber)
  • Moderate intake of fats with MUFA/SFA > 1
  • ↑ Fiber → ↓ LDL, ↓ serum cholesterol, ↓ bile acid reabsorption
  • Exercise → ↑ HDL and ↓ insulin resistance

MNT for Excess Adiposity

  • Motivational interviewing → SMART goals
  • Phase 1: Nutrient quality → 5% weight loss
  • Phase 2: Exercise + nutrient quality → weight maintenance + muscle building
  • Medications (GLP-1 agonists) can assist but ensure adequate nutrition alongside

The Big Picture — Fiber Is the Magic

Fiber is the common thread running through ALL aspects of MNT:

  • ↓ Glycemic index → controls hyperglycemia
  • ↓ LDL & cholesterol → controls dyslipidemia
  • ↑ Satiety → helps with weight management
  • ↓ Absorption of some fats → limits dyslipidemia contribution
  • Promotes gut microbiome health
  • Found in: whole grains, legumes, leafy vegetables, fruits — the foundation of both DASH and Mediterranean diets
"Fiber is magic for many things."
