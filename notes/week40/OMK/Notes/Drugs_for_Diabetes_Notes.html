LO 1

Identify medications that affect glycemic control

⚠ Key Clinical Point: These are non-diabetic drugs that can raise blood glucose. Don't diagnose diabetes from a single elevated glucose — check if the patient is on one of these agents. If a diabetic patient starts one, monitor A1C more closely.
Glucocorticoids
  • Most clinically significant class
  • Increase hepatic glucose production
  • Decrease peripheral insulin sensitivity
  • Ex: prednisone, dexamethasone
Thiazide Diuretics
  • Inhibit insulin secretion (hypokalemia-mediated)
  • Decrease peripheral glucose uptake
  • Ex: hydrochlorothiazide
Beta-Blockers
  • Mask signs of hypoglycemia (tachycardia)
  • Inhibit insulin secretion
  • Impair glucose mobilization
Atypical Antipsychotics
  • Promote weight gain → insulin resistance
  • Inhibit dopamine → ↓ glucose regulation
  • Ex: olanzapine, clozapine
Oral Contraceptives
  • Estrogen/progestin can impair glucose tolerance
  • Modest effect vs. glucocorticoids
Quick Quiz — LO 1
A patient on long-term prednisone for RA has a random glucose of 230 mg/dL and no symptoms. What's the most likely cause and is this enough to diagnose diabetes? Show Answer
Glucocorticoid-induced hyperglycemia. No — without symptoms, you need TWO diagnostic criteria met. The prednisone is likely causing the elevated glucose. If stopping the drug resolves it, it's steroid-induced, not diabetes mellitus.
A Type 1 diabetic on metoprolol (beta-blocker) experiences a hypoglycemic episode. Why is this particularly dangerous? Show Answer
Beta-blockers mask the sympathetic symptoms of hypoglycemia (tachycardia, palpitations, tremor). The patient loses the warning signs. Only diaphoresis (sweating) remains unmasked because it's cholinergic, not adrenergic.
LO 2

Identify the mechanism of action of insulin and its effects on metabolic processes

Insulin Receptor Structure

The insulin receptor is a heterotetramer — two α-β dimers connected to each other (NOT a simple dimerization event as previously taught). It is a receptor tyrosine kinase.

Insulin binds α-subunits
→
Conformational change
→
Autophosphorylation of Tyr residues on β-subunits
→
Phosphorylation of IRS (Insulin Receptor Substrate)
→
PI3K pathway (metabolic)
&
RAS/MAPK pathway (mitogenic)
PI3K → PKB/AKT Pathway (Metabolic)
  • GLUT4 translocation to membrane (muscle & adipose)
  • ↑ Glycogenesis (glucose → glycogen)
  • ↑ Glycolysis (glucose → pyruvate)
  • ↑ Lipogenesis (fatty acid synthesis)
  • ↓ Glycogenolysis (glycogen → glucose)
  • ↓ Gluconeogenesis (new glucose synthesis)
RAS/MAPK Pathway (Mitogenic)
  • Cell growth and proliferation
  • Gene expression changes
  • Also contributes to some metabolic effects
GLUT Transporters
  • GLUT4 — Insulin-dependent; muscle, heart, adipose, brain
  • GLUT2 — Insulin-independent; liver (constitutive glucose entry)
Memory Hook: Insulin is anabolic — it stores energy. It increases everything that builds up (glycogenesis, lipogenesis, glycolysis) and decreases everything that breaks down (glycogenolysis, gluconeogenesis).
Second Messenger for Insulin

Insulin's receptor is a Receptor Tyrosine Kinase (RTK). Its "second messengers" are phosphotyrosine residues on IRS, leading to activation of PI3K → PIP3 → PKB/AKT and RAS → MAPK/ERK. No cAMP or IP3/DAG involvement.

Quick Quiz — LO 2
Insulin increases glucose uptake in muscle primarily by what mechanism? Show Answer
PKB/AKT (downstream of PI3K) phosphorylates serine/threonine residues on target proteins, causing translocation of GLUT4 glucose transporters from intracellular vesicles to the cell surface membrane of muscle and adipose tissue.
What type of receptor is the insulin receptor, and what are its major downstream signaling branches? Show Answer
The insulin receptor is a Receptor Tyrosine Kinase (RTK) — specifically a heterotetramer. After autophosphorylation of tyrosine residues on β-subunits, it phosphorylates IRS, which then activates: (1) PI3K → PKB/AKT (metabolic effects) and (2) RAS/MAPK (mitogenic effects).
A patient has severe liver disease. How might this affect insulin metabolism? Show Answer
Insulin is degraded primarily in the liver (~60%), with kidney (~15-20% endogenous; up to 65% exogenous) and muscle also contributing. Severe liver disease would reduce insulin degradation, potentially increasing hypoglycemic risk. Similarly, renal disease reduces kidney-mediated insulin degradation, explaining lower insulin requirements in end-stage renal disease.

Differentiate between types of insulin and their uses for Type 1 and Type 2 DM

Mnemonic: "Rapid → Regular → NPH → Long → Ultra-Long" = fastest to slowest onset. Type 1 always needs basal + bolus (1 long-acting + 1 rapid/regular with each meal). Type 2 only uses insulin when oral agents fail.

Why Are Rapid-Acting Analogs Faster?

Regular insulin forms hexamers in zinc → must dissociate to monomers before absorption. Rapid-acting analogs have amino acid modifications that prevent hexamer formation → injected as monomers → rapid absorption.

  • Lispro: B28 Pro ↔ B29 Lys swapped
  • Aspart: B28 Pro → Asp
  • Glulisine: B3 Asn → Lys; B29 Lys → Glu
Why Is Glargine Long-Acting?

Modifications make it soluble at pH 4 (acidic vial) but precipitate at pH 7.4 (body). Creates a crystalline SC depot that slowly dissolves. Modifications:

  • A21 Asn → Gly (prevents acid-induced deamidation)
  • Two Arg added to B-chain C-terminus (shifts isoelectric point)

Degludec: B30 Thr removed; B29 Lys conjugated to hexadecanoic acid → dihexamers form multihexameric chains SC → 42h action.

T1 vs. T2 Insulin Strategy

Type 1 DM

  • MUST have insulin — no endogenous production
  • Basal: Glargine or Degludec (once daily)
  • Bolus: Rapid-acting with EVERY meal
  • OR: Insulin pump (continuous SC infusion)
  • Add pramlintide if poorly controlled on insulin

Type 2 DM

  • Insulin not first-line (oral agents preferred)
  • Use when: A1C very high (>13-15%), other agents fail
  • Usually basal insulin (glargine) added to oral regimen
  • Insulin IS used in T2, just not first
Quick Quiz — LO 3
A 17-year-old presents in DKA. Which insulin do you use and by what route? Show Answer
Regular insulin (short-acting) administered IV infusion. Rapid-acting analogs (aspart, lispro) are given SC and NOT recommended for DKA IV use. Inhaled insulin is also NOT recommended for DKA.
Why can't glargine be mixed in the same syringe as regular insulin? Show Answer
Glargine is formulated at acidic pH (pH ~4) which is required for its solubility. Mixing with regular insulin (neutral pH) would alter the pH, causing glargine to precipitate prematurely in the syringe, losing its controlled-release pharmacokinetics. NPH CAN be mixed with regular insulin because NPH is a neutral-pH suspension.
Rank these insulins from fastest to slowest onset: NPH, Glargine, Aspart, Regular. Show Answer
Aspart (<15 min) → Regular (30–60 min) → NPH (2–4 hr) → Glargine (4–5 hr). Remember: more modifications = faster OR more prolonged action depending on the engineering goal.
A patient with T1DM is on glargine at night and aspart before meals. They keep running high glucose before breakfast. What is the breakfast rapid-acting insulin used for vs. the glargine? Show Answer
Aspart is the BOLUS insulin — covers the glucose spike from the meal. Glargine is the BASAL insulin — provides continuous low-level insulin throughout the day to suppress hepatic glucose production and maintain fasting glucose. High fasting glucose (before breakfast) would suggest the basal glargine dose needs adjustment, not the aspart.

Quick Quiz — LO 4
A patient with T2DM and stage 4 CKD (eGFR 22) needs glycemic control. Which agents are problematic and which are preferred? Show Answer
PROBLEMATIC: Metformin (renally excreted unchanged → lactic acidosis risk), SGLT2 inhibitors (require adequate GFR to work; less effective with low eGFR), some sulfonylureas (renally-cleared active metabolites). PREFERRED: SGLT2i actually now have indications for CKD cardioprotection (at lower doses), GLP-1 analogs (except exenatide in severe renal failure), DPP-4 inhibitors (most safe, dose-adjust sitagliptin), insulin (requires dose reduction).
Why do alpha-glucosidase inhibitors cause flatulence and bloating? Show Answer
They prevent brush-border enzyme breakdown of complex carbohydrates in the small intestine. Undigested carbohydrates pass to the large intestine where colonic bacteria ferment them, producing gas (flatulence, bloating, osmotic diarrhea). This is the direct pharmacological consequence of the mechanism.
Why does metformin cause lactic acidosis (albeit rarely)? Show Answer
Metformin inhibits Complex I of the mitochondrial electron transport chain in liver cells. This inhibits gluconeogenesis (specifically pyruvate carboxylase) causing pyruvate/lactate to accumulate. It also inhibits mitochondrial glycerol phosphate dehydrogenase and blunts glucagon effects on lactate/glycerol → glucose conversion. The MOAs that decrease gluconeogenesis specifically increase lactic acid as a byproduct. Accumulates in renal failure because metformin is cleared unchanged by the kidney.
LO 5

Recall the mechanism of action and indications of glucagon

Physiology
  • Produced by pancreatic α cells
  • Released in response to: low blood glucose, amino acids (arginine, alanine), fasting, intense exercise
  • Inhibited by: high insulin, high fatty acids, somatostatin
  • Patients with diabetes still produce glucagon
Mechanism of Action

Glucagon binds the Glucagon receptor — a Class B G-protein-coupled receptor (GPCR) coupled to Gαs.

Glucagon binds Glucagon-R (Class B GPCR)
→
Gαs → ↑ Adenylate Cyclase
→
↑ cAMP → PKA
→
↑ Glycogenolysis & Gluconeogenesis

Second messenger: cAMP

Clinical Indications
  • Severe hypoglycemia — SC injection (most common); nasal spray (Baqsimi, approved 2019)
  • When patient is unconscious / unable to take oral glucose
  • Acts by ↑ hepatic glycogenolysis + gluconeogenesis → ↑ blood glucose
  • Beta-blocker toxicity/overdose — stimulates cardiac rate and force by bypassing blocked β-receptors (activates Gs → ↑ cAMP in cardiomyocytes directly)
  • Also used in radiology (GI hypotonia) — relaxes smooth muscle
Quick Quiz — LO 5
A patient on propranolol (non-selective beta-blocker) for hypertension develops symptomatic bradycardia and hypotension. Atropine fails. What is the pharmacological rationale for glucagon here? Show Answer
Propranolol blocks β1-adrenergic receptors, preventing catecholamine stimulation of the heart. Glucagon acts on its own separate GPCR (Glucagon receptor → Gαs → ↑cAMP → ↑PKA) on cardiomyocytes, completely bypassing β-receptors. It increases heart rate and contractility through a β-receptor-independent pathway.
What is the second messenger for glucagon and what receptor type is involved? Show Answer
Second messenger: cAMP. Receptor type: Class B G-protein-coupled receptor (GPCR) — these bind peptide hormones. Coupled to Gαs → activates adenylate cyclase → ↑cAMP → activates PKA → phosphorylates glycogen phosphorylase (activates glycogenolysis) and phosphorylates CREB (↑gluconeogenic genes).

A 58-year-old with T2DM and newly diagnosed heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF) needs a diabetes drug. Which class is first-line? Show Answer
SGLT2 Inhibitors (e.g., empagliflozin, dapagliflozin). They have FDA indications for reducing CV death and HF hospitalizations independent of glycemic control. They should be initiated as first-line in T2DM with HF or CKD, even if the patient is already on metformin.
A 45-year-old with T2DM and BMI of 38 (class II obesity) needs treatment. No other comorbidities. Rank your top treatment options. Show Answer
Top choices: (1) Tirzepatide (GIP/GLP-1 dual agonist — greatest weight loss ~25 lbs, FDA-approved for T2DM + obesity) or (2) GLP-1 Analog (semaglutide or liraglutide — ~27 lbs weight loss with semaglutide at obesity dose). Both are preferred per AACE guidelines for T2DM with obesity. Metformin is still appropriate as adjunctive therapy but not for obesity-driven first-line selection.

Tirzepatide (Dual Incretin / Twincretin)

DPP-4 Inhibitors: Sitagliptin, Saxagliptin, Linagliptin, Alogliptin
DPP-4 enzyme normally degrades GLP-1 and GIP
→
DPP-4 inhibitor blocks this enzyme
→

→

DPP-4 removes the 2 N-terminal amino acids from GLP-1 and GIP (Pro/Ala at position 2 is the cleavage site). Truncated products [GLP-1(9-36)] and [GIP(3-42)] are inactive. Blocking DPP-4 = more intact active incretin. Still reliant on endogenous GLP-1/GIP production = weaker than exogenous GLP-1 analogs. No gastric slowing, no significant weight loss, no appetite suppression.

Pramlintide (Amylin Analog)
  • MOA: Synthetic analog of amylin (co-secreted with insulin by β-cells). Activates amylin receptors.
  • ↓ Glucagon secretion (postprandial)
  • ↓ Gastric emptying rate
  • ↑ Satiety → ↓ food intake → modest weight loss
  • Indication: Add-on to insulin in T1DM or T2DM with inadequate control
  • Reduce insulin dose when initiating (hypoglycemia risk)
Meglitinides: Repaglinide, Nateglinide

Same mechanism as sulfonylureas — bind SUR subunit → block KATP channels → ↑ Ca²⁺ → insulin secretion. However, they bind with lower affinity at overlapping but distinct sites. Shorter acting → require glucose to maximally stimulate (more glucose-dependent than sulfonylureas → less hypoglycemia). Take before each meal; skip if meal skipped.

Alpha-Glucosidase Inhibitors: Acarbose, Miglitol

Competitive inhibitors of brush-border enzymes lining the small intestine: maltase, isomaltase, sucrase, glucoamylase. These enzymes break down complex carbohydrates and sucrose into absorbable monosaccharides. Blocking them → delays absorption → ↓ postprandial glucose spike. No systemic absorption effect on insulin itself. Take with first bite of each meal.

MOA Deep Dive Quiz — LO 7
Compare the mechanism of sulfonylureas vs. meglitinides vs. GLP-1 analogs in terms of insulin secretion stimulation and hypoglycemia risk. Show Answer
Sulfonylureas: Block KATP channels directly → insulin release regardless of glucose levels → HIGH hypoglycemia risk (glucose-INDEPENDENT).

Meglitinides: Same KATP blockade mechanism but lower affinity, shorter duration, more glucose-sensitive → MODERATE hypoglycemia risk (somewhat glucose-dependent).

GLP-1 Analogs: Activate GLP-1R (Gαs → cAMP) which enhances insulin secretion ONLY in the presence of high glucose (glucose-DEPENDENT) → LOW hypoglycemia risk. cAMP-mediated closure of KATP requires glucose to trigger initial depolarization.
Explain why DPP-4 inhibitors do NOT cause weight loss but GLP-1 analogs do. Show Answer
GLP-1 analogs are exogenous, long-acting, supraphysiological concentrations of GLP-1 that can strongly activate CNS appetite centers and significantly slow gastric emptying → marked satiety + weight loss (up to 27 lbs with semaglutide).

DPP-4 inhibitors only prevent breakdown of endogenous GLP-1 produced in small amounts after meals. The resulting increase in GLP-1 is within physiological range — not high enough to cause significant CNS appetite suppression or meaningful gastric slowing. Also, in T2DM, endogenous GLP-1 secretion is often already impaired, limiting the effect further.
Pioglitazone takes 4–12 weeks to show full glycemic effect. Why? Show Answer
Pioglitazone acts on PPARγ, a nuclear receptor that regulates gene transcription. It must diffuse into the nucleus, bind PPARγ-RXR heterodimers, and alter transcription of multiple target genes (GLUT4, adiponectin, fatty acid transporters, etc.). New protein synthesis takes time. This is fundamentally different from membrane receptor drugs (GLP-1, SGLT2i) that work within hours.

  • Metformin: Complex I → ↑AMP:ATP → AMPK
  • Pioglitazone: PPARγ (nuclear receptor — no cytoplasmic 2nd msg)
  • SGLT2i: Direct transporter block — no receptor signaling
  • Acarbose/Miglitol: Luminal enzyme inhibition — no receptor
Second Messenger Mastery Quiz — LO 8
Which antidiabetic drugs share cAMP as their key second messenger? What receptor type do they use? Show Answer
cAMP-generating drugs: GLP-1 analogs (liraglutide, dulaglutide, semaglutide) via GLP-1R; Tirzepatide via GIP-R and GLP-1R; DPP-4 inhibitors (indirectly); Pramlintide via amylin receptor; Glucagon via glucagon receptor. All use Class B GPCRs (peptide hormone receptors) coupled to Gαs → adenylate cyclase → ↑cAMP → PKA activation. DPP-4 inhibitors just preserve endogenous GLP-1/GIP to activate these same receptors.
Metformin is listed as activating AMPK. Is AMPK itself a "second messenger"? What is the upstream signal? Show Answer
AMPK is more accurately an intracellular energy-sensing kinase than a classical second messenger. The upstream signal: Metformin inhibits Complex I of the electron transport chain → ↓ATP production → ↑ AMP:ATP ratio. The ↑AMP:ATP ratio directly allosterically activates AMPK (AMP binds γ subunit of AMPK and enables phosphorylation by LKB1). The "second messenger" concept here centers on the AMP:ATP ratio change as the initial intracellular signal, and AMPK as the effector kinase.
A classmate says "sulfonylureas work via cAMP like GLP-1." Are they correct? Show Answer
Incorrect. Sulfonylureas bind the SUR1 subunit of ATP-sensitive K⁺ (KATP) channels — they are not GPCRs and do NOT generate cAMP. Blockade of KATP channels → K⁺ stays inside → membrane depolarizes → voltage-dependent Ca²⁺ channels open → ↑ intracellular Ca²⁺ → insulin granule exocytosis. The second messenger is Ca²⁺, not cAMP. GLP-1 analogs use Gαs → cAMP → PKA, which eventually also closes KATP channels but via PKA phosphorylation of channel subunits, not direct blockade.
Fill in the table: Drug → Receptor → 2nd Messenger for: (1) Semaglutide, (2) Empagliflozin, (3) Pioglitazone, (4) Glimepiride, (5) Tirzepatide Show Answer
(1) Semaglutide → GLP-1R (Class B GPCR, Gαs) → ↑cAMP
(2) Empagliflozin → SGLT2 transporter (not a receptor) → No 2nd messenger (direct block)
(3) Pioglitazone → PPARγ (nuclear receptor / transcription factor) → No cytoplasmic 2nd messenger (direct nuclear action)
(4) Glimepiride → SUR1 (KATP channel subunit) → ↑ intracellular Ca²⁺
(5) Tirzepatide → GIP-R + GLP-1R (both Class B GPCRs, Gαs) → ↑cAMP
How does GLUT4 translocation relate to both insulin signaling AND AMPK activation (metformin)? Show Answer
Two independent pathways can drive GLUT4 to the cell membrane:

1. Insulin: RTK → IRS → PI3K → PIP3 → PDK1 → PKB/AKT → phosphorylates AS160 (Akt substrate 160) → releases GLUT4 vesicles → membrane insertion.

2. AMPK (Metformin): ↑AMP:ATP → AMPK activation → also phosphorylates AS160 at different sites → same endpoint: GLUT4 translocation to membrane.

This is why metformin can increase glucose uptake even in insulin-resistant states — it bypasses the insulin receptor and acts through an independent energy-sensing pathway. This is the "insulin-independent" glucose uptake mechanism.
