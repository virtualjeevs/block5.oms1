Confidence Intervals — Review

📋 PLO 15 — CI with hypothesis testing
Definition

Confidence interval (CI) = a range of values that, with a specified probability, contains the true population parameter.

If we repeated our sampling process many times, 95% of the calculated intervals would contain the true parameter, but 5% would not.

Advantages of CIs over p-values alone:

  • Provides information about precision of estimate — the smaller the interval, the more precise
  • Indicates the range of clinically plausible values
  • Helps assess practical significance, not just statistical significance

CI Formulas (Ferland's cheat sheet):

Confidence Interval Calculations
99% CI = Mean ± 2.58 × SEM
95% CI = Mean ± 1.96 × SEM   ← cheat: Mean ± 2 × SEM
90% CI = Mean ± 1.65 × SEM
SEM = SD / √n
CI for a Difference Between Means
CI = (x̄₁ − x̄₂) ± z* × √(σ₁²/n₁ + σ₂²/n₂)
Used in superiority, non-inferiority, and equivalence trials
02

CI Rules of Thumb

⚠ High-Yield — Know Both Rules Cold

There are two scenarios depending on whether you're comparing a ratio or a difference between means.

Metric Type CI Includes... CI Excludes... Interpretation
Ratio (OR, RR, HR) 1 1 Includes 1 → NOT significant
Excludes 1 → IS significant
Difference between means 0 0 Includes 0 → NOT significant
Excludes 0 → IS significant
Ferland's Rule

CIs are used in many studies, but are almost always seen in superiority, equivalence, and non-inferiority trials. These trials report using CIs, not just p-values.

Odds Ratio CI calculation (for reference):

95% CI for Odds Ratio
Lower 95% CI = e^(ln(OR) − 1.96 × √(1/a + 1/b + 1/c + 1/d))
Upper 95% CI = e^(ln(OR) + 1.96 × √(1/a + 1/b + 1/c + 1/d))
2×2 contingency table: a=TP, b=FP, c=FN, d=TN

Superiority Trials

📋 PLOs 11, 13, 14
Core Definition — Superiority Trial

Superiority trial = Tests if the new intervention is better than the control.

Key phrase: "Novel vs. non-standard" — comparing a new intervention against a placebo OR non-standard intervention.

Example: A pharmaceutical company develops a new blood pressure medication and wants to test if it is more effective than an existing drug.

  • Usually a randomized controlled trial (RCT)
  • Aims to determine if new treatment is superior (better) than the active control or standard treatment
  • Used when an effective treatment already exists and researchers want to find a better option
  • Common in drug trials and medical device studies
Null Hypothesis (H₀)
New treatment is NOT better than control

Treatment − Control ≤ 0
Alternative Hypothesis (Hₐ)
New treatment IS superior to control

Treatment − Control > 0
Hypothesis Type
Two-tailed

Tests both directions of difference

Key design features:

  • Comparator: Placebo OR active standard — only trial type that can use placebo
  • Analysis: ITT (Intention-to-Treat)
  • Reporting: p-value AND confidence interval (CI)
  • Largest sample size of the three trial types
CI Interpretation — Superiority Trials
95% CI for mean difference Interpretation
✓ CI is entirely above 0 New treatment is SUPERIOR — superiority demonstrated
CI crosses 0 No significant difference — superiority NOT proven
✗ CI is entirely below 0 Control is superior — new treatment is WORSE
⚠️
Common Misconception (Ferland flags this explicitly): A non-significant superiority result is often wrongly interpreted as proof of no difference. This is incorrect — it only means you cannot clearly conclude that the new agent performs better. You also cannot conclude equivalence from a non-significant superiority trial.
04

Non-Inferiority Trials

📋 PLOs 11, 13, 14
Core Definition — Non-Inferiority Trial

Non-inferiority trial = Tests if the new intervention is not unacceptably worse than the standard.

Key phrase: "Novel vs. standard" — always uses an active comparator (never placebo).

Example: A new, once-daily antibiotic is tested against a well-established, twice-daily antibiotic for treating pneumonia. The goal is to show the new antibiotic is not significantly worse while offering improved patient adherence and fewer side effects.

Why NI trials exist: It is more and more difficult to develop truly superior treatments. New treatments are being sought that have the same approximate efficacy but demonstrate better quality in other aspects — e.g., side effects, convenience, sustained-release formulations.

  • Primary objective: the investigational product is not clinically inferior (not unacceptably inferior) to a comparative agent
  • Goal: "The new treatment is not worse than the standard by more than a pre-specified margin"
  • Common in the regulatory setting for a new label indication
  • Always requires an active control — placebo cannot be used
⚠ The Non-Inferiority Margin (Δ)

The non-inferiority margin (Δ) = the maximum clinically acceptable difference between treatments.

  • Based on clinical judgement
  • Should be smaller than the established advantage of the active control over placebo
  • For NI trials, there is only ONE non-inferiority margin (one-sided)
  • Too large → risk of accepting truly inferior treatments
  • Too small → unrealistic sample size requirements
Null Hypothesis (H₀)
New is worse than standard by more than Δ

New < Standard − Δ
Alternative Hypothesis (Hₐ)
New is not worse than standard by more than Δ

New ≥ Standard − Δ
Hypothesis Type
One-tailed

Only tests in one direction (inferiority)
CI Interpretation — Non-Inferiority Trials
95% CI lower bound vs. −Δ Interpretation
✓ CI is entirely above −Δ Non-inferiority is PROVEN
CI crosses −Δ Non-inferiority is NOT proven
✗ CI is entirely below −Δ New treatment IS INFERIOR
🎯
Ferland's key point: For NI trials, only check the lower CI bound vs. −Δ. If the lower bound is entirely above −Δ, non-inferiority is declared — the upper bound doesn't matter for the NI conclusion. Additionally, if the CI is also entirely above zero, you can additionally claim superiority.
Analysis Note

NI trials require BOTH ITT and per-protocol analysis. In NI trials, ITT can actually bias toward showing non-inferiority (by diluting differences), so per-protocol analysis is an essential check. This is different from superiority trials where ITT is the standard.

Sample Size Note

Calculated sample size for a non-inferiority trial is usually the smallest of the three trial types — the NI margin creates a wider acceptable range, reducing the precision needed.

05

Equivalence Trials

📋 PLOs 11, 13, 14
Core Definition — Equivalence Trial

Equivalence trial = Tests if the new intervention is similar to the standard (neither significantly worse nor better).

Key phrase: "Novel vs. standard" — always uses an active comparator (never placebo).

Example: A generic pharmaceutical company develops a new version of a brand-name asthma inhaler. A clinical trial is conducted to determine whether the generic inhaler delivers the same therapeutic effect as the brand-name drug within a predefined equivalence margin.

Common uses of equivalence trials:

  • Generic drugs — bioequivalent to the original drug in terms of PK and PD
  • New formulations — extended-release vs. immediate-release
  • Dosage forms — capsule vs. tablet
  • Updated medical devices — at least as safe and effective as the old device
  • Diagnostic tests — equivalent sensitivity, specificity, and accuracy
⚠ Two Deltas — This Is Critical

Unlike NI trials (which have ONE margin), equivalence trials have TWO deltas: a positive (+Δ) and a negative (−Δ).

The trial must show that the true treatment difference lies between −Δ and +Δ — neither unacceptably worse NOR unacceptably better.

Acceptable range is often ±20% for generic drug bioequivalence.

Null Hypothesis (H₀)
The two treatments are clinically different beyond the equivalence margin

|New − Standard| > Δ
Alternative Hypothesis (Hₐ)
The two treatments are statistically and clinically equivalent within the margin

|New − Standard| ≤ Δ
Hypothesis Type
Two-tailed

Tests both directions — neither better nor worse
CI Interpretation — Equivalence Trials
95% CI vs. (−Δ, +Δ) Interpretation
✓ CI is entirely within (−Δ, +Δ) Equivalence is DEMONSTRATED — treatments are equivalent
✗ CI extends beyond −Δ or +Δ Equivalence is NOT proven
Sample Size Note

Equivalence trials generally require a larger sample size than non-inferiority trials because they must demonstrate similarity in both directions (neither worse NOR better) and the equivalence delta may be small.

🎯
The equivalence CI must fit entirely inside the box (−Δ to +Δ). If any part of the CI sticks out beyond either boundary — the equivalence margin is breached and equivalence is NOT declared. Check both bounds, not just the lower.

📋 PLO 12 — Plotting and interpreting trials
How to Read the Diagrams

Each horizontal bar represents a 95% CI. The square/dot = point estimate. The reference lines (dashed red) = Δ margins. 0 = no difference.

Direction convention (standard): Right = Treatment better; Left = Control better. Watch out for flipped axes — some published figures show the axes reversed!

08

Strengths & Weaknesses

📋 PLO 13 — Strengths and weaknesses

Superiority Trials

Strengths

  • Proves a treatment is better
  • Clear clinical relevance
  • Common in drug approvals
  • Can use placebo control

Weaknesses

  • Largest sample size needed
  • Non-significant results are inconclusive (not proof of equivalence)
  • Ethical issues with placebo use
  • Non-significant results often wrongly interpreted as "no difference"

Non-Inferiority Trials (vs. Superiority)

Strengths

  • Usually less expensive, more convenient
  • May use fewer subjects
  • Useful where it may be unethical to use placebo
  • Smaller sample size than superiority
  • Compares to a gold standard

Weaknesses

  • More complex to design, conduct, and interpret
  • Dependent on the level of the non-inferiority margin — choosing margin is subjective
  • May be more susceptible to bias
  • Results cannot be used to show superiority
  • Risk of accepting truly inferior treatments if margin too large

Equivalence Trials

Strengths

  • Confirms interchangeability of treatments
  • Useful for safer, easier treatments
  • Ensures generic/biosimilar consistency
  • Avoids unnecessary superiority trials

Weaknesses

  • Large sample size required
  • Placebo not used
  • Requires strict confidence intervals
  • No placebo comparison
  • Choosing the NI margin is subjective — can falsely claim "no difference"
💡 Sample Size Summary (Ferland's Rule)

Ranked from largest to smallest required sample size:

  • Superiority = largest sample size (must detect a true difference)
  • Equivalence = large (must prove similarity in both directions, small delta)
  • Non-inferiority = smallest (one-sided, larger acceptable margin)
📌
Ferland's bottom line: Superiority, non-inferiority, and equivalence trials answer three different questions:
"Better than?" → Superiority  |  "Not worse than?" → Non-inferiority  |  "Same as?" → Equivalence
