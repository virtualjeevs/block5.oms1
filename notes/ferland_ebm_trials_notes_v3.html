Confidence intervals — the stuff he keeps using

Core idea

Confidence interval = range of values that, with specified probability, contains the true population parameter.

  • If we repeated sampling many times, 95% of calculated intervals would contain the true parameter, and 5% would not.
  • Smaller interval = more precision.
  • Also tells you the range of clinically plausible values, not just whether p < 0.05.
Quick formulas
99% CI = mean ± 2.58 × SEM
95% CI = mean ± 1.96 × SEM    (exam cheat: mean ± 2 × SEM)
90% CI = mean ± 1.65 × SEM
SEM = SD / √n

Rules of thumb for ratios

If the 95% CI for a ratio includes 1: the ratio is not statistically significant.
If the 95% CI for a ratio excludes 1: the ratio is statistically significant.

Rules of thumb for mean differences

If the 95% CI for a difference between means includes 0: the difference is not statistically significant.
If the 95% CI for a difference between means excludes 0: the difference is statistically significant.
Big exam reminder: superiority, non-inferiority, and equivalence trials are most often shown with a CI for the mean difference.

When do you use each trial?

Superiority trial (Novel vs non-standard)

Tests if the new intervention is better than the control.

  • Use when you want to know if the new thing truly outperforms the comparator.
  • Control may be placebo or a non-standard / comparison treatment.
  • Classic example: a new blood pressure medication being tested to see if it works better than an existing option.
Non-inferiority trial (Novel vs standard)

Tests if the new intervention is not unacceptably worse than the standard.

  • Use when the new treatment may have other advantages: safer, cheaper, more convenient, once-daily, sustained release.
  • You compare to an active control, not placebo.
  • Need a pre-specified non-inferiority margin (Δ).
Equivalence trial (Novel vs standard)

Tests if the new intervention is similar (neither significantly worse nor significantly better) to the standard.

  • Common for generic vs brand-name drugs, new formulations, dosage forms, devices, and diagnostics.
  • Uses an active control.
  • Needs two margins: a lower margin -Δ and an upper margin +Δ.

Trial types — definitions, hypotheses, and interpretation

Superiority

Goal: demonstrate the new treatment is better than control.

H0: Treatment − Control ≤ 0
HA: Treatment − Control > 0
  • If 95% CI is entirely above 0 → superiority demonstrated.
  • If 95% CI includes 0 → superiority not demonstrated.
  • If 95% CI is entirely below 0 → new treatment is worse.

Non-inferiority

Goal: demonstrate the new treatment is not worse than control by more than a pre-specified margin.

H0: New < Standard − Δ
HA: New ≥ Standard − Δ
  • There is one margin: Δ.
  • If lower bound of 95% CI is > -Δ → non-inferiority demonstrated.
  • If lower bound of 95% CI is ≤ -Δ → non-inferiority not demonstrated.
  • Results cannot be used to show superiority.

Equivalence

Goal: demonstrate treatments are similar within pre-specified margins.

H0: |New − Standard| > Δ
HA: |New − Standard| ≤ Δ
  • There are two margins: -Δ and +Δ.
  • If the entire 95% CI lies within (-Δ, +Δ) → equivalence demonstrated.
  • If any part of the 95% CI lies outside (-Δ, +Δ) → equivalence not demonstrated.

Classic trap in superiority trials

Non-significant superiority result does not mean the treatments are equal.

  • You can only say you did not show superiority.
  • You cannot conclude equivalence.
  • Different questions need different designs: non-inferiority or equivalence.

Margins matter

  • For non-inferiority, choosing the margin is crucial and often subjective.
  • Too large a margin → risk of accepting a truly inferior treatment.
  • Too small a margin → unrealistic sample size.
  • For equivalence, setting both deltas is critical.

Watch out!!!

High-yield traps

  • A non-significant superiority trial does NOT prove no difference.
  • A non-significant superiority trial does NOT prove equivalence.
  • Non-inferiority results cannot be used to show superiority.
  • For equivalence, the CI must be entirely within (-Δ, +Δ) — touching or crossing out = not equivalent.
  • For non-inferiority, what matters is whether the lower bound stays above -Δ.

Axis trap

The lecture explicitly warns that some figures use a flipped axis. Always check:

  • Which side means control better?
  • Which side means treatment better?
  • Is the plotted value T − C or something else?
Translation: do not memorize shapes without reading the axis labels first.
