Most Common Red EyeViral / Bacterial / Allergic

Conjunctivitis

Epidemiology
Key Stats
  • Most common cause of red eye in primary care; affects all ages
  • ~6 million cases/year in the US
  • Viral etiology predominates regardless of age; bacterial more common in children
Pathophysiology
🦠 Viral
Adenovirus most common. Follicular conjunctivitis — gray-white, round-oval elevations 0.5–1.5 mm on tarsal conjunctiva
🦠 Bacterial
S. pneumo, H. flu, N. gonorrhoeae, C. trachomatis

GC → perforation
Trachoma → pannus + cicatrization
🌿 Allergic
Mast cell / IgE-mediated → Chemosis · Lid edema (blepharitis) · Conjunctival papillary reaction
Clinical Presentation
General — All Types
  • Pain: MINIMAL — key distinguisher from serious pathology
  • Visual acuity: near-normal
  • Watery discharge · photophobia · mild foreign-body sensation
Viral
Watery / mucoserous discharge
Burning, sandy, gritty
Often starts one eye
Bacterial
Mucopurulent discharge
Morning crusting
Pus = bacterial
Allergic
Itching dominant symptom
Redness + epiphora
Bilateral, seasonal
⚠️ Red Flags → Ophthalmology Referral
Photophobia + headache + nausea + severe foreign-body sensation = refer immediately
Illness Script — Exam Findings
FindingDetail
Conjunctival injectionDiffuse redness (not focal)
Visual acuityRelatively normal
Discharge characterWatery (viral/allergic) vs. mucopurulent (bacterial)
Cultures / stainsNOT required initially; required if GC suspected
Acute vs. chronicAcute = within 3–4 weeks; Chronic = >4 weeks

#1 Blindness >65 Developed WorldDry & Wet Forms

Age-Related Macular Degeneration

Epidemiology
Population
  • Leading cause of blindness in >65 in developed countries
  • Female > Male; White > other ethnicities
  • Prevalence: 0.2% (55–64) → 13.1% (>84)
Risk Factors
  • Advanced age · family history · genetics (complement pathway, 52 variants at 34 loci)
  • Smoking · obesity · cardiovascular disease
Pathophysiology
Four Drivers of AMD
1. Lipofuscinogenesis 2. Drusogenesis 3. Inflammation 4. Neovascularization
☀️ Dry AMD (Atrophic)
Drusen (lipids + vitronectin)→RPE / Bruch's membrane→Slow RPE atrophy

Progression over decades

💧 Wet AMD (Neovascular)
VEGFA↑→Choroidal NV→Fluid/blood leakage→Macular elevation / RPE detachment

Weeks–months; usually one eye first

Pathology Progression

Early:

  • Drusen displacing Bruch's membrane
  • Microglial infiltration (inflammation)

Late:

  • Bruch's membrane severely disrupted
  • RPE + photoreceptor atrophy & loss
  • Angiogenesis + edema in choroidal vasculature
Clinical Presentation
Key Features
  • Painless central/pericentral visual impairment; difficulty adapting to lighting
  • Metamorphopsia — straight lines appear wavy (test with Amsler grid)
  • Scotoma — central blind spot
  • Dry: slow over decades · Wet: weeks–months, one eye first
Illness Script — Exam Findings
TestDry AMDWet AMD
Amsler GridMetamorphopsia (wavy lines); scotomas
FundoscopyDrusen; RPE atrophyGrayish-green discoloration; serous detachment; subretinal hemorrhage/exudate; fibrous disciform scars
OCTHistological-grade in-vivo retinal imaging; gold standard non-invasive detection
🥦 Pearl
Diet rich in antioxidants (esp. spinach & kale) reduces AMD risk

#2 Blindness in USOpen-AngleAngle-Closure

Glaucoma

Epidemiology
Key Stats
  • 2nd leading cause of blindness in US (after AMD); #1 globally after cataracts
  • 2.3–3 million US; 80 million worldwide; ~10% impairment, ~5% blindness
  • Mean age of onset: 60; Open-angle > Angle-closure in prevalence
Open-Angle Risk Factors
Age >40 · African descent · Diabetes · Myopia · Family history
Angle-Closure Risk Factors
Asian descent · Mydriasis · Anatomically narrow iridocorneal angle
Pathophysiology
Aqueous Humor Flow — Normal
Ciliary body→ Post. chamber→ Pupil→ Ant. chamber→ Trabecular meshwork→ Venous drainage
Open-Angle
Trabecular clogging / ↓drainage→↑IOP (gradual)→Optic nerve ischemia→Progressive vision loss
Angle-Closure
Iridocorneal blockage→↓AH outflow→↑↑IOP

Primary: narrow angle anatomy · Secondary: acquired obstruction

Pathology — What Glaucoma Destroys
  • Rim loss + cup enlargement → ↑cup-to-disc ratio
  • Loss of retinal ganglion cell axons in the optic nerve
  • Apoptosis via compression at lamina cribrosa
  • Loss of cell mass in lateral geniculate nucleus (thalamus)
Clinical Presentation
Open-Angle (Insidious)
  • Initially asymptomatic
  • Mild HA, impaired dark adaptation
  • Bilateral, progressive peripheral → central VF loss
  • Arcuate scotoma from blind spot
  • Vision loss typically permanent
⚡ Angle-Closure Acute — EMERGENCY
  • Sudden, unilateral, severely painful red eye
  • Frontal HA · N/V · blurred vision · halos
  • Corneal clouding
  • Mid-dilated, irregular, unreactive pupil
Angle-Closure Chronic
Asymptomatic early → progressive peripheral VF loss
Illness Script — Exam Findings
TestOpen-AngleAngle-Closure
TonometryPossibly elevated (~40%); normal 10–21 mmHgIOP >21 mmHg
Gonioscopy / Slit lampNormal anterior chamber angleNarrowed iridocorneal angle
FundoscopyOptic disc cupping & pallor; disc hemorrhageEdema; microhemorrhages of optic disc
Visual fieldsPeripheral loss → arcuate scotomaPeripheral loss; central sparing
Cup-to-disc ratio>0.6 = suspicious for glaucoma
🔍 Pearl
Glaucoma is undetected in 9/10 affected people; undiagnosed in 50% in undeveloped countries

Lens OpacitySurgically CorrectableAge-Related

Cataract

Epidemiology
Key Stats
  • Common cause of blindness worldwide: 12.7% (N. America) → 42% (SE Asia)
  • Typically age >60; Women > Men (1.3:1); White > African American > Hispanic
  • Risk factors: age · smoking · alcohol · sunlight · malnutrition · DM · HIV/AIDS · systemic corticosteroids
Pathophysiology
Core Mechanism

Lens transparency = orderly cell arrangement + crystallin proteins. Unlike other epithelia, the lens cannot shed nonviable cells — they compress toward center and progressively lose transparency.

Age-Related
Degenerative cell changes + photo-oxidative insult
Env. stressors → ↑oxidative toxins, ↓antioxidants
Non-Age-Related
Trauma · Uveitis / Scleritis · Radiation · Metabolic disease · Topical corticosteroids · Anticholinesterases
Mechanisms by Cataract Type
TypeMechanism
CongenitalDisturbances in lens growth
Posterior subcapsularFibrous metaplasia of lens epithelium
CorticalCortical hydration between lens fibers
NuclearPigment deposition (urochrome)
Clinical Presentation
Symptoms
  • Painless, progressive, bilateral vision decline
  • Difficulty with night driving — glare from headlights · colored halos
  • Fading/yellowing of objects; difficulty reading signs
  • Myopic shift — early sign before opacity causes vision loss (↑refractive power of cataractous lens)
Classification
By Maturity
Immature Red reflex present

Mature Red reflex absent; VA ≤ 20/400

Hypermature Cortex liquefied → 2° glaucoma
Nuclear
Dark brown/black lens · Iris shadow on PE · Dull colors and whites
Cortical & PSC
Cortical: wedge-shaped peripheral opacities; less vision-impacting

PSC: posterior subcapsular → disabling glare
Illness Script — Exam Findings
FindingDetail
Red reflexAbsent / opacified in mature or hypermature cataract
Slit lampLens opacity (nuclear, cortical, or posterior subcapsular)
Iris shadowPresent in nuclear cataract
Dilated fundus examRule out other pathology; view may be obscured
VAProgressively declining; ≤20/400 in mature; glare worst in PSC type
Surgery triggerWhen cataract interferes with ADLs; outcome generally restorative
⚠️ Key Complication
Mature and hypermature cataracts can cause secondary glaucoma
