Definition & Epidemiology

LO: Define osteoporosis · Describe epidemiology & etiology

What is Osteoporosis?

A systemic skeletal disease characterized by low bone mass and microarchitectural deterioration of bone tissue, leading to increased bone fragility and susceptibility to fracture.

Diagnosis: Bone Mineral Density (BMD) T-score ≤ −2.5 at the lumbar spine, femoral neck, or total hip (DXA scan). Osteopenia = T-score −1.0 to −2.5.

📊 Epidemiology
  • ~10 million Americans affected; 44 million with low BMD
  • Women 4× more affected than men
  • Most common in postmenopausal women (estrogen ↓ → bone resorption ↑)
  • Risk ↑ with age — #1 cause of fractures in elderly
  • Common fracture sites: vertebra, hip, wrist
  • Hip fractures: 20% 1-year mortality
⚙️ Etiology / Risk Factors
  • Primary: Postmenopausal (estrogen deficiency), age-related (type II)
  • Secondary: Glucocorticoid use, hyperthyroidism, hypogonadism, malabsorption, RA, chronic kidney/liver disease
  • Smoking, alcohol, low Ca/Vit D intake
  • Small frame, White/Asian ethnicity
  • Family history of fracture
  • Immobilization
🔑 Core Pathophysiology
Normal bone undergoes constant remodeling: osteoclasts resorb bone → osteoblasts form new bone. In osteoporosis, this balance shifts toward resorption > formation. The RANK/RANKL/OPG axis is central: RANKL (produced by osteoblasts) binds RANK on osteoclast precursors → osteoclast activation. OPG acts as a decoy receptor, blocking RANKL. Many drugs target this axis.

Calcium & Vitamin D Supplementation

Foundation of prevention & adjunct to all pharmacotherapy
⚙️ Why They Matter
Calcium is the primary mineral of bone matrix (hydroxyapatite). Vitamin D (specifically calcitriol / 1,25-OH₂-D₃) is essential for intestinal Ca²⁺ absorption, renal Ca²⁺ reabsorption, and bone mineralization. Without adequate Ca & D, all pharmacologic agents are less effective.
Calcium Carbonate
Os-Cal, Tums — take WITH food
▼
⚙️ Key Point
Requires acidic environment (gastric acid) for absorption → must be taken with food. Avoid taking with PPIs if possible.
📋 Dosing
Total elemental Ca: 1000 mg/day (ages 19–50, men 51–70); 1200 mg/day (women ≥51, men ≥71). Do not exceed 500–600 mg elemental Ca per dose (split doses).
Calcium Citrate
Citracal — take WITHOUT food
▼
⚙️ Key Point
Does NOT require acid → can be taken without food. Preferred in patients on PPIs, H2 blockers, achlorhydria, or gastric bypass.
📋 Dosing
Same total elemental Ca targets. Less constipation than carbonate.
Vitamin D
D3 (Cholecalciferol) preferred > D2 (Ergocalciferol)
PO / IM
▼
⚙️ MOA
D3 is converted in liver → 25-OH-D₃ (storage form, measured in labs) → kidney → 1,25-(OH)₂-D₃ (calcitriol) (active form). Calcitriol binds VDR → ↑ intestinal Ca²⁺ absorption via calbindin, ↑ renal Ca²⁺ reabsorption, promotes bone mineralization, modulates PTH secretion.
📋 Dosing
  • Prevention/treatment: 800–2000 IU/day
  • Measure serum 25-OH-D; target ≥20 ng/mL (≥30 ng/mL preferred in osteoporosis)
  • D3 raises 25-OH-D levels more effectively than D2
⚠️ Toxicity (high dose)
  • Hypercalcemia, hypercalciuria
  • Nephrolithiasis
  • Soft tissue calcification
🧪 Lab Monitoring
Check serum 25-OH-D (not 1,25-OH₂-D₃ for monitoring), serum Ca²⁺, 24-hr urine Ca²⁺. In renal failure (CKD Stage 4–5), use calcitriol (active form) since kidneys can't convert 25-OH-D.

⚙️ Mechanism of Action
Bisphosphonates bind avidly to hydroxyapatite at sites of active bone remodeling → internalized by osteoclasts. Nitrogen-containing bisphosphonates (all of the above) inhibit farnesyl pyrophosphate synthase (FPPS), a key enzyme in the mevalonate/cholesterol pathway → prevents prenylation of small GTPases (Ras, Rho, Rac) → osteoclast cytoskeletal disruption and apoptosis → ↓ bone resorption. Net result: antiresorptive, preserves bone mass.
Oral Bisphosphonates — Key Prescribing Rules
Alendronate · Risedronate · Ibandronate (oral)
▼
⚠️ Critical Administration Rules
  • Take on an empty stomach first thing in the morning with a full 8-oz glass of plain water
  • Remain upright (sitting/standing) for at least 30 minutes (alendronate/risedronate) or 60 minutes (ibandronate)
  • No food, drink, or other medications for ≥30 min after
  • Failure to follow → esophageal injury
⚠️ Adverse Effects
  • GI: Esophagitis, esophageal ulceration, dysphagia, abdominal pain (oral)
  • Esophageal cancer — rare, long-term concern (alendronate)
  • Osteonecrosis of the Jaw (ONJ) — rare; risk ↑ with IV, invasive dental procedures, cancer patients
  • Atypical femoral fractures (AFF) — subtrochanteric/diaphyseal; after prolonged use (>5 yrs); often preceded by thigh/groin pain
  • Musculoskeletal pain — arthralgia, myalgia, bone pain
  • Hypocalcemia — must correct before starting
  • Acute phase reaction (IV only): fever, myalgia, flu-like symptoms within 72h of first IV infusion (zoledronic acid)
🚫 Contraindications
  • Hypocalcemia (must correct first)
  • Abnormalities of esophagus (achalasia, stricture) — oral forms
  • Inability to stand/sit upright for ≥30–60 min — oral forms
  • CrCl < 35 mL/min (most bisphosphonates; zoledronic acid CrCl < 35 mL/min)
  • Hypersensitivity to drug
🧪 Renal Impairment
  • Bisphosphonates are renally cleared
  • Avoid if CrCl < 35 mL/min (accumulation → renal toxicity)
  • Zoledronic acid: also avoid CrCl < 35; infuse slowly
  • No hepatic dose adjustment needed
🤰 Pregnancy
  • Avoid in pregnancy — may impair fetal bone development (bisphosphonates incorporate into bone long-term)
  • Category C/D; not routinely used in premenopausal women
  • Breastfeeding: not recommended
🔄 Drug Interactions
  • Ca²⁺, antacids, Fe, Mg supplements: chelate bisphosphonates → ↓ absorption (oral) — separate by ≥30–60 min
  • NSAIDs: additive GI irritation
  • Aminoglycosides: additive risk of hypocalcemia
💉 Allergy / Hypersensitivity
Rare hypersensitivity reactions. If oral bisphosphonate not tolerated (GI), switch to IV formulation or different drug class. No cross-reactivity with salmon in bisphosphonates (unlike calcitonin).
⚠️ Drug Holidays
Consider bisphosphonate holiday after 3–5 years (oral) or 3 years (IV zoledronic acid) in patients at lower risk → reduces AFF risk. Reassess BMD and FRAX before continuing.

Antiresorptive · Monoclonal Antibody

RANK-L Inhibitor

PLO 1 · Denosumab
Denosumab
Prolia (osteoporosis 60 mg SQ q6mo) · Xgeva (oncology 120 mg SQ qmo)
SQ q6mo
▼
⚙️ Mechanism of Action
Fully human monoclonal IgG2 antibody that binds to RANKL (Receptor Activator of Nuclear Factor κB Ligand) → prevents RANKL from binding RANK on osteoclast precursors and mature osteoclasts → ↓ osteoclast formation, function, and survival → ↓ bone resorption. Mimics the action of OPG (osteoprotegerin), the endogenous RANKL decoy receptor.
🔑 Critical Clinical Point — Rebound Effect
Do NOT discontinue abruptly. Stopping denosumab causes a rebound surge in bone resorption (osteoclast reactivation) → rapid BMD loss and multiple vertebral fractures within 6–12 months. If stopping denosumab, transition to a bisphosphonate to prevent rebound.
⚠️ Adverse Effects
  • Hypocalcemia — most serious; ↑ risk in CKD/dialysis patients
  • Serious infections: cellulitis, UTI, pneumonia (immunomodulatory effects — RANKL expressed on immune cells)
  • ONJ (osteonecrosis of jaw)
  • Atypical femoral fractures (long-term)
  • Dermatologic: eczema, dermatitis, rash, urticaria
  • Back pain, musculoskeletal pain
  • Injection site reactions
🚫 Contraindications
  • Hypocalcemia (correct before starting)
  • Pregnancy (Category X)
  • Hypersensitivity to denosumab
  • Latex allergy (needle cap contains latex in some formulations)
🧪 Renal Impairment
  • Denosumab is a monoclonal antibody → NOT renally cleared → no dose adjustment needed based on CrCl
  • However, CKD/dialysis patients have ↑ risk of hypocalcemia — monitor closely
  • Can be used when bisphosphonates are contraindicated (CrCl < 35)
🤰 Pregnancy
  • Category X — contraindicated in pregnancy
  • RANKL pathway important in fetal development (lymph node formation)
  • Long half-life of monoclonal antibody — counsel on contraception
🔄 Drug Interactions
  • Agents that ↓ serum Ca²⁺ (loop diuretics, aminoglycosides, calcitonin) → additive hypocalcemia risk
  • Immunosuppressants → additive infection risk
  • Minimal CYP-based interactions (not metabolized by CYP enzymes)
💉 Allergy Considerations
  • Latex allergy: prefilled syringe needle cap may contain latex — use alternative formulation
  • Hypersensitivity reactions (rare): anaphylaxis, angioedema, urticaria — have epinephrine available

Antiresorptive · SERM

Mixed Estrogen Agonist/Antagonist (EAA) — SERM

PLO 1 · Selective Estrogen Receptor Modulator
Raloxifene
Evista — 60 mg PO daily
PO Daily
▼
⚙️ Mechanism of Action — Tissue Selectivity

Raloxifene binds estrogen receptors (ERα, ERβ) but induces a different receptor conformation than estradiol, recruiting different coactivators/corepressors in different tissues:

  • AGONIST at bone: ↓ osteoclast activity → antiresorptive (like estrogen)
  • AGONIST at lipids: ↓ LDL cholesterol
  • ANTAGONIST at breast: ↓ breast cancer risk (approved for chemoprevention)
  • ANTAGONIST at uterus: Does NOT stimulate endometrium → no ↑ endometrial cancer risk (unlike estrogen alone)
  • ANTAGONIST for vasomotor symptoms: May worsen hot flashes
⚠️ Adverse Effects
  • VTE (DVT/PE) — significantly ↑ risk; most important side effect
  • Hot flashes / vasomotor symptoms — worsens these (agonist effect lost at hypothalamus)
  • Leg cramps
  • Edema
  • No endometrial stimulation (advantage over estrogen)
🚫 Contraindications
  • Active or prior VTE (DVT, PE, retinal vein thrombosis)
  • Premenopausal women (not effective; may disturb cycle)
  • Pregnancy and breastfeeding
  • Prolonged immobilization (↑ VTE risk — hold before elective surgery)
🧪 Renal / Hepatic Impairment
  • Primarily hepatically metabolized; use with caution in hepatic impairment
  • Mild renal impairment: no dose adjustment needed
  • Avoid in Child-Pugh C cirrhosis
🤰 Pregnancy
  • Category X — teratogenic in animals
  • Contraindicated in premenopausal women, pregnancy, breastfeeding
  • Only for postmenopausal women
🔄 Drug Interactions
  • Cholestyramine: ↓ raloxifene absorption (↓ 60%) — avoid concurrent use
  • Warfarin: slight ↓ PT — monitor INR
  • Other highly protein-bound drugs: may compete for albumin binding
💉 Allergy
Hypersensitivity to raloxifene. No specific cross-reactivities of clinical significance noted.

Antiresorptive · TSEC

Tissue Selective Estrogen Complex (TSEC)

PLO 1 · Conjugated Estrogens + Bazedoxifene
Conjugated Estrogens / Bazedoxifene
Duavee (Aprela) — 0.45 mg CE / 20 mg BZA · PO Daily
PO Daily
▼
⚙️ Mechanism of Action

Combination of:

  • Conjugated Estrogens (CE): ER agonist → ↓ bone resorption (antiresorptive) + relieves vasomotor symptoms (hot flashes)
  • Bazedoxifene (BZA): SERM that acts as an ER antagonist at the uterus → blocks CE-induced endometrial proliferation

Key innovation: BZA replaces progestin as uterine protector → no progestin needed (avoids progestin side effects like breast cancer risk ↑, mood changes).

Indicated for: Postmenopausal women with a uterus who have vasomotor symptoms AND want bone protection. Not for women who have had hysterectomy (just use estrogen alone).

⚠️ Adverse Effects
  • VTE (DVT/PE) — estrogen component
  • Nausea, abdominal cramps
  • Muscle spasms
  • Oropharyngeal pain
  • Dizziness
  • No endometrial hyperplasia (BZA protects)
  • Potential CV effects of estrogen (discuss risk/benefit)
🚫 Contraindications
  • Undiagnosed abnormal uterine bleeding
  • Active or prior VTE or arterial thromboembolic disease
  • Known/suspected estrogen-dependent malignancy (breast, endometrial cancer)
  • Pregnancy
  • Hepatic impairment
  • Hypersensitivity
🧪 Renal / Hepatic
  • Hepatically metabolized — contraindicated in hepatic impairment
  • Renal impairment: no specific dose adjustments; use caution
🤰 Pregnancy
  • Category X — contraindicated
  • For postmenopausal women only
🔄 Drug Interactions
  • CYP3A4 inducers (rifampin, phenytoin, carbamazepine): ↓ CE and BZA levels
  • CYP3A4 inhibitors (ketoconazole, erythromycin): ↑ CE levels
  • Cholestyramine: ↓ BZA absorption — avoid concurrent use

Antiresorptive · Peptide Hormone

Calcitonin

PLO 1 · Salmon Calcitonin — Miacalcin, Fortical
Salmon Calcitonin
Miacalcin, Fortical — 200 IU intranasal daily OR 100 IU SQ/IM
Intranasal / SQ / IM
▼
⚙️ Mechanism of Action
Synthetic salmon calcitonin (more potent than human calcitonin) binds calcitonin receptors on osteoclasts → directly inhibits osteoclast activity (↓ ruffled border, ↓ proton pump activity) → ↓ bone resorption. Also has analgesic effect via central ↑ endorphins → useful for pain of acute vertebral compression fractures.
⚠️ FDA Warning — Malignancy Signal
In 2013, FDA raised concerns about possible increased risk of malignancy with long-term calcitonin use. Now considered a second-line or last-resort agent. Use only when other agents cannot be used.
⚠️ Adverse Effects
  • Intranasal: Rhinitis, epistaxis, nasal dryness, sinusitis
  • Injectable: Nausea/vomiting, flushing, local injection site reactions
  • Headache, dizziness
  • Hypocalcemia (mild)
  • Antibody formation → tachyphylaxis (↓ efficacy over time)
  • Possible malignancy risk (FDA warning)
🚫 Contraindications
  • Allergy to salmon — test dose may be required before first injection
  • Hypersensitivity to calcitonin-salmon
🧪 Renal / Hepatic
  • Renally excreted as metabolites
  • Use with caution in renal impairment (no specific dosing guidelines)
  • Hepatic impairment: minimal concern (not hepatically metabolized significantly)
🤰 Pregnancy
  • Category C — avoid unless clearly necessary
  • Calcitonin may ↓ maternal serum Ca²⁺ → crosses placenta, potential fetal effects
  • Breastfeeding: not recommended (may ↓ milk production)
💉 Allergy — CRITICAL
Allergy to salmon is a key contraindication. Perform skin test before first dose in patients with suspected fish allergy. If skin test positive → do not administer. If positive prior reaction to calcitonin-salmon → contraindicated.
🔄 Drug Interactions
  • Prior bisphosphonate use: ↓ calcitonin receptor sensitivity (possible ↓ effect)
  • Lithium: may ↓ lithium levels (calcitonin may ↑ renal Li clearance)
  • Limited significant interactions overall

Anabolic · PTH Analog

Recombinant Human PTH 1-34

PLO 1 · Teriparatide (Forteo)
🔑 Intermittent vs. Continuous PTH — High-Yield
Intermittent PTH (daily SQ injection) = ANABOLIC — ↑ osteoblast activity > osteoclast activity → net bone formation.
Continuous PTH (as in primary hyperparathyroidism) = CATABOLIC → ↑ bone resorption. This distinction is critical for Dr. Ly's clinical case questions.
Teriparatide
Forteo — 20 mcg SQ daily · Max 2 years cumulative
SQ Daily
▼
⚙️ Mechanism of Action
Recombinant human PTH (amino acids 1–34, the biologically active N-terminal fragment). When administered intermittently, binds PTH1R (GPCR) on osteoblasts → anabolic signaling (↑ Wnt signaling, ↑ IGF-1) → ↑ osteoblast proliferation, differentiation, and survival → ↑ bone formation. Also transiently stimulates osteoclasts (RANKL ↑), but net effect is anabolic. Increases both trabecular AND cortical bone.
⚫ BLACK BOX WARNING
☠️ Black Box — Osteosarcoma Risk
  • Dose- and duration-dependent ↑ osteosarcoma in rats
  • Risk in humans unknown but drug is contraindicated in patients at ↑ baseline risk for osteosarcoma
  • Do not use > 2 years cumulative lifetime exposure (combined with abaloparatide)
⚠️ Adverse Effects
  • Orthostatic hypotension — especially within 4h of injection; sit/lie down after
  • Nausea, vomiting, dizziness
  • Leg cramps
  • Hypercalcemia (mild, transient)
  • Hypercalciuria — ↑ nephrolithiasis risk
  • Hyperuricemia
  • Injection site reactions
🚫 Contraindications
  • Paget's disease of bone
  • Prior radiation therapy to the skeleton
  • Open epiphyses (children, adolescents)
  • Metabolic bone disease (other than osteoporosis)
  • Pre-existing hypercalcemia
  • Bone metastases or skeletal malignancy
  • Cumulative > 2 years lifetime use
🧪 Renal / Hepatic
  • Hepatically metabolized (peptidase enzymes) + renal excretion of metabolites
  • Mild-to-moderate renal impairment: use with caution (hypercalcemia risk ↑)
  • Severe renal impairment (CrCl < 30): data limited — use caution
  • Active nephrolithiasis: use with caution (↑ hypercalciuria)
🤰 Pregnancy
  • Category C — use in pregnancy only if clearly needed
  • Not routinely used in premenopausal or pregnant women
  • Breastfeeding: unknown excretion; not recommended
🔄 Drug Interactions
  • Digitalis/digoxin: teriparatide-induced hypercalcemia may predispose to digitalis toxicity — monitor closely
  • Concurrent antiresorptives: Adding teriparatide after antiresorptive therapy is fine; combining simultaneously may blunt anabolic effect of teriparatide (avoid simultaneous use)

Anabolic · PTHrP Analog

Human PTHrP[1-34] Analog

PLO 1 · Abaloparatide (Tymlos)
Abaloparatide
Tymlos — 80 mcg SQ daily · Max 2 years (combined with teriparatide)
SQ Daily
▼
⚙️ Mechanism of Action — vs. Teriparatide
Synthetic analog of PTH-related Peptide (PTHrP) — shares N-terminal homology with PTH. Binds PTH1R but preferentially stabilizes the RG (Gs-coupled) conformation over the R0 (arrestin-recruiting) conformation → more cAMP-mediated anabolic signaling with less arrestin signaling → ↑ bone formation with less hypercalcemia and hypercalciuria than teriparatide.

Abaloparatide has a greater selectivity for anabolic vs. catabolic PTH signaling compared to teriparatide.

⚫ BLACK BOX WARNING
☠️ Black Box — Osteosarcoma Risk
Same BBW as teriparatide. Do not use > 2 years cumulative (combined with teriparatide).
⚠️ Adverse Effects
  • Orthostatic hypotension — monitor after injection
  • Dizziness, nausea, headache
  • Palpitations
  • Hypercalcemia (less than teriparatide)
  • Hypercalciuria (less than teriparatide)
  • Injection site reactions (pain, erythema)
🚫 Contraindications
  • Same as teriparatide: Paget's disease, prior skeletal radiation, open epiphyses, hypercalcemia, bone mets/malignancy
  • >2 years cumulative use (combined with teriparatide)
🧪 Renal Impairment
  • Renally excreted; exposure ↑ in renal impairment
  • Avoid in severe renal impairment (CrCl < 30 mL/min)
  • Mild-moderate: use with caution, monitor Ca²⁺
🤰 Pregnancy
  • Not recommended in pregnancy or breastfeeding
  • Limited human data; animal studies show fetal effects

Dual Mechanism · Anabolic + Antiresorptive

Sclerostin Inhibitor

PLO 1 · Romosozumab (Evenity)
Romosozumab
Evenity — 210 mg SQ monthly × 12 months (two 105 mg injections/month)
SQ Monthly × 12 mo
▼
⚙️ Mechanism of Action — Dual Effect
Monoclonal antibody targeting sclerostin, a glycoprotein produced by osteocytes that inhibits Wnt signaling (normally puts the brakes on bone formation). By blocking sclerostin:
  • ↑ Wnt/β-catenin signaling → ↑ osteoblast activity → ↑ bone formation (anabolic)
  • ↓ RANKL expression + ↑ OPG → ↓ osteoclast activity → ↓ bone resorption (antiresorptive)

Net result: The ONLY agent that simultaneously ↑ bone formation AND ↓ bone resorption. Produces rapid, large BMD gains early on, then effect wanes (hence 12-month limit).

⚫ BLACK BOX WARNING
☠️ Black Box — Cardiovascular Risk
  • ↑ Risk of MI and stroke
  • Do not initiate in patients who have had a MI or stroke within the past year
  • If CV event occurs during treatment → discontinue
  • Weigh CV risk vs. bone benefit before use
⚠️ Adverse Effects
  • Injection site reactions (most common)
  • Arthralgias, headache
  • Hypocalcemia — monitor; correct before use
  • ONJ (rare)
  • Atypical femoral fractures (rare)
  • CV events (MI, stroke — see BBW)
🚫 Contraindications
  • Hypocalcemia (correct before starting)
  • MI or stroke within the past year
  • Hypersensitivity to romosozumab
  • Use beyond 12 months not recommended (diminishing returns)
🧪 Renal / Hepatic
  • Monoclonal antibody — not renally cleared; no dose adjustment for renal impairment
  • ↑ Hypocalcemia risk in severe CKD — monitor Ca²⁺ closely
  • Hepatic: no specific adjustments
🤰 Pregnancy
  • Avoid in pregnancy — animal data show fetal skeletal anomalies
  • Category X equivalent — postmenopausal use only
🔄 Drug Interactions
  • Agents causing hypocalcemia (loop diuretics, aminoglycosides): additive risk
  • Minimal CYP-based interactions (monoclonal antibody)
  • After completing 12 months → transition to an antiresorptive agent (bisphosphonate or denosumab) to maintain gains
💉 Allergy
Hypersensitivity reactions (angioedema, erythema multiforme, urticaria) — rare. Discontinue if serious hypersensitivity occurs.

Clinical Decision Guide

Dr. Ly LO: Select most/least likely drug based on patient constraints
💉 Based on Allergies
  • Salmon allergy: Avoid calcitonin → choose bisphosphonate, denosumab, or SERM
  • Latex allergy: Avoid denosumab prefilled syringe (needle cap) → use alternative
  • GI intolerance (oral bisphosphonate): Switch to IV zoledronic acid or denosumab
🧪 Based on Renal Function
  • CrCl < 35 mL/min: Avoid bisphosphonates → preferred: denosumab or raloxifene
  • Dialysis patients: Use denosumab with very close Ca²⁺ monitoring (high hypocalcemia risk)
  • Abaloparatide: Avoid if CrCl < 30 mL/min (teriparatide: caution with severe impairment)
  • CKD Stage 4–5: Use calcitriol (active vitamin D) rather than D3/D2
⚠️ VTE History / Risk
  • Active or prior DVT/PE: Avoid raloxifene and TSEC/CE → choose bisphosphonate or denosumab
  • Raloxifene ↑ VTE risk comparably to estrogen
❤️ Based on Cardiovascular History
  • Recent MI or stroke (<1 year): Avoid romosozumab (BBW)
  • High CV risk patient: Prefer bisphosphonate or denosumab over romosozumab or TSEC
🌡️ Vasomotor Symptoms + Osteoporosis
  • Postmenopausal woman WITH uterus + hot flashes + osteoporosis: Best agent = TSEC (CE/Bazedoxifene) — treats both vasomotor Sx AND osteoporosis without progestin
  • Raloxifene worsens hot flashes → avoid in this scenario
  • TSEC contraindicated if VTE history or estrogen-sensitive cancer
🩺 Breast Cancer Risk Reduction + Osteoporosis
  • Postmenopausal woman at high breast cancer risk + osteoporosis: Consider raloxifene (FDA-approved for breast cancer chemoprevention AND osteoporosis)
  • No VTE history required; avoids endometrial stimulation
🏗️ Severe Osteoporosis / High Fracture Risk — Anabolic Agents
  • Severe osteoporosis (T-score ≤ −3.0), prior fragility fractures on antiresorptives, or failure of antiresorptive therapy → consider teriparatide or abaloparatide
  • Abaloparatide preferred if ↑ risk of hypercalcemia/nephrolithiasis (less hypercalciuric than teriparatide)
  • Follow anabolic therapy with antiresorptive (bisphosphonate) to maintain gains — do not stop with nothing
  • After completing romosozumab (12 mo) → must transition to antiresorptive
🤰 Pregnancy Considerations
  • All major osteoporosis drugs are avoided in pregnancy (Category C–X)
  • Premenopausal women with osteoporosis: optimize Ca²⁺ + Vitamin D; consider risks/benefits carefully with specialist
  • Denosumab, SERM, TSEC, teriparatide, abaloparatide, romosozumab: all contraindicated/not recommended in pregnancy
  • Bisphosphonates: Category C/D — avoid if possible; long-term skeletal incorporation is a concern
🔬 Based on Lab Data
  • Low serum Ca²⁺ (hypocalcemia): Correct BEFORE starting bisphosphonates, denosumab, or romosozumab (all contraindicated with active hypocalcemia)
  • Low 25-OH-D: Supplement with Vitamin D; check prior to starting any pharmacotherapy
  • Elevated Ca²⁺ (hypercalcemia): Contraindicated for teriparatide/abaloparatide; evaluate for primary hyperparathyroidism before initiating PTH analogs
  • Low CrCl (<35): Avoid bisphosphonates; prefer denosumab
  • Elevated ALP: May suggest high-turnover state or Paget's — relevant to teriparatide contraindication in Paget's
  • Serum markers of bone turnover (CTX for resorption, P1NP for formation): guide treatment monitoring

💡 Quick LO Cheatsheet — "Select Most/Least Likely"
  • Can't take oral meds / poor GI tolerability: → Zoledronic acid (IV annual) or Denosumab (SQ q6mo)
  • CrCl <35: → Denosumab (most likely), avoid bisphosphonates (least likely)
  • Salmon allergy: → Avoid calcitonin (least likely)
  • Recent MI/stroke: → Avoid romosozumab (least likely)
  • VTE history: → Avoid raloxifene and estrogen/TSEC (least likely)
  • Hot flashes + uterus + osteoporosis: → TSEC/Duavee (most likely); avoid raloxifene
  • Paget's disease + osteoporosis: → Avoid teriparatide/abaloparatide (least likely)
  • Severe osteoporosis / on antiresorptive but still fracturing: → Teriparatide or abaloparatide (most likely)
  • Risk of hypercalcemia / nephrolithiasis + needs anabolic: → Abaloparatide > teriparatide
  • Breast cancer risk reduction + osteoporosis: → Raloxifene (most likely)
  • Hypocalcemia present: → Correct Ca²⁺ first; all agents (bisphosphonates, denosumab, romosozumab) contraindicated
