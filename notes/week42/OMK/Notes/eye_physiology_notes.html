LO 1

Properties of Light & Its Interactions

What is Light?

Light is electromagnetic radiation in the visible spectrum (~380–700 nm). It behaves as both a wave and a particle (photon). The eye is sensitive only to this narrow band of the electromagnetic spectrum.

  • Wavelength determines color — short (~420 nm) = violet; long (~700 nm) = red
  • Intensity (amplitude) determines brightness
  • Photons are discrete quanta of light energy — a single photon can activate a rod photoreceptor

Refraction

Light bends when it passes from one medium to another (change in density). The degree of bending depends on the refractive index of each medium.

  • Most bending: air → cornea interface
  • Additional fine-tuning: lens (adjustable)
  • Measured in diopters (D) — reciprocal of focal length in meters

Reflection & Absorption

Objects reflect certain wavelengths of light to our eyes — this is how we perceive color. The choroid and RPE absorb stray light to reduce scatter and improve visual acuity.

  • Melanin in RPE absorbs extraneous photons
  • Reduces internal reflections that would blur the image
Key Principle: The goal of the optical system is to precisely focus a sharp image onto the photoreceptors of the fovea. Any imprecision in refraction results in a refractive error (myopia, hyperopia, etc.).
LO 2

Optical Features of the Eye

Three Tunics (Layers)

External — Fibrous

  • Sclera — protective structural shell; maintains eyeball shape
  • Cornea — transparent, provides ~70% of total refractive power (~43D); fixed curvature; most refraction occurs at the air-corneal interface

Middle — Uvea (Vascular)

  • Choroid — vascular; nourishes outer retina via diffusion; melanin reduces scatter
  • Ciliary body — ciliary processes secrete aqueous humor; ciliary muscles change lens shape
  • Iris — controls pupil diameter (dilator & constrictor muscles)

Internal — Retinal

  • Neural retina — photoreceptors + processing neurons + glia
  • RPE (Retinal Pigment Epithelium) — single pigmented cell layer; critical support for photoreceptors

Three Chambers

The Lens

Transparent, biconvex, elastic — composed mainly of water and soluble proteins (crystallins α, β, γ and filensin). Has no blood supply; nutrients come from aqueous humor.

  • Provides ~30% of refractive power (~20D), but crucially is adjustable
  • Ciliary muscles contract → zonule fibers relax → lens rounds up → more refractive power (for near vision)
  • Ciliary muscles controlled by parasympathetic CN III (Edinger-Westphal nucleus)
  • Glucose is the primary energy source for the lens
⚕
Clinical — Cataracts

High blood glucose (diabetes) → sorbitol accumulates in lens → reduces crystallin solubility → lens opacity. Treated by surgical removal and replacement with intraocular prosthetic lens.

⚕
Clinical — Glaucoma

Impaired drainage of aqueous humor at the Canal of Schlemm → elevated intraocular pressure → compresses retinal blood vessels → retinal neuron damage. Open-angle (90%): painless, gradual peripheral vision loss. Acute angle-closure: emergency — blurry vision, eye pain, halos, nausea, rapid vision loss.

Image Inversion — Camera Obscura Effect

Because light rays cross at the nodal point of the lens, the image projected onto the retina is inverted (upside-down) and mirror-reversed relative to the external world.

  • Superior visual field → inferior retina
  • Temporal/nasal fields split at the optic chiasm
  • The brain "corrects" this inversion during cortical processing

Accommodation

The process by which the eye adjusts focus for objects at different distances:

  • Near vision: Ciliary muscles CONTRACT → zonule fibers relax → lens becomes more rounded (greater curvature) → increased refractive power
  • Far vision (unaccommodated): Ciliary muscles relax → zonule fibers taut → lens flattened → less refractive power
📌
Remember

The pupil also constricts during near vision (part of the accommodation reflex), which increases depth of field and reduces aberrations.

LO 4

Refractive Errors & Corrections

Normal Vision — Emmetropia

Parallel rays from a distant object (≥6m) are focused precisely on the retina without any accommodation. The eye's axial length matches its total refractive power.

Condition Defect Problem Correction
Myopia
Nearsighted		 Eye too long OR cornea too curved Image focused in front of retina; distant objects blurry Concave (diverging) lens to move focal point back
Hyperopia
Farsighted		 Eye too short OR cornea too flat Image focused behind retina; near objects blurry Convex (converging) lens to move focal point forward
Astigmatism Irregular corneal or lens curvature Different meridians have different focal lengths; blurry at all distances Cylindrical lens to equalize refractive power across meridians
Presbyopia
Age-related		 Lens loses elasticity with age Reduced accommodation; difficulty with near vision Reading glasses (convex lens); bifocals; accommodative lens implants
💡
Clinical Tip

LASIK reshapes the cornea (the primary refractive surface) to permanently correct myopia, hyperopia, and astigmatism. Presbyopia correction requires different approaches since the lens, not the cornea, has lost elasticity.

⚕
Clinical — Dry Eye Disease

Affects ≥5% of US adults; more common in women and those over 50. Caused by decreased tear production from the lacrimal gland (CN V1) and/or decreased oil secretion from meibomian glands → excess evaporation. Symptoms: redness, burning/itching, gritty sensation.

Structural Anatomy (Both Types)

Outer Segment

  • Contains stacked membranous disks packed with photopigment molecules
  • In rods: disks are internalized (separate from plasma membrane)
  • In cones: disk membranes are continuous with plasma membrane
  • This is where phototransduction occurs

Inner Segment + Synaptic Terminal

  • Inner segment: contains mitochondria (high metabolic demand), nucleus, and biosynthetic machinery
  • Connected by a cilium
  • Synaptic terminal: releases glutamate tonically (in dark); hyperpolarization reduces release
Color Vision: Individual cones cannot detect color by themselves — color vision requires comparison of signals across the three cone types. The brain assigns colors based on the relative outputs of S, M, and L cones. This is why wavelength discrimination (not single-cell activity) underlies color perception.
LO 6

Retinal Anatomy & Organization

⚠
The Great Paradox

The retina is organized in an "inside-out" manner: light must pass through ganglion cell and bipolar cell layers before reaching the photoreceptors at the back. These inner layers are mostly transparent. The photoreceptors point away from the incoming light (toward the RPE).

Layers — From Vitreous to Choroid (inner → outer)

  1. Nerve fiber layer — ganglion cell axons coursing to optic disk
  2. Ganglion cell layer — cell bodies of retinal ganglion cells (RGCs); only cells in retina that fire action potentials
  3. Inner plexiform layer (IPL) — synapses between bipolar/amacrine cells and ganglion cells
  4. Inner nuclear layer (INL) — cell bodies of bipolar, horizontal, and amacrine cells
  5. Outer plexiform layer (OPL) — synapses between photoreceptors and bipolar/horizontal cells; first synaptic layer
  6. Outer nuclear layer (ONL) — photoreceptor cell bodies
  7. Photoreceptor outer segments — rods and cones (where transduction occurs)
  8. Retinal Pigment Epithelium (RPE) — single layer of pigmented support cells

Five Neural Cell Types

Vertical Pathway (signal to brain)

  • Photoreceptors — detect light; graded potentials
  • Bipolar cells — relay to ganglion cells; graded potentials (on-center or off-center subtypes)
  • Retinal Ganglion Cells (RGCs) — output neurons; only cells producing action potentials; axons form CN II

Lateral Pathway (contrast enhancement)

  • Horizontal cells — in INL, processes in OPL; mediate lateral inhibition from photoreceptors
  • Amacrine cells — in INL, processes in IPL; modulate bipolar-to-ganglion cell transmission
📌
Basic 3-Neuron Circuit

Photoreceptor → Bipolar Cell → Retinal Ganglion Cell (→ optic nerve → LGN → V1)

Key Retinal Landmarks

Fovea Centralis

  • Small pit (~300μm) in center of macula lutea
  • Almost entirely cones; no rods
  • Nearly 1:1 photoreceptor-to-ganglion cell ratio
  • Minimal blood vessels and inner cell layers (pushed aside)
  • Greatest visual acuity — smallest receptive fields

Optic Disk (Papilla)

  • Where ganglion cell axons exit as CN II
  • Blind spot — no photoreceptors at all
  • Also entry/exit point for ophthalmic artery and vein
  • Subarachnoid space is continuous with brain → window for intracranial pressure (papilledema)

Glial Support: Müller Cells

The dominant glial cell of the retina. Functions include: K⁺ regulation, neurotransmitter reuptake (especially glutamate), debris removal, and mechanical scaffolding of the neural retina.

⚕
Clinical — Age-Related Macular Degeneration (ARMD)

Leading cause of blindness in those over 50. Begins with accumulation of extracellular debris (drusen) between the RPE and choroid capillaries. This leads to excess vascularization (blood leaking under retina) and later atrophy of the RPE and choroid, resulting in photoreceptor death and central vision loss.

⚕
Clinical — Detached Retina

Separation of the neural retina from the RPE. Causes: trauma, vascular disease, metabolic disorders, aging. Impairs vision and threatens viability of the neural retina because the RPE can no longer supply nutrients or phagocytose photoreceptor debris. Medical emergency requiring prompt treatment.

LO 7

Phototransduction → Action Potential

The Dark State — "Dark Current"

In the absence of light, rod outer segments are in a partially depolarized state (~−30 to −40 mV):

  • Guanylate cyclase constitutively produces cGMP
  • cGMP keeps cGMP-gated Na⁺ channels open → steady Na⁺ influx (dark current)
  • Depolarized photoreceptor continuously releases glutamate at its synapse

The Light State — Phototransduction Cascade

1
Photon absorption by rhodopsin (a 7-TM GPCR). The chromophore 11-cis retinal (derived from Vitamin A) undergoes photoisomerization → all-trans retinal (active), changing rhodopsin's conformation to its active form (metarhodopsin II)
2
Activated rhodopsin activates transducin (a G-protein) by exchanging GDP for GTP. One activated rhodopsin can activate ~500 transducin molecules → massive amplification
3
GTP-bound transducin activates phosphodiesterase (PDE), which hydrolyzes cGMP → GMP. cGMP levels drop rapidly.
4
Reduced cGMP causes cGMP-gated Na⁺ channels to close. Na⁺ influx stops but K⁺ continues to leave → membrane hyperpolarizes (to ~−65 mV)
5
Hyperpolarized photoreceptor reduces glutamate release at its synapse onto bipolar cells. This change in synaptic drive is propagated through the retinal circuit to ganglion cells, ultimately generating action potentials that travel via CN II to the brain.
Oddity of the Retina: Light causes hyperpolarization (not depolarization) of photoreceptors — opposite to most sensory receptors. Also, photoreceptors are "on" (depolarized, releasing glutamate) in the dark, and "turn off" (hyperpolarize, reduce glutamate release) in the light.

Signal Amplification

  • One photon → one rhodopsin activated → ~500 transducin → ~500 PDE → hydrolysis of thousands of cGMP molecules → closure of hundreds of channels
  • Rods can respond to a single photon
  • In bright light: cGMP drops to zero → all channels close → system saturated (no further hyperpolarization possible)
  • Light adaptation: recovery mechanisms (Ca²⁺-mediated feedback on guanylate cyclase, rhodopsin kinase) restore sensitivity in sustained light

Role of the RPE in Photopigment Regeneration

  • Photoreceptors cannot re-isomerize all-trans retinal back to 11-cis retinal
  • All-trans retinal is shuttled to the RPE, where re-isomerization occurs
  • 11-cis retinal is then recycled back to the photoreceptor outer segments
  • RPE also phagocytoses shed outer segment membrane disks (daily renewal essential to prevent photoreceptor death from oxidative damage)
  • Epithelial transport of glucose and Vitamin A from choroid capillaries

LO 8

Ganglion Cells & Center/Surround Responses

Retinal Ganglion Cell (RGC) Properties

  • Only cells in the retina that generate action potentials
  • Have a spontaneous (baseline) firing rate even in the absence of visual input — any change in firing is a signal to the brain
  • Each RGC responds to light in a specific small region of the visual field = its receptive field
  • Receptive fields have an antagonistic center-surround structure
  • Axons of all RGCs form the optic nerve (CN II), exit at the optic disk

Two Fundamental Types of RGCs

ON-Center OFF-Surround
  • Increase firing when light hits center of receptive field
  • Decrease firing when light hits surround
  • Best stimulus: bright spot on dark background
  • Driven by on-center bipolar cells
OFF-Center ON-Surround
  • Increase firing when darkness hits center (light decrements)
  • Decrease firing when darkness hits surround
  • Best stimulus: dark spot on bright background
  • Driven by off-center bipolar cells

How Bipolar Cells Create ON/OFF Responses

Photoreceptors release glutamate. Two types of bipolar cells respond to glutamate differently:

Cell TypeReceptorResponse to GlutamateResponse to Light (↓ glut)
OFF-center bipolar AMPA/Kainate (ionotropic) Depolarizes (Na⁺ influx) Hyperpolarizes → OFF-center ganglion fires
ON-center bipolar mGluR6 (metabotropic, Gi-coupled) Hyperpolarizes (cation channel closed) Depolarizes → ON-center ganglion fires

Retinofugal Projection — From Eye to Brain

  • RGC axons exit at optic disk → form optic nerve
  • At the optic chiasm: nasal fibers from each eye cross to the contralateral side; temporal fibers remain ipsilateral
  • Result: left visual field → right hemisphere; right visual field → left hemisphere
  • Optic tracts → Lateral Geniculate Nucleus (LGN) of thalamus
  • Optic radiations → Primary Visual Cortex (V1) in occipital lobe (calcarine sulcus)
  • Brain reconstructs the proper image orientation and combines inputs from both eyes
LO 9

Physiological Principles of Center/Surround

Lateral Inhibition — The Mechanism

Center-surround receptive fields are generated by lateral inhibition, largely mediated by horizontal cells:

  1. Light hits center photoreceptors → they hyperpolarize → reduce glutamate release
  2. Simultaneously, center photoreceptors were releasing glutamate onto horizontal cells in the dark → horizontal cells depolarize
  3. Horizontal cells release GABA back onto surrounding photoreceptors (and onto bipolar cells), inhibiting the surround signal
  4. Net effect: the center signal is amplified relative to the surround → contrast enhancement
Key Insight: Lateral inhibition sharpens the spatial profile of excitation. It reduces the "spreading" of activation to neighboring neurons and creates a sharp contrast between stimulated and non-stimulated areas — greatly increasing spatial resolution and edge detection.

Why Center/Surround Matters — Edge Detection

  • Center-surround ganglion cells are poor detectors of uniform illumination (even if very bright)
  • They are excellent detectors of luminance contrast — transitions between light and dark (edges)
  • When a light/dark edge falls across the retina, it activates different populations of on-center and off-center cells differentially
  • The retina encodes differences in luminance, not absolute luminance — it "emphasizes regions where there are differences in luminance"
  • This is why we can see well across a huge range of background illumination (from dim room to full sunlight)

Response Profile of a Ganglion Cell

  • Small centered spot (optimally sized for receptive field center) → maximal response
  • Larger spot including surround → reduced response (surround inhibition cancels center activation)
  • Uniform full-field illumination → least response; center and surround cancel each other
  • Edge or border stimulus → differential activity across ON and OFF cell populations → perceived edge
💡
Think About It

On-center and off-center ganglion cells have overlapping receptive fields across the entire retina — so every point in the visual field is analyzed by both types simultaneously. Information about light increments and decrements is carried separately to the brain via their respective axons.

From Retina to Perception of Edges

In higher visual cortical areas (beyond V1), orientation-selective neurons respond to the output of many retinal ganglion cells. Each such cortical neuron only captures a fraction of the information in a scene, but collectively they reconstruct the full visual image:

  • Retinal ganglion cells → detect luminance contrast at many orientations
  • V1 simple cells → detect oriented edges at specific positions
  • V1 complex cells → detect oriented edges regardless of exact position
  • Higher areas → detect shapes, faces, objects, motion
The "Neural Image": What the retinal ganglion cells send to the brain is essentially an edge-filtered, contrast-enhanced version of the visual scene — not a photographic copy. The brain then uses this to reconstruct our rich visual experience.
