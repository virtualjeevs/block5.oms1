Part I · Upper GI Infections
01 🫁 Esophagitis — Inflammation of the Esophagus
🎯 Big Picture: Infectious esophagitis is almost always in immunocompromised patients. Non-infectious causes (GERD, eosinophilic, meds) are more common in healthy patients. Know the histology buzzwords cold.
Types of Esophagitis
Non-Infectious (Common) GERD — most common cause; can → erosive esophagitis
Eosinophilic — eosinophil infiltration from allergens
Medication-Induced — NSAIDs, antibiotics, bisphosphonates
Radiation Esophagitis
Infectious (Immunocompromised ★) Viral: HSV, CMV, VZV (rare), HIV, HPV, EBV
Fungal: Candida (most common!), Aspergillus, Histoplasma
Parasitic: Trypanosoma cruzi (Chagas disease)
Bacterial: Very rare — M. tuberculosis

02 🦠 Gastritis & Helicobacter pylori
🎯 H. pylori Overview: Gram-negative spiral bacterium · Most common in antrum of stomach · Transmitted fecal-oral, oral-oral · Only reservoir = human stomach · ~50% of world population infected
Gastritis — Inflammation of Stomach Mucosa
Presentation Epigastric pain + burning · Nausea · Vomiting
Often asymptomatic
Non-Infectious Causes NSAIDs — inhibit prostaglandins → ↓ mucosal protection
Alcohol — direct mucosal damage
H. pylori — most common bacterial cause
H. pylori — Virulence Factors ★
FactorMechanismWhy It Matters
Flagella Enables movement through stomach mucus to reach epithelium Essential for initial colonization
Urease ★ Breaks down urea → ammonia → ↑ local pH Allows survival in acidic stomach; basis for Urea Breath Test
Adhesins Facilitate attachment to gastric epithelium Ensure stable colonization
CagA toxin Cytotoxin-associated gene A Associated with gastric cancer development
VacA toxin Vacuolating cytotoxin A Induces inflammation + contributes to tissue damage
Urea Breath Test (UBT) — How It Works
  1. Patient ingests urea labeled with 13C or 14C isotope
  2. If H. pylori present, its urease cleaves urea → labeled CO₂ + ammonia
  3. Labeled CO₂ absorbed into bloodstream → exhaled in breath
  4. Patient breathes into collection bag; CO₂ measured by spectrometer
🔑 Absence of labeled CO₂ = No H. pylori infection. This is a non-invasive test — classic exam trigger!
Complications of Untreated H. pylori / Gastritis ★
Peptic Ulcer Disease (PUD) Ulceration of stomach or duodenal mucosa
Risk factors: H. pylori, NSAIDs, smoking
Complications: GI bleeding, perforation, gastric cancer
Systemic Consequences Iron deficiency anemia — H. pylori ↓ stomach acid → ↓ bioavailable Fe³⁺
B12 deficiency — from gastric atrophy
Gastric MALTomas — marginal B-cell lymphomas at site of chronic inflammation
Diagnostic Methods — Upper GI
EGD with Biopsy ★ Method of choice
Evaluates esophagus, stomach, duodenum gross appearance + allows tissue sampling
Urea Breath Test Non-invasive, detects active H. pylori infection via labeled CO₂
Other Tests Esophageal Barium Study — swallowing function via X-ray
CBC — overall immune/health status
Stool antigen — also used for H. pylori

04 🩸 Bacterial Gastroenteritis — Bloody (Dysentery) Diarrhea
🎯 Bloody diarrhea = invasive pathogens that penetrate the intestinal mucosa → trigger inflammation → small-volume bloody stools with mucus. Always think: Shiga toxin, invasion, or both.

05 💧 Bacterial Gastroenteritis — Non-Bloody (Watery) Diarrhea
🎯 Watery diarrhea = non-invasive pathogens that act via toxins without penetrating the mucosa → large-volume watery stools, no blood. Think cAMP/cGMP, ion channels, and enterotoxins.
High-Yield Non-Bloody Diarrhea Pathogens ★
PathogenGram / ShapeKey CluePathogenesisClinicalDiagnosis
S. aureus Gram + Cocci Mayo/dairy/meats left at room temp Heat-stable enterotoxin → activates mast cells → 5-HT release → vagus nerve → vomiting Explosive onset vomiting, mild diarrhea, very short incubation (1–6 hrs) Detection of enterotoxins or S. aureus in food
C. difficile Gram + Rod

ETEC
(Traveler's Diarrhea)		 Gram − Bacilli Contaminated food/water (travel) Heat-labile (LT) → ↑ cAMP → secretory diarrhea

06 ☠️ Toxin Mechanisms — Deep Dive ★
Cholera Toxin (V. cholerae) — AB Toxin Pathway
  1. Toxin is endocytosed → transported to ER
  2. CT-A subunit separates from B pentamer → enters cytosol
  3. CT-A ADP-ribosylates Gsα protein → permanently activates adenylate cyclase
  4. ↑↑ cAMP → activates PKA → phosphorylates CFTR → massive Cl⁻ secretion → water follows → rice-water diarrhea
ETEC — LT vs ST Toxin Mechanisms
Heat-Labile Toxin (LT) — AB Toxin Similar to cholera toxin (AB₅ structure)
B-subunits bind GM1 ganglioside
A-subunit ADP-ribosylates Gsα → ↑ cAMP → activates PKA → CFTR phosphorylation → Cl⁻ secretion → diarrhea
Heat-Stable Toxin (ST) Binds guanylyl cyclase receptors (brush border)
Stimulates guanylyl cyclase → ↑ cGMP
Activates cGMP-dependent kinases (PKGII + PKA) → ↑ CFTR activity → Cl⁻ secretion → diarrhea
Other Key Toxin Summaries
BacteriaToxinKey MechanismClinical Punchline
S. aureus Heat-stable enterotoxin Crosses intestinal epithelium → activates mast cells → 5-HT → vagus nerve → emetic center in medulla Rapid vomiting; classic: mayo sandwich left out
C. difficile Toxin A + Toxin B (AB toxins) Modify (ribosylate) GTPases (Rho, Rac) → disrupt cytoskeleton + tight junctions → inflammation → apoptosis → pseudomembrane formation Watery diarrhea → pseudomembranous colitis; Gram + rod, spores, fecal-oral
EHEC (O157:H7) Shiga-like toxin (AB toxin) Enters bloodstream via GI mucosa → damages endothelial cells → platelet aggregation → clots damage RBCs → hemolysis (HUS) Bloody diarrhea + HUS; anemia, thrombocytopenia, kidney failure — NO ANTIBIOTICS
EAEC EAST-1 Toxin Similar to ETEC ST toxin, but causes less damage; A for Aggregate → forms adherence fimbriae (AAF) Watery diarrhea with mucous aggregates

09 🌿 The Healthy Gut Microbiome (~3 exam questions)
⚠️ She emphasized: "IT'S NOT THAT SIMPLE." Health ≠ absence of disease. A healthy microbiome = Diversity + Composition + Resilience + Functionality — all four pillars matter. This is high-yield framing for the exam.
What Is the Gut Microbiome?
Composition Overview Community of microorganisms in GI tract: mainly bacteria, but also unicellular eukaryotes + viruses

Most common: obligate anaerobes

Dominant phyla (~90% together):
Bacillota (Firmicutes)
Bacteroidota (Bacteroidetes)
Other: Proteobacteria (many "pathobionts")
Functions of Gut Microbiome Digestion + nutrient metabolism (esp. complex carbs)
Energy harvest
Vitamin synthesis
Immune signaling + modulation
Colonization resistance against pathogens

Key theme: it's not just WHO is there — it's WHAT they do (metabolites + immune effects)
4 Pillars of a Healthy Microbiome ★
PillarDescription
DiversityHigher microbial diversity generally = better gut health; reduced diversity linked to IBD, obesity, type 2 DM, CVD
CompositionBalanced Firmicutes/Bacteroidetes ratio; high levels of Bifidobacterium and Lactobacillus; overrepresentation of Clostridium/Escherichia = dysbiosis signal
ResilienceAbility to bounce back from disturbances (antibiotics, dietary changes); chronic exposures → long-term shifts
FunctionalityEffective enzymatic activity supporting digestion; production of SCFAs, bile acids, tryptophan metabolites
Measurable Markers of Gut Health
Metabolites (Big Ones) SCFAs (acetate, propionate, butyrate)
Bile acids
Tryptophan metabolites
Gases (IBS Clue) Methane ↑ → constipation IBS (IBS-C)
H₂/H₂S ↑ → diarrhea IBS (IBS-D)
Stool Inflammation Markers Calprotectin ↑ = neutrophils in gut = inflammation
Lactoferrin ↑ = innate immune activation
Optimal pH: 5.5–7.0

What Are SCFAs?
Main SCFAs Acetate — helps support growth of other bacteria (cross-feeding)
Propionate

Where SCFAs Come From Produced by bacterial fermentation of dietary fiber (indigestible carbs)

Maintains gut barrier integrity
Modulates immune responses
Supports anti-inflammatory environment
Anti-Cancer Angle Promotes apoptosis in colon cancer cells

ELI5: Think "premium fuel" for the colon lining — lose butyrate producers → gut lining weakens

🔑 Testable Punchline: Ultra-processed foods → emulsifiers → dysbiosis + barrier disruption + systemic inflammation risk. Animal studies show: ↓ diversity, ↑ E. coli, ↓ beneficial groups, bacteria become more virulent (↑ flagellin, ↑ mucosa adherence).
11 🔥 Dysbiosis → Inflammatory & Infectious Disease
🎯 Core Dysbiosis → Disease Pathway: Dysbiosis → ↓ SCFAs + ↑ pro-inflammatory bacteria → impaired barrier ("leaky gut") → immune activation → systemic effects. The neighborhood changes: fewer helpful neighbors, more troublemakers → more inflammation.

Part IV · Liver Infections
12 🫀 Viral Hepatitis — A, B, C, D, E ★
🔑 Big Memory Hook: A & E = Non-enveloped = Fecal-Oral transmission (think food/water). B, C, D = Enveloped = Blood transmission (think needles/sex). Hepatitis B vaccine also prevents D!
3 Phases of Hepatitis Infection
PhaseTimelineSymptoms
Preicteric (Prodromal)Post-exposure: 40–180 daysMalaise, lethargy, nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain (RUQ)
Icteric Phase2–14 days post-preictericYellow sclera, dark urine, pale stools
Convalescent PhaseLasts weeksFatigue, malaise
💡 Why pale stools + dark urine? Bilirubin not being conjugated → no bile in stool (pale) → bilirubin excreted in urine (dark/tea-colored). Eyes are yellow from bilirubin accumulation.
