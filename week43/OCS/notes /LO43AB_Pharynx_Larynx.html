43APharyngeal Muscles, Innervation & Development

Overview of the Pharynx

The pharynx is a fibromuscular tube extending from the base of the skull to the level of C6, where it becomes the oesophagus. It is divided into three regions: the nasopharynx (behind the nasal cavity), the oropharynx (behind the oral cavity), and the laryngopharynx (hypopharynx), which lies posterior to the larynx. The wall consists of an outer muscular layer and an inner mucosal lining; between them lies the pharyngobasilar fascia, a dense connective tissue membrane that anchors the superior edge of the superior constrictor to the base of the skull — filling the gap where muscle is absent superiorly.

🔬 Dr. Willard — Dissection View
Looking at the pharyngeal constrictors from behind (posterior approach, laminectomy): the superior constrictor sits highest, the middle constrictor in the centre, and the inferior constrictor blending into the oesophagus below. A large venous plexus is visible lateral to the constrictors, along with the sympathetic trunk, vagus, and carotid vessels.

Constrictor Muscles

Three pairs of constrictors overlap each other from below upward — the inferior overlaps the middle, which overlaps the superior — creating a telescoping arrangement that generates a peristaltic wave during swallowing. All three constrictors insert posteriorly into the pharyngeal raphe (a fibrous seam running in the midline on the posterior wall).

Gaps Between Constrictors — the "Leaky Pharynx"

Because the constrictors do not form a continuous tube, several structures enter the pharynx through defined gaps:

Innervation — The Pharyngeal Plexus

The pharyngeal plexus sits on the posterolateral wall of the pharynx (on the middle constrictor) and is formed by contributions from:

  • CN X (Vagus) — pharyngeal branch: motor to all pharyngeal muscles except stylopharyngeus
  • CN IX (Glossopharyngeal) — contributes sensory fibres to the plexus; motor to stylopharyngeus alone
  • Sympathetic fibres from the superior cervical ganglion
⚡ Key Innervation Rule
Stylopharyngeus = CN IX (only muscle). All other pharyngeal muscles (constrictors + palatopharyngeus + salpingopharyngeus) = CN X via pharyngeal plexus.
Exception: the cricopharyngeal portion of inferior constrictor may also receive input from the external branch of the superior laryngeal nerve.

Development of the Pharynx — Pharyngeal Arches

The pharyngeal muscles are derived from paraxial mesoderm of the pharyngeal arches. This is clinically important: the nerve that accompanies a pharyngeal arch mesoderm derivative migrates with those muscle cells wherever they go — this explains seemingly paradoxical innervations.

🧬 Why Does CN IX Innervate Stylopharyngeus?
The stylopharyngeus is derived from 3rd arch mesoderm, which is innervated by CN IX. When those muscle cells migrated to form stylopharyngeus, CN IX followed. This is why the nerve that carries pain sensation from the tonsil (also 3rd arch territory) is the same nerve that elevates the pharynx — both structures share a common embryological origin.
🧬 Pharyngeal Pouch Derivatives — Clinical Anatomy
The pharyngeal pouches (endodermal outgrowths between the arches) also contribute adult structures:
  • Pouch 1 → tympanic cavity & auditory tube
  • Pouch 2 → palatine tonsil (tonsillar fossa)
  • Pouch 3 → inferior parathyroid glands & thymus
  • Pouch 4 → superior parathyroid glands; ultimobranchial body (C-cells of thyroid)

Structure–Function: Swallowing (Deglutition)

The pharyngeal constrictors act sequentially from top to bottom during swallowing. The superior constrictor initiates the pharyngeal phase; the middle and inferior follow in a peristaltic sequence. Simultaneously, the elevators (stylopharyngeus, palatopharyngeus, salpingopharyngeus) shorten and widen the pharynx, pulling it up over the descending food bolus — similar to pulling a sock over a foot.

Constrictors (Propulsors)

  • Sequential peristalsis top → bottom
  • Narrow and elongate the pharynx
  • Create propulsive pressure wave
  • All via CN X (pharyngeal plexus)

Longitudinal Muscles (Elevators)

  • Shorten & widen the pharynx
  • Elevate larynx → protects airway
  • Stylopharyngeus (CN IX), others (CN X)
  • Also open auditory tube (salpingopharyngeus)
⚠️ Clinical — Killian's Dehiscence (Pharyngeal Pouch)
Between the thyropharyngeal and cricopharyngeal parts of the inferior constrictor there is a potential triangular weakness — Killian's dehiscence. Increased intraluminal pressure can cause the mucosa to herniate outward here, forming a Zenker's diverticulum (pharyngeal pouch). Patients present with dysphagia, regurgitation of undigested food, and halitosis.

43BAnatomy of the Larynx, Musculature & Development

Overview & Function

The larynx is situated at the top of the trachea, below the hyoid bone, at approximately the C3–C6 vertebral level. As Dr. Willard emphasises, it serves three functions: (1) a protective valve — sorting air from fluid and particulate matter, routing air into the trachea; (2) a phonation device — vibrating to produce sound; and (3) part of the Valsalva mechanism — allowing intra-abdominal pressure to rise (e.g., during lifting). It is suspended from the hyoid bone and contains nine cartilages, several ligaments, two major membranes, and a series of intrinsic muscles.

Cartilaginous Skeleton

There are nine cartilages total: three unpaired and three paired.

🛡️
Thyroid (unpaired)
The largest cartilage
Two laminae fused anteriorly at ~90° (male) or ~120° (female). Superior & inferior horns. Thyroid notch & prominence ("Adam's apple").
💍
Cricoid (unpaired)
Signet-ring shape
Broad posterior lamina, narrow anterior arch. Only complete ring in the airway. Articulates with thyroid below, arytenoids above.
🌸
Epiglottis (unpaired)
Flower-petal shape
Narrow stalk attached to thyroid cartilage (thyroepiglottic ligament); broad superior leaf faces the tongue. Folds over glottis during swallowing.
🔺
Arytenoids (paired)
Pyramid-shaped, most important
Vocal process (anterior, attaches vocal ligament) + Muscular process (lateral, attaches most intrinsic muscles). Rocks on cricoid to open/close glottis.
🔹

Tiny, atop arytenoids

🔸

Embedded anterior to corniculares in the aryepiglottic folds. Variable; stiffens the fold.

Articulations

There are two types of synovial joints in the larynx:

  • Cricothyroid joints (2): between the inferior horn of the thyroid cartilage and the cricoid. Allows rocking and slight gliding. When the cricothyroid muscle contracts, it rocks the thyroid cartilage forward on the cricoid, increasing the distance between the thyroid angle and the arytenoids → stretches the vocal ligament → raises pitch.
  • Cricoarytenoid joints (2): between the base of each arytenoid and the sloped superior lamina of the cricoid. The arytenoids can rock (rotating vocal processes in/out) and glide medially/laterally → directly opens or closes the glottis.

Membranes & Ligaments

External Membranes

  • Thyrohyoid membrane: between thyroid cartilage and hyoid bone. Thickened centrally = median thyrohyoid ligament; thickened laterally = lateral thyrohyoid ligaments. Pierced by the internal branch of SLN and superior laryngeal artery.
  • Cricothyroid membrane (conus elasticus, external part): between cricoid and thyroid. Thickened centrally = cricothyroid ligament. Site of emergency cricothyrotomy.
  • Cricotracheal ligament: connects cricoid to first tracheal ring.

Internal Membranes

  • Quadrangular membrane: extends from the lateral border of the epiglottis to the arytenoid/corniculate cartilages. Its upper free edge = aryepiglottic ligament/fold (marks the inlet of the larynx). Its lower free edge = vestibular (false vocal fold) ligament. Forms the walls of the vestibule.
  • Conus elasticus (triangular/cricovocal membrane): rises cone-like from the upper border of the cricoid arch. Its upper free edge is the vocal ligament — the core of the true vocal fold. Forms the borders of the infraglottic space.

Internal Compartments of the Larynx

The laryngeal cavity is divided into three compartments by the two pairs of folds:

Vestibule (inlet → false vocal folds) → Ventricle + Sacculus (between folds; sacculus = blind pouch upward) → Infraglottic space (below true folds → trachea)
📍 The Glottis
The glottis is the narrowest part of the laryngeal cavity — it consists of the two true vocal folds (each = a vocal ligament + vocalis muscle covered by mucosa) plus the rima glottidis (the opening between them). When the vocal ligaments are tense and the glottis is narrow, high-pitched sound is produced; relaxation produces lower pitch.

⚡ Dr. Willard's Muscle Summary
Only abductor = PCA (posterior cricoarytenoid). All adductors + all tensors/relaxors = RLN (except cricothyroid = external SLN). Lose RLN unilaterally → hoarseness (one VF cannot abduct). Lose both RLN → airway emergency (VFs close; cannot breathe effectively).

Structure–Function: How the Muscles Work

Opening the Glottis

The posterior cricoarytenoid (PCA) is the sole abductor. It arises from the posterior surface of the cricoid lamina and runs obliquely up to the muscular process of the arytenoid. When it contracts, it pulls the muscular process downward and backward, rocking the vocal process laterally — widening the rima glottidis. This is the most important muscle for breathing.

Closing the Glottis

The transverse and oblique arytenoids act as sphincters — they draw the two arytenoids toward each other. The lateral cricoarytenoid rocks the vocal process medially. Together these close the rima glottidis for swallowing, coughing, and Valsalva.

Raising Pitch (Tensing VF)

The cricothyroid muscle (straight + oblique heads) rocks the thyroid cartilage anteroinferiorly on the cricoid — increasing the distance between the thyroid angle and the arytenoids. This stretches the vocal ligament, raising its fundamental frequency (pitch). It is the primary tensor and the only intrinsic muscle innervated by the external branch of the SLN. Losing it causes dropout of high frequencies.

Lowering Pitch (Relaxing VF)

The thyroarytenoid muscle draws the arytenoid cartilage toward the thyroid, shortening the distance and slackening the ligament → lowers pitch. The vocalis (the medial fibres of thyroarytenoid, intimately related to the vocal ligament) provides fine, local tension adjustment — likely responsible for the ability to bend the ligament and produce complex vocalisation.

🎵 Phonation Mechanics
Sound is produced by the Bernoulli effect: air forced through the narrowed rima glottidis creates a pressure drop, pulling the VFs together; the sub-glottic pressure then blows them apart again. This cycle repeats at the frequency of the vibrating ligament (~80–1100 Hz in normal voice). Tension ↑ = frequency ↑ = pitch ↑. The supraglottic spaces (pharynx, oral and nasal cavities) then modify this raw sound into the phonemes of language.

Blood Supply

Superior Thyroid A. (ECA) → Superior Laryngeal A. pierces thyrohyoid membrane with internal branch of SLN
Inferior Thyroid A. (thyrocervical trunk) → Inferior Laryngeal A. enters through defect in cricothyroid membrane with RLN

The two arteries anastomose within the submucosa to create a rich vascular network.

⚠️ Clinical — Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve Injury
The RLN's long intrathoracic course makes it vulnerable. Causes of RLN palsy:
  • Left RLN: aortic arch aneurysm, mediastinal masses, thoracic surgery (cardiac, oesophageal), apical lung cancer (Pancoast)
  • Right RLN: subclavian artery aneurysm; less commonly malignancy
  • Both sides: thyroid surgery (bilateral thyroidectomy) — emergency if not recognised
  • Effect: Unilateral = hoarseness (PCA cannot abduct; VF sits in paramedian position). Bilateral = acute stridor and respiratory distress (both VFs fixed near midline).

Development of the Larynx

The larynx develops from the 4th and 6th pharyngeal arches, with contributions from the foregut endoderm (mucosal lining) and lateral plate mesoderm (cartilages & muscles).

4th arch (SLN) → Cricothyroid muscle + epiglottis, thyroid cartilage, aryepiglottic folds
6th arch (RLN) → All other intrinsic muscles + cricoid cartilage, arytenoid cartilages

The laryngeal inlet forms from a groove in the floor of the primitive pharynx (the laryngotracheal groove) which deepens into a diverticulum. This diverticulum separates from the oesophagus by the tracheo-oesophageal septum (failure → tracheo-oesophageal fistula). The laryngeal lumen is initially obliterated by epithelial proliferation and then recanalized during the 10th week — failure of recanalization → laryngeal atresia/webs.

🧬 Why Two Nerves for One Larynx?
The external SLN innervates the cricothyroid (4th arch derivative). The RLN loops around the aortic arch (left) / subclavian (right) because these vessels are the remnants of the 6th aortic arch arteries — the same arch that gave rise to the RLN-innervated laryngeal muscles. As the heart descended caudally during development, the nerve was pulled down with the arch vessel, giving rise to the RLN's long course.
