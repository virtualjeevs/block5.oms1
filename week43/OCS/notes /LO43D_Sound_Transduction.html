Sound Energy: From External Ear to Auditory Nerve

This learning objective traces how an acoustic pressure wave in air is converted—step by step—into a neural signal that the brain can interpret as sound. The process spans three anatomical regions: the external ear, the middle ear, and the inner ear (cochlea).

Sound Wave (air) → Pinna + EAM → Tympanic Membrane → Ossicles (M → I → S) → Oval Window → Cochlear Fluids → Basilar Membrane → Organ of Corti → CN VIII (Auditory Nerve)

Stage 1 — The External Ear: Funneling Sound (Figs. 7.92, 7.93)

The external ear consists of the pinna (auricle) and the external acoustic meatus (EAM). The pinna is shaped like a trumpet — it captures and funnels sound waves into the narrow canal. The outer one-third of the EAM is cartilaginous and the inner two-thirds is bony (petrous part of the temporal bone). At the end of this canal sits the tympanic membrane (eardrum).

The EAM has a length of approximately 2.5 cm in adults. Sound waves traveling down this canal strike the tympanic membrane and cause it to vibrate — mechanical energy is now in the system.

Stage 2 — The Middle Ear: Impedance Matching (Figs. 7.94–7.97)

The tympanic membrane's vibrations are transmitted across the middle ear (tympanic cavity) by a chain of three tiny bones called the ossicles:

🔊 Why the Ossicles Matter: Impedance Matching
Sound in air carries very little energy. The cochlea is fluid-filled — fluid is ~800× harder to move than air. Without the ossicles, 99.9% of sound energy would be reflected back. The ossicular chain solves this with two mechanisms: (1) the area ratio — the tympanic membrane (~55 mm²) is ~17× larger than the oval window (~3.2 mm²), concentrating force; and (2) a lever action from the ossicular joints. Together they amplify pressure ~25–30× dB, allowing efficient transfer of acoustic energy into the cochlear fluids.

The middle ear also communicates with the nasopharynx via the pharyngotympanic (Eustachian) tube, which allows the tympanic cavity to equilibrate with atmospheric pressure — essential for the tympanic membrane to vibrate freely.

Stage 3 — The Inner Ear: Fluid Waves & the Basilar Membrane

The stapes footplate rocks in the oval window, generating a pressure wave in the perilymph of the cochlea. The cochlea is a bony spiral coiled 2¾ turns (in humans) around a central bony column called the modiolus. A cross-section reveals three fluid-filled compartments running the full length of the spiral:

🔼
Upper Chamber
Scala Vestibuli
Begins at oval window; filled with perilymph (similar to ECF)
🔽
Lower Chamber
Scala Tympani
Ends at round window; also perilymph. Pressure is dissipated here — the round window bulges outward
🟦
Middle Chamber
Scala Media (Cochlear Duct)
Completely isolated; filled with endolymph (high K⁺); bounded above by Reissner's membrane, below by the basilar membrane

The scala vestibuli and scala tympani communicate at the apex of the cochlea through a tiny opening called the helicotrema, allowing slow fluid equilibration. The scala media remains isolated — its unique ionic environment (endolymph: high K⁺, low Na⁺) is critical for hair cell depolarization.

🌊 Stria Vascularis — Generating Endolymph
On the outer wall of the scala media is the stria vascularis, a highly vascular epithelium with tall secretory cells that actively pump K⁺ ions into the endolymph. This maintains the endocochlear potential (+80 mV inside scala media vs. the perilymph), which is the electrochemical driving force for K⁺ to enter hair cells when their stereocilia are deflected.

Stage 4 — The Basilar Membrane: Frequency Mapping (Tonotopy)

The pressure wave in the cochlear fluids causes a traveling wave along the basilar membrane (which forms the floor of the scala media and the roof of the scala tympani). The mechanical properties of this membrane vary along its length:

Base of Cochlea

Basilar membrane is narrow and stiff. Vibrates maximally in response to high-frequency sounds (up to ~20,000 Hz).

Apex of Cochlea

Basilar membrane is wide and floppy. Vibrates maximally in response to low-frequency sounds (down to ~20 Hz).

This graded mechanical tuning is what allows the cochlea to spread the entire audible frequency spectrum spatially along its spiral — this principle is called tonotopy.

⚠️ Round Window — Pressure Relief Valve
As the stapes pushes perilymph inward at the oval window, the round window (covered by the flexible secondary tympanic membrane) bulges outward. Without this safety valve, the incompressible perilymph would have nowhere to go and the basilar membrane could not deflect. The oval window input and round window output are in phase opposition.

Stage 5 — The Organ of Corti: Mechanoelectrical Transduction

Sitting on the basilar membrane is the organ of Corti — the actual sensory organ of hearing. It contains the mechanosensory hair cells, which convert basilar membrane movement into electrical signals.

Architecture of the Organ of Corti

The organ of Corti is sandwiched between the basilar membrane below and the tectorial membrane above. Key structural elements include:

  • Inner hair cells (IHC) — one row; ~3,500 total; the primary afferent sensory cells (~95% of auditory nerve fibers contact these)
  • Outer hair cells (OHC) — three rows; ~12,000 total; act as cochlear amplifiers (electromotility)
  • Inner and outer pillar cells — form the rigid triangular tunnel of Corti separating IHCs from OHCs; made of tightly woven reticular fibers
  • Reticular lamina — the stiff plate into which hair cells are embedded; stereocilia project upward through it
  • Tectorial membrane — gelatinous overhanging shelf; tallest stereocilia of OHCs are embedded in it
  • Supporting cells — Deiter's, Hensen's, and border cells surround and support hair cells
  • Spiral ligament — fibrous collagenous band anchoring the lateral edge of the basilar membrane to the bony cochlear wall
  • Spiral lamina — bony shelf with two plates (upper/lower) through which auditory nerve fibers pass

Stage 6 — Stereocilia Deflection: Opening the Transduction Channel

Each hair cell bears a staircase-shaped bundle of stereocilia (actually modified microvilli) arranged from shortest to tallest. At the tips of adjacent stereocilia are tiny extracellular filaments called tip links. These are the direct mechanical gate of the transduction channel.

Basilar membrane deflects upward → Stereocilia shear against tectorial membrane → Tip links stretch (deflection toward tallest) → MET channel opens → K⁺ flows INTO hair cell (from endolymph) → Hair cell DEPOLARIZES
⚡ Why Potassium — Not Sodium — Depolarizes Hair Cells
The endolymph in the scala media is extraordinarily rich in K⁺ (~150 mM) compared to perilymph (~5 mM). The hair cell interior is also relatively negative. When mechanotransduction (MET) channels open at the stereociliary tips, K⁺ rushes into the cell down its electrochemical gradient, depolarizing the cell. This is the opposite of most neurons, where K⁺ flows out. The stria vascularis continuously replenishes the K⁺ in the endolymph.

Stage 7 — Synaptic Transmission: Hair Cell → Auditory Nerve

Hair cell depolarization causes Ca²⁺ influx at the base of the hair cell, triggering the fusion of synaptic vesicles at specialized synaptic ribbons (synaptic bars). Neurotransmitter (glutamate) is released onto the afferent terminals of the cochlear nerve fibers that wrap around the base of the inner hair cells.

These cochlear nerve fibers have their cell bodies in the spiral ganglion (located in the modiolus). Their central processes form the auditory (cochlear) nerve, which travels in the internal acoustic meatus as part of CN VIII (vestibulocochlear nerve) and enters the brainstem at the pontomedullary junction, synapsing in the cochlear nuclei.

Tonotopic Organization of the Auditory Nerve

The spiral arrangement of the cochlea is preserved in the organization of the auditory nerve fibers as they spiral into the modiolus:

Center of the Nerve (Modiolus)

Fibers from the apex of the cochlea enter first — they represent low-frequency sounds.

Outside of the Nerve

Fibers from the base of the cochlea add to the periphery — they represent high-frequency sounds.

💡 Clinical Pearl: Cochlear Implants
The beautiful tonotopic map of the auditory nerve is the basis of the cochlear prosthesis (cochlear implant). A multi-electrode array is threaded into the cochlea and electrically stimulates different positions along the cochlear nerve — base electrodes deliver high-frequency signals, apical electrodes deliver low-frequency signals. Because the nerve itself is intact (the device bypasses the damaged hair cells), this can restore functional hearing even in those born deaf, provided implantation occurs early enough during the critical period of auditory cortex development.

Common causes of hair cell loss: Presbycusis (age-related, natural attrition), acoustic trauma (rock concerts, industrial noise), ototoxic drugs (aminoglycosides, cisplatin).
