PLO 1

Inherited Cancer · DNA Repair

Lynch Syndrome

Lynch syndrome (formerly HNPCC — Hereditary Non-Polyposis Colorectal Cancer) is the most common hereditary cause of colorectal cancer, accounting for approximately 2–4% of all colorectal cancers. It is caused by germline (inherited) mutations in DNA mismatch repair (MMR) genes.

🔑 Definition

Lynch syndrome = autosomal dominant condition caused by pathogenic germline variants in MMR genes, predisposing individuals to colorectal cancer and other cancers through impaired DNA mismatch repair.

The Causative Genes

Four primary MMR genes are implicated in Lynch syndrome:

MLH1

Chromosome 3p22

Most commonly mutated. MutL Homolog 1. Partners with PMS2 to form MutLα complex.

MSH2

Chromosome 2p21

Second most common. MutS Homolog 2. Recognises mismatched bases; partners with MSH6 or MSH3.

MSH6

Chromosome 2p16

Partners with MSH2. Loss often causes endometrial cancer predominance.

PMS2

Chromosome 7p22

Partners with MLH1. Mutations often have lower penetrance than MLH1/MSH2 variants.

Mechanism: How Loss of MMR Causes Cancer

Under normal conditions, MMR proteins detect and repair single base mismatches and small insertions/deletions (indels) that occur during DNA replication. When MMR is lost:

  1. Germline mutation (1st hit) — Patient inherits one defective copy of an MMR gene (e.g., MLH1) in every cell of their body. This does not by itself cause cancer.
  2. Somatic mutation (2nd hit) — A random somatic mutation inactivates the remaining functional allele in a particular cell (Knudson "two-hit" model).
  3. Loss of MMR function — With both copies inactivated, the cell cannot correct replication errors. Mutations accumulate at an accelerated rate.
  4. Microsatellite instability (MSI) — Repetitive DNA sequences (microsatellites) accumulate errors, producing a molecular fingerprint called microsatellite instability — a hallmark of Lynch syndrome tumours.
  5. Accumulation of driver mutations — Over time, mutations accumulate in oncogenes and tumour suppressor genes (e.g., APC, KRAS, TP53), driving tumorigenesis.
⚙️ Why Are These Caretaker Genes?

MMR proteins stabilise the genome by repairing replication errors. They do not directly regulate cell division. Their inactivation is "environmentally equivalent to exposing the cell to mutagens incessantly" (Dr. Harrison's slide). This classifies them as caretaker genes, not gatekeeper genes or oncogenes.

Genetic Classification

🔐

Gatekeeper Genes

Directly prevent growth of potential cancer cells. E.g., APC (prevents β-catenin signalling), RB1 (cell cycle brake).

🛡️

Caretaker Genes (MLH1, MSH2, etc.)

Stabilise the genome through DNA repair. Loss → accumulation of mutations throughout the genome → increased cancer risk.

Lynch syndrome is autosomal dominant — a 50% chance of passing the mutation to each child. Early-onset colorectal cancer (<50 yrs) or multiple Lynch-associated cancers in the family history should raise suspicion. Testing involves immunohistochemistry for MMR protein expression in tumour tissue, followed by germline sequencing confirmation.

Q: A patient has a pathogenic variant in MLH1. Explain the mechanism by which this inherited mutation leads to colorectal cancer.

Answer

MLH1 is a DNA mismatch repair (MMR) gene — a caretaker gene. The inherited pathogenic variant inactivates one allele in all cells (1st hit). When a somatic mutation inactivates the remaining allele in a colon epithelial cell (2nd hit, Knudson model), MMR function is lost entirely. This leads to microsatellite instability and accelerated accumulation of somatic mutations in proto-oncogenes and tumour suppressor genes, driving colorectal cancer development via multi-step tumorigenesis.

Molecular Biology · Oncology

Cell Cycle Regulation

The cell cycle is a precisely regulated series of events that culminates in cell division. Dysregulation of cell cycle checkpoints is a hallmark of cancer. Understanding cell cycle control is essential for understanding how oncogenic mutations drive uncontrolled proliferation.

Phases of the Cell Cycle

G1 Phase

Gap 1 / Growth

Cell grows, synthesises proteins. Critical decision point: commit to division (START/restriction point) or exit to G0. Key regulator: Cyclin D–CDK4/6.

S Phase

DNA Synthesis

Entire genome replicated. Each chromosome duplicated to form sister chromatids. Key regulator: Cyclin E–CDK2 (initiation), Cyclin A–CDK2 (progression).

G2 Phase

Gap 2 / Preparation

Cell grows further and prepares for mitosis. DNA replication fidelity is checked. Key regulator: Cyclin A/B–CDK1.

M Phase

Mitosis / Division

Chromosome segregation and cell division. Includes prophase, metaphase, anaphase, telophase, cytokinesis. Key regulator: Cyclin B–CDK1 (MPF).

G0 Phase

Quiescence

Cells exit the cycle and remain metabolically active but non-dividing. Terminal differentiation (e.g., neurons) or reversible quiescence.

Cyclin–CDK Complexes

Cyclins are proteins whose levels oscillate through the cell cycle, activating their partner CDKs (cyclin-dependent kinases). CDKs are constitutively expressed but require cyclin binding to become active.

The Rb–E2F Pathway (G1/S Checkpoint)

The retinoblastoma protein (Rb) is the key gatekeeper at the G1/S checkpoint:

  1. Rb is hypophosphorylated (active) — binds and inhibits E2F transcription factors, preventing expression of S-phase genes. Cell remains in G1.
  2. Cyclin D–CDK4/6 activated by mitogenic signals — Growth factors (e.g., EGF) → Ras → MAPK pathway → Cyclin D synthesis.
  3. Rb partially phosphorylated — Cyclin D–CDK4/6 partially phosphorylates Rb → partial release of E2F → Cyclin E transcription.
  4. Cyclin E–CDK2 completes Rb phosphorylation — Positive feedback loop drives full commitment to S-phase (restriction point passed).
  5. E2F released → S-phase gene transcription — Genes for DNA replication machinery are expressed. Cell irreversibly commits to division.
⚠️ Cancer Mutations in the Rb Pathway
  • Cyclin D amplification (CCND1) — Overactive CDK4/6 → constitutive Rb phosphorylation → uncontrolled S-phase entry. Seen in breast cancer, mantle cell lymphoma.
  • CDK4/6 amplification — Same effect. Target of CDK4/6 inhibitors (palbociclib, ribociclib).
  • Loss of p16/CDKN2A — p16 inhibits CDK4/6; its loss removes the brake on Rb phosphorylation.
  • Rb1 mutation/deletion — Direct loss of the gatekeeper (retinoblastoma, osteosarcoma).

⚙️ Telomeres and the Hayflick Limit

Normal somatic cells can divide only a finite number of times (Hayflick limit ~50–70 divisions), because telomeres — TTAGGG repeat caps at chromosome ends — shorten with each replication. When critically short, the cell enters senescence or apoptosis. Cancer cells bypass this by reactivating telomerase (TERT), which extends telomeres and confers immortality. This is why TERT is a clinically important gene in cancer genomics.

Q: A tumour biopsy shows amplification of CCND1 (Cyclin D1). Explain how this drives uncontrolled cell proliferation.

Answer

Cyclin D1 partners with CDK4/6 to phosphorylate and inactivate Rb at the G1/S checkpoint. When Cyclin D1 is amplified, CDK4/6 activity is constitutively elevated, resulting in persistent Rb phosphorylation. Rb releases E2F transcription factors, which drive expression of S-phase genes, causing cells to bypass the G1/S restriction point and enter S-phase continuously — resulting in uncontrolled cell division.

Tumour Suppressor · Signalling

The p53 Pathway

p53 is encoded by the TP53 gene (chromosome 17p13.1) and functions as a transcription factor and the most critical tumour suppressor in human biology. It is mutated in >50% of all human cancers, earning the title "Guardian of the Genome." Dr. Harrison's own academic lineage traces back to its discoverer, Sir David Lane.

🔑 Core Concept

DNA damage → Activation of p53 by phosphorylation → p53 transcribes target genes → Either cell cycle arrest (mild/repairable damage) or apoptosis (severe/irreparable damage).

Activation of p53

  1. DNA damage stimulus — Double-strand breaks (DSBs), UV-induced crosslinks, reactive oxygen species, replication stress, or oncogene activation.
  2. ATM/ATR kinase activation — ATM responds to DSBs; ATR responds to single-stranded DNA. Both phosphorylate CHK1/CHK2, which phosphorylate p53.
  3. p53 stabilisation — Normally, p53 is rapidly degraded via MDM2-mediated ubiquitination. Phosphorylation of p53 prevents MDM2 binding → p53 protein accumulates.
  4. p53 binds DNA response elements — As a tetrameric transcription factor, p53 binds specific promoter sequences and activates target gene transcription.

Two Downstream Outcomes

🛑

Cell Cycle Arrest

When: Mild/repairable DNA damage.

  • p53 → transcribes p21 (CDKN1A)
  • p21 inhibits CDK2–Cyclin E and CDK2–Cyclin A
  • Rb remains hypophosphorylated → S-phase blocked
  • G1/S arrest gives time for DNA repair
  • If repair succeeds → cell cycle resumes
💀

Apoptosis

When: Severe/irreparable DNA damage.

  • p53 → transcribes BAX, PUMA, NOXA (pro-apoptotic Bcl-2 family)
  • BAX forms pores in mitochondrial outer membrane
  • Cytochrome c released → apoptosome
  • Caspase cascade activation → cell death
  • Also transcribes FAS/TRAIL (extrinsic pathway)

The MDM2 Negative Feedback Loop

p53 transcribes its own negative regulator MDM2, which acts as an E3 ubiquitin ligase. MDM2 binds the N-terminus of p53, ubiquitinates it, and targets it for proteasomal degradation. This creates a tight autoregulatory loop — when the DNA damage signal resolves, p53 levels fall and the cell cycle resumes.

⚠️ Cancer Relevance: MDM2 Amplification

In some cancers (e.g., liposarcoma), MDM2 is amplified, leading to excessive p53 degradation even in the presence of DNA damage. This functionally inactivates p53 without a TP53 mutation — the tumour suppressor pathway is silenced through its own negative regulator.

Cancer Hotspot — R273 on p53

Dr. Harrison highlighted that when you sequence TP53 across thousands of cancers, mutations are not randomly distributed — they cluster at hotspots. The most commonly mutated residue is Arginine 273 (R273), often mutated to cysteine (R273C) or histidine (R273H). R273 is a DNA-contact residue in the DNA-binding domain — its mutation abolishes sequence-specific binding. This was confirmed at cancerhotspots.org during the lecture.

🔬 Gain-of-Function TP53 Mutations

Most TP53 mutations are missense mutations in the DNA-binding domain. Some confer not just loss-of-function but active gain-of-function oncogenic properties — mutant p53 protein can inhibit wild-type p53 (dominant negative effect), activate pro-survival genes, and promote metastasis.

Q: A patient's tumour has a homozygous TP53 R273C mutation. What is the downstream consequence for cell fate after DNA damage?

Answer

R273 is a critical DNA-contact residue in p53's DNA-binding domain. The R273C mutation abolishes p53's ability to bind its target gene promoters. Consequently, p53 cannot transcribe p21 (cell cycle arrest) or BAX/PUMA (apoptosis). When DNA damage occurs, the cell continues cycling despite genomic instability, accumulating further mutations. This drives cancer progression. The cell escapes the normal "repair or die" decision governed by wild-type p53.

PLO 4

Molecular Genetics · Week 2 Review

Gene Organisation in the Human Genome

The human genome contains approximately 3.2 billion base pairs organised across 23 pairs of chromosomes. Of this, only ~1–2% encodes protein. Understanding gene structure is foundational for interpreting sequencing results, GWAS data, and genomic browser outputs.

Scale of the Genome

🔑 Key Numbers
  • ~20,000 protein-coding genes in the human genome
  • ~180,000 exons — but exons only encode ~1% of the total genome sequence
  • ~85% of mutations with large disease effects are present within exons (hence exome sequencing efficiency)
  • Chromosomes are divided into a short arm (p arm) and a long arm (q arm)

Anatomy of a Gene

A typical protein-coding gene comprises the following elements, from 5' to 3':

  1. Promoter & Regulatory Region — Upstream of the transcription start site (TSS). Contains TATA box, enhancers, and transcription factor binding sites. Not part of the mature mRNA. Located in non-coding / intergenic DNA.
  2. 5' Untranslated Region (5' UTR) — Part of the first exon(s); transcribed into mRNA but NOT translated. Regulates translation initiation efficiency. Extends from TSS to the start codon (AUG).
  3. Coding Sequence (CDS) — The exonic sequence from the start codon (AUG) to the stop codon. This is the sequence that is translated into protein. Consists exclusively of codons (triplets).
  4. Introns — Intervening sequences between exons. Transcribed into pre-mRNA but removed by splicing (spliceosome) before the mature mRNA is exported from the nucleus. Represented as thin lines/chevrons in genome browsers.
  5. Exons — Expressed sequences; retained in mature mRNA after splicing. Include the UTRs and the CDS. Represented as thick boxes in genome browsers.
  6. 3' Untranslated Region (3' UTR) — Part of the last exon; transcribed into mRNA but not translated. Contains polyadenylation signal. Regulates mRNA stability, localisation, and translation via miRNA binding.
  7. Intergenic Region — DNA between genes. Once called "junk DNA" — now known to contain enhancers, lncRNAs, miRNA genes, regulatory elements.

Codons and the Genetic Code

A codon is a sequence of three consecutive nucleotides in mRNA that specifies a single amino acid (or a stop signal).

⚙️ The Genetic Code is Redundant (Degenerate)
  • 64 codons encode 20 amino acids — multiple codons can encode the same amino acid (e.g., UCU and UCC both encode serine)
  • Start codon → AUG (methionine) — defines the reading frame
  • Stop codons → UAA, UAG, UGA — terminate translation; do not encode amino acids
  • Redundancy explains why synonymous SNPs often have no effect on protein function
  • Third codon position ("wobble position") is most degenerate — changes here are often synonymous

Splice Variants (Transcript Variants)

A single gene can produce multiple different mRNA isoforms through alternative splicing. Different combinations of exons are joined together, generating protein isoforms with distinct functions, localisations, or binding properties.

🏥 Example: TP53

The TP53 gene on chromosome 17p13.1 has approximately 18 transcript variants (splice variants), visible as multiple "layers" in the UCSC Genome Browser. Each isoform has subtly different properties. The gene has 10 exons and runs from right to left on the genome browser display (note the arrow direction indicates transcriptional orientation).

Reading the Genome Browser

On exams, Dr. Harrison expects you to interpret genome browser images. Key things to identify:

  • Chromosome and arm: Identified in the chromosome ideogram (p arm = short arm, q arm = long arm; red line shows your current position)
  • Scale bar: Shows kilobases (kb) represented across the view (e.g., 5 kb = 5,000 bases)
  • Gene name and orientation: Arrow direction indicates which strand is transcribed (left-to-right = sense strand; right-to-left = antisense)
  • Exons vs. introns: Thick boxes = exons; thin lines / arrows = introns
  • Transcript variants: Multiple rows below the gene = different splice isoforms
  • Adjacent genes: Genes in the intergenic regions on either side (e.g., TP53 neighbours: ATP1B2 and WRAP53/TP53 antisense)
  • Intronic vs. exonic variants: SNPs shown in SNPedia track — note whether each falls on an exon or intron

Q: In a genome browser image of a gene, you see thick boxes connected by thin chevron-shaped lines pointing right. What does each element represent, and what is their fate during gene expression?

Answer

Thick boxes = exons — these are transcribed into pre-mRNA and retained in the mature mRNA after splicing. They include the 5' UTR, coding sequence (CDS), and 3' UTR. The CDS within exons is translated into protein.

Thin chevron lines = introns — also transcribed into pre-mRNA, but removed by the spliceosome during RNA processing and are absent from the mature mRNA. They are therefore NOT translated.

Methods · Omics Technologies

High-Throughput Sequencing: WGS & RNA-Seq

High-throughput (next-generation) sequencing (NGS) allows the rapid, massively parallel sequencing of millions to billions of DNA or RNA fragments simultaneously. This is the technological backbone of genomics and transcriptomics in precision medicine.

🔑 Core Principle

Instead of sequencing one gene at a time (low-throughput), NGS sequences the entire genome or transcriptome in a single experiment. The key steps are fragmentation → sequencing → assembly/alignment.

Whole-Genome Sequencing (WGS)

WGS sequences all DNA in a sample — the entire ~3.2 billion base pairs of the human genome:

  1. DNA extraction — Isolate genomic DNA from sample (blood, saliva, tissue biopsy).
  2. Fragmentation — The long chromosomal DNA is sheared into short fragments (~150–300 bp). This is necessary because current sequencers cannot read entire chromosomes end-to-end.
  3. Library preparation — Adaptor sequences are ligated to fragment ends. These allow fragments to bind to the sequencing flow cell and are used for PCR amplification of clusters.
  4. Sequencing — Each fragment is sequenced simultaneously (sequencing-by-synthesis: fluorescently labelled nucleotides are incorporated and imaged). Produces millions of short "reads."
  5. Assembly / Alignment — Short reads are aligned back to the reference human genome (GRCh38) using overlapping sequences. Variants are called where reads differ from the reference.
⚙️ What Can WGS Detect?
  • Gross structural variants — large deletions, insertions, inversions, translocations
  • Gene copy number variants (CNVs) — e.g., MET amplification
  • Single Nucleotide Polymorphisms (SNPs) — both inherited and somatic
  • Haplotypes — sets of co-segregating variants
  • Somatic mutations in tumours (when compared to normal tissue)

Exome Sequencing (WES — Whole Exome Sequencing)

A targeted variant of WGS that sequences only the protein-coding exons (~1% of the genome):

Why WES Over WGS?

  • ~180,000 human exons, representing only ~1% of the genome
  • Yet ~85% of mutations with large disease effects are exonic
  • Much cheaper, faster, and computationally simpler than WGS
  • Ideal for identifying pathogenic variants in known disease genes
  • Method: Hybridisation capture — biotinylated RNA baits anneal to exonic DNA fragments, which are pulled down with streptavidin beads before sequencing

Somatic vs. Inherited Mutations — Experimental Design

🏥 Key Distinction (Exam-Ready)

To distinguish inherited germline variants from somatic tumour mutations, sequence two samples from the same patient:

  • Normal (non-tumour) tissue — e.g., blood or buccal cells. Variants found here are germline/inherited — present in every cell.
  • Tumour tissue — Variants present in the tumour but absent from normal tissue are somatic mutations — acquired during cancer development.
  • This paired tumour–normal sequencing approach is standard in clinical oncology genomics.

RNA-Sequencing (RNA-Seq) — Transcriptomics

RNA-Seq measures the entire transcriptome — all mRNA transcripts expressed in a cell at a given time. The methodology is analogous to WGS but applied to RNA:

  1. RNA extraction — Total RNA or poly-A-selected mRNA is isolated from sample (e.g., tumour biopsy).
  2. Reverse transcription — RNA is converted to complementary DNA (cDNA) using reverse transcriptase, because current sequencers read DNA, not RNA directly.
  3. Fragmentation & library prep — cDNA is fragmented and adaptors are ligated (same as WGS).
  4. Sequencing — High-throughput sequencing produces millions of reads from cDNA fragments.
  5. Alignment & quantification — Reads are aligned to the reference genome or transcriptome. Read counts per gene reflect mRNA abundance = gene expression level.

Q: Explain in simple terms how whole-genome sequencing works. What are the three fundamental steps?

Answer

1. Fragmentation — Chromosomal DNA is sheared into small pieces (~150–300 bp), because sequencers cannot read whole chromosomes.
2. Sequencing — All fragments are sequenced in massively parallel fashion (NGS), generating millions of short reads.
3. Assembly/Alignment — Reads are aligned back to the reference human genome using overlapping sequences, identifying where each fragment originated and where variants exist relative to the reference.

Epidemiology · Statistical Genetics

Genome-Wide Association Studies (GWAS)

GWAS are epidemiological studies that scan the entire genome to identify common genetic variants (usually SNPs) statistically associated with a disease or trait. They are the primary tool for discovering common, low-penetrance risk alleles — the variants that account for the "missing heritability" in complex diseases.

Why GWAS? — The Heritability Gap

⚙️ The Problem GWAS Solves

By the 1980–90s, genetic linkage studies had identified ~70 high-penetrance cancer susceptibility genes (e.g., BRCA1/2, APC, MLH1, MSH2) — mutations conferring 5–100× elevated risk. But these high-penetrance syndromes account for only a small fraction of familial cancer risk. The vast majority of cancer heritability was unexplained.

GWAS hunt for the common variants of small effect that collectively contribute much of the remaining familial risk — variants too subtle to be found by linkage studies.

The Risk–Frequency Spectrum

Genetic variants can be plotted on a graph of allele frequency (x-axis) vs. risk conferred (y-axis):

  • High penetrance, low frequency — BRCA1/2, MLH1 mutations. Rare in population, but individually very risky (OR 5–100). Found by linkage studies.
  • Moderate penetrance, uncommon — CHEK2, ATM mutations. Intermediate category now being described.
  • Low penetrance, common — SNPs with OR 1.1–1.5. Very common in population, individually small effect. Found by GWAS.

GWAS Study Design

  1. Recruit cases & controls — Assemble a large cohort of individuals with the disease (cases) and matched individuals without (controls). Typically requires thousands to hundreds of thousands of participants for statistical power.
  2. Genotype entire genome — Use SNP genotyping arrays (or WGS) to determine each participant's genotype at up to ~1 million SNP positions across the genome.
  3. Statistical comparison — At each SNP position, compare the frequency of each allele in cases vs. controls. Calculate a P-value (how likely is this difference to be due to chance?).
  4. Plot Manhattan plot — X-axis = chromosomal position (chromosomes 1–22 + X, Y); Y-axis = –log₁₀(P-value). Tall spikes ("skyscrapers") indicate strongly associated SNPs.
  5. Calculate odds ratio (OR) — Quantify the effect size: how much does having this SNP change the odds of disease?
  6. Replication — Top hits are validated in independent cohorts from different populations.

Key Statistical Concepts

⚠️ Genome-Wide Significance Threshold — MEMORISE THIS

P < 5 × 10⁻⁸

This extremely stringent threshold corrects for ~1 million independent statistical tests performed across the genome (Bonferroni-style correction). It is the universally accepted threshold for declaring a GWAS hit "genome-wide significant." A standard threshold of p < 0.05 would generate thousands of false positives by chance alone.

⚙️ The Odds Ratio (OR)

The OR quantifies the magnitude of risk associated with a SNP:

OR = Odds(disease | SNP present) ÷ Odds(disease | SNP absent)

  • OR = 1 — No association; SNP has no effect on disease risk
  • OR > 1 — Risk allele; presence of SNP increases odds of disease (e.g., OR = 1.5 → 50% increased odds)
  • OR < 1 — Protective allele; presence of SNP decreases odds of disease (e.g., OR = 0.8 → 20% reduced odds)

P-value vs. Odds Ratio — Two Different Questions

MetricQuestion it AnswersInterpretation
P-value"How confident are we this association is real?"Must be < 5×10⁻⁸ for genome-wide significance. Reflects statistical robustness, NOT effect size.
Odds Ratio (OR)"How risky is having this SNP?"Reflects effect size. A highly significant result (tiny P) can still have a small OR (e.g., 1.05). Clinically, you need both significance AND a meaningful OR.

Polygenic Risk Score (PRS) — Preview

Once GWAS has identified many significant SNPs associated with a disease, all of them can be combined into a single Polygenic Risk Score that estimates an individual's cumulative genetic risk. (See LO 3 for full details.)

Q: Why is the P-value threshold for genome-wide significance set at 5 × 10⁻⁸ rather than the conventional 0.05?

Answer

In GWAS, a statistical test is performed at every SNP position across the genome — approximately 1 million independent tests. If we used p < 0.05, we would expect 50,000 false positives by chance alone. The threshold of p < 5 × 10⁻⁸ is a Bonferroni-style correction for this multiple-testing burden: 0.05 ÷ 1,000,000 ≈ 5 × 10⁻⁸. This stringent cutoff ensures that reported associations are very unlikely to be false positives.

Genetic Variation · Population Genetics

SNPs, Haplotypes & Polygenic Risk Scores

Single Nucleotide Polymorphisms (SNPs)

🔑 Definition of a SNP

A Single Nucleotide Polymorphism (SNP) — pronounced "snip" — is a variation at a single base position in the DNA sequence that is present in at least 1% of the population. It is the most common type of genetic variation in humans. Approximately 4–5 million SNPs distinguish any two unrelated individuals.

SNPs are given reference IDs in the format rs###### (e.g., rs63750710 in MLH1 associated with Lynch syndrome). Each SNP has two alleles — the major allele (more common) and the minor allele.

Types of SNPs

✅

Synonymous SNP (Silent)

Changes the codon but NOT the encoded amino acid (due to redundancy of the genetic code).

UCU → UCC
Both encode Serine

Usually no functional consequence. May still affect splicing or mRNA stability.

⚠️

Non-Synonymous SNP (Missense)

Changes the codon AND the encoded amino acid — alters the protein sequence.

UCU → ACU
Serine → Threonine

May affect protein folding, function, or stability. Pathogenic variants are often non-synonymous.

⚙️ Other SNP Types
  • Nonsense SNP — Non-synonymous SNP that creates a premature stop codon → truncated protein, usually loss-of-function
  • Intronic SNP — Located within an intron; often no effect unless at splice site
  • Regulatory SNP — Located in promoter or enhancer regions; can alter gene expression levels
  • Most SNPs have no health effect — Because they are synonymous, intronic, or in intergenic non-functional regions

SNPs and Genotype

An individual's genotype at a SNP locus refers to the two alleles they carry (one from each chromosome). For a biallelic SNP (A or G), the three possible genotypes are AA, AG, or GG. Genotype at a single SNP does not usually determine phenotype alone — it is the combination of many variants across the genome that matters.

Haplotypes — Co-Segregating Variants

🔑 Definition of a Haplotype

A haplotype is a set of DNA variations (SNPs and/or other polymorphisms) that tend to be inherited together because they are physically close on the same chromosome. "Haplo" = one chromosome; a haplotype describes the particular combination of alleles on one chromosome.

Why Do Nearby SNPs Tend to Be Inherited Together?

During meiosis, chromosomes from each parent are reshuffled via crossover (recombination) events. Two SNPs that are physically close to each other on a chromosome are less likely to have a crossover event between them — they travel together as a unit. This relationship is called linkage disequilibrium (LD).

⚙️ Linkage Disequilibrium

LD describes the non-random association of alleles at two or more loci. SNPs in high LD are almost always inherited together — if you see one, you predict the other. SNPs in LD with a causal variant can serve as proxies or tags for that variant even if they themselves are not functional. This is how GWAS works in practice — the "hit" SNP may not be the causal variant but is in high LD with it.

Polygenic Risk Score (PRS)

🔑 Definition of PRS

A Polygenic Risk Score (PRS) is a single numerical estimate of an individual's genetic predisposition to a disease, calculated by aggregating the effects of many SNPs across the genome — each weighted by its GWAS-derived effect size (odds ratio or beta coefficient).

How PRS is Calculated

For each significant GWAS SNP, an individual receives a "score" based on:

  • Whether they carry 0, 1, or 2 copies of the risk allele at that SNP
  • Multiplied by the log(OR) — the weight/effect size from GWAS
  • The scores from all significant SNPs are summed → one overall PRS
🏥 Clinical Interpretation of PRS

Imagine a bell-curve distribution of PRS in the population. Individuals at the high tail (top ~10%) have a combination of SNPs that collectively confer substantially elevated risk — comparable to some single-gene risk factors. Individuals at the low tail have a protective combination. The middle group has average population risk. PRS is being developed for clinical use in breast cancer screening stratification, cardiovascular risk, and other polygenic diseases.

Q: Define haplotype and explain why SNPs close together on the same chromosome tend to be inherited together.

Answer

A haplotype is a set of DNA variations that tend to be inherited together because they are physically located close to each other on the same chromosome. During meiosis, homologous chromosomes exchange segments through recombination (crossover). Two SNPs that are close together have a very low probability of having a crossover event occur between them — they are therefore co-inherited as a block. This non-random association is called linkage disequilibrium (LD).

LO 4

Evidence Appraisal · Clinical Application

Critically Assessing GWAS Data

As a future clinician, patients will present with GWAS results from DTC genetic tests (23andMe, etc.) or formal clinical genomics reports. You need a systematic framework to evaluate whether a reported association is real, significant, and clinically meaningful.

⚠️ Dr. Harrison's Core Message

"You have to be careful about making clinical decisions based on a lot of this information. Some of it is really good, very strong, clear, and penetrant/actionable, and some of it is not." The goal is to be a critical consumer of genomic data, not just a passive recipient.

Step-by-Step Framework for Evaluating a GWAS Result

  1. Check the P-value — Is it below the genome-wide significance threshold of p < 5 × 10⁻⁸? If not, the association is not genome-wide significant and should be interpreted cautiously. Even a p-value of 10⁻⁶ is not adequate.
  2. Check the Odds Ratio (OR) — What is the effect size? An OR of 1.05 (5% increased risk) is statistically real but clinically trivial. An OR of 2.5 may be clinically actionable. Always ask: does this OR warrant changing clinical management?
  3. Check the sample size and study population — Was the study large enough to detect this effect reliably? What ancestry/ethnicity was the cohort? Most GWAS have been performed in populations of European ancestry — results may not generalise to other populations.
  4. Consider replication — Has the finding been replicated in independent cohorts? Single-study hits without replication should be treated with scepticism.
  5. Assess biological plausibility — Is there a mechanistic explanation? Is the SNP in an oncogene, tumour suppressor, or caretaker gene? An intergenic SNP may tag a regulatory element — or may be a false positive.
  6. Consider clinical utility — Even if an association is statistically robust, can the patient act on this information? Is there an effective intervention? If no action can be taken, the information may cause anxiety without benefit.

Population Stratification — A Critical Limitation

⚠️ Population Bias in GWAS

Most GWAS to date have been conducted in populations of European ancestry (and more recently East Asian ancestry). A SNP that is a risk factor in Europeans may be:

  • Absent or very rare in other populations (different allele frequencies)
  • Protective in a different population (different LD patterns, different environmental context)
  • A different "tag" for an entirely different causal variant in a non-European background

Example from lecture: rs2736098 in the TERT gene is protective against pancreatic cancer in Europeans (OR 0.8) — but at least one study found it may be a risk factor in Asian populations. This is the norm, not the exception.

The Problem with Direct-to-Consumer Tests

DTC tests (23andMe, etc.) report on a subset of known GWAS variants. Critical concerns:

  • Most reported variants have small ORs (1.05–1.3) and are not clinically actionable
  • Results are calibrated for specific ancestry groups — may be inaccurate or uninformative for patients of other backgrounds
  • Finding a "risk variant" does not mean the patient will develop the disease — OR is a population-level statistic, not a deterministic prediction
  • Missing a variant does not mean zero risk — DTC tests cover a fraction of all possible variants
  • Privacy and data security concerns (23andMe data breach)
  • Psychological burden of uncertain results

Clinical Counselling Framework

🏥 When a Patient Brings You a Genetic Result
  1. Identify the SNP — Use GeneCards, UCSC Genome Browser, SNPedia, or GeneReviews to find the gene, its function, and the specific variant.
  2. Assess p-value and OR — Is the association genome-wide significant? Is the OR clinically meaningful?
  3. Consider population relevance — Does this result apply to your patient's ancestry background?
  4. Contextualise within total risk — A small genetic risk increase is often dwarfed by modifiable lifestyle factors. Eating well and not smoking may far outweigh a genetic OR of 1.1.
  5. Identify actionable interventions (if any) — Enhanced surveillance? Chemoprevention? Lifestyle modification? Or no actionable intervention?

Practical Example — The TERT rs2736098 Variant

Case: rs2736098 in the TERT gene

  • Gene: TERT — telomerase reverse transcriptase (maintains telomeres; enables cellular immortality)
  • P-value: 9.8 × 10⁻¹⁴ — well below 5 × 10⁻⁸ → genome-wide significant ✓
  • OR: 0.8 — protective against pancreatic cancer in the studied European cohort
  • Study: 7,683 European cases vs. 14,397 controls
  • Interpretation: This patient's "risk variant" is actually slightly protective in her ancestry background — she should be reassured
  • Caveat: Not necessarily applicable to non-European populations; conflicting data exists for Asian populations

Q: A patient brings you a DTC genetic test result showing SNP rs4645881 with an OR of 1.5 and a P-value of 1 × 10⁻¹¹ in the BAX gene. How do you critically evaluate this result?

Answer

P-value: 1 × 10⁻¹¹ is well below the 5 × 10⁻⁸ genome-wide significance threshold — the association is statistically robust.

Odds ratio: OR = 1.5 means this patient has 1.5× the odds of developing the associated disease compared to someone without the risk allele. This is a moderate effect — potentially clinically relevant but not deterministic.

Variant location: Check whether this SNP is intronic or exonic in BAX. If intronic, it may be tagging a nearby functional variant. BAX is a pro-apoptotic gene — loss of BAX function would inhibit apoptosis, a hallmark of cancer.

Population: Ask which ancestry cohort the study used; consider whether this applies to your patient.

Clinical utility: Determine whether there are actionable interventions. This is a small-to-moderate increase in risk — lifestyle counselling, regular screening, and contextualising against other stronger risk factors (smoking, obesity) is appropriate. This OR does not warrant drastic intervention in isolation.
